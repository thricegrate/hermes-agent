# Launch Models & Million-Dollar Funnel (Cole/Bush)

Source: Nicolas Cole & Dickie Bush (Ship 30 for 30), Low-Ticket Product M&M Course, Modules 2 & 5

## Million-Dollar Funnel: 5-Step Framework

The funnel is deceptively simple. The hard part is consistency over months and years, not setup.

### Step 1: Drive Traffic

3 traffic types:
- **Cold Traffic:** Ads, affiliate links, outreach
- **Warm Traffic:** Organic content, inbound (Cole's primary recommendation)
- **WOM Traffic:** Word of mouth from happy customers

Recommended platforms:
- Text-based: X, LinkedIn
- Video-based: Instagram, YouTube

The ONLY purpose of traffic: collect emails. Social content without an email CTA is wasted effort.

### Step 2: Email Opt-In

Two options depending on experience level:

**Beginner -- Newsletter Landing Page:**
- Simple newsletter signup page
- Worked fine for Cole's first $1M in Ship 30 for 30 sales

**Advanced -- Educational Email Course (EEC):**
- 5-day educational email course as lead magnet
- Higher opt-in conversion rate than plain newsletter signup
- After course completion, subscribers move to weekly newsletter
- Alternatives to EEC: free digital download, ultimate guide, industry report, template pack

### Step 3: Weekly Email Nurture

Core insight: Products sell via email, NOT social media. Social drives traffic. Email drives revenue.

Cole's internal audit (2024): the number of emails needed before someone buys is "way more than you think."

**Beginner cadence -- 1 email/week:**
- 800-1,500 words of free value
- Go deeper on topics performing well on social
- Monday send preferred (but day/time barely matters early on)
- Ship 30 has sent weekly for 4 years straight without missing once

**Advanced cadence -- 2 emails/week (The Clicker System):**
- **Monday:** Free-value email to entire list
- **Thursday:** Objection-handling email to "Clickers" only

### The Clicker Email System

"Clickers" = people who clicked a product link but did NOT buy. Thursday email targets only them.

**3-Part Clicker Email Formula:**
1. **The Question (Objection):** State the objection the person has to taking the next step
2. **The Answer (Reframe):** Help them overcome that faulty belief
3. **The Proof (Testimonial):** Show someone just like them who had the same objection and overcame it

Send some version of this Clicker email 1x per week, every week.

Internal mantra: "The more emails you send, the more money you make." All their data confirms this.

### Step 4: Collect Payment

Landing page sells the product. Checkout form at the bottom collects payment.

### Step 5: Unlock Digital Product (Post-Purchase Flow)

5 moving pieces after purchase:
1. Checkout form (bottom of landing page)
2. Post-purchase Thank You page (redirects immediately)
3. Post-purchase Welcome Email (micro-steps to access product)
4. Automated Skool email invite
5. Skool group (where product content lives)

Flow from customer perspective: Buy -> Thank You page -> Check inbox -> Welcome email + Skool invite -> Click Skool link -> Access product.

## 3 Launch Models

### Model 1: Open/Close Cart

The Jeff Walker model (book: *Launch*). Default since early 2000s.

How it works:
- Build email list in a niche
- Run 1-3 weeks of promotion before launch
- Product available for purchase for 3-7 days ONLY
- Remove product completely (FOMO is the engine)

The "closed" part is what makes sales spike. Without real scarcity, conversions drop.

### Model 2: Evergreen

The opposite of open/close:
- Put product up for sale
- It stays available forever
- Drive traffic and push people to it continuously

Example: Justin Welsh's digital products are always available for sale.

### Model 3: Hybrid (Open/Close Cart + Evergreen Later)

Combine both strategies:
- Execute an open/close cart launch first
- Take the product away for an extended period
- At some point in the future, put it back up as evergreen

**Critical rule:** For open/close to work, you MUST actually take it away. "I'm never selling this again" is a completely different level of FOMO than "Don't worry, it'll be back next week."

---

## Decision Tree: Which Model to Use

| Experience Level | Recommended Model |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Beginner | Evergreen |
| Advanced | Open/Close Cart |
| Expert | Open/Close Cart + Evergreen Later |

**If you're a beginner, do NOT start with open/close cart.** Three reasons:
1. It's more advanced and requires more orchestration
2. You learn more with evergreen (more customers = more learnings)
3. You make more money long-term with evergreen (not as fast, but more over a year+)

General rule: Don't attempt open/close until you already have a solid evergreen product selling consistently.

Cole's own approach: Ship 30 for 30 is always available (evergreen). They layer open/close cart launches 1-3x per year on top.

## Open/Close Cart: Exact Timeline

| When | Action | Details |
|------|--------|---------|
| 3 weeks out | Waitlist email #1 | Email entire list about upcoming product. CTA: "Join the Waitlist." Purpose: gauge interest + build pre-selection bias (saying "I'm interested" increases likelihood of buying). |
| 2 weeks out | Waitlist email #2 | Different version of same email. CTA: "Join the Waitlist." |
| 1 week out | Daily pre-launch sequence | 1 educational email/day for 7 days. Pre-educate on the topic, problems it solves, opportunities it unlocks. CTA: "Join the Waitlist." |
| Launch week | Daily launch sequence | 1 educational email/day. Hard-sell on why this product solves their specific problem and unlocks a specific outcome. |
| Last day | Up to 3 emails | Morning, midday, evening. "Hey, this product goes away tomorrow." |

After cart closes: expect a wave of emails from people who missed the window. Your call whether to let them in.

## Cart Close Redirect Strategy

When cart closes, redirect the product URL to a live Notion page that says:

1. **Product is no longer for sale** -- "See? We told you we were serious about it going away."
2. **Alternative CTA #1** -- Link to a related evergreen product/funnel
3. **Alternative CTA #2** -- Link to another product/funnel in the ecosystem

This turns the closed cart into a redirect page that funnels people to other products. Dead pages earn nothing.

## Evergreen Execution Model

Ship 30 for 30's exact evergreen playbook:

1. **Collect Emails:** Drive all niche social traffic to an opt-in (e.g., startwritingonline.com)
2. **Weekly Newsletter:** Send a free-value email every week to keep the list engaged
3. **Organic CTA/PS:** In every newsletter, mention the product organically. Example: "PS -- If your hooks need work, we have an entire module on hook writing inside Ship 30 for 30. Check it out here."

That's it. Repeat weekly.

Then 4-6x per year, send 5-7 hard-pitch emails to everyone on the list who hasn't bought yet.

## Creative Double-Dipping Strategies

After an open/close launch, there are creative ways to continue selling the "closed" product:

1. **Private Funnel Continuation:** Keep selling that same product inside a specific funnel (not publicly, only behind the scenes). The public cart is closed but the product lives on in a private sequence.

2. **Bundle as Bonus:** Add the "no longer available" product into another product, program, or evergreen community as a bonus asset. It's not for sale standalone, but it's included elsewhere.

3. **Flash Squeeze (Later):** A month, quarter, or year later, email the list: "I've been getting non-stop requests to make this available again. For the next 48 hours, you can grab it. Then it's going away again."

**Warning:** Be careful how hard you lean into "IT'S GOING AWAY FOREVER" messaging if you plan to sell it again later. Credibility matters.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't start with open/close as a beginner.** You'll learn less, make less long-term, and the orchestration is harder.
- **You must ACTUALLY remove the product when cart closes.** If people can still buy it after you said it's gone, you destroy trust and future launch credibility.
- **Don't skip the waitlist phase.** Pre-selection bias (someone saying "I'm interested") significantly increases conversion when the product launches.
- **Don't underestimate email volume needed.** Cole's data shows it takes far more emails than creators expect to convert a subscriber into a buyer.
- **Don't treat social as the sales channel.** Social drives traffic to email. Email drives revenue. Mixing these up is the #1 funnel mistake.
