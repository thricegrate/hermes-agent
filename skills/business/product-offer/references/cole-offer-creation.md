# Cole's Offer Creation System (Low-Ticket Launchpad)

> Source: Nicolas Cole, Low-Ticket Launchpad course
> Module: 3 - Offer Creation Checklist
> Context: Complete framework for creating compelling low-ticket ($350 or below) digital product offers. Use when designing product offers, naming products, or crafting offer copy.

---

## The Core Principle

An Offer is what you are offering the customer. The simplest version:
- What problem are you helping the customer solve?
- And how?

Example: "I will help you fix your golf swing and hit the ball 250 yards... by giving you 10 simple wrist exercises to strengthen your forearms."

But this is just the basic form. The checklist below stacks levers on top of each other until the offer becomes irresistible.

---

## 10-Point Offer Creation Checklist

### 1. Specific Problem

**"Whoever frames the problem owns the solution."**

If you successfully educate someone on a problem, they automatically assume you are equipped and qualified to help solve it. Otherwise, how would you articulate their problem so accurately?

**The customer can't care about your solution until they first care about their problem.** You could have the best solution in the world. But the customer will not see the value unless they have first been made aware of:
- A) A problem they didn't know they had, or
- B) A problem they know they have, but haven't gotten around to solving

Most digital product creators spend too much time thinking about how to make the best product but fail to address the true problem they're solving.

**Pro Tip: Lead with 1 specific problem.**

Selling digital products is more about leading with the right problem to solve, rather than building the perfect product.

- If you try to lead with 10 problems, the customer doesn't know which of the 10 is the priority. (If you say they're "all" the priority, then none of them are the priority.)
- If you lead with 1 problem, the customer immediately knows where to put you in their mind. Then, once you're "in their mind," you can educate them on the other problems you ALSO help them solve.

Take your list of 10 Biggest Problems, and pick the Most Important One. Lead with that.

**Example:** Ship 30 for 30 = "We help you build a daily writing habit." They also help writers solve all the other problems they know are important. But they LEAD with one: you aren't writing consistently, and we can help you fix that.

---

### 2. Solved For A Specific Person

Name a specific target buyer. While you CAN get away with a product that just solves a Specific Problem in a Specific Way, it's much more effective to NAME a specific target buyer.

**Notice the differences:**
- "I help *SalesForce contractors* build a Notion Time-Tracking Dashboard for cold outreach so you stop wasting so much time managing your outbound sales process."
- "I help *founders of marketing agencies doing <$3M in yearly revenue* build a Notion Time-Tracking Dashboard for cold outreach so you stop wasting so much time managing your outbound sales process."
- "I help *freelance graphic designers* build a Notion Time-Tracking Dashboard for cold outreach so you stop wasting so much time managing your outbound sales process."

Same "product" solving the same "problem" but you would speak to the benefits completely differently for each audience.

**5 categories to identify your specific person:**
- **Experience Level:** Are you helping beginners, people who are moderately proficient, or industry veterans with 10+ years of experience?
- **Job Title:** Are you helping freelancers, contractors, gig economy workers, consultants, full-time employees, and/or someone with a very specific job title? (Ex: "Head of Affiliate Partnerships")
- **Language:** Are you helping people in English? Spanish? French? German?
- **Life Transition:** Are you helping people who *just* experienced a large life event? Just got divorced, just got pregnant, just had their first kid, just got laid off, just moved cities, just got a promotion or raise, just changed careers, etc.
- **Purchasing Power:** Are you helping people with no money? People making $10k/mo+? People with a net worth in excess of $1M?

**Pro Tip:** If you're really not sure, just pick your Specific Person by experience level. It's very easy to determine whether you're building a product for "beginners" or "people who are proficient, but don't know how to {solve a specific problem}." If both are the priority, neither is the priority. Pick one.

---

### 3. Solved In A Specific Way

Be extremely clear about the "way" in which you help this Specific Person solve this Specific Problem. The biggest mistake: not making the "Specific Way" clear.

**The difference:**
- A) "I help freelance graphic designers stop wasting so much time managing your outbound sales process." (WAY not specified)
- B) "I help freelance graphic designers **build a Notion Time-Tracking Dashboard for cold outreach** so you stop wasting so much time managing your outbound sales process." (WAY is tangible)

The mistake gets compounded when the "Specific Way" is vague, open-ended, and intangible:
- "I help freelance graphic designers stop wasting so much time managing your outbound sales process by up-leveling their approach."

This fails three tests:
- **It's vague:** we have no idea what that means
- **It's open-ended:** we don't know how to define success
- **It's intangible:** we can't hold or measure "up-level their approach"

**How to make your Specific Way tangible -- deliver a "physical" digital asset:**
- Roadmap
- Blueprint
- Checklist
- Excel Template
- Notion Dashboard
- Keynote Presentation
- Data Analysis Whitepaper
- 30 Pieces Of Published Content

The secret: make the "Specific Way" you solve this problem TANGIBLE. It has to feel like they are getting an object of some kind. Otherwise it's hard for the customer to evaluate why this is valuable and/or how they will objectively measure success.

---

### 4. Achieving An Objective Outcome

Promise the achievement of an OBJECTIVE outcome, not a Subjective one.

**Subjective Outcomes (BAD):**
- "I'll help you live the life of your dreams."
- "I'll help you finally be the person you want to be."
- "I'll help you make that transition and be your best self."

If you ask 10 different people to tell you what a subjective outcome promises, you will hear 10 different answers. That means nobody actually knows what's being promised.

**Objective Outcomes (GOOD) -- binary, measurable:**
- "I'll show you how to land your first $5,000 ghostwriting client."
- "I'll show you how to buy your first single-family rental property."
- "I'll show you how to overcome your fear of social anxiety and go on your first 5 dates."

These either happened... or they didn't.

**Critical nuance for low-ticket products:**

Low-ticket ($350 or below) = selling *information* (Do-It-Yourself).
High-ticket = selling information + some form of implementation (Done-With-You or Done-For-You).

There is a cataclysmic difference between these two promises:
- **BAD:** "If you use this strategy, I guarantee you will attract your first 10,000 followers." (You don't control implementation. Promising and GUARANTEEING an outcome is a mistake.)
- **GOOD:** "If you use this strategy, you will have everything you need to attract your first 10,000 followers." (You promise the INFORMATION. The implementation is up to them.)

---

### 5. In A Specific Time Frame

Not required, but if you can add it your offer immediately becomes more compelling.

**The difference:**
- "I'll help you build your first 10 automations with AI."
- "I'll help you build your first 10 automations with AI **in 72 hours (or less)**."

**3 ways to create urgency through time compression:**
- State the amount of time overtly
- Make "something big" feel like something small, manageable, and quickly achievable
- REMOVE a large time investment

**Example:** Ship 30 for 30 didn't just promise "You'll learn how to start writing online." Many customers had been TRYING for YEARS. Time was the problem. So they compressed the timeframe: "We will help you start writing online in 30 days."

---

### 6. Price-Anchored To Upside 5x-10x What You're Charging

Nothing is "cheap" or "expensive" in isolation. For something to be cheap, it must be cheap *relative to something else*. One of the best ways to overcome price-aversion is to put your offer into context.

**NOTE:** You do NOT accomplish this by simply picking a price and discounting it. That's lazy, ineffective, and means you make less money.

**3 methods to price-anchor to the upside:**

- **DIY Cost (Time):** "You could figure this out on your own. But it's going to take you a long time. Probably north of 100 hours. And whenever you're trying to do something on your own, you run the risk of giving up the moment things get frustrating. Or, you could let me save you all that time."

- **DIY Cost (Money):** "You could figure this out on your own. But what do you value an hour of your time at? If this takes you 100 hours on your own, and you value your time at even just $10 per hour, that means you're going to spend $1,000 trying and failing. Or, you could spend a fraction of that and I can just tell you all the answers."

- **Earning Potential:** "Once you know how to do this, there is no limit on your earning potential. You have the skill. Which means you can monetize the skill over and over again for the rest of your life."

**Example:** Low-Ticket Launchpad itself: "You pay $350 once, to acquire the skill of selling as many $350 products as you want for the rest of your life."

---

### 7. Price-Anchored To Downside 5x-10x What You're Charging

Make the customer aware of what happens if they don't take action. Not fear-mongering. Just making them aware of the risks and hidden costs. Tell the truth.

**The dentist example:**
- "If you don't get Invisalign and fix your bite, you're going to keep grinding your teeth."
- "If you keep grinding your teeth, you're going to suffer more cavities."
- "And if you suffer more cavities and your teeth continue to break down, you're going to have to get multiple root canals later in life."

When you successfully educate customers on what happens if they DON'T fix this problem, they become emotionally connected to the problem. And when they become emotionally connected, they sell themselves.

**4 methods to price-anchor to the downside:**

- **FOMO:** "How much money will you miss out on if you don't fix this problem/take action?"
- **Lost Time/Money:** "How much time/money will you lose if you don't fix this problem/take action?"
- **Emotional Regret:** "How will you feel, at this point in the future, if you don't fix this problem/take action?"
- **Opportunity Cost:** "If this problem persists and you don't fix it, and it keeps eating up time/money, what other things that you care about will you miss out on?"

---

### 8. Named Something Tangible

This might be the most important part of Offer Creation.

**Intangible Offers (BAD):**
- "I'll help you up-level your sales skills."
- "I'll show you how to become the person you were meant to be."
- "I'll give you what you need to be a successful change-maker in the world."

Intangible offers are things you can't hold, can't measure, can't feel, and can't "collect." They're not objects. They're loose phrases with vague, implied meaning.

**How to make your Offer TANGIBLE:**

You want the WAY you help the customer solve their problem be Tangible, or the OUTCOME to be Tangible. (Bonus points if both are Tangible.)

**Example:** Ship 30 for 30's tangible promise: "You'll create 30 pieces of published writing in public."
- You can "hold" 30 pieces of writing
- You can measure 30 pieces of writing
- You can feel and "collect" your 30 pieces of writing
- It's Objective: you either wrote 30 pieces, or you didn't

**The Tangible x Objective combination is the most important part of the entire game.**

If you nail this, your offer is impossible to ignore. If your offer is Intangible x Subjective, it's worthless because the customer doesn't know what you're promising or why it's valuable to them.

---

### 9. Positioned In A Niche Where You Have Experience

You should not launch offers in niches where you have:
- No experience
- No content library
- No audience

**Any offer you create should be based on:**
- Content you've already written for free
- Content you've already validated in public
- In a niche where you have compounding attention

People want to buy products from people who visibly and consistently live and breathe their niche. They reject products from people who are just trying to capitalize on a trend. CONTEXT MATTERS. You don't have to "be an expert," but you need to be actively building experience.

---

### 10. And Proof It Works

Stories and testimonials. The final piece of the puzzle.

**5 Proof Levels (escalating credibility):**

- **Level 1: "This worked for me."** You should be your own case study. Easiest form of proof because you control all variables. Share what you did and the impact it had.

- **Level 2: "This worked for people like me."** Show how other people with similar background, education, starting point, skills, and limiting beliefs used your approach and achieved similar results.

- **Level 3: "This also worked for people NOT like me."** True social proof territory. Your offer carries a completely different weight when you can prove it also works for people who are NOTHING like you. Allows you to tap into wider audiences.

- **Level 4: "This has also worked across these different industries."** Dismantles the objection: "OK I see how that worked for you in that niche, but I'm in a different niche so I don't think it will work for me." Widens your addressable market.

- **Level 5: "This has also worked for a long time."** Time-tested. The longer you stay in business, the more compelling your offer becomes. More proof acquired, more credibility, easier to launch next offer. Compounding game.

**No proof yet?** Give 10-20 people free access as "beta-users" in exchange for a testimonial at the end. Easy way to power-level your first testimonials.

## 20 Product Naming Conventions

The rule: as long as it's tangible, it's probably a good name. Here are 20 naming conventions:

1. **[Number] for [Number]**
   - Example: "Ship 30 for 30"
   - Why It Works: Anchors the user to a specific quantity and a defined timeframe. Clear, immediate, and tangible outcome: "I'll achieve X in Y days."

2. **[Topic/Skill] OS**
   - Example: "LinkedOS"
   - Why It Works: Positions your product as an "Operating System," implying a structured, foundational system the user can install and run.

3. **The [Adjective] [Blueprint/Playbook/Toolkit]**
   - Example: "The Ultimate Ghostwriter's Blueprint"
   - Why It Works: "Blueprint," "Playbook," "Toolkit," etc., all sound like physical artifacts. Adding an adjective (e.g., "Ultimate," "Rapid," "Essential") emphasizes completeness.

4. **[Topic/Skill] in a Box**
   - Example: "Podcasting in a Box"
   - Why It Works: Conjures the image of an all-inclusive "kit" that contains everything you need to solve a specific problem.

5. **The [Number]-Day [Challenge/Accelerator]**
   - Example: "The 14-Day YouTube Accelerator"
   - Why It Works: Creates built-in urgency, a definitive time commitment, and a sense of community involvement ("challenge") or speed ("accelerator").

6. **The [Topic] Roadmap**
   - Example: "The Ghostwriting Roadmap"
   - Why It Works: "Roadmap" implies step-by-step direction, with tangible checkpoints along the way.

7. **The [Topic] Toolkit**
   - Example: "The Freelance Pricing Toolkit"
   - Why It Works: "Toolkit" suggests practical, hands-on resources or templates a user can "pick up" and apply.

8. **The [Topic] Vault**
   - Example: "The Content Growth Vault"
   - Why It Works: Suggests a hidden repository of resources, templates, or guides that feel exclusive and valuable.

9. **The [Topic] Launch Pad**
   - Example: "The Low-Ticket Launchpad"
   - Why It Works: Communicates that your program will "launch" users toward a goal. Quick momentum and a clear starting point.

10. **[Number] Steps to [Outcome]**
    - Example: "7 Steps to Your First $1,000 Ghostwriting Client"
    - Why It Works: Highlights an exact pathway with a finite number of steps, making the product feel concise and doable.

11. **The [Topic] Playbook**
    - Example: "The LinkedIn Leads Playbook"
    - Why It Works: "Playbook" indicates a strategic, tactical document, like a sports team's key plays that can be followed for consistent results.

12. **The [Topic] Bootcamp**
    - Example: "The 30-Day Content Writing Bootcamp"
    - Why It Works: Implies hands-on, rigorous training with tangible milestones. No fluff, just direct, action-focused learning.

13. **The [Topic] Sprint**
    - Example: "The Blog Launch Sprint"
    - Why It Works: Connotes speed and intensity over a short period. Perfect for someone who wants quick wins.

14. **The [Topic] Command Center**
    - Example: "The Social Media Command Center"
    - Why It Works: A "command center" sounds like a centralized control hub, giving the impression of powerful oversight and organization.

15. **The [Topic] Protocol**
    - Example: "The Ghostwriting Client Acquisition Protocol"
    - Why It Works: "Protocol" suggests a tested, formalized procedure, giving the product a sense of authority and rigor.

16. **The [Topic] Lab**
    - Example: "The Email Marketing Lab"
    - Why It Works: Implies experimentation and discovery. Users expect hands-on exercises, templates, and the freedom to test ideas.

17. **The [Topic] Method**
    - Example: "The 5-Figure Freelancer Method"
    - Why It Works: "Method" implies a replicable system. Highly useful for establishing credibility ("I have a *method*, not just random tips").

18. **The [Topic] Starter Pack**
    - Example: "The Instagram Growth Starter Pack"
    - Why It Works: Positions your offer as a curated "starter kit" of resources, checklists, or tools for immediate set-up and quick wins.

19. **The [Topic] Accelerator**
    - Example: "The Ghostwriter Accelerator"
    - Why It Works: Suggests rapid progress or fast-track success toward a specific outcome, with the connotation of leveling up quickly.

20. **The [Topic] Workbook**
    - Example: "The Content Repurposing Workbook"
    - Why It Works: A "workbook" is inherently tangible. People envision step-by-step exercises, worksheets, and fill-in-the-blank templates they can literally (or digitally) write in.

## Offer 1-Liner / Subtitle Templates

Once you have a tangible product name, assemble a simple 1-liner (the product's "subtitle"). This is the short explanation and elevator pitch for your landing page, marketing copy, emails, and social content CTAs.

**Lead with these 6 pieces in your 1-liner:**
1. Specific Problem
2. Solved For A Specific Person
3. Solved In A Specific Way
4. Achieving An Objective Outcome
5. In A Specific Time Frame
6. Named Something Tangible

**Save these 4 for further down the landing page / in marketing emails:**
1. Price-Anchored To Upside
2. Price-Anchored to Downside
3. Positioned In Your Niche
4. Proof It Works

**3 templates for mixing and matching:**

- **Template 1:** {Problem Solved} AND {Achieve Objective Outcome} in {A Specific Time Frame}
  - Example: "Overcome your fear of writing online and publish your first 10 Tweets in 10 days"

- **Template 2:** {Problem Solved or Achieving An Objective Outcome} BY DOING {Specific Way}
  - Example: "Build the ultimate productivity tracker and instantly reclaim 10 hours per week using The Excel Shortcut System"

- **Template 3:** {Achieving An Objective Outcome} FOR {Specific Person}
  - Example: "Build your first revenue-generating SaaS tool as a No-Code Developer"

## Integration Notes: Mapping to Existing Frameworks

### Cole's 10-Point Checklist vs. Hormozi's Value Equation

Hormozi's Value Equation: Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)

| Cole's Checklist | Hormozi's Value Equation |
|---|---|
| Specific Problem + Objective Outcome | Dream Outcome (numerator) |
| Proof It Works (5 levels) | Perceived Likelihood of Achievement (numerator) |
| Specific Time Frame | Time Delay (denominator -- compress it) |
| Solved In A Specific Way (tangible asset) | Effort & Sacrifice (denominator -- reduce it with a ready-made system) |
| Specific Person | Targeting (not in equation, but determines WHICH dream outcome resonates) |
| Price-Anchored Upside/Downside | Framing the equation's output as a no-brainer relative to price |

Cole's checklist is essentially the practical implementation layer for Hormozi's equation. Hormozi gives you the math. Cole gives you the 10 fields to fill in.

### Cole's Tangibility Principle vs. Kennedy's "Thud Factor"

Dan Kennedy's "thud factor" = the physical weight and perceived volume of what the customer receives. The heavier the package hits the table, the more valuable it feels.

Cole's tangibility principle is the digital equivalent:
- Kennedy: physical binders, DVDs, printed manuals, bonus booklets
- Cole: Roadmaps, Blueprints, Checklists, Dashboards, Templates, Workbooks

Both solve the same problem: intangible promises feel worthless. Tangible objects feel valuable. Cole's naming conventions (OS, Vault, Toolkit, Command Center, Lab, etc.) are all ways to create "digital thud factor." The name itself should make the customer feel like they're getting an object, not a concept.

### Cole's 5 Proof Levels vs. Hormozi's 13-Point Proof Checklist

Cole's proof levels are a simplified, progressive version of Hormozi's proof architecture:

- **Cole Level 1** ("worked for me") = Hormozi's "personal results" + "case studies you participated in"
- **Cole Level 2** ("worked for people like me") = Hormozi's "client testimonials" + "similar demographics"
- **Cole Level 3** ("worked for people NOT like me") = Hormozi's "diverse case studies" + "unexpected niches"
- **Cole Level 4** ("worked across industries") = Hormozi's "cross-industry proof" + "scalability evidence"
- **Cole Level 5** ("worked for a long time") = Hormozi's "longevity proof" + "compounding trust"

Cole's model is simpler and specifically designed for low-ticket context where you don't need all 13 Hormozi proof points. For a $27-$350 product, hitting Levels 1-3 is usually sufficient. Levels 4-5 are for scaling and widening the market.
