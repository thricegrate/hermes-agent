## 1. Business Genie Framework

**Source:** $100M Pricing Playbook, pp. 7-15
**Type:** Mental model (pricing leverage hierarchy)

The genie gives you three wishes. Each one doubles a different revenue lever. Only one of them is the right wish.

**The stable business baseline:**
- 30 new clients/month
- 33% monthly churn
- $100/month price
- 100 total clients at steady state (30 / 0.33)
- $10,000/month revenue
- 20% margins = $2,000/month profit

**Wish 1: Double your customers (60 new/month)**
- Revenue: $20,000/month
- But CAC roughly halves per-customer margin
- Effective margin rises to ~35%
- Profit: ~$7,000/month
- Result: 3.5x profit

**Wish 2: Double your purchases (halve churn to 16.5%)**
- Steady state: 30 / 0.165 = ~182 clients
- Revenue: $18,200/month
- Delivery costs rise proportionally, but acquisition costs stay flat
- Effective margin: ~35%
- Profit: ~$7,000/month
- Result: 3.5x profit

**Wish 3: Double your price ($200/month)**
- Revenue: $20,000/month
- Costs stay flat (same number of clients, same delivery)
- Margin jumps to 60%
- Profit: $12,000/month
- Result: 6x profit

**Why pricing wins:**
- Pricing improvement is 4x more efficient than acquisition at increasing profit
- Pricing improvement is 2x more efficient than retention at increasing profit
- Source: Profitwell study of 512 SaaS companies
- Additional finding: companies that test pricing more frequently make more profit AND grow faster

**Bottom line:** When someone asks you how to grow, the answer is almost always "raise your prices" before "get more customers."

## 2. Three Models of Pricing

**Source:** $100M Pricing Playbook, pp. 18-20
**Type:** Methodology

Three approaches ranked from worst to best:

**Model 1: Cost Plus**
- Your costs + a margin percentage
- Simple but ignores willingness to pay
- Costs change, so your price floats on variables you don't control
- Default for lazy operators

**Model 2: Competitor Based**
- Average what the market charges, price around it
- "You're using a pricing strategy based on THEIR business and THEIR customers"
- Copycat strategy that assumes competitors priced correctly (they didn't)

**Model 3: Value Based**
- Charge based on what the customer is willing to pay for the outcome
- Forces you to continuously increase value
- Enables price raises as you add value
- The only model worth pursuing

**Three metrics to find the right value-driven price:**
1. Conversion rate at current price
2. Churn rate at current price
3. The Optimization Table:

```
Price x Clicks x Conv Rate x Sales x Churn x LTV x Total Return
```

Goal: maximize Total Return (conv rate x LTV), NOT maximize sales volume. A lower conversion rate at a higher price often produces more total money.

## 3. 16 Rules of Pricing

**Source:** $100M Pricing Playbook, pp. 22-24
**Type:** Decision checklist

These are the beliefs and constraints behind all pricing decisions. The 10 Plays are built on top of these rules.

1. Raising prices makes money two ways: more gross profit per customer AND less cost to deliver (fewer customers to serve)
2. Price to make the most MONEY, not sell the most CUSTOMERS
3. Higher prices get fewer customers but often make more money
4. High close rate (>50%) = prices too low
5. Full capacity = prices too low
6. Fixed capacity + sold out = raise prices immediately
7. Keep raising until extra money no longer offsets loss in sales
8. Raising prices usually means raising value (which is the point)
9. More "no"s doesn't mean less money
10. NPS scores reveal when prices are too high (track satisfaction alongside price changes)
11. Multiple customer types need different prices (2-3 avatars with 5-10x willingness-to-pay difference)
12. Name pricing tiers after aspirational titles customers want for themselves
13. Believe in your price (conviction sells)
14. Bill less frequently = lower churn
15. Display price in smallest increment, bill on longest increment ("$5/day" but bill $1,825/year)
16. Match billing to how you provide value (separate one-time value from ongoing value)

## 4. 10 Instant Profit Pricing Plays

**Source:** $100M Pricing Playbook, pp. 27-58
**Type:** Tactical system (stackable)

Each play includes the math, implementation, and expected impact. They're designed to stack. Applied together: +26.8% to +63.8% revenue increase.

### Play 1: 28-Day Billing Cycles (+8.3% revenue)

Switch from calendar-month billing to every-28-day billing.

```
Monthly billing:  12 cycles/year
28-day billing:   13 cycles/year
Revenue increase: +8.3%
At 20% margins:   margins jump to 26.1% = +41.5% profit increase
```

Display the price weekly ("$X/week"), bill every 4 weeks. Zero change in conversion. Customers don't notice 28 days vs "monthly."

### Play 2: Processing Fees + Second Payment (+3-4% or +14-51% LTV)

Two sub-plays that stack:

**2a: Processing fee pass-through**
- Add 3.99% credit card processing fee as line item
- Offer to waive it if they provide a backup payment method
- Revenue bump: +3-4%

**2b: Second form of payment**
- Backup card eliminates involuntary churn from expired/declined cards
- Card info changes cause 1.2-1.7% monthly involuntary churn
- That's 24-34% of total churn if your total is around 5%
- Eliminating it: +14% to +51% LTV increase depending on base churn rate

### Play 3: Sales Tax Pass-Through (0-10%)

Stop absorbing sales tax. Break it out as a separate line item.

Script: cite the tax code, be matter-of-fact. "Sales tax is required by [state]. Here's the breakdown." Don't apologize. Don't explain.

Impact varies by state/country: 0% to 10%+ depending on jurisdiction.

### Play 4: Annual CPI Increases (+3-10%/yr compounding)

Build automatic annual price increases into every contract, tied to Consumer Price Index.

Script: "To reinvest in keeping our integrity and delivering at the highest quality, we keep prices standard with the CPI."

```
5% annual increase compounding:
  Year 1: $100
  Year 2: $105
  Year 3: $110.25
  Year 4: $115.76
  = +22% increase over 4 years

12% annual increase compounding:
  Year 1: $100
  Year 4: $157.35
  = +57% increase over 4 years
```

This is free money. Build it in from day one and nobody questions it.

### Play 5: Annual Billing Options (+10-15%)

Offer annual billing as the default option instead of monthly.

```
Monthly churn rate by billing cycle (Profitwell data):
  Monthly billing:    10.7% churn
  Quarterly billing:  5.0% churn
  Annual billing:     2.0% churn

LTV multiplier:
  Annual vs Monthly = 5.35x LTV
  Quarterly vs Monthly = 2.14x LTV
```

Take rates:
- On page (option visible): 10-15% select annual
- Default to annual (opt out to monthly): 30% select annual
- Over phone (sales rep recommends): 35-40% select annual

Frame it as: "Buy 10 months, get 2 free" (that's a 16% discount but you get 100% of the cash upfront).

### Play 6: Round Up (+1-3%)

Change 7s to 9s. Add .99 to all prices.

```
$47 -> $49.99/week
= +$155.48/year per client
= +6.36% to +11.1% price increase
At 12.5% margins: this nearly doubles profit
```

Exception: luxury/premium items use round numbers ($5,000 not $4,999). Round numbers signal prestige.

### Play 7: Annual Renewal Fee (+10%)

Add a yearly fee on top of the monthly rate. Frame it as "rate protection" or "cancellation fee prepayment."

```
0.5x monthly fee as annual fee = +4.15% revenue
1.0x monthly fee as annual fee = +8.3% revenue
2.0x monthly fee as annual fee = +16.6% revenue
3.0x monthly fee as annual fee = +24.9% revenue
```

Advertise the low monthly price. The annual fee is disclosed but doesn't anchor the decision. Gyms, SaaS, and membership businesses use this everywhere.

### Play 8: Automatic Continuity (+10%)

After the main offering ends, automatically roll customers into a stripped-down version at 5-20% of the original price.

```
Main program:     $2,000/mo x 4 months x 70% margins = $5,600 LTV
Continuity:       $200/mo x 20 months x 90% margins x 50% take rate = $1,800
New LTV:          $7,400 (+32%)
```

Sunk cost fallacy keeps them subscribed. The continuity product is cheap to deliver (community access, recorded content, group calls). The margin is enormous.

### Play 9: Ultra High Ticket Anchor (+10-15%)

Add a "mac daddy" version priced at 10x the standard offer.

```
Before anchor:
  80% buy Standard at $500 + 20% buy Basic at $200
  = $440 average LTV

After anchor:
  10% buy Premium at $5,000 + 70% buy Standard at $500 + 20% buy Basic at $200
  = $890 average LTV (2x!)
```

Even if only 10% take the ultra-high option, it doubles average LTV. Sneaky secondary benefit: anchoring makes the standard offer feel cheap, boosting close rates across the board.

### Play 10: Guarantee/Warranty Upsells (+5-20%)

Sell risk insurance at 5-30% of the product price. Pure profit engine.

```
20 warranties sold x $100 each = $2,000 revenue
1 claim honored x $100 cost    = $100
Net profit from warranty program = $1,900 (95% margin)
```

Apple built a multi-billion dollar business on this play alone. The close is almost passive: "You just want the standard warranty on that?"

## 5. RAISE Price Increase Letter

**Source:** $100M Price Raise Playbook, pp. 13-18
**Type:** Communication template

Five-section letter for telling existing customers about a price increase. Proven across 500+ price increases (Patrick Campbell / Profitwell collaboration).

**R - Remind them of value already provided**
- Personalize with data: revenue generated, features used, time as customer
- "Over the past [X months], you've [specific results/usage]..."

**A - Address the price change directly**
- One sentence. Rip off the bandaid.
- "Starting [date], your rate will change from $X to $Y."

**I - Invest in their future**
- List 3+ investments you're making with the extra revenue
- Frame as more good stuff / less bad stuff FOR THEM
- "Here's what we're putting the additional investment into..."

**S - Soften with a loyalty reward**
- Vanishing discount for 3-6 months
- Position as credit, not discount
- "Because you've been with us since [date], you'll keep your current rate for the next [3-6] months."
- New customers pay full price starting today

**E - Explain away concerns**
- PS statement: "If this materially impacts your business, reach out to me personally and we'll work something out."
- Turn off comments if posting in community
- Route all concerns to 1-on-1 conversation

**Three types of respondents:**
1. See the value, comply without issue (majority)
2. Actually financially affected (extend their discount, case by case)
3. Were going to cancel anyway ("pulled forward" churn, not caused by the raise)

For new customers going forward: "This is the cheapest it'll ever be."

## 6. Price Raise Checklist (13 Steps)

**Source:** $100M Price Raise Playbook, p. 26
**Type:** Operational checklist (sequential)

1. Decide on the price increase amount
2. Test with new customers first (validate they buy AND stay at rates that increase total money)
3. Segment "old" customers who came in before the raise
4. Write 1-5 bullets of current value with personalized data per customer
5. Tell them the price raise happens now (direct, one sentence)
6. Write 3 bullets of your biggest investments with the additional profit
7. Explain how those investments benefit THEM specifically
8. Give an expiring discount as a loyalty reward (3-6 months)
9. Tell them you respond personally to concerns
10. Sign in ink if possible (personal touch)
11. Finish with PS statement ("reach out if this ruins your life/business")
12. Do individually (phone/video) if raise is 50%+ and you can manage call volume
13. Stair-step the discount at 6-month intervals for large raises (disappearing discounts beat sudden price jumps)

## 7. Vicious vs Virtuous Price Cycle

**Source:** $100M Price Raise Playbook, pp. 7-8
**Type:** Mental model (feedback loops)

Two opposing spirals created by pricing direction. Every pricing decision pushes you into one or the other.

### The Vicious Cycle (lowering prices)

1. Client emotional investment decreases (they paid less, so they care less)
2. Perceived value decreases (cheap = must not be that good)
3. Results decrease (less invested clients do less work)
4. Client demandingness increases ("cheapest customers ask for the most")
5. Service level decreases (less money to spend on delivery)
6. Business impact: profit drops, self-worth drops, conviction in sales drops, gratitude for customers drops
7. So you lower prices again to attract more volume... and the spiral accelerates

### The Virtuous Cycle (raising prices)

1. Client emotional investment increases (they paid more, they show up harder)
2. Perceived value increases (expensive = must be premium)
3. Results increase (invested clients do the work)
4. Client demandingness decreases ("people who pay the most are the most easygoing")
5. Service level increases (more money = race to the top on delivery)
6. Business impact: profit grows, conviction grows, results get better, referrals increase
7. So you raise prices again... and the spiral accelerates upward

The cycles are self-reinforcing. You pick which spiral you're on with every pricing decision.

## 8. Crazy Eight LTV Framework

**Source:** $100M Lifetime Value Playbook, pp. 10-27
**Type:** System (8 ways to increase LTV)

Hormozi drills his team on this framework for every business they evaluate. Eight levers, split into upsells and downsells.

### Way 1: Raise Prices
- Test every quarter
- 20% price increase on a 10% margin business = 3x profit (not 20% more profit)
- Method: nudge price 20% every 10 sales until you see a dramatic drop, then step back to the sweet spot

### Way 2: Decrease Costs
Nine specific tactics:
1. Increase employee-to-customer ratio
2. Offshore talent for non-customer-facing roles
3. Productize delivery (systematize what was custom)
4. Shift DFY to DWY (done-for-you to done-with-you)
5. Cap usage (limit hours, sessions, access)
6. Move from lifetime to annual commitments
7. Switch in-person to remote delivery
8. Cut meeting times (60 min to 30 min)
9. Buy in bulk / prepay vendors for 10-20% discounts

### Way 3: Increase Purchase Frequency
Three methods:
1. Add a recurring component (membership, subscription, retainer)
2. Decrease churn (fewer people leaving = more repeat purchases)
3. Follow-up engine: quarterly promotions + ongoing value to your existing list

### Way 4: Cross-Sell Something Different
- Add a complementary product
- LTV math: old LTV + (conversion rate x gross profit of cross-sell)
- Keep it easy to add with existing infrastructure (don't build an entirely new business)

### Way 5: Upsell Quantity (Sell More)
Three sub-types:
1. Bulk: prepay for a year instead of monthly
2. More often: monthly service to every 3 weeks
3. Bigger: 1-hour session to 3-hour session
- Always offer the upsell first, then downsell to standard if they decline

### Way 6: Upsell Quality (Sell Better)
17 premium version dimensions you can charge more for:
1. Faster response times
2. Extended time availability (evenings, weekends)
3. More days of week
4. More times of day
5. Longer session length
6. More location access (multiple offices, virtual + in-person)
7. Cancellation fees (creates stickiness)
8. Faster speed of response
9. Faster speed of delivery
10. Better service ratio (fewer clients per rep)
11. Better communication method (text > email, video > text)
12. Higher provider qualifications (senior team, certified experts)
13. Live vs recorded
14. In-person vs remote
15. DIY -> DWY -> DFY (each step up = higher price)
16. Longer expirations (access for 12 months vs 3 months)
17. More personalization (custom vs template)

### Way 7: Downsell Quantity (Sell Fewer)
- Fewer units, less frequent, smaller version
- ONLY for unqualified prospects
- "I forbid my sales team from selling a qualified person a downsell."

### Way 8: Downsell Quality (Sell Worse)
- Reverse the quality upsell list above
- Lower quality version at a lower price
- ONLY for unqualified prospects
- Never offer a downsell before the prospect has said no to the full offer

## 9. LTGP Calculation Method

**Source:** $100M Lifetime Value Playbook, pp. 7-9
**Type:** 3-step formula

### Step 1: Calculate Gross Profit per Unit

```
Revenue per unit - Cost to deliver = Gross Profit
```

Example: $3,000/mo revenue, $600/mo delivery cost = $2,400 gross profit (80% margin)

### Step 2: Determine Transactions or Churn Rate

- For product businesses: pull average number of transactions per customer from your CRM
- For recurring businesses: Churn = People Who Left / Original Customer Pool
- Note: new signups during the measurement period do NOT affect the churn calculation

### Step 3: Multiply or Divide to Get LTGP

```
Products:   LTGP = Gross Profit per Unit x Avg # of Transactions
Recurring:  LTGP = Gross Profit per Period / Churn Rate %
```

Example (recurring): $2,400 / 0.05 = $48,000 LTGP

**Key insights:**
- LTGP always increases as the business ages (long-tenured customers keep buying)
- "The cost of getting a customer can only hit zero, but how much you make from each can go infinitely high"
- Track LTGP monthly. If it's not going up, something is broken.

## 10. Favorite Upsell Formula

**Source:** $100M Lifetime Value Playbook, pp. 5-6
**Type:** Formula

Hormozi used this to 2.2x LTV and enable 5x advertising scale.

**The formula:**

> "More of, or more help with, what they just bought... but with faster results, less risk, less effort, less hassle... for more money."

**Components:**
1. More of what they just bought (or more help with it)
2. Faster results
3. Less risk
4. Less effort / less hassle
5. For more money

**Rules:**
- Must solve "their next problem," not your passion project
- "If customers like your stuff, they want to buy more stuff like it"
- Test by surveying customers: "Which of these would you want next?"
- The best upsells feel like a natural continuation, not a new sales pitch

## 11. Big Head Long Tail Model

**Source:** $100M Retention Playbook, p. 27
**Type:** Pricing structure

Charge a large one-time fee upfront for high-value one-time deliverables, then a small recurring fee for ongoing value.

```
Example:
  Big Head:  $6,800 upfront (education, setup, implementation)
  Long Tail: $199/mo recurring (community, support, accountability)
  LTV grows from $6,800 to $12,800+ as months stack
```

**Two entry paths:**
1. Sell high-ticket in a free community, then upsell to paid recurring
2. Sell paid recurring first, then upsell high-ticket over phone

**Critical rule:** Include the recurring fee WITH the upfront fee at point of sale, not as a separate sale later. This preserves the price anchor. "It's $6,800 and that includes your first 3 months of community access at $199/mo after that."

The recurring base compounds as customer count grows. Even small monthly fees become meaningful at scale. "The bigger the head, the longer the tail."

## 12. Lookback Window of Value

**Source:** $100M Pricing Playbook, p. 24
**Type:** Mental model

Customers evaluate value within the lookback window of their last billing cycle, not the entire relationship.

If you bill monthly and deliver $60K in value in month 1 but $0 in month 2, they cancel in month 2. Doesn't matter that they're $60K ahead overall. Their lookback window only sees the current billing period.

**Three implications:**
1. Value is judged per billing cycle, not cumulatively
2. Longer billing = larger lookback window = more forgiveness for uneven delivery
3. Longer billing also pre-qualifies customers (they pay more upfront, so they're more committed)

**Application:** If your product delivers value in bursts (big implementation upfront, then maintenance), bill annually or semi-annually. Monthly billing on lumpy value delivery is a churn machine.

## 13. Customer Surplus Model

**Source:** $100M Pricing Playbook, p. 24
**Type:** Mental model

```
Customer Surplus = Perceived Value - Price
```

The surplus is also called "goodwill." It's the gap between what customers think they got and what they paid. This gap drives three things:
1. Word of mouth (they tell people because the deal was so good)
2. Repeat purchases (they come back because they trust the value)
3. Brand loyalty (they defend you against competitors)

**The wrong way:** Create surplus through low prices (race to the bottom, see Vicious Cycle).

**The right way:** Charge premium prices, use the margin to make a far superior product, create surplus through excellence. The customer pays more AND gets more surplus because the product is genuinely 10x better than alternatives.

## 14. Value Grid

**Source:** $100M Lost Chapters, pp. 75-85
**Type:** Strategic framework (replaces the Value Ladder)

The traditional "Value Ladder" is a stair-step: free -> low ticket -> mid ticket -> high ticket. Linear. Customers climb one rung at a time.

The Value Grid replaces this with a two-dimensional structure:
- **Rows** = price tiers (free, low, mid, high, ultra-high)
- **Columns** = different product categories or problem areas

**Why it's better:**
- Multiple entry points at different price levels (a customer can start at high-ticket)
- Lateral movement (buy different products at the same price tier)
- Non-linear customer journeys (skip tiers, move sideways, jump around)
- Customers navigate based on their needs, not a forced ascension path

**Application:** Map your current products onto a grid. Identify empty cells. Those empty cells are product opportunities. Don't just build a ladder and force everyone to climb it in order.

## 15. Ultimate Offer Stacking Process

**Source:** $100M Lost Chapters, pp. 75-90
**Type:** 4-phase methodology

Each phase has a specific financial job in the business.

**Phase 1: Attract**
- Free or low-cost offer that gets them in the door
- Lead magnet, free trial, loss leader, free tool
- Financial job: generate leads

**Phase 2: Up Front Cash**
- Initial paid offer presented immediately
- Front-end product ($27-$97)
- Financial job: cover customer acquisition cost (CAC)

**Phase 3: Upsell / Downsell**
- Additional offers presented right after purchase
- Order bumps, one-time offers, cross-sells
- Financial job: generate immediate profit

**Phase 4: Continuity**
- Recurring revenue component
- Subscription, membership, retainer, ongoing service
- Financial job: build lifetime gross profit (LTGP)

Stack all four phases. Phase 1 fills the pipeline. Phase 2 funds acquisition. Phase 3 creates profit on day one. Phase 4 compounds over time.

## 16. Product Delivery Cheat Codes

**Source:** $100M Offers, pp. 118-120
**Type:** 6 divergent-thinking questions

For each problem on your customer's list, run it through all six questions:

1. What would I do if I could only charge **one time**?
2. What would I do if I could only charge on a **recurring** basis?
3. What would I do if it had to be done **in person**?
4. What would I do if it had to be done **remotely/digitally**?
5. What would I do if I had to let them **do it themselves** (DIY)?
6. What would I do if I had to **do it for them** (DFY)?

Then combine the best answers into your offer. These force you beyond your default delivery model. Most entrepreneurs only think in one dimension (usually digital DIY). These questions break that pattern.

Based on the Brick Exercise: "How many uses can you think of for a brick?" Applied to offer design through divergent thinking.

## 17. 5-Step Grand Slam Creation

**Source:** $100M Offers, pp. 103-126
**Type:** Sequential build process

1. **Identify Dream Outcome** - What does your customer want more than anything? Not what you sell. What THEY want.

2. **List Every Problem** - Every obstacle, objection, concern, and worry they'll encounter before, during, and after getting the result. Be exhaustive. 20-50 problems minimum.

3. **Create Solutions List** - For every problem, brainstorm solutions using the 6 Cheat Code questions above. Go wide. Quantity over quality at this stage.

4. **Choose Delivery Vehicles** - Package solutions into concrete deliverables: courses, templates, calls, software, done-for-you services, community access, etc.

5. **Trim and Stack** - Remove low-value items, keep high-value/low-cost items, stack them into one compelling offer.

**Trim rule:** Cut items that are high cost to you but low perceived value to them. Keep items that are low cost to you but high perceived value to them. The goal is maximum value gap at maximum margin.

## 18. Three Types of Scarcity

**Source:** $100M Offers, pp. 142-150
**Type:** Categorized scarcity tactics

**Type 1: Total Business Cap**
- "I only take on X clients total."
- Strongest form of scarcity
- Limits growth, but creates genuine exclusivity
- Best for: ultra-premium positioning

**Type 2: Growth Rate Cap**
- "I only take on X new clients per month/quarter."
- Sustainable scarcity with natural replenishment
- Can grow while maintaining scarcity
- Best for: high-ticket services, coaching programs

**Type 3: Cohort Cap**
- "This cohort only has X spots. Next cohort opens in Y weeks."
- Recurring scarcity with built-in urgency
- Naturally creates deadlines
- Best for: courses, group programs, seasonal offers

**Implementation rule:** Only use scarcity you can actually enforce. Fake scarcity destroys trust permanently. If you say 10 spots, sell 10 spots and close enrollment. Period.

## 19. Four Types of Urgency

**Source:** $100M Offers, pp. 152-157
**Type:** Categorized urgency tactics

**Type 1: Cohort-Based Rolling**
- "Next group starts [date]. After that, wait X weeks for the next one."
- Creates natural enrollment windows
- Pairs well with Cohort Cap scarcity

**Type 2: Rolling Seasonal**
- Tied to calendar events, seasons, annual cycles
- "Back to school special ends Friday." "New Year enrollment closes Jan 15."
- Genuine external deadlines

**Type 3: Pricing/Bonus-Based**
- "Price goes up Monday." "Bonus disappears at midnight."
- The offer changes, not access
- You can still buy after the deadline, but at worse terms
- Most flexible and repeatable

**Type 4: Exploding Opportunity**
- External market conditions create genuine urgency
- "Algorithm changed. Act now before competitors adapt."
- Hardest to manufacture, most compelling when real
- Use when market shifts create a genuine window

## 20. Bonus Bullets (11 Rules)

**Source:** $100M Offers, pp. 159-166
**Type:** Checklist for bonus construction

1. Always offer bonuses (no exceptions)
2. Give each bonus a benefit-driven name (not a description)
3. Tell them: what it is, how you discovered/created it, how it will specifically help them
4. Provide proof that it works (testimonials, case studies, data)
5. Paint a vivid picture of how their life will be different with the bonus
6. Assign a price tag to each bonus (what it would cost if sold separately)
7. Each bonus should address a specific objection or barrier to the core offer
8. Bonuses should have a higher perceived value than the core offer (total bonus value > price)
9. Always anchor the combined value against the actual price ("$12,000 in total value, yours for $497")
10. Stack bonuses in order of ascending value (save the best for last)
11. Use scarcity/urgency on at least one bonus ("first X buyers also get...")

**Key principle:** Add bonuses instead of discounts. Bonuses expand value. Discounts shrink it. Same revenue impact, opposite psychological effect.

## 21. Guarantee Stacking

**Source:** $100M Offers, pp. 169-184
**Type:** Multi-layer guarantee construction

**Guarantee creation formula:**
> "If you do not get [X result] in [Y time period], we will [Z]."

**Five types of guarantees (stackable):**

| Type | Structure | When to Use |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Unconditional | No questions asked money back | Low-ticket, high volume |
| Conditional | Money back if you do X, Y, Z and don't get result | Mid-to-high ticket, filters for effort |
| Anti-guarantee | "All sales final" | When demand exceeds supply |
| Implied | "We don't stop until you get the result" | Service businesses, ongoing delivery |
| Performance-based | "We work for free until you hit [metric]" | High-ticket, high confidence in delivery |

**Stacking example:**
"If you complete all 8 modules and don't get [result] in 90 days, we'll refund you in full (conditional). Plus, you can cancel in the first 30 days for any reason (unconditional). And if you stay, we'll keep working with you at no extra cost until you hit the goal (implied)."

**Counterintuitive truth:** The stronger the guarantee, the fewer refunds you get. Bold guarantees signal confidence. Confidence increases trust. Trust reduces refund requests. Weak guarantees signal doubt, which creates doubt in the buyer.

## 22. CFA with 3 Levels / 3 Levers

**Source:** $100M Lost Chapters, pp. 45-73
**Type:** Financial framework for scaling

**Definition:** Customer Financed Acquisition. Using customers' money to fund the acquisition of more customers. The business pays for its own growth from revenue, not from the owner's pocket or investors.

### Three Levels of CFA

**Level 1: Break Even on First Sale**
- Front-end offer covers ad spend exactly
- You profit on the back-end (upsells, continuity, high-ticket)
- Minimum viable CFA: sustainable but slow growth

**Level 2: Profit on First Sale**
- Front-end offer generates profit immediately
- Back-end is pure gravy
- Can reinvest front-end profits to scale faster

**Level 3: First Sale Funds Next Customer**
- Each customer's payment funds acquiring 2+ more customers
- Exponential growth. The machine feeds itself.
- Rare. The holy grail of business economics.

### Three Levers of CFA

**Lever 1: CAC (Cost to Acquire Customer)** - Lower this
- Better targeting, better ads, better conversion, better sales
- But this has a floor (can't go below zero)

**Lever 2: LTGP (Lifetime Gross Profit)** - Raise this
- Use the Crazy Eight framework (Section 8 above)
- This has no ceiling (can go infinitely high)

**Lever 3: PPD (Payback Period)** - Shorten this
- How fast you get your CAC back
- Even if LTGP is high, a long payback period strangles cash flow

**Key metric: 30D Cash**
- 30-Day Cash = how much gross profit a customer generates in their first 30 days
- If 30D Cash > CAC, the business funds its own growth
- This is the single number that determines whether you can scale with paid ads

```
Example:
  CAC: $200
  30D Cash from front-end product: $47
  30D Cash from upsells: $83
  30D Cash from first month of continuity: $97
  Total 30D Cash: $227
  $227 > $200 = CFA Level 2 (profit on first sale)
  = Can scale immediately
```

## 23. Pricing Formulas

All core formulas in one place for quick reference.

### Core Revenue Formula
```
Revenue = # Customers x Price x # Purchases
```

### LTGP (Products)
```
LTGP = Gross Profit per Unit x Avg # of Transactions
```

### LTGP (Recurring)
```
LTGP = Gross Profit per Period / Churn Rate %
```
Example: $2,400 GP / 5% churn = $48,000 LTGP

### Churn Calculation
```
Churn = People Who Left / Original Customer Pool
```
New signups during the period do NOT affect the denominator.

### Stable Customer Pool
```
Total Customers at Steady State = New Customers per Month / Monthly Churn Rate
```
Example: 30 new/mo / 33% churn = ~100 steady state customers

### Price Increase Profit Impact
```
At 10% margins, a 20% price increase = 3x profit (not +20%)
Because the 20% flows entirely to the bottom line.
```

### Annual Billing LTV Multiplier (Profitwell Data)
```
Annual billing (2% monthly churn):     5.35x LTV vs monthly
Quarterly billing (5% monthly churn):  2.14x LTV vs monthly
Monthly billing (10.7% monthly churn): baseline
```

### Involuntary Churn from Card Declines
```
1.2% - 1.7% monthly involuntary churn from card info changes
= 24% - 34% of total churn if total churn is ~5%
Fix: Second form of payment = +14% to +51% LTV
```

### Automatic Continuity LTV Math
```
Main:       $2,000/mo x 4 months x 70% margins = $5,600 LTV
Continuity: $200/mo x 20 months x 90% margins x 50% take = $1,800
New LTV:    $7,400 (+32%)
```

### Ultra High Ticket Anchor LTV Math
```
Before: 80% x $500 + 20% x $200 = $440 avg LTV
After:  10% x $5,000 + 70% x $500 + 20% x $200 = $890 avg LTV (2x)
```

### Guarantee/Warranty Profit Math
```
20 warranties x $100 = $2,000 revenue
1 claim x $100 = $100 cost
Net profit = $1,900 (95% margin)
```

### Round Up Impact
```
$47 -> $49.99/week = +$155.48/year per client
= +6.36% to +11.1% price increase
At 12.5% margins: nearly doubles profit
```

### Annual Renewal Fee Impact
```
0.5x monthly = +4.15% revenue
1.0x monthly = +8.3% revenue
2.0x monthly = +16.6% revenue
3.0x monthly = +24.9% revenue
```

### 28-Day Billing Cycle
```
12 cycles/year -> 13 cycles/year = +8.3% revenue
At 20% margins: jumps to 26.1% margins (+41.5% profit)
```

### CFA 30-Day Cash
```
If 30D Cash > CAC, the business self-funds growth
30D Cash = sum of all gross profit from a customer in first 30 days
```

### Customer Surplus
```
Customer Surplus = Perceived Value - Price
Surplus drives: WOM, repeat purchases, loyalty
```
