## 3 Digital Business Models

Every digital knowledge business falls into one of three monetization models. Pick one. Master it. Don't try to run all three at once.

### Model 1: Recurring

**What it is:** Ongoing information, access, or assets in exchange for ongoing payment.

**Price range:** $9/mo -- $99/mo

**Examples:**
- Paid newsletter
- Async membership community (Skool, Patreon)
- Sync real-time chat channel (Telegram, Discord)
- SaaS platform

**When to use:** After you've proven you can sell a stand-alone product. Recurring revenue sounds great until you realize it takes recurring work. High customer expectations (people want interaction with you). Much harder to execute than people expect.

**Key warning:** If you can't successfully sell a stand-alone product, you'll never successfully build a recurring product -- because a recurring product is really just an ongoing version of a stand-alone product.

### Model 2: Stand-Alone

**What it is:** A single asset that solves a specific problem for a specific person, delivered once for a one-time payment. No ongoing interaction or feedback required.

**Price range:** $9 -- $350

**Examples:**
- eBook or book ($9--$49 range)
- Text & video course ($99--$350 range)
- Ultimate guide ($9--$49 range)
- Paid educational email course ($99--$350 range)
- Template pack ($9--$49 range)

**When to use:** First. Always first. This is the fastest, easiest, and lowest time-cost way to monetize. It builds all the skills needed to graduate to other models later.

### Model 3: Experiential

**What it is:** Assets + community, accountability, coaching, mentorship, or other live/interactive elements. One-time payment.

**Price range:** $350 -- $10,000+

**Examples:**
- Cohort-based course
- Live paid webinar or event
- Coaching sprint
- Group coaching program
- Mastermind

**When to use:** After you've successfully sold stand-alone products. Experiential has all the same flaws as Recurring plus significantly more operational complexity. This is the most advanced model.

**Pricing note:** The wide range exists because the value depends heavily on who the customer is. A mastermind for small creators with <5K followers should not be priced the same as a mastermind for CEOs of $30M+ startups.

### Model Selection Rule

Most people who generate millions do it with 1 or maybe 2 models. Cole/Bush have done it with 8 and explicitly warn that each model comes with an entirely new learning curve. Start with Stand-Alone. Period.

## 3 Pricing Tiers for Low-Ticket Digital Products

### Tier 1: Single Asset / Template ($9 -- $99)

**What fits here:** A micro-product. A single asset the target customer finds valuable but doesn't need a full multi-module course to explain.

**Examples:**
- eBook or print book
- Stand-alone ultimate guide
- Swipe file (curated examples + breakdowns)
- Paid educational email course
- Single valuable template
- Template pack

**Pricing rule:** Smaller asset = lower price. Larger/more in-depth/advanced = higher price, up to the ~$99 ceiling. Most creators can't push past $99 for a single asset.

### Tier 2: Bundle of Assets ($99 -- $199)

**What fits here:** Multiple Tier 1 assets packaged together. The "more stuff" justifies the higher price.

**Examples:**
- Bundle of eBooks
- Bundle of ultimate guides
- Bundle of swipe files
- Bundle of templates/template packs

**Critical mistake to avoid:** Creating a Tier 3 product (full text & video course) but pricing it in Tier 2. This happens because of faulty beliefs:
- "I don't believe I can charge more"
- "Cheaper price = more people buy" (wrong)
- "Cheaper price = easier to sell" (wrong)

### Tier 3: Full Text & Video Course ($199 -- $350)

**What fits here:** In-depth multi-module digital product with text, video, and supporting assets.

**This is where you want to be.** You will almost always make more money selling a higher-priced, more valuable product than selling cheaper stand-alone assets.

**The sweet spot is $350.** This is the answer. Default to it.

## The $350 Rule

$350 is the impulse buy ceiling for digital knowledge products.

### Why $350 specifically:

- **Below $350:** All the same people were going to buy anyway. You just decided to undercharge and make less money.
- **Above $350:** You've crossed the impulse buy threshold. People start needing to think about whether it's "worth it." Friction spikes.

### The Work-Backwards Framework

Do NOT build a product and then slap a price on it. Instead:

1. **Start with $350 as the price**
2. **Ask:** "What do I need to include to make $350 a no-brainer?"
3. **Build the product to justify the price**

This is the opposite of what most people do (build first, guess price later, then discount by 50% out of fear).

### The Undercharging Problem

The reason one person charges $49 and another charges $350 almost always comes down to their individual relationship with money -- not the actual value of their product. Undercharging is a faulty belief problem, not a value problem.

## 5x-10x Stress Test

Use this to validate your price point and silence imposter syndrome.

### The Rule

Your product's price should be 5x-10x cheaper than the potential upside the customer could unlock.

### How to apply it:

**Financial upside:** If your $350 product teaches someone to sell their own $350 product, one sale = break-even, two sales = 100% ROI. The price is laughable relative to the potential upside.

**Non-financial upside:** The 5x-10x multiplier can also apply to:
- Time saved (5x-10x the hours back)
- Stress reduced (5x-10x reduction in friction/overwhelm)
- Speed of results (5x-10x faster than figuring it out alone)

### Stress Test Worksheet

```
Your price:                          $[PRICE]
Customer's potential upside:         $[UPSIDE] (or time/stress equivalent)
Ratio:                               [UPSIDE] / [PRICE] = [X]x

If X >= 5: Price is justified. Ship it.
If X < 5:  Either increase the value delivered or lower the price.
```

### Case Study: Low-Ticket Product M&M Course (Cole/Bush)

- **Price:** $350
- **Upside:** Student sells one $350 product of their own = break-even. Two sales = 2x return. The system produces returns for years.
- **Ratio:** Effectively infinite over time.

## Bonus Psychology

When you feel your product "isn't worth $350," bonuses are how you make the price feel like a steal.

### Why Bonuses Work

The grocery store chips analogy: expensive chips feel affordable when the sign says "Buy One, Get One Free." Getting something "extra" makes the original price seem more reasonable. This psychological mechanism is universal and constant.

### The One Secret That Makes or Breaks Bonuses

**You MUST attach a dollar figure to every bonus. Without a dollar figure, the bonus holds no perceived value.**

- Wrong: "And you also get this free thing!" -- people don't value it.
- Right: "And you also get this $200 thing for free!" -- people feel like they just got $200 for nothing.

This tiny nuance is the entire secret.

### 5 Proven Bonus Types

| Bonus Type | What It Is | How to Price It |
|---|---|---|
| **Templates** | Bundle of assets that helps the customer accomplish the core promise of the product | Price as a Tier 1 asset ($9--$99 depending on depth/quantity) |
| **AI Prompts** | Bundle of AI prompts that automate or accelerate the core promise | Price as a Tier 1 asset ($9--$99 depending on depth/quantity) |
| **Automations** | Bundle of automations/walkthroughs for the core promise | Price as a Tier 1 asset ($29--$99 depending on complexity) |
| **Swipe Files** | Collection of proven examples showing what success looks like | Price as a Tier 1 asset ($19--$99 depending on curation depth) |
| **Behind-The-Scenes** | Walkthrough of proprietary info / "how you do what you do" | Price as a Tier 1-2 asset ($49--$199 depending on exclusivity) |

### How to Set the Dollar Figure

1. Look at the 3 pricing tiers above
2. Ask: "If I sold this bonus as a stand-alone product, what would I charge?"
3. That number is the bonus value you display
4. Tell the customer: "This is what it's worth. But I'm giving it to you for free."

### The Combined Value Rule

**The combined value of all bonuses should exceed the price of the product.**

If your product is $350, your bonuses should add up to more than $350 in stated value. This makes the customer feel like the product itself is practically free -- they're getting more in bonuses than they're paying total.

**Example stack:**
```
Core product:                    $350
Bonus 1 -- Template Pack:       $99 value (FREE)
Bonus 2 -- AI Prompt Library:   $79 value (FREE)
Bonus 3 -- Swipe File:          $149 value (FREE)
Bonus 4 -- BTS Walkthrough:     $99 value (FREE)
                                 ----
Total bonus value:               $426 (exceeds $350 product price)
Customer perception:             "I'm getting $776 worth of stuff for $350"
```

## Module 1 Exercise: 4-Step Pricing Worksheet

Complete these four steps before moving to sales page or launch planning.

### Step 1: Set the Price

Start with **$350**. Do not negotiate with yourself. Do not discount preemptively.

### Step 2: Justify the Price

Ask: "What would I need to show my target customer how to DO in order to rationalize charging $350?"

Make a giant list. Every item on this list must be delivered in the curriculum of your product. If the list feels thin, add more value to the product -- don't lower the price.

### Step 3: Build the 5x-10x Anchor

Ask: "How can I explain the insane value I'm providing at the laughably low price of $350 by anchoring to 5x-10x the potential upside?"

Frame the anchor in terms of:
- **Money:** 5x-10x return on investment
- **Time:** 5x-10x hours saved
- **Stress:** 5x-10x reduction in overwhelm/friction

This anchor goes on your sales page, in your emails, and in every piece of launch content.

### Step 4: Stack the Bonuses

Ask: "What 2-3 bonuses, with dollar figures attached, can I add FOR FREE that would make $350 seem ridiculously cheap?"

Requirements:
- Each bonus has a specific dollar figure
- Combined bonus value exceeds $350
- Each bonus directly supports the core promise of the product

### Validation Check

If you can answer all four steps and feel good about it, your product is priced to perfection. If any step feels weak, fix the value delivered -- not the price.

## Anti-Patterns and Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Pricing by Gut Feel
Building the entire product first, then asking "how much should I charge?" and picking a random number. Always work backwards from $350.

### Mistake 2: Defensive Discounting
Taking whatever price you landed on and cutting it by 50% because "cheaper = more buyers." This is false. The same people who would buy at $175 would have bought at $350. You just left money on the table.

### Mistake 3: Tier Mismatch
Building a Tier 3 product (full course) but pricing it in Tier 2 ($99-$199) or even Tier 1 ($9-$99). This is the most common mistake Cole sees. It's always driven by fear, never by market data.

### Mistake 4: Running Multiple Models Too Early
Trying to sell a course AND run a membership AND do coaching simultaneously. Most million-dollar businesses use 1-2 models. Each new model is an entirely new learning curve.

### Mistake 5: Bonuses Without Dollar Figures
Adding "free extras" without specifying their value. Without a dollar figure, the bonus doesn't register as valuable. Always state: "This is a $X value -- yours free."

### Mistake 6: Bonuses Worth Less Than the Product
If your bonuses add up to less than $350, the psychology doesn't hit right. Stack until the combined bonus value exceeds the product price.

### Mistake 7: Starting with Recurring
Jumping straight to memberships or paid newsletters before proving you can sell a one-time product. A recurring product is just an ongoing stand-alone product. If you can't sell the stand-alone, the recurring version won't save you.

## Full Stack Writer Case Study

Cole & Bush's own $350 digital product, referenced throughout Module 1 as proof the framework works.

- **Product type:** Tier 3 -- Full text & video course
- **Price:** $350 (at the impulse buy ceiling)
- **Positioning:** "7 Skills to 7 Figures" -- playbook for leveraging writing skills into a scalable career
- **5x-10x anchor:** If a student takes what they learn and sells one $350 product of their own, they've broken even. Two sales = 100% ROI. The system produces returns indefinitely.
- **Business model:** Stand-alone (Model 2), not recurring, not experiential
- **Bonuses:** Used the bonus psychology framework with dollar-figure-attached extras to make $350 feel like a steal
- **Meta-lesson:** The M&M course itself ($350) teaches you to build and sell $350 products -- making the 5x-10x stress test essentially infinite over the customer's lifetime
