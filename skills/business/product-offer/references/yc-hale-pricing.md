# YC Framework: Startup Pricing 101 (Kevin Hale)

Source: Y Combinator Startup School 2020 -- Kevin Hale

## Core Principle: Price on Value, Not Costs

Most founders price based on what it cost them to build. Wrong. Price based on the VALUE the customer receives. Your cost of production is irrelevant to the customer.

**Value-based price = (Problem severity) x (How well you solve it) x (Price of alternatives)**

If your tool saves someone 10 hours/week and their time is worth $50/hr, the value is $500/week ($2,000/mo). Charging $47/mo for that is leaving massive money on the table.

## The Three-Tier Pricing Strategy (Good/Better/Best)

Three tiers capture more revenue than a single price point. Each tier serves a different buyer psychology.

| Tier | Purpose | Psychology |
|------|---------|-----------|
| Good (lowest) | Entry point, reduces risk | "Let me try this" |
| Better (middle) | The one you want most people to buy | Anchored as the "smart choice" |
| Best (highest) | Anchors value, makes middle feel reasonable | "Wow, at least I'm not paying THAT much" |

**How to structure tiers:**
- Good: Core value only. Enough to be useful, not enough to be complete.
- Better: Core + most requested features. The "obvious" choice for most.
- Best: Everything + premium touches (1:1 access, done-for-you, speed).

**The decoy effect:** The "Best" tier makes "Better" look like a bargain. Even if nobody buys Best, it increases Better conversions by 20-40%.

**Application to the product ladder:**
- $27-47 Skill Pack = Good tier entry
- $97-197 the product Playbook = Better tier (the one you want most people to buy)
- $47-97/mo Membership = Recurring Better (different model, same psychology)
- $2.5-3.5K Cohort = Best tier for serious buyers
- $5-15K DFY = Ultra-premium anchor

## Willingness to Pay (WTP) Framework

Don't ask "Would you pay $X?" -- people lie. Instead:

**Method 1: Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter**
Ask four questions:
1. At what price would this be so cheap you'd doubt its quality?
2. At what price is this a bargain -- a great deal?
3. At what price does it start to feel expensive but you'd still consider it?
4. At what price is it too expensive, no matter what?

Plot answers. The intersection points reveal optimal pricing range.

**Method 2: Behavior-Based Pricing**
- What are they currently paying for alternatives?
- What's the most they've ever paid for something in this category?
- What other purchases do they make at similar price points?

Past spending behavior > stated willingness to pay. Always.

**Method 3: The "Shut Up" Test**
State your price. Then shut up. Don't justify. Don't discount. Don't fill the silence.
- If they say yes immediately = you're probably too cheap
- If they negotiate = you're in the right range
- If they walk away = you might be too high (or wrong audience)

## Price Elasticity Rules

**Inelastic demand (price doesn't affect demand much):**
- Must-have products (painkillers)
- No close substitutes
- Small % of customer's budget
- Time-sensitive needs

**Elastic demand (price significantly affects demand):**
- Nice-to-have products (vitamins)
- Many alternatives available
- Large % of customer's budget
- Low urgency

**For digital products specifically:**
- $0-$10: Impulse buy zone. Volume play. Works with cold traffic.
- $10-$50: Low consideration. Single piece of content can drive purchase.
- $50-$200: Moderate consideration. Needs social proof + clear ROI.
- $200-$500: High consideration. Needs testimonials, guarantee, detailed sales page.
- $500-$2000: Very high consideration. Needs multiple touchpoints, maybe a call.
- $2000+: Relationship required. Calls, demos, trust-building over time.

## Most Startups Underprice

Common reasons founders charge too little:
1. **Fear of rejection:** "Nobody will pay that much" (test it before assuming)
2. **Imposter syndrome:** "My product isn't worth that" (your audience decides, not you)
3. **Competition anchoring:** "Competitors charge less" (differentiation > price matching)
4. **Volume fantasy:** "I'll make it up in volume" (you won't if margins are thin)

**The fix:** Raise prices 20-50%. Track conversion rate. If conversions don't drop significantly, raise again. Keep going until you find the ceiling.

**Annual pricing hack:**
- Offer monthly AND annual
- Annual = 2 months free (16.7% discount)
- Annual customers have higher LTV, lower churn, and more commitment
- Position annual as the "smart" choice, not the default

## Pricing Signals Quality

Higher price = higher perceived quality. This is especially true for:
- Coaching and consulting (cheap = amateur)
- Digital courses (too cheap = "probably garbage")
- B2B tools (cheap = not enterprise-ready)

**Hormozi's frame combines well here:** Stack value to $10K+, then price at $350 = "insane deal." YC's addition: make sure the $350 is based on actual value delivered, not just perceived value stacking.

## Key Takeaway

Revenue = (Users x Conversion Rate) x (Optimal Price)

Most founders obsess over Users and Conversion Rate while leaving Optimal Price untouched. A 30% price increase with zero change in users or conversion = 30% more revenue instantly. It's the highest-leverage growth lever most founders ignore.
