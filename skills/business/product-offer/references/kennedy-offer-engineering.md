# Kennedy Offer Engineering

Dan Kennedy's direct response frameworks for building offers that convert. These layer on top of the existing offer stack process. Use alongside the standard Steps 1-6.

---

## The Irresistible Offer Stack (7 Components + Thud Factor)

Every offer needs all 7 elements. Missing even one weakens the whole thing.

### 1. Core Product, Clearly Defined

No ambiguity. The prospect should be able to explain what they're buying in one sentence. "A 6-week program that teaches you to build AI automations" beats "a comprehensive learning experience."

### 2. Price Justification (Value Build Before Reveal)

Never lead with price. Build the value first, then reveal the price as a contrast. The prospect should be thinking "this must cost thousands" before you show them $350.

**Value build sequence:**
1. Show the transformation (what their life looks like after)
2. Quantify the value ("this saves you 10 hours per week, that's worth $X")
3. Stack the components ("Module 1 alone is worth $X")
4. THEN reveal the actual price

### 3. Bonuses: Minimum 2-3, Each Addresses an Objection

Bonuses aren't random extras. Each bonus should dissolve a specific buying objection.

**Objection-to-bonus mapping:**
- "I don't have time" -> Quick-start templates, done-for-you assets
- "What if it doesn't work for my situation?" -> Case study library with diverse examples
- "I'm not technical enough" -> Step-by-step walkthrough videos, AI prompt library

Kennedy's killer move: "Refund the product but keep the bonuses." This makes the guarantee feel even safer AND makes the bonuses do double duty as standalone value.

### 4. Deadline: Real, Specific Date

"Limited time" is weak. "Enrollment closes Friday, March 21 at midnight EST" is strong. The deadline must be real. If you extend it, your audience learns your deadlines are fake.

### 5. Incentive for Fast Response

Reward the people who act quickly. Early bird pricing, extra bonus for first 50 buyers, founding member perks. This separates the action-takers from the "I'll think about it" crowd.

### 6. Penalty for Late Response

The flip side of the incentive. Price goes up after the deadline. Bonuses disappear. Spots fill. Something tangible is lost by waiting.

### 7. Guarantee (See 4 Types Below)

Every offer needs one. The type depends on your price point and confidence level.

### The Thud Factor

Kennedy's concept of visual volume. When someone buys, what do they "receive"? A thick binder feels more valuable than a login link, even if the content is identical. For digital products, create thud factor through:
- Visual mockups of everything included (stacked product images)
- Component count ("237 prompts, 45 templates, 12 video modules")
- Physical bonus option (printed workbook, shipped USB drive)
- Dashboard showing all unlocked content at once

### Take-Away Selling

"This isn't for everyone. Only serious marketers who are ready to implement should apply." Making the offer harder to get makes it more desirable. Works especially well for mid and high ticket.

## The 4 Guarantee Types

### Pure Guarantee
"100% money-back, no questions asked, within 30 days."

Best for: Low to mid ticket ($5-199). Removes all risk. Kennedy says pure outperforms conditional in most tests because it requires zero mental energy from the buyer.

### Conditional Guarantee
"Complete all modules and do the exercises within 60 days. If you don't see results, full refund."

Best for: Mid ticket ($99-350). Filters out tire-kickers while still removing risk for serious buyers.

### Performance Guarantee
"If you don't land 3 clients in 90 days using our system, we refund 100%."

Best for: High ticket ($3K+). Shows extreme confidence. Only offer this if you can actually deliver.

### Anti-Guarantee
"All sales final. Here's why: the personalized nature of this service means we invest significant resources the moment you enroll."

Best for: Premium/ultra-premium. Screens for committed buyers. Must have strong justification.

### Bonus Retention Strategy

Regardless of guarantee type, always include: "If you refund, keep the bonuses." This makes the guarantee feel safer (they can't lose), and it keeps your content in their hands (future re-conversion opportunity).

## Premium Pricing: Kennedy's 9 Failures

Nine ways businesses screw up pricing. Avoid all of them.

1. **Obsessing over competitors' prices.** Their costs, margins, and positioning are different from yours.
2. **Attracting price-only buyers.** If price is the main reason they bought, price is the main reason they'll leave.
3. **No premium option.** Always offer a top tier. Some people want to buy the best, and you're not letting them.
4. **Discounting without reason.** Random discounts train your audience to wait for sales.
5. **Not showing value before price.** If they see the price before understanding the value, they anchor on cost.
6. **Assuming price sensitivity.** Most markets have a segment that will pay 3-5x more for the premium version. You just haven't found them yet.
7. **Competing on price.** The race to the bottom has exactly one winner, and they still barely make money.
8. **Apologizing for price.** If you don't believe your price is fair, why should they?
9. **Linking price to time, not value.** "$200/hour" caps your earnings. "$5,000 for the outcome" doesn't.

### Context Controls Price

The same product sells for 3-5x more in a premium context. A $47 PDF becomes a $197 "Implementation Kit" with the same content plus templates, a checklist, and a video walkthrough. The context (packaging, positioning, delivery experience) is what changed, not the core content.

### Marketing to Affluent Buyers

When selling premium: lead with aspiration and status, not features. Exclusivity language matters. "By invitation only," "limited to 20 members," "application required." The difficulty of access itself becomes a selling point.

## Info-Product Ascension Ladder

Kennedy's framework for building a product ladder where each rung funds the next.

| Level | Price Range | Purpose | the product Example |
|-------|------------|---------|---------------|
| Free | $0 | Attract and qualify | Free tool demo, quiz, lead magnet |
| Low | $10-50 | Convert to buyer, cover ad costs | $27-47 Skill Pack |
| Mid | $100-500 | Build relationship, deliver results | $97-197 the product Playbook |
| High | $1K-5K | Core profit center | $2.5-3.5K Cohort |
| Ultra | $5K-15K+ | Maximum value, maximum access | $5-15K Done-For-You |

**The critical insight:** 2/3 to 3/4 of total revenue comes from the back end. The front end exists to acquire customers, not to make profit. If you're trying to profit on the $27 Skill Pack, you're playing the wrong game.

**Recurring layer:** Add a $47-97/mo membership at any point in the ladder. This creates predictable monthly revenue independent of launch cycles.

**The rule:** The business that can spend the most to acquire a customer wins. A higher-value back end means you can afford to spend more on the front end, which means you outcompete everyone fighting over the same audience with lower budgets.

## Kennedy Offer Stack Checklist

Run this before finalizing any offer. Every "no" is a conversion leak.

- [ ] Core product is defined in one clear sentence
- [ ] Value build happens before price reveal
- [ ] At least 2-3 bonuses, each tied to a specific objection
- [ ] Real deadline with a specific date
- [ ] Fast-action incentive (early bird, extra bonus, limited spots)
- [ ] Penalty for waiting (price increase, bonus removal)
- [ ] Guarantee type selected and written
- [ ] Thud factor created (visual volume of what they receive)
- [ ] Take-away selling language included (if mid/high ticket)
- [ ] Ascension path defined (what do they buy next?)
- [ ] P.S. restates the offer and deadline
