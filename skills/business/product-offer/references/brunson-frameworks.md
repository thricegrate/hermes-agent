# Brunson Offer Frameworks

Russell Brunson's frameworks for structuring offers, positioning, and building product ladders. Use alongside the existing pricing tiers (Steps 1-6) and Kennedy offer engineering.

---

## 1. The Value Ladder

A 5-stage ascension model with continuity running alongside every level. The core idea: you can't sell expensive offers to people who don't trust you yet. Each rung builds enough trust and delivers enough value to justify the next.

### The Rungs

**Bait (Free) -- Attract and Qualify**
- Lead magnet, free tool, quiz, content piece
- Goal: get them into your world and prove you can help
- Must deliver real standalone value (not a teaser)
- the product example: Free Claude Artifact tool demo, AI readiness quiz

**Front End ($7-47) -- Convert to Buyer**
- Low-risk entry product (tripwire)
- Goal: turn a lead into a buyer. The transaction itself matters more than the revenue.
- Price should feel like a no-brainer ("why wouldn't I?")
- Revenue covers ad spend (break-even is a win at this level)
- the product example: $27-47 Skill Pack

**Middle ($97-297) -- Core Product**
- Main price point where real value delivery happens
- Goal: deliver the transformation they came for
- This is where the relationship deepens and trust compounds
- the product example: $97-197 the product Playbook

**Back End ($500-5,000) -- Premium Offering**
- Higher access, more personalized, faster results
- Goal: serve the segment that wants more speed, more access, more done-for-them
- Requires proof from Middle tier results
- the product example: $2.5-3.5K Cohort program

**Peak ($5,000-25,000+) -- Highest Tier**
- Maximum access, done-for-you, exclusive
- Goal: capture the top 1-3% who will pay anything for the best
- Often involves direct access to the founder or team
- the product example: $5-15K Done-For-You implementation

**Continuity ($27-197/mo) -- Runs Alongside ALL Levels**
- Monthly recurring sits beside every rung, not just one
- Can be offered at any point in the ladder
- Examples: community membership, tool access, ongoing updates, monthly coaching calls
- This is the revenue floor that makes the business predictable
- the product example: $47-97/mo the product Membership

### Value Ladder Design Checklist

- [ ] Each rung delivers standalone value (no rung feels like a hostage situation)
- [ ] Price gap between rungs is 3-5x (not 10x -- too big a jump kills ascension)
- [ ] Each rung naturally creates desire for the next ("I got X result, now I want Y")
- [ ] Continuity offer exists and is available from Front End onward
- [ ] Free tier generates enough volume to feed the entire ladder
- [ ] Back End and Peak tiers exist even if not yet built (plan the ladder before building every rung)
- [ ] Frontend economics work without backend profit (backend is gravy, not survival)

### Value Ladder vs Kennedy Ascension Ladder

Kennedy's Info-Product Ascension Ladder (in `kennedy-offer-engineering.md`) covers similar ground with different emphasis. Key differences:

- Kennedy focuses on direct response economics (the business that can spend the most to acquire a customer wins)
- Brunson focuses on trust-building and belief progression (each rung earns the right to offer the next)
- Use both: Kennedy for the math, Brunson for the psychology

## 2. New Opportunity vs. Improvement Offer

The single most important positioning decision. Gets this wrong and everything downstream underperforms.

### The Problem with "Improvement" Offers

When you position as "better than what you're doing now," you're competing with:
- Every other improvement offer in the category
- The prospect's own past failures with similar approaches
- Status quo bias ("what I'm doing is fine enough")

Improvement offers target roughly 2% of the market -- the people actively looking to optimize. The other 98% have either given up on the category or don't believe incremental improvement is worth the effort.

### The New Opportunity Frame

Instead of "better version of what failed," position as "entirely different vehicle to the same destination."

**The 4-Step Process:**

**Step 1: Define the Desired Result**
What does the customer actually want? Not features, not process. The end state.
- Bad: "Learn to use AI tools"
- Good: "Run your entire business on autopilot while you focus on the parts you enjoy"

**Step 2: Identify Failed Vehicles**
What has the customer already tried that didn't work? List every approach they've likely attempted.
- Hired a VA (too expensive, quality issues)
- Bought courses on productivity (didn't implement)
- Tried free AI tools (too scattered, no system)
- Attempted to build automations (too technical)

**Step 3: Opportunity Switch (New Category)**
Position your offer as categorically different from all failed vehicles. Not "better AI tools" but "an operating system that replaces the need for tools entirely."

The test: "Is this a new vehicle or just a better version of what they already tried and failed with?"

If the answer is "better version," go back to Step 2 and find a sharper distinction.

**Step 4: Opportunity Stack (Same Vehicle, Multiple Packages)**
Once they buy the new vehicle, offer different ways to consume it:
- DIY: They implement with your system ($47-197)
- DIWY (Done-It-With-You): They implement with your guidance ($997-3,500)
- DFY (Done-For-You): You implement for them ($5,000-15,000)

### New Opportunity Positioning Checklist

- [ ] Desired result defined in customer's words (not your jargon)
- [ ] Minimum 3 failed vehicles identified (the more, the stronger the contrast)
- [ ] Your offer is categorically different, not incrementally better
- [ ] You can explain the difference in one sentence
- [ ] The "new vehicle" language is used in all copy (headlines, emails, sales pages, ads)
- [ ] Opportunity Stack exists with at least 2 consumption formats (DIY + DIWY minimum)

### the product New Opportunity Example

- **Desired result:** Make money with AI without becoming a developer or spending 80 hours learning
- **Failed vehicles:** YouTube tutorials (scattered), ChatGPT (no system), Zapier (too technical), hiring developers (too expensive), courses (theory, no implementation)
- **Opportunity Switch:** An AI Business Operating System that runs your business. Not tools. Not courses. A system.
- **Opportunity Stack:** Skill Pack (DIY) -> Playbook (guided DIY) -> Membership (DIWY) -> Cohort (DIWY+) -> Done-For-You (DFY)

## 3. The Opportunity Stack

Expanding a single offer into multiple revenue streams by varying format and fulfillment level.

### Format Progression (Same Content, Different Delivery)

Each format serves a different learning preference and justifies a different price:

| Format | Description | Price Multiplier | Example |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| Written | Text guides, PDFs, templates | 1x (base) | $47 Skill Pack PDF |
| Audio | Podcast lessons, audio walkthroughs | 1.5x | $67 Audio companion |
| Video | Screen recordings, tutorials, workshops | 2-3x | $97-147 Video course |
| Live | Live calls, workshops, Q&A sessions | 5-10x | $497+ Live workshop |

### Fulfillment Progression (Same Outcome, Different Effort)

| Level | Customer Effort | Your Effort | Price Range | Margin |
|-------|----------------|-------------|-------------|--------|
| DIY | High | Low (create once) | $27-197 | Highest |
| DIWY | Medium | Medium (group calls, feedback) | $497-3,500 | Medium |
| DFY | None | High (custom implementation) | $3,000-15,000+ | Lowest per hour, highest per project |

### 8 Monetization Levels

Stack these from bottom to top. Each level is a natural upsell from the previous:

1. **Free tool/lead magnet** ($0) -- Entry point, proves competence
2. **Low-ticket digital** ($7-47) -- Converts lead to buyer
3. **Core digital product** ($97-297) -- Delivers the main transformation
4. **Premium digital** ($297-497) -- Adds implementation support (templates, done-for-you assets)
5. **Group program** ($497-1,997) -- Adds live access and community
6. **Mastermind/cohort** ($1,997-5,000) -- Adds accountability and peer network
7. **High-ticket coaching** ($5,000-10,000) -- Adds personalized strategy
8. **Done-for-you** ($10,000+) -- You do the work, they get the result

### Opportunity Stack Design Checklist

- [ ] At least 3 monetization levels active (free + low-ticket + core minimum)
- [ ] Each level has a clear "upgrade trigger" (what makes someone want the next level)
- [ ] DIY and DIWY versions exist for the core offer
- [ ] Format options match audience preferences (survey or test to find out)
- [ ] Pricing gaps between levels follow 3-5x rule
- [ ] Each level can be sold independently (no level requires purchasing a previous one)
- [ ] Continuity offer threads through multiple levels

## 4. The Big Domino Statement

Before writing any sales copy, identify the ONE belief that, if the prospect accepts it, makes all other objections irrelevant.

### The Template

> "My goal is to get you to believe that **[new opportunity]** is the key to unlock **[what you desire most]**, and I'm going to show you my proprietary frameworks that will make it simple."

### Why It Works

Most offers try to knock down 10-15 objections one by one. The Big Domino approach finds the single load-bearing belief. If they believe the Big Domino, the smaller objections collapse on their own.

### Finding Your Big Domino

**Step 1: List every objection your prospect has** (price, time, skill, trust, relevance, timing)

**Step 2: Identify which objection, if resolved, makes the others irrelevant**
- If they believe your system works -> price becomes "investment not cost"
- If they believe it's simple -> time and skill objections disappear
- If they believe you've done it -> trust is handled

**Step 3: Write the Big Domino as a single declarative statement**
- Not a question. Not a hedge. A bold claim.

**Step 4: Build your entire sales argument around proving this ONE statement**
- Every story, testimonial, case study, and demo should reinforce the Big Domino
- Anything that doesn't support it gets cut

### Big Domino Checklist

- [ ] Big Domino statement written in one sentence
- [ ] Statement targets the #1 belief barrier (not a secondary objection)
- [ ] Every section of the sales page/email/pitch reinforces the Big Domino
- [ ] At least 3 proof elements support it (testimonial, case study, demo, data)
- [ ] The statement uses "new opportunity" language, not "improvement" language
- [ ] You can say it out loud without hedging or qualifying

### the product Big Domino Example

> "My goal is to get you to believe that an AI Business Operating System -- not courses, not tools, not tutorials -- is the key to running a profitable business on autopilot, and I'm going to show you the exact system I built that manages my 300K+ subscriber newsletter portfolio while I focus on the work I actually enjoy."

**Why this works as a Big Domino:**
- If they believe the product is the vehicle -> they stop comparing to courses/tools (price objection handled)
- If they believe it's a system, not learning -> time/skill objection handled
- If they believe you built it and it works -> trust handled
- One belief. All objections addressed.
