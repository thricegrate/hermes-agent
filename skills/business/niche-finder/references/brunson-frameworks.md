# Brunson Niche Positioning Frameworks

Russell Brunson's frameworks for positioning a niche as a New Opportunity, crafting the Big Domino belief, and placing the niche on a Value Ladder. Use these during niche validation to stress-test positioning before building products.

---

## 1. New Opportunity Positioning

People don't buy "improvement offers" -- they buy new opportunities. An improvement offer says "do what you're already doing, but better." A new opportunity says "here's a completely different vehicle to get the result you want."

### Why This Matters for Niche Selection

If your niche positions you as "another option in a crowded category," you're fighting for attention against everyone else in that category. If your niche positions you as a new vehicle, you skip the comparison game entirely.

### The 3 Diagnostic Questions

Ask these after defining the niche-within-niche (Step 1) and before brainstorming problems (Step 2):

**Q1: Is this a new opportunity or an improvement offer?**

- **New opportunity:** "I'll show you a system for building a business you've never seen before." The customer has no reference frame to compare against. You define the category.
- **Improvement offer:** "I'll help you get better results from the thing you're already doing." The customer immediately compares you to everything else they've tried.
- If it's an improvement offer, reframe it. Find the angle that makes it feel like a different vehicle, not a better version of the old vehicle.

**Reframe checklist:**
- [ ] Can you name the vehicle? (e.g., "The Builder Method," "The Skill Stack System")
- [ ] Does the vehicle sound different from what they've tried before?
- [ ] Would a stranger hear this and say "I haven't tried THAT" instead of "I've tried something like that"?
- [ ] Does your positioning avoid category words the customer associates with past failures? (e.g., "course," "coaching program," "masterclass" are burned for many audiences)

**Q2: What failed vehicles does the customer already know?**

List every solution your target customer has already tried or considered. This is critical because their emotional baggage from those failures shapes how they hear your pitch.

- [ ] List 3-5 specific failed vehicles (products, methods, tools, DIY approaches)
- [ ] For each: what did it promise?
- [ ] For each: why did it fail them? (too complex, too expensive, too slow, no support, didn't fit their situation)
- [ ] For each: what emotional residue remains? (skepticism, frustration, guilt, "I'm the problem" feeling)
- [ ] What pattern connects all the failures? (This pattern becomes your positioning gold -- "The reason none of those worked is because they all assumed X. We don't.")

**Q3: What identity shift is needed?**

People don't just buy products. They buy a new version of themselves. The niche must offer an identity the customer wants to step into.

- [ ] Who does the customer see themselves as NOW? (e.g., "a person who's not technical," "someone who missed the boat," "a busy parent who can't commit")
- [ ] Who do they need to BECOME to succeed with your product? (e.g., "an AI-powered business operator," "a newsletter entrepreneur," "a systems thinker")
- [ ] Is the identity gap small enough to cross? (Too big = scary, they won't buy. Too small = boring, they won't care.)
- [ ] Can you bridge the gap with a story? ("I was [old identity] until I discovered [vehicle]. Now I'm [new identity].")
- [ ] Does your niche language reflect the NEW identity, not the old one?

### Red Flags

- Your niche sounds like "better version of what already exists" -- reframe as new vehicle
- Customer has tried 5+ things in your category and failed -- you need a STRONG identity shift and clear break from the category
- The identity gap requires expertise the customer doesn't believe they can learn -- simplify or provide more scaffolding

## 2. Big Domino Statement

The Big Domino is the ONE belief that, if the customer accepts it, makes all other objections irrelevant. Every niche has one. Finding it is the difference between a landing page with 15 objection-handling sections and one that converts on a single idea.

### The Principle

If you can make a person believe that [your new opportunity] is the key to [what they desire most], and that they can ONLY get it through [your specific vehicle], then all other objections become irrelevant.

### Template

Fill in this template after completing Step 4 (Pick THE Most Valuable Problem):

```
If [target customer] believes that [your vehicle/method]
is the only way to [achieve their #1 desirable outcome],
and that [specific mechanism] makes it possible even for [their current identity],
then every other objection disappears.
```

### Building Your Big Domino (Step by Step)

1. **Identify the #1 desirable outcome** from Step 3's outcome mapping
2. **Name your vehicle** -- not a generic category, a specific named method or system
3. **State the specific mechanism** -- the thing inside your vehicle that makes the result possible (a framework, a tool, an automation, a process)
4. **Address the identity objection** -- why someone like THEM (with their limitations) can still succeed

### Validation Checklist

- [ ] Is it ONE belief, not three beliefs stacked together?
- [ ] Does it feel bold but not unbelievable?
- [ ] If they accept this belief, do the common objections (price, time, difficulty) genuinely dissolve?
- [ ] Can you back it with proof? (Your own results, a case study, data, a demo)
- [ ] Does it naturally exclude competitors? (If they accept your Big Domino, the competitor's approach looks incomplete)

### Examples

**Weak:** "If you believe AI can help your business, you'll want our product."
(Too vague. Doesn't name a vehicle. Doesn't address identity. Doesn't dissolve objections.)

**Strong:** "If you believe that a pre-built AI operating system can automate 80% of your newsletter business in 30 days, and that it works even if you've never written a line of code, then the only question is which skill pack to start with."
(Named vehicle. Specific mechanism. Identity objection addressed. Next step is implied.)

**Strong:** "If you believe that the reason your writing hasn't taken off is not talent but the lack of a publishing system, and that a 15-minute daily writing framework can fix that, then the course price is irrelevant -- the cost of NOT having a system is months of wasted effort."
(Reframes the problem. Named mechanism. Makes price objection irrelevant by framing cost of inaction.)

## 3. Value Ladder Position

Every niche sits somewhere on a value ladder. Knowing where BEFORE you build prevents the two most common mistakes: building a $5K product for a $47 audience, or building a $27 product when the market wants hands-on help.

### The Standard Value Ladder

| Rung | Price Range | What They Get | Trust Required |
|------|-------------|---------------|----------------|
| Free | $0 | Lead magnet, free tool, quiz | Zero (this earns initial trust) |
| Low | $27-47 | Template pack, mini-course, single skill | Low (impulse buy, low risk) |
| Mid | $97-197 | Complete system, playbook, toolkit | Medium (needs proof it works) |
| Membership | $47-97/mo | Ongoing access, community, updates | Medium-high (recurring commitment) |
| High | $1-5K | Group coaching, cohort, intensive | High (needs social proof + results) |
| Ultra | $5-15K+ | Done-for-you, 1:1, custom build | Very high (needs case studies + calls) |

### The 3 Diagnostic Questions

**Q1: Where does this niche naturally enter the ladder?**

Not every niche starts at "free." Some niches are already educated and want to skip to mid or high. Others are skeptical and need extensive free value before they'll spend $27.

- [ ] Has this audience bought products in this category before? (Yes = they can enter higher. No = start at free.)
- [ ] What price points are competitors charging? (This sets the anchor, not your price, but the frame of reference.)
- [ ] How urgent is the problem? (Urgent = willing to pay more, enter higher. Chronic but tolerable = need more nurturing.)
- [ ] Is the audience B2B or B2C? (B2B enters higher because the business pays, not the person.)

**Q2: What's the rung BEFORE your main product?**

You need something below your main product to build the relationship and qualify buyers. Without a lower rung, you're asking cold traffic to make a trust leap.

- [ ] What free asset earns the right to pitch? (Quiz, tool demo, checklist, case study)
- [ ] What low-ticket product proves your method works? (Template, single module, quick-win tool)
- [ ] Does the lower rung naturally create desire for the main product? (The best lower rungs solve part of the problem and reveal the rest.)

**Q3: What's the rung AFTER your main product?**

The most profitable niche businesses have an upsell path. If you can't imagine what comes after, you might be in a "one purchase" niche (not fatal, but limits growth).

- [ ] What does the customer need AFTER they succeed with your main product? (More advanced help, done-for-you, community, ongoing updates)
- [ ] Would they pay more for closer access to you? (If yes, high-ticket coaching or DFY is the next rung.)
- [ ] Is there a recurring need? (If yes, membership or subscription is the next rung.)
- [ ] Can the next rung be built with the same assets? (Ideal: the content from your mid-ticket becomes the curriculum for your high-ticket coaching.)

### Niche-Ladder Fit Test

- [ ] You can describe at least 3 rungs of the ladder for this niche (free + 2 paid)
- [ ] The price gap between rungs is no more than 3-5x (e.g., $47 to $197, not $47 to $5,000)
- [ ] Each rung solves a progressively deeper version of the same core problem
- [ ] The audience has demonstrated willingness to pay at the entry price point
- [ ] You can build the first 2 rungs with existing knowledge/assets (no massive upfront investment)

### Red Flags

- You can only imagine one product at one price point -- the niche may be too narrow or the problem too simple
- The natural entry point is $1K+ but you have no audience or credibility yet -- you need lower rungs first
- The audience only wants free content and has no purchase history in the category -- reconsider the niche or reframe the problem to connect to money/time/status

## Integration with Niche Finder Workflow

These three frameworks slot into the existing workflow:

- **New Opportunity Positioning** -- use after Step 1 (niche drill-down), before Step 2 (problem brainstorming). It reframes how you think about the niche before listing problems.
- **Big Domino Statement** -- use after Step 4 (pick the most valuable problem). The primary problem + desirable outcome feed directly into the Big Domino template.
- **Value Ladder Position** -- use after Step 4 and before Step 5 (Product Idea Brief). Knowing the ladder position shapes the brief's product recommendation.
