# 5-Layer Persona Depth System

The single most reliable predictor of whether content converts cold traffic is the depth of the persona work done before the first script is written. A deep persona produces content that makes a specific person feel personally addressed. A shallow persona produces content that gestures toward a demographic without making anyone feel seen.

## The Research Foundation

Before building layers 3-5, spend 60 minutes mining real customer language:

1. **Review mining** (30 min): Read reviews of your product and direct competitors on Amazon, Trustpilot, and app stores. Copy exact phrases. Do not paraphrase. The exact language is the asset.
2. **Community mining** (20 min): Read Reddit threads, Facebook groups, and forum posts where your persona discusses their problem. Screenshot the most emotionally raw posts.
3. **Support ticket mining** (10 min): If available, read customer support conversations. The language people use when frustrated is the most authentic.

**Output:** A raw language bank of 30-50 exact phrases organized by pain, failed attempts, and desired outcomes.

## Layer 1: Demographic Shell

This is the starting point, not the destination. Most operators stop here, which is why most content resonates with nobody specifically.

**Fields:**

- **Age range:** Specific 8-12 year window (e.g., 28-38), not "adults" or "millennials"
- **Life stage:** Specific situation (e.g., "postpartum 6-18 months," "agency owner with 10-25 clients," "first full-time job 1-3 years in")
- **Income level:** Range that determines purchase behavior, not just demographics (e.g., "$55K-85K household, feels squeezed but not broke")
- **Location characteristics:** Urban/suburban/regional + relevant context (e.g., "mid-size US city, no local community for this problem")

**Test:** Can you describe this person in one sentence that a stranger would recognize as a specific type of person? If not, go more specific.

---

## Layer 2: Psychographic Core

The values, priorities, lifestyle constraints, and decision patterns that determine how this person evaluates information and makes purchasing decisions. Most operators write 2 generic sentences here. Operators running campaigns that convert write 2 full pages and use every word in their scripts.

**Fields:**

- **Primary values:** What this person genuinely cares about most, in their own language (not marketer language). E.g., "being a good parent" not "work-life balance"
- **Lifestyle constraints:** The specific real-world limitations that affect their ability to pursue solutions. E.g., "45 minutes of uninterrupted time per day max," "partner is skeptical of online purchases," "already tried 3 things this year that didn't work"
- **Decision patterns:** How they typically evaluate and purchase:
  - Price-first: Compares cost before considering benefits
  - Transformation-first: Asks "will this actually work?" before looking at price
  - Proof-first: Needs to see someone like them succeed before considering
- **Content consumption habits:** What they watch, when they watch it, how they engage (lurker vs. commenter vs. sharer), which platforms, what time of day
- **Trust signals:** What makes them believe a recommendation is genuine rather than promotional. E.g., "specific numbers," "admits downsides," "sounds like a real person not a brand," "someone who looks like them"

## Layer 3: Pain Architecture

Documents the specific pain points at both functional and emotional levels, using the EXACT language the persona uses to describe their experience. Not marketer language. Their words.

**Fields:**

- **Functional pain points (3-5):** Specific, measurable problems in the persona's exact language
  - E.g., "I've been eating clean for 3 months and the scale hasn't moved" (not "struggles with weight loss")
- **Emotional pain points (3-5):** The feelings underneath the functional problems
  - E.g., "I don't recognize myself in photos anymore" (not "low self-esteem")
- **The "finally someone understands" moment:** The ONE sentence that makes this persona feel completely seen if you say it out loud. This is the single highest-leverage line in any script.
  - E.g., "You're doing everything right and nothing is working, and the worst part is nobody believes you when you say that"
- **The daily experience:** What a specific difficult day looks like for this persona as a result of the unresolved pain. Written as a narrative, not a bullet list.
  - E.g., "You wake up, step on the scale, see the same number, feel that sinking feeling, put on the same stretchy pants because nothing else fits right, skip the mirror, and spend the rest of the day pretending you're fine"

**Source:** Layer 3 should be built almost entirely from the raw language bank. If you're paraphrasing, you're doing it wrong.

---

## Layer 4: Failed Alternatives Map

Documenting what this persona has already tried, why it didn't work, and how they felt about each failure. This is one of the most powerful inputs to script writing because it determines the mechanism bridge language.

**Fields (repeat for 3+ alternatives):**

- **Failed alternative:** [Specific product, approach, or solution they tried]
- **Why it failed:** [The specific mechanism that didn't work -- not "it didn't work for me" but WHY it didn't work]
- **Emotional residue:** [How they feel about having tried it -- frustrated, embarrassed, skeptical, defeated]

**The pattern across failures:** The common mechanism that explains why ALL the alternatives failed. This pattern becomes your unique mechanism bridge in scripts.

- E.g., "Every approach she tried addressed the symptom (the weight) while ignoring the root cause (postpartum hormonal environment). That's why effort alone wasn't producing results."

**Why this matters:** The mechanism bridge is the most technically demanding part of any script because it simultaneously validates the viewer's experience of failure (not their fault) AND introduces the category of solution the product belongs to (without sounding promotional).

---

## Layer 5: Transformation Vision

The specific transformation this persona genuinely wants, in the most emotionally specific language possible. Generic transformation = generic scripts. Specific transformation = scripts that feel personally made.

**Fields:**

- **Specific functional transformation:** Measurable outcome in the persona's own language
  - E.g., "Fit into my pre-pregnancy jeans" (not "lose weight")
- **Specific emotional transformation:** How they want to feel, in vivid specific language
  - E.g., "Feel like myself again when I look in the mirror" (not "feel confident")
- **The specific moment they'll know it worked:** The concrete real-world experience that will tell them they've arrived
  - E.g., "Put on a dress I haven't worn since before I was pregnant and feel good in it"
- **Secondary transformations:** The downstream effects of the primary transformation that the persona cares about but may not articulate as the main goal
  - E.g., "Actually be present with my kids instead of running on empty," "Stop avoiding photos," "Have energy for date nights again"

**Test:** Read the transformation description out loud. Can you see a specific person in a specific moment? If it sounds like a marketing claim, rewrite it.

## 5 Resonance Psychology Checklists

Apply these to every script before production. A script that passes all 5 is ready. A script that fails any checklist needs revision.

### Checklist 1: Persona Identification Test

Read the first 7 seconds of the script out loud:

- [ ] Does the opening name or directly address the target persona in language they would use to describe themselves?
- [ ] Would a viewer in the exact target situation feel personally spoken to within the first 5 seconds?
- [ ] Does the opening avoid generic language that applies to a broad demographic?
- [ ] Would a viewer who is NOT in the target situation immediately know this content is not for them?

**Why it matters:** A script that passes this test earns the first 5 seconds from the right audience AND loses the wrong audience early, which improves engagement signal quality for the algorithm.

### Checklist 2: Pain Authenticity Test

Read the pain section out loud:

- [ ] Does the pain description use specific language from the Layer 3 research rather than paraphrased or generic language?
- [ ] Does it name specific attempts the persona has made that didn't work (not just the problem in general)?
- [ ] Does it include at least 1 emotional-level pain point, not just functional pain?
- [ ] Would someone experiencing this pain feel the script describes their specific experience rather than a general version?

### Checklist 3: Mechanism Bridge Test

Read the mechanism bridge section out loud:

- [ ] Does the bridge explain specifically why the failed alternatives failed, using the Layer 4 pattern?
- [ ] Does it introduce the product's unique mechanism without sounding like a marketing claim?
- [ ] Does it feel like a logical continuation of the pain section rather than an abrupt pivot to promotion?
- [ ] Would a skeptical viewer who has tried everything find the mechanism explanation genuinely different?

### Checklist 4: Transformation Specificity Test

Read the transformation section out loud:

- [ ] Does it name at least 1 specific, concrete moment or experience (not just a general positive outcome)?
- [ ] Does it use emotional language from Layer 5 rather than generic positive sentiment?
- [ ] Can the viewer imagine themselves experiencing the specific transformation?
- [ ] Does it go beyond the obvious functional outcome to include at least 1 secondary transformation?

### Checklist 5: CTA Alignment Test

Read the call to action out loud:

- [ ] Does it feel like a genuine recommendation from someone who wants the viewer to experience the same result?
- [ ] Is it specific enough that the viewer knows exactly what to do next?
- [ ] Does it match the trust level built in the preceding content (not asking for more than trust justifies)?
- [ ] Does it avoid pressure language that might activate advertising skepticism?
