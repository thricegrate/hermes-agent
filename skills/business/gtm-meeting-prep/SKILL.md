# GTM Meeting Prep

Research the company, profile each contact, review relationship history, define a single meeting objective, prepare questions, anticipate objections, and produce a one-page pre-meeting brief. Works for any meeting type: discovery, demo, follow-up, negotiation, QBR, or executive alignment.

## Context Gathering

Before starting, collect from the user:

- **Meeting type** -- discovery, demo, follow-up, negotiation, QBR, exec alignment, etc.
- **What you sell** -- product/service, key differentiators, common objections
- **Your sales methodology** -- MEDDIC, SPIN, Challenger, or your own framework
- **The account** -- company name, deal stage, prior interactions, known contacts
- **Your goal** -- what does a successful outcome look like?

## What This Skill Produces

A structured, one-page pre-meeting brief covering: company context, contact profiles, relationship history, meeting objective, prioritized questions, objection responses, and a defined closing ask.

## Meeting Prep Methodology

### Step 1 -- Understand the Meeting Context

Meeting type determines what to research, what questions to prepare, and what a good outcome looks like.

**Discovery call:**
Goal: Qualify deeply. Understand problem, current solution, buying process, real opportunity.
Prep focus: Company context, contact background, likely pain points, open-ended questions.

**Demo / product walkthrough:**
Goal: Show relevant capability, not everything. Make them feel understood, not presented at.
Prep focus: Their use case, expressed interests, competitor context, what to skip.

**Follow-up / proposal review:**
Goal: Advance the deal. Address concerns, confirm alignment, identify blockers.
Prep focus: What happened last time, what was left open, where deal could stall.

**Negotiation:**
Goal: Close or advance to close. Agree on terms without unnecessary concessions.
Prep focus: Their likely anchors, your walk-away position, flexibility and limits.

**Executive alignment / multi-stakeholder:**
Goal: Build executive sponsorship. Align on business outcome, not product features.
Prep focus: Exec priorities and communication style, business case framing, political dynamics.

**QBR / customer success review:**
Goal: Demonstrate value delivered, identify expansion or risk.
Prep focus: Usage data, outcomes achieved, open issues, expansion signals, renewal timeline.

### Step 2 -- Research the Company

Research depth should match meeting importance.

**For any meeting:**
- What do they do, how do they make money, who are their customers?
- Current size, growth trajectory, recent news (last 90 days)
- Relevant business context: funding, leadership changes, expansion, restructuring
- Market position: growing category, mature, disrupted?

**For strategic or late-stage meetings, also:**
- Competitors and market position
- Public statements from leadership about priorities
- Technology stack relevant to your solution
- Open roles: what they're hiring for reflects real priorities

Flag anything that changed recently. A company that just announced layoffs needs a different conversation than one that closed a Series B.

### Step 3 -- Research the Contacts

Know who is in the room before you walk in.

For each contact:
- Current role, tenure, scope of responsibility
- Career background: where did they come from? What have they built?
- Public presence: LinkedIn activity, published content, conference talks
- Their likely agenda: what does a win look like for them personally?
- Prior interaction: have they spoken to anyone on your team?

Identify each person's role:

**Champion:** Wants you to win. Arm them with what they need to sell internally after the call.
**Decision maker:** Final authority, cares about business outcomes. Connect to their priorities, not features.
**Evaluator / end user:** Cares about daily usability. Show specific workflows, not strategy.
**Skeptic / blocker:** Has concerns or competing interests. Acknowledge directly -- don't work around them.
**Procurement / legal:** Focused on risk and terms. Prepare standard compliance and commercial responses.

### Step 4 -- Review the Relationship History

Never walk into a meeting without knowing what has already been said. Recreating context signals you don't listen.

Pull and review:
- All prior call notes and email threads
- Any demos given: what was shown, what resonated, what didn't
- Open questions or commitments from last interaction
- Proposals or pricing already shared
- CRM notes and deal stage: does official stage reflect reality?

Synthesize into:
- What they told you about their problem
- What they said about buying process and timeline
- What concerns or objections surfaced
- What commitments were made on either side, and whether they were met

If commitments were broken by either side, address directly in or before the meeting.

### Step 5 -- Define the Meeting Objective

One meeting, one primary objective. Not a list of things you hope to cover -- one outcome that defines success.

Write it as: "This meeting is successful if [specific outcome]."

Examples:
- "...if we get agreement on a pilot scope and a defined start date."
- "...if the CFO is on board and we have a clear path to contract."
- "...if we understand their renewal timeline and identify the expansion opportunity."
- "...if we uncover the real reason the deal stalled."

Secondary objectives: 1-2 max. More = lost focus.

Share the primary objective with everyone on your side before the call.

### Step 6 -- Prepare the Questions

Questions are the core of any good sales meeting. Prep them before you walk in.

**Discovery / qualification questions (early-stage):**
- What is driving the focus on this now, specifically?
- What have you tried before and what happened?
- Who else is involved in evaluating this?
- What does success look like 12 months from now?
- What happens if you don't solve this?

**Advancement questions (mid-to-late stage):**
- What is your timeline for making a decision?
- What would need to be true for this to move forward?
- Who else needs to be comfortable before you can commit?
- What is the biggest remaining concern?
- Anything internally that could slow this down?

**Relationship / exec questions (strategic or exec-level):**
- What is your biggest priority for the business this year?
- Where does this initiative sit relative to other budget competitors?
- What would make this a clear win for you personally?

A discovery call should be 70% questions. A closing call should be 30% questions and 70% confirming alignment.

### Step 7 -- Anticipate Objections

Every meeting has predictable objections. Being caught off guard by one you could have anticipated is avoidable.

For each likely objection, prepare:
1. Acknowledge genuinely -- don't dismiss or deflect
2. Reframe or provide evidence
3. Test if it's resolved

Common categories:

**Price / budget:** Show ROI, reframe as cost of inaction, or offer phased start.
**Timing ("come back in Q3"):** Understand what drives the timeline; explore advantages of earlier.
**Competition:** Acknowledge, stay focused on your differentiated outcome.
**Internal priority:** Connect to stated priorities; make the case for enablement.
**Risk / trust:** Proof points, references, pilot options, implementation support.
**Champion concern ("need to convince my boss"):** Arm the champion -- narrative, business case, one-pager.

Focus on the 1-2 most likely objections given deal stage and contact profile.

### Step 8 -- Prepare the Brief

Synthesize into a single document. One page. Scannable in 5 minutes.

    MEETING SNAPSHOT
    Date / time / format:
    Attendees (their side):
    Attendees (our side):
    Deal stage and value:

    COMPANY CONTEXT (3-5 bullets)

    CONTACT PROFILES (1-3 bullets per person)

    RELATIONSHIP HISTORY (3-5 bullets)

    MEETING OBJECTIVE
    Primary: [one sentence]
    Secondary: [1-2 items]

    QUESTIONS TO ASK (5-7, prioritized)

    OBJECTIONS TO PREPARE FOR (top 2-3, with prepared response)

    WHAT WE NEED TO LEAVE WITH
    [Concrete next step to push for]

### Step 9 -- Define the Ask

Every meeting should end with a concrete next step agreed on the call.

The ask should be:
- **Specific:** not "let's stay in touch" but "can we get 30 minutes with your CFO next week?"
- **Time-bound:** a specific date or deadline, not "sometime soon"
- **Proportional to stage:** don't ask for contract signature at end of a discovery call

If they say no, have a fallback. A smaller commitment is better than walking away with nothing.

## Quick Reference

| Meeting Type | Research Focus | Question Goal | Ideal Ask |
|-------------|---------------|---------------|-----------|
| Discovery | Context, pain signals | Understand problem + process | Follow-up with wider team |
| Demo | Use case, what to show | Confirm fit, surface concerns | Agree on eval criteria |
| Follow-up | Open items, blockers | Advance or disqualify | Proposal or next milestone |
| Negotiation | Walk-away, their anchors | Resolve final blockers | Signed agreement or timeline |
| Exec alignment | Exec priorities, business case | Sponsorship + political buy-in | Exec champion committed |
| QBR | Usage data, outcomes, risk | Confirm value, find expansion | Renewal or expansion commitment |
