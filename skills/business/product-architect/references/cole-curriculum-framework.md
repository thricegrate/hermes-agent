# Cole's Product Curriculum Framework (Low-Ticket Launchpad)

> Source: Nicolas Cole, Low-Ticket Launchpad course
> Modules: 4 (Product Curriculum) + 5 (Module Creation Template)
> Context: Complete framework for outlining digital product curriculums and structuring individual modules. Use when designing course/product structure, creating module outlines, or reviewing product curriculum quality.

---

## Core Principle: "Learning Is Bad. Action Is Good."

Nobody wants to learn. People only want to learn if it helps them take ACTION -- because action is what increases the likelihood they achieve their desired outcome.

**There are 2 things people want:**
- ACTIONS
- ASSETS

Most digital products fail because they are "a giant bucket of learning":
- No Tangible, Objective, measurable Goal
- That goal is NOT broken down into actionable, simple-to-understand Action Steps
- Instead: a mosh-posh of modules filled with information presented without coherent progression

**The fix:** Start with the end goal. Reverse-engineer it into action steps. Make those action steps the outline.

---

## 5-Step Curriculum Outline Process

### Step 1: Start With The End Goal

Take the promise of your Offer. Reverse-engineer the Tangible, Objective Outcome into Action Steps. Make those Action Steps the outline of your digital product.

**Examples:**
- Offer: "I will help you write your first 10 LinkedIn posts" -> 10 modules, each helping write 1 post
- Offer: "I will show you how to build & automate your first 7 Zapier integrations" -> 7 modules, each building 1 Zap
- Offer: "Optimize your X profile + pinned asset + niche content strategy for 6 months" -> 3 overarching parts, each broken into action steps

The logic: Offer promise -> Reverse-engineer into Action Steps -> Action Steps = outline

### Step 2: Make A Bulleted List Of Action Steps

Someone should be able to see your Offer, then skim your curriculum outline (your Action Steps), and clearly & linearly see how they get from "where they are" to "where they want to be."

DO NOT skip this step. If you dive straight into creating content, you will waste time, rewrite most of it, and frustrate yourself.

**Example -- Spine Surgery Recovery Playbook** ("A 90-Day Training Regimen To Speed Up Healing, Accelerate Mobility, And Get You Back In The Gym In Record Time"):

- Module 1: Days 1-3 (Post-Surgery)
- Module 2: Days 3-7 (Slow & Steady)
- Module 3: Days 8-16 (Speed Up Healing)
- Module 4: Days 17-30 (Mobility Test #1)
- Module 5: Days 31-45 (Strength Exercises)
- Module 6: Days 45-60 (Mobility Test #2)
- Module 7: Days 61-75 (Back In Action)
- Module 8: Days 75-89 (Home Stretch)
- Module 9: Day 90 (Mobility Test #3)

**Example -- Zapier Mastery For Agency Owners** ("8 Zaps To Automate Payroll, Client Analytics Reviews, And Internal Data Storage"):

- Module 1: Zap #1
- Module 2: Zap #2
- Module 3: Zap #3
- ...through Module 8: Zap #8

When the outline is OBSESSIVELY focused on action steps and the promised goal, all the other information doesn't really matter.

### Step 3: Name Modules Tangibly (Action or Asset)

Two naming conventions:

1. **Action Step naming:** Don't overthink it. "Module 1: Audit Your LinkedIn Profile." "Module 2: Create Your Pinned Asset." "Module 3: Write Your First Post." Whatever the action steps are, make them clearly explained in the module name.

2. **Tangible Asset naming:** Get creative. Instead of "Audit Your LinkedIn Profile" (action step), name it after the tangible asset you give them: "Module 1: Your LinkedIn Audit Checklist" or "Module 1: Your ChatGPT LinkedIn Audit Generator." Checklists, templates, generators -- these are tangible.

You don't HAVE to name every module as a tangible asset, but upgrade wherever you can.

**V1 to V2 Progression (Zapier course example):**

V1 (generic):
- Module 1: Zap #1
- Module 2: Zap #2
- Module 3: Zap #3
- ...

V2 (tangible):
- Module 1: Your Payroll Zap
- Module 2: Your Client Monthly Analytics Zap
- Module 3: Your Employee Performance Review Zap
- Module 4: Your Personal Content Strategy Review Zap
- Module 5: Your Invoicing Zap
- Module 6: Your CRM Zap
- Module 7: Your Outbound Sales Zap
- Module 8: Your Accounting Expenses Zap

### Step 4: Pair Modules with Assets/Templates

You want to make it impossible for the person NOT to complete the goal of the module.

Each module should flow like this:
1. "Do this thing."
2. "Here's how to do it."
3. "And here's an asset/template that essentially guarantees you will complete this action step successfully."

The easier you make it for customers to take ACTION, the more successful they will be. More success = more testimonials + more word-of-mouth.

**Example -- Zapier course with paired assets:**

- Module 1: Your Payroll Zap + **Payroll Excel Template**
- Module 2: Your Client Monthly Analytics Zap + **Monthly Analytics Template**
- Module 3: Your Employee Performance Review Zap + **Employee Performance Review Questions Checklist**
- Module 4: Your Personal Content Strategy Review Zap + **Personal Content Strategy Notion Dashboard**
- Module 5: Your Invoicing Zap + **Invoicing Template**
- Module 6: Your CRM Zap + **CRM Notion Template**
- Module 7: Your Outbound Sales Zap + **Outbound Sales Messaging Cheat Sheet**
- Module 8: Your Accounting Expenses Zap + **Accounting Expenses Excel Template**

At this point your outline becomes your "To Do" list to create the product.

### Step 5: List 3-7 Key Points per Module

Fill in your outline with 3-7 key points per module using the Perfect Course Module Framework (see below). At this point you should have enough clarity over the goal and the A-to-B path.

## The Perfect Course Module Framework (7 Sections)

Cole's framework from scaling Ship 30 for 30 and Premium Ghostwriting Academy ($15M+ total revenue, 12,000+ students, 4M+ words written across all products).

Every module should aim to cover these 7 sections, in this order:

### Section 1: Reasons Why (Benefits)

Before diving into the Action Step, SET THE FRAME. Explain WHY this action step is so important.

Think of it as a mini sales pitch for each action step. If you don't tell them WHY, they won't trust you and won't understand how it connects to their broader goal.

The formula: "Here are all the good things that will happen if you take this approach."
- Benefit #1
- Benefit #2
- Benefit #3
- ...

**Example:** "Hey, you should fly to LA" (action step alone) = nobody goes. But sell the benefits -- Venice Beach, weather, healthy restaurants, Malibu hikes -- and they start packing.

You HAVE to sell the customer on each Action Step before you dig into "now here's how you actually do it."

### Section 2: Mistakes To Avoid (Problems)

Once the person has bought into the step, prepare them by saying: "Make sure you don't make these mistakes."

Telling them what mistakes to avoid BEFORE they get started is more helpful than letting them fail on preventable errors.

**Example (online tutoring product, Module 1: Optimize LinkedIn Profile):**
- Reasons Why: "Here's why you should optimize your LinkedIn profile FIRST, before promoting your tutoring business."
- Mistakes To Avoid: "Don't spam-connect with everyone, don't post promotional content with links, don't leave your bio blank."

Set the frame first. Then dig into sub-Action Steps.

### Section 3: Steps How To (Action Steps)

This is the core of the module. Straightforward:

1. Take the big Action Step of the module
2. Break it into 3-7 smaller, more digestible action steps
3. Walk through each one in logical order

**Success measure:** Someone could follow your 3-7 smaller action steps, accomplish the big Action Step, AND have no lingering questions.

**Ways to make Action Steps easier to follow:**
- Example screenshots (especially for digital platform walkthroughs -- people are visual learners)
- Proof screenshots (something you did yourself, or another person accomplished)
- Graphic designed visuals (advanced, not required, but elevates quality -- see Jack Butcher's Visualize Value courses)

### Section 4: Commonly Asked Questions (Objections)

Critical insight: **These aren't actual questions. They're OBJECTIONS disguised as questions.**

After clear Action Steps, information is no longer the problem. You've given them the information. The only thing left is their own faulty beliefs.

Common disguised objections:
- "Well sure, this works for YOU. But it won't work for ME."
- "What if my niche is different? Does this work for every niche?"
- "If everybody uses this framework, won't every product feel the same?"

This section must be 100% dedicated to overcoming these objections. Otherwise the person talks themselves out of doing what you just told them to do.

### Section 5: Walkthrough Example (Proof)

Even after answering all their questions / overcoming objections, the doubt doesn't fully go away. You need PROOF that it works.

**Different forms of proof:**
- A step-by-step "now watch me do it" video (from you)
- A step-by-step "now watch someone else do it" video (from another student)
- A video testimonial from a successful student speaking to THIS specific Action Step
- Text-based testimonials (or collection) from successful students for THIS specific Action Step

The real goal is to SHOW (not tell) how the Action Step actually gets executed. The more you show, the more likely they are to believe it works for them.

### Section 6: Bonus Asset (Template)

Make it nearly impossible for the person NOT to be successful. Give them a tangible asset.

**Types of bonus assets:**
- A literal "fill in these blank spaces" template
- A pre-filled Excel spreadsheet or Notion document
- A saved template inside a platform (email automation in Kit, landing page in Carrd, etc.)
- A checklist or cheat sheet
- A calendar, time-tracking spreadsheet, invoicing template
- An AI Prompt that automates or accelerates the action step

Frame it as a BONUS asset. "You don't have to use it, but it's there if you need it."

Cole's take: "After building a dozen different digital products over 4+ years, people need WAY more help than you realize. They need you to slow down. They need bite-sized pieces. They need guardrails. Like bowling bumpers for beginners."

### Section 7: Assignment (Homework)

Recap VERY SPECIFICALLY what the customer now needs to go do.

**Template:**
- "We just talked about {Big Action Step}."
- "You know a) why it's important, b) what mistakes to avoid, and c) what smaller action steps to take."
- "So, what I'd like you to do is {Homework/Exercise}."
- "And don't forget... use the {Template} I've provided you here!"

Tell the person to go do the thing you just told them how to do, again.

## Sizing Guidelines

- **10-20 modules** for a low-ticket product ($350 or less)
- **2,000 words per module minimum.** Less than that feels thin. Don't be surprised if some modules hit 10,000+ words.
- **If you need 100+ modules,** one of two things is happening:
  1. You're tackling too big a problem or promising too big an outcome. Get more specific.
  2. You're successfully tackling a massive problem, but it's too big for low-ticket. It should be a high-ticket coaching program (information + accountability/implementation).
- **Interesting info that ISN'T attached to an Action Step** = bonus content, NOT core curriculum. Break it out as a side-quest. Don't let it clog the core action-step path.

## Q&A / Common Objections

### "Multiple action steps inside an action step?"

Totally fine. Sometimes you get the big Action Steps right on the first try and explain sub-steps within. Other times, you realize a big Action Step needs to be broken in half into 2 separate steps.

**Example (teenage grass-cutting business):** First big Action Step = put fliers on doors. Sub-steps: come up with a compelling offer, find a Kinkos for printing, find time to distribute, set up a way to field calls. But you might realize "come up with a compelling offer" is its own separate big Action Step with its own sub-steps.

Outline should be clear enough for direction, flexible enough to change as you build.

### "This is so much work!"

Cole's reframe: "Did you think making internet money, with a 99% margin business, with infinite scale potential, that could replace your full-time income and make you hundreds of thousands or millions... was going to be EASY?"

It's not DIFFICULT. It just takes time, patience, and more than 1 brain cell.

### "Outline needs to change midway?"

Then change it. Every time you realize things could be done better, simplified, or increase likelihood of customer success, make those changes.

Cole: "I have written & re-written our product curriculums dozens of times. It is what it is."

The game isn't "quickly build a product and relax on the beach." The game is falling in love with iteration, falling in love with helping a specific type of person solve a specific problem.

### "Do I need all 7 sections in every module?"

Not always. Some modules might not need a walkthrough example, or a bonus asset, or a Mistakes To Avoid section.

But stress-test each module against all 7 sections to make sure you're doing everything you can to make the customer successful. Consider how you COULD include each one, even if you ultimately leave some out.

### "Do the 7 sections have to be in this exact order?"

No. But this order is the most logical and easy to follow. Cole: "I've created a lot of course modules and without even meaning to I almost always revert back to these sections in this particular order."

## Curriculum Outline Template

Use this as a starting skeleton:

**CUSTOMER WANTS TO ACHIEVE A TANGIBLE, OBJECTIVE GOAL.**

- **Module 1: Action Step 1**
  - Section 1: Reasons Why
  - Section 2: Mistakes To Avoid
  - Section 3: Steps How To
  - Section 4: Commonly Asked Questions (FAQ)
  - Section 5: Walkthrough Example (Proof)
  - Section 6: Bonus Asset/Template
  - Section 7: Assignment
- **Module 2: Action Step 2**
  - (same 7 sections)
- **Module 3: Action Step 3**
  - (same 7 sections)
- ...continue for 10-20 modules...

**CUSTOMER HAS SUCCESSFULLY ACHIEVED THE PROMISED TANGIBLE, OBJECTIVE GOAL!**

Focus on nailing the Action Steps (Modules) first. Then fill in each module using the 7-section framework.

## Key Takeaways for Product Architects

1. **Action > Learning.** Every module must have a clear action step. If it's just "interesting info," it's bonus content, not core curriculum.
2. **Reverse-engineer from the goal.** Start with the Offer promise, work backward to action steps.
3. **Outline before building.** Skipping the outline = wasted time + rewrites + frustration.
4. **Name tangibly.** "Your Payroll Zap" + "Payroll Excel Template" beats "Module 1: Zap #1" every time.
5. **Pair every action step with an asset.** Templates, checklists, AI prompts, spreadsheets -- whatever makes completion nearly guaranteed.
6. **Sell each step.** Reasons Why before Steps How To. People need to buy into each action step before they'll execute it.
7. **Overcome objections proactively.** FAQ section = objection-handling section. Those "questions" are beliefs holding people back.
8. **Show, don't tell.** Walkthrough examples and proof push people over the edge more than any amount of explaining.
9. **Iterate relentlessly.** The outline will change. The product will get rewritten. Fall in love with iteration.
10. **Less is more.** If it doesn't serve the Tangible Objective Goal, cut it or make it bonus content.
