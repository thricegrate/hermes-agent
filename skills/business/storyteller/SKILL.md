# Storyteller

The storyteller skill teaches narrative craft. Use it to make any piece of marketing content -- newsletter, ad, landing page section, social post, case study, email -- more memorable, emotionally resonant, and effective by applying proven story structures and devices.

This skill is about HOW to tell a story. For WHAT to write about, use content-strategy or content-social. For WHERE to publish, use content-social or newsletter-writer. For persuasion formulas and copy, use copy-writing.

## Prerequisites

- A topic, angle, or message to communicate
- The format being written for (email, social, long-form, ad, case study, brand story)
- A rough sense of the emotional response you want to produce

## Workflow

### Step 1: Define the Story Goal

Clarify what the story needs to accomplish:
- What should the reader feel? (See `references/emotional-arc-library.md` for the full emotion menu)
- What action should the story move them toward?
- What format is this for? (determines length, structure, and device selection)
- What does the protagonist want (external) vs. what do they need (internal)? See Objects of Desire in `references/story-grid-marketing.md`

**Emotional Hook Rule (MANDATORY):** Every story must open with a line that triggers a specific emotion. Pick your target: WOW, WTF, OMG, LOL, UGH, WHOA, NAH, YEP, OOF, HMM, DAMN, SMH. The opening line sets the emotional contract with the reader. "I lost $40K in one week because I forgot to check one box" (OOF) is a story opener. "Communication is important in business" is a Wikipedia article. In casual formats, you CAN use the abbreviation literally. See `skills/humanizer/SKILL.md` for the full list.

### Step 2: Choose a Story Structure

Select the appropriate structure for your goal. Read `references/storytelling-frameworks.md` for the full decision guide and worked examples.

Quick selection guide:
- **TORS** (Transformation > Hurdle > Rise > Fall): Inspirational before/after, counterintuitive lessons, "less is more" arguments
- **SBI** (Situation > Behavior > Impact): Case studies, behavior breakdowns, lessons from real events
- **Hero's Journey (5-act compressed)**: Brand origin stories, founder narratives, customer testimonials
- **Epiphany Bridge** (14-question origin story script): Origin stories, product launches, new opportunity pitches -- makes the audience FEEL your aha moment. See `references/brunson-frameworks.md` Section 1.
- **Pixar Spine** ("Once upon a time...Every day...Until one day...Because of that...Until finally..."): Any story needing a complete arc from setup to resolution
- **Mini-story (3 sentences)**: Email openers, social captions, newsletter intros, P.S. sections
- **6-Element Campaign Arc** (Setting > Characters > Conflict > Climax > Resolution > Moral): Full campaign narratives, ad series, landing page story sections
- **Story Brand**: Brand positioning where the customer is the hero and your product is the guide
- **Creator Video Framework** (HOOK > BRAG > VALUE > INTEGRATION > PAYOFF): For YouTube, Reels, TikTok, any video content 60s+. See `skills/video-scriptwriter/SKILL.md` for the full 5-act breakdown. Uses South Park "but/therefore" principle for emotional unpredictability throughout.
- **Case Study Metrics Arc** (Old Metrics > Setup > New Metrics > Revenue Result > CTA): Product demos, build-in-public content, social proof posts, before/after stories, client success stories. See details below.
- **Sheridan Narrative** (Window > Surrogate > Simple Engine > Gap > Reveal > Cost > Question): Scene-driven storytelling where the story IS the product. No neat lessons, no preaching. Raise questions, withhold answers. Best for: morally complex stories, trade-off narratives, build-in-public content about costs and hard choices, content where you don't have the answer. Uses Taylor Sheridan's screenwriting craft (Sicario, 1883, Yellowstone, Landman). See `references/sheridan-framework.md`.

### Case Study Metrics Arc

A 5-beat structure designed specifically for product demos and build-in-public content where the transformation is measurable. Best for: social proof posts, before/after stories, product launch narratives, client success stories.

| Beat | Purpose | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| 1. Old Metrics | Establish the painful "before" with specific numbers | "This ecom brand was getting 2-3 DMs/day, converting maybe 1/week" |
| 2. The Setup | What changed and why (keep it simple, one key decision) | "Set them up with AI DM automation. Took 15 minutes to connect." |
| 3. New Metrics | The "after" with equally specific numbers | "Now 20-30 DMs/day, AI handles all of them, books 8-12 calls/week" |
| 4. Revenue Result | The money proof (this is what people actually care about) | "Closed $43k in sales last month from Instagram DMs alone. Owner was on vacation." |
| 5. CTA | What the viewer should do next | "DM me 'AUTO' and I'll show you how it works" |

**Why this structure works:** Each beat answers the next logical question the viewer has. "How bad was it?" > "What changed?" > "How good is it now?" > "Did it actually make money?" > "How do I get this?"

**Key rules:**
- Never skip beats or reorder them. The emotional arc depends on the sequence.
- Specific numbers beat vague claims every time. "went from 12% to 34%" > "improved significantly"
- The revenue result is the climax, not the setup. Build to it.
- Keep the setup beat short. One sentence about what changed. The viewer cares about results, not process.

For Brunson's Attractive Character storyline types (Loss and Redemption, Us vs. Them, Before and After, Amazing Discovery, Secret Telling, Third-Person Testimonial) and the AC identity archetype system, see `references/brunson-frameworks.md`.

### Story Grid Diagnostic (optional, applies to ALL structures)

After selecting a framework, define the value shift and controlling idea. See `references/story-grid-marketing.md` for the full system. Every piece must shift a value (Safety, Connection, Insight, or Fairness) from one pole to another. Write the controlling idea in one sentence: "[Value] is [won/lost] when [circumstances]." If you cannot name the value shift, the story is information, not narrative. Use the Five Commandments (Inciting Incident > Complications > Crisis > Climax > Resolution) as a diagnostic overlay to verify all five beats are present.

### The South Park "But/Therefore" Rule (applies to ALL story structures)

Never connect story beats with "and then." Always use "but" (introduces conflict/surprise) or "therefore" (shows consequence). This creates tension and unpredictability.
- BAD: "I started a newsletter AND THEN grew it AND THEN made money"
- GOOD: "I started a newsletter, BUT nobody opened it. THEREFORE I studied what top creators do. BUT what I found surprised me..."
This rule applies to every structure above, not just video. Use it in emails, social posts, case studies, everywhere.

### Step 3: Apply Narrative Devices

Layer in devices from `references/narrative-devices.md` to make the story vivid and sticky:
- Specificity: numbers, names, dates, places
- Metaphor: connect unfamiliar to familiar
- Contrast: before vs. after, expected vs. actual
- Curiosity gap: withhold the resolution
- Dialogue: quotes make abstract concrete
- Sensory detail: sight, sound, feeling, temperature
- Vulnerability: failure before the win
- Future pacing: project reader forward into the outcome

### Step 3b: Apply Advanced Retention Techniques

These create the "can't stop reading/watching" effect. Layer on top of any structure:

- **Oscillation Pattern:** Every up followed by a down, every down by an up. Never let the audience settle emotionally. Aim for 5+ emotional state changes per piece. The lows make the highs feel higher.
- **Near-Miss Pattern:** Show 2-3 almost-wins before the real win. Each more painful than the last. Frustration compounds. Makes the real win feel earned and euphoric.
- **Vulnerability Before Victory:** Lowest emotional point RIGHT BEFORE the breakthrough. "I was ready to give up." Then the turn. Without vulnerability, victory feels hollow.
- **Expectation Subversion:** Promise one ending in the hook, deliver something deeper at the close. Audience came for X, leaves thinking about Y. This makes content shareable.
- **Tension-Release-Tension Cycle:** Insert breather moments (humor, character intro, reflection) between intensity peaks. You can't sustain maximum tension. Breathers make the next spike feel fresh.
- **Escalating Stakes:** Each beat raises what's at risk. Property to relationships to safety to identity/meaning.
- **Triple Hook Stack:** Open with 3 layered hooks: situation + action + payoff tease. Three hooks > one.
- **Show-Don't-Tell Brag:** Let the story PROVE competence. Audience concluding "this person is impressive" on their own = 10x more powerful than being told.
- **The Reframe Close:** End by reframing what the story was really about. Transform surface topic into deeper meaning. People share surprises, not confirmations.
- **Recurring Character:** Plant a minor character early. Bring them back 2-3 times. Pay off at the end. Emotional investment compounds with each return. Works in email sequences too (reference the same person across issues).
- **Full-Circle Callback:** Return to the exact opening scene/state at a low point. "Nothing changed" creates urgency for the REAL change. True transformation lands only after the callback proves surface fixes failed.
- **Hidden Thesis:** Don't state your message upfront. Let the story prove it. State it only at the end, when the audience already believes it. They feel smarter for figuring it out first.
- **Controlled Disaster:** Set up a scenario everyone KNOWS will fail. Tension = "how bad will it get?" not "will it work?" Irresistible to watch/read. Failure must serve the larger message.
- **Permission to Be Vulnerable:** Break the performance layer ONCE. Raw, unedited, direct. This bonds the audience more than production value. Once per piece max.
- **Cross-Dimensional Escalation:** Escalate across dimensions (physical, social, emotional, financial), not just intensity. Unpredictable escalation keeps the audience off-balance.
- **Comedic Anchors:** 2-3 running gags planted early, referenced throughout. Structural breathers + inside jokes with the audience.
- **The Question Framework:** Frame everything around ONE driving question. Topics inform. Questions create tension. Every beat = evidence for or against the answer.
- **Naming the Abstract:** Give concepts a name or personality. Named things are relatable and quotable. "The algorithm" = forgettable. "Eve" = memorable.

### Step 3c: Kennedy Story Selling Techniques

Three principles from Dan Kennedy that make stories convert, not just entertain.

**Damaging Admission in Narrative:** Open with a real failure, flaw, or mistake before the win. "I spent $4,000 on ads and got zero sales" hits harder than "I figured out a great ad strategy." The admission makes the success believable. Without it, you're just another person claiming results. One admission per story. It must be real.

**Vulnerability Before Victory Arc:** Structure every transformation story so the lowest emotional point comes RIGHT BEFORE the breakthrough. "I was ready to quit. My partner said I was wasting our savings. I had one last idea." Then the turn. Without vulnerability, victory feels like a press release. With it, the reader trusts the transformation is earned.

**Sell the Result Through Story:** Never state the benefit directly. Let the story prove it. "After implementing the system, I checked my inbox Monday morning and had 14 new subscriber notifications" sells the tool better than "our tool gets you more subscribers." The reader draws the conclusion themselves, which is 10x more persuasive than being told. Show the moment of realization, the numbers on the screen, the reaction. Let them FEEL the result.

### Step 4: Execute and Connect

Write the story, then connect it to lesson, application, and CTA using the 4-step arc:
1. **Story** (the narrative)
2. **Lesson** (what it means)
3. **Application** (how they can use it)
4. **CTA** (what to do next)

Apply the South Park Rule throughout: connect every paragraph with "but," "therefore," or "meanwhile" -- never "and then."

## Hormozi Narrative Frameworks

### Six-Point Testimonial Script
A structured narrative arc for capturing customer stories that hit all emotional and logical proof points. Use this for case study content, testimonial ads, social proof sections, and customer story posts.

1. **Internal Struggle** -- "What was rock bottom before doing this? What did you have to do that you hated? What things did you want to do that you couldn't?"
2. **External Struggle** -- "What were your objective metrics?" (revenue, weight, score, etc.)
3. **Skepticism** -- "What were your main concerns about buying/clicking/joining?"
4. **Overcome** -- "What made you do it anyway?"
5. **External Victory** -- "What are your objective metrics now?"
6. **Internal Victory** -- "What's been the best moment since? What can you do now you couldn't do before? What things did you hate doing you don't have to do anymore?"

The most important part is the hook -- have the subject say a few different hooks before telling their story. This gives you multiple opening options for different platforms/audiences.

Source: $100M Marketing Machine. See `content-strategy/references/hormozi-marketing-machine.md` for the full Marketing Machine system and `ads-creative/references/hormozi-goated-ads.md` for testimonial ad formats.

### Narrative Hooks (7 Verbal Types)
All verbal hooks fall into seven categories applicable to story openers:
- **Labels** -- Name the audience directly ("Business owners who...")
- **Questions** -- Yes or open-ended ("Would you...?" / "Which would you rather...?")
- **Conditionals** -- "If you're [situation], [implication]"
- **Commands** -- "Read this if..." / "Watch this if..."
- **Statements** -- "The [superlative] thing you can do..."
- **Lists/Steps** -- "[Number] ways to [result]"
- **Narratives** -- Scene-setting story openers ("One day I was...")
- **Exclamations** -- Strong emotion + big promise

These map to the story structure step: use Labels/Questions/Conditionals for the hook beat, Narratives for in media res openings, and Exclamations for emotional punch. See `video-hook/references/hormozi-hooks.md` for templates and 121 proven examples.
