## 1. Framework Decision Guide

Use this table to select a structure based on format, goal, and available length.

| Goal | Format | Best Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Inspire belief in transformation | Any | TORS |
| Teach through evidence | Case study, newsletter, thought leadership | SBI |
| Build brand credibility or founder narrative | Long-form, landing page, video | Hero's Journey |
| Tell any story with a complete arc | Any | Pixar Spine |
| Hook in a single paragraph | Email opener, social caption, newsletter intro | Mini-Story |
| Tell a campaign narrative with a customer | Ad, landing page, campaign sequence | 6-Element Campaign Arc |
| Position customer as hero | Brand copy, homepage, about page | Story Brand |
| Teach counterintuitive lesson | Thought leadership, newsletter, social | TORS + SBI combined |
| Create episodic content | Newsletter series, video series | Hero's Journey + Cliffhanger |
| Diagnose whether any draft has narrative energy | Any structure, any format | Story Grid Five Commandments (overlay) |

**Tiebreaker rule:** When uncertain, start with Pixar Spine. It forces a beginning, middle, and end every time and works for any length.

## 2. TORS: Transformation > Hurdle > Rise > Fall

### What It Is

TORS is a four-beat arc designed to make counterintuitive lessons believable. The "Fall" beat -- a setback after the initial Rise -- is what separates TORS from a simple before/after story. It signals that the transformation was earned, not handed over.

### When to Use

- Counterintuitive arguments ("less is more," "slower is faster," "smaller can outperform bigger")
- Personal origin stories where the protagonist nearly gave up
- Brand narratives where a setback preceded the real breakthrough
- Case studies where the client's first attempt failed before succeeding

### The Structure

**Transformation (T):** Name the state the protagonist achieves -- or the reader could achieve. This is the "after" the reader is buying into.

**Hurdle (H):** The specific obstacle or failure. Must be concrete and recognizable. The more specific, the more credible. Vague struggles ("it was really hard") build no trust. Named struggles ("open rate dropped from 37% to 19% in six weeks") do.

**Rise (R):** The pivot, insight, or change that begins to reverse the problem. Not the full win -- just the moment things start to shift.

**Fall (F, optional but powerful):** A setback after the Rise. The plan almost worked, then didn't. This beat earns the final resolution. Skip it for very short-form content; include it whenever length allows.

**Resolution:** The transformation delivered. Match the specificity of the Hurdle -- if you named a number going down, name the number coming back.

### Worked Example (Long-Form)

Transformation: A newsletter operator who doesn't need daily sends to stay relevant.

Hurdle: I sent daily for 14 months. Open rate fell from 37% to 19%. CTR went from 1.5% to 0.5%. I convinced myself the problem was content quality, so I started writing longer, researched more, spent more time per issue. Nothing moved.

Rise: A reader survey changed everything. The feedback was brutal: "I'm overwhelmed," "I skim now," "I used to love this." I cut to 5x per week and removed two ad slots per issue.

Fall: The first three weeks, my list shrank by 600 subscribers. Two sponsors asked if I was "pivoting." I almost reversed course.

Resolution: By week eight, open rate was back above 31%. CTR was 1.1%. The engaged list was generating more revenue than the larger, fatigued one. The 600 who left were the ones who had already stopped opening.

### Short-Form Variation (Social/Email)

Compress TORS to three sentences: name the transformation (one sentence), name the hurdle (one sentence), deliver the resolution (one sentence). Skip the Fall beat in very short content.

Example: "I stopped sending my newsletter daily. Open rates dropped before they climbed -- and I nearly reversed course when I lost 400 subscribers in week two. By month two, my CTR had tripled. The list that was left was worth twice as much as the one I started with."

## 3. SBI: Situation > Behavior > Impact

### What It Is

SBI is a case study skeleton. It delivers a lesson through documented evidence rather than argument. The key is that Behavior names a specific action (not a vague direction), and Impact quantifies the result. Readers trust specificity.

### When to Use

- Case studies (positive or negative)
- Lessons from reader questions or real events
- Thought leadership content ("here's what happened when someone did X")
- Post-mortem analyses ("here's what went wrong and why")
- Credibility-building content where the lesson needs to feel evidence-based

### The Structure

**Situation (S):** The context. Who was involved? What was the setup? Keep this to 2-3 sentences. Do not over-explain -- readers fill in context fast.

**Behavior (B):** The specific action taken. This is the causal variable. Be precise. "They increased ad frequency to 5 per issue" is Behavior. "They experimented with monetization" is vague and fails to teach anything.

**Impact (I):** What changed as a result. Quantify wherever possible. The Impact is the lesson. Name both the metric and the direction.

### Worked Example 1 (Positive)

Situation: A cybersecurity newsletter with 40,000 subscribers was monetizing entirely through network ad fills at $3 CPM.

Behavior: They added one direct sponsor slot per issue at $800 per placement, sent 4x per week.

Impact: Monthly ad revenue increased from $1,440 to $4,640 in eight weeks -- a 222% increase -- without adding subscribers or changing content.

### Worked Example 2 (Negative -- the Cautionary Case)

Situation: A SaaS marketing newsletter with a 32% open rate decided to test frequency.

Behavior: They scaled from 3x per week to daily sends over 60 days. Ad load stayed at 3 per issue.

Impact: Open rate fell to 15%. CTR dropped from 2.3% to 0.7%. Two direct sponsors declined to renew. Recovery to baseline took four months after reverting to 3x per week.

### Worked Example 3 (Team/Process Lesson)

Situation: A content team was producing 12 pieces per month with two writers and no documented brief process.

Behavior: They introduced a 15-minute story brief for every piece, specifying protagonist, conflict, lesson, and CTA before writing began.

Impact: Average time-to-complete per piece dropped 35%. Editor revision rounds decreased from 3.2 to 1.4 per piece. Reader engagement (time on page) increased 28%.

## 4. Hero's Journey (5-Act Compressed for Marketing)

### What It Is

The Hero's Journey is the universal story arc -- present in every culture, every era. For marketing, the full 12-stage version is too long. This compressed 5-act version retains the emotional engine while fitting into a brand story, founder narrative, or customer testimonial.

### When to Use

- Brand origin stories and about pages
- Founder narratives for long-form content, pitch decks, or press
- Customer testimonials you want to elevate beyond a quote
- Campaign narratives with a protagonist

### Marketing Adaptation Rule

In marketing Hero's Journey, your brand is NOT the hero. Your brand is the guide (Gandalf, not Frodo). The customer -- or your founder, or the protagonist -- is the hero. Your product enters the story at the Ordeal as the guide that enables the transformation.

### The 5-Act Structure

**Act 1: Ordinary World**
Who is the hero? What is their life before the problem? Establish enough normalcy that the disruption feels disruptive.
Marketing function: Creates identification. Readers see themselves before they see the solution.

**Act 2: Call to Adventure (Inciting Incident)**
What disrupts the ordinary world? This is the problem, the crisis, the moment when the status quo becomes untenable.
Marketing function: Names the pain. Readers recognize their own situation.

**Act 3: Ordeal (The Crisis Point)**
The hero faces their biggest challenge. This is where they are most likely to fail. Your product or brand enters here -- as the guide or the tool that enables the next step.
Marketing function: This is the product reveal or the brand value proposition -- but told as story, not feature list.

**Act 4: Transformation**
The hero uses the guide's help to overcome the ordeal. This is the turning point. Show the specific action that changed things.
Marketing function: Demonstrates the mechanism of your solution.

**Act 5: Return (The New World)**
The hero has changed. They bring something back. The "after" state is concrete, specific, and contrasted against Act 1.
Marketing function: Delivers the transformation promise. This is the proof.

### Worked Example 1: Brand Story

Act 1 (Ordinary World): "In 2019, I was running a cybersecurity blog with 3,000 readers. I had a day job, wrote on weekends, and posted whenever I could. It was a hobby with an audience."

Act 2 (Call to Adventure): "A vendor offered me my first sponsorship. $150 for a mention. I realized: this list was worth something. And I had no idea how to actually build it."

Act 3 (Ordeal): "I started daily sends. Hired a freelancer. Chased growth aggressively. 18 months later: 80,000 subscribers, 14% open rate, and a business I was embarrassed by."

Act 4 (Transformation): "I almost shut it down. Instead, I rebuilt from first principles: less content, better content, zero ads until I understood what my readers actually wanted."

Act 5 (Return): "Three years later: 215,000 subscribers, 37% open rate, and a business I'm proud to own. The lesson wasn't about sending more. It was about earning the right to be in the inbox."

### Worked Example 2: Customer Testimonial

Act 1: "Sarah had been running her agency for six years. She was good at the work and terrible at finding it. Revenue was feast or famine."

Act 2: "When her largest client left, she had 90 days of runway and no pipeline."

Act 3: "She tried cold outreach, paid ads, networking events. Nothing worked fast enough. Two months in, she found [Product] and committed to one methodology."

Act 4: "In 30 days, she had three qualified conversations. In 60 days, she'd signed two clients."

Act 5: "Today, Sarah's pipeline has four active prospects and six more in nurture. She hasn't had a feast-or-famine month since."

## 5. Pixar Spine

### What It Is

The Pixar Spine (also called the Story Spine) is a six-beat framework that generates a complete narrative arc from six sentence starters. It forces a beginning, middle, and end every time. Works at any length: compress for a 3-sentence email opener, expand into a full article.

### When to Use

- Any story that needs a complete arc
- When the writer is stuck on structure
- Pitch decks, brand videos, newsletter intros, social content
- The "starting framework" when other structures feel unclear

### The Six Beats

1. "Once upon a time..." (Setup: establish the world and protagonist)
2. "Every day..." (The norm: what life looked like before disruption)
3. "Until one day..." (Disruption: the inciting event that breaks the norm)
4. "Because of that..." (Consequence: what the disruption set in motion)
5. "Because of that..." (Escalation: stakes climbing -- can repeat this beat)
6. "Until finally..." (Resolution: the transformation delivered)
7. "Ever since then..." (optional) (New normal: what life looks like after)

### Marketing Adaptation

The beats do not need to use the literal sentence starters in published content. The beats are a planning scaffold. Write the story using the structure, then remove or rephrase the beat markers.

### Worked Example (Newsletter Brand Story)

Planning version (with beat markers):
- Once upon a time, I was sending my newsletter every day.
- Every day, I'd write, hit publish, and watch my open rates decline another tenth of a percent.
- Until one day, my CTR hit 0.4% and a reader emailed me: "I used to love this. I don't open it anymore."
- Because of that, I ran a reader survey. The feedback was uncomfortable.
- Because of that, I cut to 4x per week, removed two ad slots, and committed to better writing instead of more writing.
- Until finally, my open rate returned to 31% and CTR climbed to 1.2% -- and the smaller, more engaged list was generating more revenue.
- Ever since then, I've measured quality of attention, not quantity of sends.

Published version (beat markers removed): "I was sending my newsletter every day. Open rates declined every month. One reader's reply changed everything: 'I used to love this. I don't open it anymore.' That email forced a reader survey, uncomfortable feedback, and a hard cut to 4x per week. Open rate returned to 31%. CTR climbed to 1.2%. The lesson wasn't about sending more -- it was about earning the right to be in the inbox."

## 6. Mini-Story: 3-Sentence Maximum Impact Format

### What It Is

A mini-story delivers a complete narrative arc in exactly three sentences. No preamble, no backstory padding. Just the essential beats: enter the scene, add context, deliver the resolution or lesson.

### Formula

- **Sentence 1:** Drop into the most tense or interesting moment (In Medias Res). Start in the middle of action.
- **Sentence 2:** Provide the one piece of context that makes Sentence 1 make sense.
- **Sentence 3:** Deliver the resolution, lesson, or open loop.

### When to Use

- Email openers (hook before the deep dive)
- Newsletter intro paragraphs
- Social captions (especially LinkedIn, X threads)
- Ad intro copy
- Case study opener
- P.S. section of any email or newsletter

### 5 Worked Examples Across Formats

**Email opener:**
"I almost deleted my entire subscriber list in 2024. I'd confused the re-engagement segment with the full send list, and 47,000 emails went out to the wrong audience. That near-miss is why I now test every send on a 100-subscriber segment before touching the full list."

**Social caption (X/LinkedIn):**
"She had 800 subscribers and a 45% open rate. Every newsletter operator with 100,000 subscribers and a 12% open rate should study what she's doing. Volume is not the variable that matters."

**Ad intro copy:**
"The servers went down at 11 PM. Our biggest client was waiting, and we had four hours. That night changed how we build everything -- here's what we built."

**Newsletter paragraph (mid-issue illustration):**
"My best-performing issue was about a mistake. The worst-performing was about my biggest win. Readers root for the struggle, not the achievement -- and this pattern has repeated 40 times in two years."

**Case study opener:**
"Jane was spending two hours a day on tools that didn't talk to each other. In one week with [product], that dropped to 20 minutes. She now ships three times as many campaigns with the same team."

## 7. 6-Element Campaign Arc

### What It Is

A six-element structure that maps the complete story arc of a marketing campaign or brand narrative. Every persuasive campaign uses all six elements as a chain -- skip one and the story falls flat.

### The Elements

**Setting:** Establishes context. Grounds the audience in a world they recognize before the problem is introduced. Example: "In a world where every team runs on too many tools..."

**Characters:** Gives the audience someone to root for. Real customer stories outperform fictional ones. Names and faces make characters concrete. Example: "Jane, a marketing manager at a 30-person SaaS team..."

**Conflict:** The engine of the story. States the specific problem the character lives with. Without stated conflict, there is no reason to keep reading. Example: "Jane was losing two hours every morning to tool-switching and manual reporting."

**Climax:** The turning point. The moment your product enters the story as the inflection point -- not as a feature list, but as the tool that made the change possible. Example: "Then she connected [Product] to her stack. In 48 hours, the reporting was automatic."

**Resolution:** The transformed state. Show the "after" in concrete, specific terms. Example: "Now Jane's mornings are free. She ships three times as many campaigns with the same headcount."

**Moral / Message:** Crystallizes the brand promise. One clear takeaway the audience carries away. Example: "You don't need a bigger team. You need better tools."

### When to Use

- Full campaign narratives (ad series, email sequences)
- Landing page story sections
- Customer spotlight content
- Brand video scripts (provide to video-scriptwriter as narrative brief)

## 8. Story Brand Framework

### What It Is

Donald Miller's StoryBrand framework positions the customer as the hero of the story and your brand as the guide. The single most important positioning shift for any brand that tends to talk about itself instead of the customer's transformation.

### The Core Reframe

Wrong: "We are an innovative platform that uses AI to streamline marketing operations."
Right: "Marketing teams are drowning in tools. We built the one that ties them together -- so your team can focus on the work that moves the needle."

### The Structure

- **Hero (your customer):** Define them by what they want, not who they are.
- **Obstacle:** Name the specific external problem and the internal frustration it creates.
- **Guide (your brand):** You enter as the trusted, credible guide. Show empathy and competence.
- **Plan:** Give a clear, 3-step path forward. Complexity kills.
- **Success:** Paint the specific "after" state in concrete terms.
- **Failure avoided:** Name what happens if they don't act. Make the stakes real.

### Who-What-Why Formula (Short-Form Story Brand)

Three sentences. Works for introductions, bios, pitches, and about pages:
- Identity: "I'm a [role] who [unique value]"
- Offer: "I help [specific audience] achieve [concrete outcome]"
- Impact: "So that [bigger benefit that matters to them]"

## 9. Framework Combinations

### TORS + SBI (for Thought Leadership)

Use TORS to set up the emotional arc (the transformation you want the reader to believe in), then deploy SBI to prove it happened. The TORS arc tells the story; the SBI beats document the evidence.

Example: Lead with a TORS arc about why reducing content frequency improves results. Then introduce three SBI case studies that document newsletters that cut frequency and saw specific metric improvements. The combination is persuasive because it works on both emotional and rational levels.

### Hero's Journey + Pixar Spine (for Brand Videos)

Use Hero's Journey to establish the narrative beats (ordinary world, ordeal, transformation), then use Pixar Spine as the sentence-by-sentence planning scaffold within each act. The Hero's Journey gives you the macro structure; Pixar Spine ensures every micro-transition has causal momentum (Because of that... Because of that...).

### Mini-Story + Any Framework (for Entry Points)

Any long-form story using TORS, SBI, or Hero's Journey should open with a mini-story. The mini-story is the hook that earns the reader's attention for the fuller structure to follow. Write the mini-story first (3 sentences, In Medias Res opening), then expand into the full framework.

## 10. Story Grid Five Commandments as Framework Overlay

The Five Commandments of Storytelling (from Shawn Coyne's Story Grid methodology) are not a competing framework -- they are a diagnostic overlay that works inside any of the eight structures above. Every TORS arc, every SBI case study, every Hero's Journey contains all five commandments. Naming them makes the story tighter.

The five beats: **Inciting Incident** (disrupts the status quo) > **Progressive Complications / Turning Point** (the "but/therefore" chain escalates) > **Crisis** (a binary dilemma with no easy answer) > **Climax** (the protagonist's active choice) > **Resolution** (the consequence that completes the value shift).

**Example: TORS with Five Commandments layered on:**

- **T (Transformation):** "A newsletter operator who doesn't need daily sends." = The Resolution (value shift complete).
- **O (Hurdle):** "Open rate fell from 37% to 19%. I wrote longer, researched more. Nothing moved." = Inciting Incident + Progressive Complications.
- **R (Rise):** "A reader survey changed everything: 'I'm overwhelmed.' I cut to 5x per week." = Turning Point.
- **S (Fall then resolution):** "First three weeks, list shrank by 600." = Crisis (reverse course or stay?). "By week eight, open rate back above 31%." = Climax + Resolution.

The commandments do not add steps -- they name what is already there, so you can check for gaps. If your draft is missing the crisis (the dilemma), the story will feel flat even if the structure is correct.

## 11. Pixar's 22 Rules -- The 10 Most Applicable to Marketing

Pixar's 22 rules are real production principles from Pixar's story development process. The 10 most transferable to marketing content, with marketing-specific application notes:

### Rule 1: Audiences root for effort and journey, not just success.

Marketing application: Show the path. Readers connect with your attempt, not your arrival. Your failures and pivots are more engaging than your achievements. Do not skip to the win -- show the stumble that made it meaningful.

### Rule 4: Simplify. Focus. Combine. Hop over detours.

Marketing application: Every marketing story should have one point. Cut every sentence that does not serve that point. If you can say it in fewer words, those extra words are working against you. Constraints are a creative tool, not a compromise.

### Rule 6: The Story Spine (the Pixar Spine above).

Marketing application: Use this for any piece of content where you feel stuck on structure. It forces a beginning, middle, and end every time.

### Rule 7: What is the essence? Find the most economical telling.

Marketing application: Before writing any story, write one sentence that captures what you want the reader to walk away with. Then build to that sentence. Every paragraph either supports it or should be cut.

### Rule 9: What is interesting to the reader is not the same as what is interesting to you as the writer.

Marketing application: Your reader wants the fastest path to the result you are promising. Spend 90% of your effort delivering value. Your process, your craft, your journey -- secondary. Serve the reader's curiosity, not your own need to explain.

### Rule 11: If you were your reader, in this situation, how would you feel?

Marketing application: Before finishing any piece, read it as the person who signed up for your newsletter or clicked your ad. Are they getting what they came for? Is the payoff worth the read time? Be honest. Cut anything that exists for the writer's benefit rather than the reader's.

### Rule 12: Come up with your ending before you figure out your middle.

Marketing application: Know your takeaway or CTA before writing the body. The middle becomes obvious once you know what you are building toward. Writers who draft backward from the conclusion produce more coherent content than those who write forward from an intro.

### Rule 16: Give your characters opinions. Avoid hedging language.

Marketing application: Your content needs a point of view. "It seems like maybe this could potentially be worth considering" is not a point of view. "This is the only metric that matters -- everything else is noise" is a point of view. Strong opinions invite engagement. Hedges produce forgettable content.

### Rule 21: Discount the first idea. The obvious angle is what everyone else uses.

Marketing application: Your first story idea for any piece is probably the expected one -- the one every competitor in your niche would choose. After drafting it, ask: "What angle would surprise my reader?" Then write that one instead.

### Rule 22: Finish and publish. Perfectionism is procrastination.

Marketing application: An 80% piece that ships is worth more than a perfect piece still in drafts two weeks later. Readers value consistency and presence more than occasional perfection. Publish. Learn. Improve the next one.
