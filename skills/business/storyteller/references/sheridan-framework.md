# The Sheridan Framework

Storytelling principles extracted from Taylor Sheridan's screenwriting craft across Sicario, Yellowstone, 1883, 1923, and Landman. Adapted for marketing content, newsletters, social posts, and video scripts.

Sheridan's work is "simple but deep" -- stripped-down plots carrying enormous emotional and philosophical weight. His craft is the opposite of most marketing storytelling: instead of explaining everything and resolving with a CTA, he withholds information, raises questions, and lets behavior speak louder than dialogue.

**When to use this framework:** When you want to write content that FEELS like a great scene from a movie, not a marketing playbook. When the story itself IS the product. When you want readers sitting with a question, not rushing to click.

## 1. The 10 Sheridan Principles

### Principle 1: Simple Plot, Complex Character

> "The simpler the plot, the more time I have to really look inside the character."
> -- Taylor Sheridan (Writing Studio interview)

The plot is a container. Character is the product. Sicario's plot is one sentence: CIA helps install a cartel monopoly. 1883's plot: family travels west, most people die, family reaches Montana. Yellowstone: don't take my land, I want your land. That's it. Everything else is character.

**Application:** Your content topic is the simple plot. The person in the story -- you, a client, a character -- is where the depth lives. Don't over-complicate the premise. One situation, infinite human complexity.

### Principle 2: No Outline, Listen to the Story

> "I don't outline. I spend months playing the movie in my head until I really understand what this character is trying to do."

> "There's a story that wants to be told. If you listen, it will tell itself really easily."

Sheridan writes first drafts with no rewrites. All three Frontier Trilogy scripts (Sicario, Hell or High Water, Wind River) were shot from first drafts. He lets characters dictate direction.

**Application:** Don't start with the lesson. Start with the person. What do they want? What are they afraid of? Let the story unfold from their behavior, not from a pre-planned takeaway. If you're forcing three scenes to justify one point, you're fighting the story.

### Principle 3: The Audience Wonders What Happens Next, Never What's Happening Now

> "You always want the audience wondering what's going to happen next, never what's happening."

Present-tense confusion is a writing failure. The reader should always be pulled forward, never looking around trying to figure out what's going on.

**Application:** Clarity of moment, mystery of outcome. Every sentence should be immediately understandable but leave the reader wanting the next one. If you have to re-read a paragraph to understand what happened, you lost.

### Principle 4: Show, Don't Tell (The Camera Rule)

> "Never let a character tell me something that the camera can show me."

This comes from his acting career. He spent 15 years delivering bad exposition on TV and hated every second of it.

**Application:** Don't tell the reader "I was devastated." Show the moment: "I sat in the parking lot for 40 minutes staring at the dashboard." Don't say "the product was a game-changer." Show the numbers, the behavior change, the specific moment it clicked.

### Principle 5: Every Line Reveals Character

> "Anywhere that I see hello, yes or no, there must be a more interesting way to say those things that tell us something about somebody."

No pleasantries. No filler dialogue. Every exchange in a Sheridan script tells you something about who that person is. When Alejandro tells Kate "You're asking me how a watch works. For now just keep your eye on the time" -- that's not just deflection. It reveals his worldview: she's a tool, not a partner.

**Application:** Cut every sentence that doesn't reveal something about the person in your story. "We had a call" says nothing. "He picked up on the first ring, which told me everything" says everything.

### Principle 6: Write Toward the Voice

> Sheridan writes toward specific actors. He told Billy Bob Thornton: "I'm writing something with you in mind, I'm writing it in your voice." He cast Harrison Ford for 1923 before the script existed.

**Application:** Know whose voice you're writing in before you write the line. For newsletters, that's your voice (read private voice-reference notes). For case studies, it's the client's voice. For social, it's the character in the story. The voice dictates the rhythm, vocabulary, and emotional register.

### Principle 7: Landscape Is Not Decoration

Across all Sheridan's work, the physical environment makes thematic arguments the dialogue never has to. Montana in Yellowstone is the thing being fought over AND the thing doing the judging. The border in Sicario is the moral line. The trail in 1883 is the cost of the dream.

**Application:** Where the story happens matters. "I was sitting in a WeWork at 11 PM" tells you something different than "I was on a call from my kid's soccer game." Use setting to make the argument your words don't have to.

### Principle 8: Don't Preach -- Raise Questions, Withhold Answers

> "I can't stand to pay money and have somebody preach to me their ideas. That's the fastest way to get me out of it."

> "The holy grail as a storyteller is entertain, educate, and enlighten. Don't give anybody answers, just lots of questions to think about."

> "My job as a storyteller is to pick a world and look through the window and not judge it."

Sheridan never tells you what to think. Sicario ends with no resolution -- the cartel system continues, Kate signs away her moral authority, soccer games resume after gunfire. 1883 doesn't argue whether westward expansion was right. It shows what it cost.

**Application:** End with a question, not a lesson. Resist the urge to wrap everything in a neat bow. The content that sticks is the content that makes someone think after they close the tab. "I don't know the answer to this, but I can't stop thinking about it" is more powerful than "Here are 5 steps to solve this."

### Principle 9: Subtext Over Text -- What's NOT Said Carries the Meaning

Alejandro in Sicario never explains his backstory. His dead wife and daughter are revealed through a subordinate clause from another character, late in the film. His grief is his behavior across the entire movie. Sheridan originally wrote much more dialogue for Alejandro. 90% of it was cut. The character became iconic.

In 1883, Shea Brennan doesn't give a speech about grief. He burns his house down and walks to the ocean. That's the speech.

**Application:** The most powerful thing in your story might be what you don't say. Leave gaps. Let the reader fill them in. "I never opened that email again" is more powerful than a paragraph explaining why.

### Principle 10: Write for Yourself First

> "I always write the movie that I want to go see."

**Application:** If you wouldn't stop scrolling to read this, nobody else will either. Write the thing that makes YOU feel something. The audience follows genuine emotion, not calculated hooks.

## 2. The Sheridan Story Structure (8-Beat)

Adapted from the structural patterns across Sicario (five-act nested structure), 1883 (odyssey with flash-forward), and Yellowstone (pressure-cooker family dynamics).

### Beat 1: THE WINDOW

Pick a world. Look through it. Don't judge it.

Open with a scene that drops the reader into a specific moment in a specific world. No context. No setup. No "let me tell you about..." Just the window. The reader is looking through it before they know what they're seeing.

Sheridan's Sicario opens with a SWAT raid that reveals 42 bodies in the walls. 1883 opens with a flash-forward of Elsa wounded. Yellowstone opens with a livestock agent getting shot. You're IN it before you understand it.

*2-3 sentences. Specific. Sensory. Immediate.*

### Beat 2: THE SURROGATE

Give the audience ONE person to see through.

Kate Macer in Sicario is not the protagonist -- Alejandro is. But Kate is the audience. She asks the questions we're thinking. She knows only what we know. She's as lost as we are. That's the power.

Introduce the person whose eyes we see through. This person doesn't have to be the hero. They have to be the one experiencing what we need to feel.

*1-2 sentences establishing who we're following and what they want.*

### Beat 3: THE SIMPLE ENGINE

One sentence plot. Infinite character depth.

State the situation in the simplest possible terms. "I needed to make payroll by Friday." "She had 30 days to prove the tool worked or lose the client." "I told my team we were pivoting. Nobody said anything." The simpler the engine, the more room for what matters.

*1-2 sentences. The situation, stripped bare.*

### Beat 4: THE GAP

Withhold information. Create tension through not-knowing.

This is Sheridan's primary tension tool. In Sicario, Kate is never told the real mission. The audience doesn't know either. The gap between what we suspect and what we're told is where dread lives. In 1883, we know from the opening that Elsa dies. But Sheridan makes us forget. Then it hits harder.

Something doesn't add up. Information is missing. The reader leans in because they need to know what happens next.

*2-4 sentences. Build the tension. Don't resolve it yet.*

### Beat 5: THE REVEAL

Let behavior show truth before dialogue confirms it.

Sheridan doesn't do revelation speeches. Alejandro's revenge mission is revealed through what he DOES in the tunnel, not through a monologue. In 1883, Elsa's transformation is shown through how she rides, who she loves, how she faces death -- not through a lesson she delivers.

The truth of your story arrives through action, behavior, or consequence. Not through explanation.

*2-4 sentences. Show what happened. Don't explain what it means yet.*

### Beat 6: THE COST

Every choice has a price. Show the price.

Kate signs away her moral authority at gunpoint. Elsa reaches Montana in a coffin. Shea Brennan reaches the Pacific alone. The Duttons keep the ranch but lose every relationship that matters. In Landman, Tommy keeps the rigs running but his family falls apart.

Nothing in Sheridan's world comes free. The cost is what makes the story true. What was lost? What was sacrificed? What can never be undone?

*1-3 sentences. The price paid.*

### Beat 7: THE QUESTION

End with a question, not an answer.

Sicario ends with the soccer game resuming after gunfire. Is this justice? Is this order? Is there a difference? 1883's final narration asks what we'd sacrifice for a dream we might never see. Sheridan never resolves his central moral tensions. He leaves them open.

Your ending is not a lesson. It's a question the reader sits with after they close the tab. Make it specific. Make it personal. Make it uncomfortable.

*1-2 sentences. The question you can't stop thinking about.*

### Beat 8: THE TOOL (Content-Specific)

Soft CTA -- the tool/product connects to the story's theme, not its lesson.

This beat doesn't exist in Sheridan's work (he's not selling products). But in content marketing, the CTA is necessary. The key: the product/tool connects to the QUESTION, not to an answer. "I built something that helps with this. Want to see it?" not "Here's the 5-step solution to the problem I just described."

*1-2 sentences. Separated from the editorial. Thematic connection, not sales pitch.*

## 3. Dialogue Rules

From Sheridan's 15 years as an actor watching bad TV dialogue:

### Rule 1: Kill Pleasantries
No hello. No goodbye. No "how are you." No "thanks for taking the time." Every line earns its place by revealing character.

### Rule 2: Subtext > Text
What the character says and what they mean are rarely the same thing. "You're asking me how a watch works. For now just keep your eye on the time" means: you're a tool, not a partner. Write the surface. Let the reader hear the depth.

### Rule 3: Find More Interesting Ways to Say Ordinary Things
"I lost the client" vs. "The client stopped returning my calls on a Tuesday. By Thursday, I stopped trying." Same information. One reveals character.

### Rule 4: Let Silence Speak
Sheridan's most powerful moments often have no dialogue at all. Shea Brennan watching the ocean. Kate staring at her gun. James Dutton sitting on the porch. Silence after a statement can say more than the statement itself.

### Rule 5: Rough Characters, Beautiful Language
1883's poetry comes from cowboys and pioneers, not professors. Elsa says "God is the land." Shea says "When you love somebody, you trade souls with them." The beauty is earned through suffering, not education. Don't polish the voice. Let the rough edges carry the beauty.

## 4. Scene Construction

### Each Scene Is Independently Compelling
Sheridan structures around individual scenes, not act breaks. Every scene must work on its own. If someone read only one paragraph of your content, would it be worth reading?

### Environment Makes the Argument
The border crossing in Sicario makes the moral argument without a single speech. The empty Montana landscape in Yellowstone says "this is worth dying for" without anyone saying it. Let where the scene happens tell the reader what the scene means.

### Violence (Conflict) Without Catharsis
The border shootout in Sicario happens, and then... nothing. No debrief. No processing. The operators walk away. Kate stands there. Conflict in Sheridan's world doesn't resolve cleanly. Apply this: don't wrap up the tension. Let it linger.

### The Foreshadow-Forget-Deliver Pattern
1883 opens by showing you Elsa's death. Then makes you fall in love with her for nine episodes until you forget. Then delivers what it promised. Put the ending at the beginning. Make the reader forget. Then remind them.

## 5. Adaptation Guide

### Newsletter Application

| Sheridan Technique | Newsletter Application |
|---|---|
| The Window | Open with a scene, not a tip. Drop into a moment. |
| The Surrogate | Write from ONE person's POV. Don't switch perspectives. |
| The Gap | Withhold the lesson. Build tension about what it means. |
| The Reveal | Show what happened through behavior, not explanation. |
| The Cost | Name what was lost or sacrificed. Don't skip this beat. |
| The Question | End with a question, not a takeaway. |
| Subtext | Subject lines that hint but don't explain. |
| Simple Engine | One situation per issue. Not three tips. One story. |

**Best for:** Build-in-public content, founder stories, "I don't have the answer" essays, stories about cost and trade-offs, content that builds long-term trust over short-term clicks.

### Social Post Application

One scene. One character reveal. One question.

Format: 3-5 lines max.
- Line 1: The Window (specific moment)
- Line 2-3: The Gap + Reveal (what happened, what it showed)
- Line 4: The Question (what you're still thinking about)

No lessons. No tips. No "here's what I learned." Just the scene and the question.

### Video Script Application

Sheridan's "observer not judge" principle is the key for video:
- Don't tell the viewer what to think about what they're seeing
- Show the moment. Let silence do the work.
- Simple premise, complex character moment, no preaching
- The best video scripts are the ones where the viewer says "wait, what happened?" and keeps watching

### Email Sequence Application

Gap-and-reveal structure across emails:
- Email 1: The Window (drop into a world, no context)
- Email 2: The Surrogate (who are we following?)
- Email 3: The Gap (something doesn't add up)
- Email 4: The Reveal (behavior shows truth)
- Email 5: The Cost + Question (what was the price? what would you do?)
- Email 6: The Tool (soft CTA, thematic connection)

## 6. When to Use Sheridan vs Other Frameworks

| Situation | Use Sheridan | Use Something Else |
|---|---|---|
| You have a personal story with no neat lesson | Yes | -- |
| You want to build trust through vulnerability | Yes (but real, not tactical) | Welsh Narrative (if lesson is clear) |
| The topic is morally complex with no easy answer | Yes | -- |
| You need to teach a specific technique | -- | SBI or Deep Dive |
| You want maximum engagement/shares | -- | Hook-Story-Offer |
| You need a CTA-heavy conversion piece | -- | Brunson Epiphany Bridge |
| You want the reader to feel something they can't name | Yes | -- |
| You want to show, not tell | Yes | -- |
| You have a before/after with clear metrics | -- | Case Study Metrics Arc |
| Build-in-public content about trade-offs and costs | Yes | -- |
| Origin story with a clear aha moment | -- | Epiphany Bridge |
| Origin story where the cost was the point | Yes | -- |

**Rule of thumb:** If your story has a neat lesson, use Welsh Narrative or SBI. If your story has a question you're still sitting with, use Sheridan.
