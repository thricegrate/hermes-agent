# Brunson Storytelling Frameworks

Russell Brunson's storytelling system from Expert Secrets and DotCom Secrets. These frameworks focus on story as a selling mechanism -- turning personal experiences into narratives that move people toward a new opportunity and through a buying decision.

Use alongside the structural frameworks in `storytelling-frameworks.md`. Brunson's frameworks define the WHAT of the story (which story to tell, which archetype to inhabit). The existing frameworks define the HOW (structure, beats, devices).

## 1. Epiphany Bridge

The Epiphany Bridge is a 14-question script that extracts a complete origin story from any experience. The goal is not to make the audience understand your idea logically. The goal is to make them FEEL the same epiphany you felt -- so they arrive at the same conclusion on their own.

### The Core Principle

People do not buy because they understand. They buy because they feel. The Epiphany Bridge takes your reader on the same emotional journey you went through, so they experience the aha moment themselves rather than being told about it.

### Two Techniques That Make Epiphany Bridges Work

**Oversimplification:** Tell the story at a third-grade reading level. Strip jargon. Strip complexity. If a 10-year-old cannot follow the story, simplify further. Complexity makes people feel stupid. Simplicity makes people feel smart. Feeling smart is what makes them buy.

**"Kinda Like" Bridges:** When you must explain a complex concept, connect it to something the reader already understands. "The email sequence works kinda like a first date -- you don't propose on the first meeting. You build trust, show value, then ask." Every unfamiliar concept gets a "kinda like" bridge to a familiar one.

### The 14-Question Script

Work through these questions in order. Your answers become the raw material for the story.

#### Phase 1: Backstory (Questions 1-3)

- [ ] **Q1 -- Personal Backstory:** What is your background? Where were you before this journey started? What was your ordinary world? (Keep it relatable. If the audience cannot see themselves in your starting point, the bridge collapses.)

- [ ] **Q2 -- Desires:** What did you want? Name both:
  - External desire: the tangible goal (money, freedom, subscribers, revenue)
  - Internal desire: the emotional need (respect, confidence, identity, proving someone wrong)

- [ ] **Q3 -- Failed Vehicles:** What did you try that did not work? List every approach, method, course, strategy, or tool you attempted before finding the new opportunity. Each failed vehicle validates the audience's own frustration. "I tried X too and it didn't work for me either" = instant rapport.

#### Phase 2: The Journey (Questions 4-6)

- [ ] **Q4 -- The Call / Trigger:** What specific event forced you to change? A conversation, a loss, a number on a screen, a moment of humiliation. Name the exact trigger. Vague triggers ("I just knew something had to change") do not create emotional resonance. Specific triggers ("I checked my account and saw $247 left") do.

- [ ] **Q5 -- The Villain:** What or who was working against you? The villain can be a person, a system, a belief, an industry norm, or an internal voice. Name it. Stories without a villain have no tension. The villain gives the audience someone to root against.

- [ ] **Q6 -- Consequences of Failure:** What would have happened if you had not found the solution? Make the stakes real and specific. "I would have had to go back to my old job" is good. "I would have had to tell my kids we were moving again" is better. The higher the personal stakes, the more invested the audience becomes.

#### Phase 3: The New Opportunity (Questions 7-9)

- [ ] **Q7 -- The Guide / Mentor:** Who or what showed you the way? A person, a book, a conversation, a random discovery. The guide is the bridge between your old world and the new opportunity. (Note: In your customer's story, YOU become the guide. This question helps you model the pattern.)

- [ ] **Q8 -- The Aha Moment:** What was the specific epiphany? The single insight that changed everything. Describe the moment, not the lesson. Where were you? What were you doing? What did you see, hear, or realize? The more sensory and specific, the more the reader re-experiences it with you.

- [ ] **Q9 -- The New Opportunity:** What is the new vehicle, method, or approach you discovered? This is NOT an improvement on the old thing. It is a replacement. "A better diet" is an improvement. "Intermittent fasting" is a new opportunity. New opportunities are easier to sell because they carry no baggage of past failure.

#### Phase 4: The Framework (Questions 10-12)

- [ ] **Q10 -- Your Strategy:** What is the specific system, process, or framework you built? Describe the mechanism. This is where the abstract insight from Q8 becomes a concrete, repeatable method.

- [ ] **Q11 -- Your Results:** What happened when you applied the strategy? Name specific outcomes. Numbers, timelines, before/after metrics. Your results are proof that the epiphany was real, not just a nice feeling.

- [ ] **Q12 -- Others' Results:** Who else has used this and gotten results? Third-party proof is stronger than self-proof. Even one other person's transformation story dramatically increases credibility.

#### Phase 5: The Transformation (Questions 13-14)

- [ ] **Q13 -- External Achievement:** What tangible result did you achieve? The metric, the milestone, the number. This is what the audience consciously wants.

- [ ] **Q14 -- Internal Identity Shift:** Who did you become? This is the deeper transformation. Not just "I made more money" but "I stopped seeing myself as someone who needs permission." The internal shift is why the external result sticks. Without it, the achievement feels hollow and temporary.

### Epiphany Bridge Checklist (Post-Draft)

After writing, verify:

- [ ] The story starts where the audience is right now (not where you are now)
- [ ] Failed vehicles are named specifically (not "I tried everything")
- [ ] The aha moment is a scene, not a statement
- [ ] Complex concepts have "kinda like" bridges
- [ ] Language passes the third-grade test (no jargon, no showing off)
- [ ] The audience can feel the epiphany, not just understand it
- [ ] Both external achievement AND internal identity shift are present

## 2. Hero's Two Journeys

Every compelling story has two journeys running in parallel. Most marketing stories only tell Journey 1. The ones that create real connection tell both.

### Journey 1: The Achievement (External)

The visible, measurable goal. The thing the audience came for.

- The revenue number
- The subscriber count
- The product launch
- The business built
- The freedom achieved

This is the plot. The audience consciously tracks this journey and evaluates whether the outcome is desirable.

### Journey 2: The Transformation (Internal)

The invisible identity shift. The beliefs that had to die for the achievement to happen.

- "I'm not a real business owner" dies and becomes "I run a real business"
- "I need more credentials" dies and becomes "I know enough to help people"
- "Charging money for this feels wrong" dies and becomes "My work has clear value"
- "I need someone's permission" dies and becomes "I give myself permission"

This is the character arc. The audience subconsciously connects with this journey. It is why they trust you, feel inspired, and ultimately buy.

### Why Both Journeys Matter

- **Journey 1 only:** "I built a newsletter to 215K subscribers and it makes $25K/month." Impressive but cold. The audience thinks "good for you" but does not feel connected. No emotional residue.

- **Journey 2 only:** "I went through a deep personal transformation about my relationship with work." Vague and self-indulgent. No proof, no credibility, no reason to keep reading.

- **Both journeys together:** "I built a newsletter to 215K subscribers. But the real shift was the moment I stopped apologizing for being a non-native English speaker building a media company. That insecurity held me back for two years. Killing it is what made the growth possible." Now the audience feels something. They see themselves in the internal struggle. The external result becomes proof that the internal shift works.

### Two Journeys Checklist

For any story you write, answer:

- [ ] **Journey 1:** What is the external achievement? (Name it in one sentence with a number.)
- [ ] **Journey 2:** What belief had to die? What new identity replaced it? (Name both.)
- [ ] **The Connection:** How did the internal shift enable the external result? (If you cannot draw the causal line, the two journeys are running in parallel but not intersecting. They need to intersect.)
- [ ] **The Reveal:** Where in the story do you show Journey 2? (Best placement: after the external result is established. First show what happened, then show who you became. The internal shift reframes the external achievement.)

### Integration with Existing Frameworks

The Two Journeys layer onto any structure in `storytelling-frameworks.md`:

- **TORS:** The Transformation beat covers Journey 1. Add Journey 2 in the Resolution.
- **Hero's Journey (5-Act):** Act 4 (Transformation) is Journey 1. Add Journey 2 in Act 5 (Return).
- **Story Grid:** Journey 1 = External Object of Desire. Journey 2 = Internal Object of Desire (see `story-grid-marketing.md` Section 5). Brunson's framework and Story Grid agree here completely.

## 3. Six Core Attractive Character Storyline Types

Six recurring story patterns that build audience connection. Every piece of story-driven content you create fits into one of these types. Know which one you are using before you write.

### Type 1: Loss and Redemption

Hit bottom. Came back stronger. The audience roots for someone who has been down and refuses to stay there.

**Pattern:** Stable life > catastrophic loss (financial, relational, health, identity) > rock bottom (specific, painful, named) > the turn (what changed) > the climb back > current state (earned, not given)

**When to use:**
- Awareness stage: builds immediate trust with skeptical audiences
- Origin stories and about pages
- When you need to establish credibility through earned wisdom
- Re-engagement campaigns when audience trust has eroded

**Checklist:**
- [ ] The loss is specific and real (not "things got tough")
- [ ] Rock bottom has sensory detail (where were you, what did it feel like)
- [ ] The turn is a choice, not luck
- [ ] Current state is contrasted against the bottom, not against "normal"

### Type 2: Us vs. Them

Taking a stand. Drawing a line. Declaring what you are FOR and what you are AGAINST. Polarizing content attracts true fans and repels wrong-fit audience.

**Pattern:** Name the enemy (a belief, a practice, an industry norm) > show why it harms YOUR people > declare your alternative > show the result of your way > invite people to your side

**When to use:**
- Awareness stage: cuts through noise by taking a clear position
- Brand positioning and manifestos
- Social content designed to spark engagement and shares
- When the market is crowded and "me too" messaging fails

**Checklist:**
- [ ] The "them" is a belief or system, not a person (attack ideas, not individuals)
- [ ] You clearly state what you stand FOR, not just what you oppose
- [ ] The stakes are real (why does this battle matter to the audience)
- [ ] There is a clear invitation to join your side

### Type 3: Before and After

The classic transformation story. Simple, direct, powerful.

**Pattern:** Paint the "before" in vivid detail > name the turning point > paint the "after" with equal specificity > connect the dots (what caused the shift)

**When to use:**
- Consideration stage: proves transformation is possible
- Case studies and testimonials
- Product demonstrations and results showcases
- Email sequences that build desire

**Checklist:**
- [ ] "Before" is specific enough that the audience recognizes themselves
- [ ] The turning point is a named action, not a vague "everything changed"
- [ ] "After" is quantified (numbers, timelines, concrete outcomes)
- [ ] The mechanism is clear (what specifically caused the transformation)

### Type 4: Amazing Discovery

You stumbled onto something that changed everything. The audience gets to experience the discovery with you.

**Pattern:** You were doing X > you accidentally found Y > you tested it > the results surprised you > here is what it means for you

**When to use:**
- Awareness stage: curiosity-driven content that earns clicks and shares
- Newsletter content and thought leadership
- Product origin stories ("how we accidentally built this")
- Social content optimized for engagement

**Checklist:**
- [ ] The discovery feels accidental and authentic (not manufactured)
- [ ] You tested it (discovery without proof is just a claim)
- [ ] The results are specific and surprising
- [ ] There is a clear "here is what this means for you" bridge

### Type 5: Secret Telling

You have access to hidden knowledge, insider information, or overlooked data -- and you are sharing it with your audience.

**Pattern:** "Most people believe X" > "but insiders know Y" > reveal the secret with proof > show what changes when you apply it > invite them deeper

**When to use:**
- Consideration stage: positions you as the insider guide
- Lead magnets and gated content
- Email sequences building toward a sale
- Webinar and workshop content

**Checklist:**
- [ ] The "secret" is genuinely valuable (not manufactured scarcity)
- [ ] The conventional belief is stated clearly first (the contrast sells the secret)
- [ ] Proof accompanies the reveal (data, case study, personal result)
- [ ] There is a "level 2" secret behind the first one (creates desire for more)

### Type 6: Third-Person Testimonial

Someone else's transformation story, told through your lens. Proof that your method works for others, not just you.

**Pattern:** Introduce the person (relatable to your audience) > their starting point > what they tried (failed vehicles) > finding your method > their result > their words (direct quotes)

**When to use:**
- Decision stage: removes "will this work for me?" objection
- Sales pages and landing pages
- Email sequences in the close phase
- Social proof sections on any page

**Checklist:**
- [ ] The person resembles your target audience (not an outlier or celebrity)
- [ ] Their starting point matches where your audience is now
- [ ] Failed vehicles are named (validates the audience's own struggles)
- [ ] Results are specific and the person's own words are quoted
- [ ] The story serves the sale without feeling forced

### Storyline Type by Funnel Stage (Quick Reference)

| Funnel Stage | Best Storyline Types | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Loss and Redemption, Us vs. Them, Amazing Discovery | Trust-building, attention-grabbing, polarizing |
| Consideration | Before and After, Secret Telling, Amazing Discovery | Proof, insider positioning, desire-building |
| Decision | Third-Person Testimonial, Before and After, Secret Telling | Objection removal, social proof, urgency |

## 4. Attractive Character Storytelling System

The Attractive Character (AC) is the persona your audience follows, trusts, and buys from. It is not a fictional character -- it is a deliberate, consistent presentation of who you actually are, amplified for connection.

### The 4 Elements of an Attractive Character

#### Element 1: Backstory

Your origin. Where you started. The relatable beginning that lets the audience see themselves in you.

**Rules:**
- Start where the audience is, not where you are now
- Include specific struggles they recognize
- The backstory earns the right to teach (no backstory = no credibility for advice)

**Test:** If someone reads your backstory and thinks "that person started way ahead of me," it is not relatable enough. Rewrite from an earlier, messier starting point.

#### Element 2: Parables

Daily life moments turned into lessons that sell. The AC takes everyday experiences and extracts insights that connect to the product, service, or philosophy.

**The Parable Formula:**
1. Something happens in your day (real, specific, mundane is fine)
2. You notice a parallel to a business/marketing principle
3. You draw the connection for the audience
4. The connection naturally leads to your offer or philosophy

**Example:** "I watched my kid try to build a Lego set without the instructions. After 20 minutes of frustration, he finally opened the manual. Built the whole thing in 30 minutes. That is exactly what happens when people try to build an email funnel without a framework -- they burn hours on something that takes minutes with the right system."

**Frequency:** Every email, every social post, every newsletter issue should contain at least one parable. Parables are the daily storytelling fuel of the AC.

#### Element 3: Character Flaws

Deliberate vulnerability. Sharing imperfections, quirks, and limitations that make you human rather than polished.

**Rules:**
- Share real flaws, not humble brags ("my flaw is I work too hard" is not a flaw)
- Flaws create rapport because perfection creates distance
- One flaw per story is enough. Do not overdo it.
- Flaws should be relatable to the audience, not alienating

**Examples of effective flaws:**
- "English is my third language. Every piece of content I publish, I wonder if it sounds weird."
- "I procrastinate on video because I hate how I look on camera."
- "I still check my subscriber count three times a day. It is not healthy."

#### Element 4: Polarity

Strong stances that attract true fans and repel wrong-fit audience. The AC does not try to appeal to everyone.

**Rules:**
- Take clear positions on topics in your space
- Accept that strong positions will lose some people -- that is the point
- Polarity creates passionate followers, not lukewarm ones
- Never be offensive for its own sake. Be genuinely opinionated about things that matter to your work.

**Examples:**
- "Daily newsletters are a trap. I will die on this hill."
- "If your content sounds like ChatGPT wrote it, your audience already knows."
- "Free tools are not charity. They are the best sales mechanism ever invented."

### The 4 AC Identity Archetypes

Choose ONE primary archetype. You can have secondary traits from others, but your primary archetype should be consistent across all content.

#### The Leader

Confident. Directive. "I have been where you are. Follow me. I know the way."

**Tone:** Authoritative but earned. Not lecturing -- leading from experience.
**Best for:** Coaches, consultants, course creators, anyone selling expertise.
**Content style:** Clear prescriptions, frameworks, "do this not that," definitive statements.
**Risk:** Coming across as arrogant if not balanced with vulnerability (Element 3).

#### The Adventurer / Crusader

On a mission. Fighting for something. Building something bigger than themselves.

**Tone:** Passionate, driven, slightly obsessive. "I am building this thing and you can come along."
**Best for:** Founders, builders, activists, anyone on a visible journey.
**Content style:** Build-in-public updates, mission statements, progress reports, rallying cries.
**Risk:** Mission fatigue if every post is a manifesto. Balance with parables (Element 2).

#### The Reporter / Evangelist

Discovers and shares. Curates the best of what they find. Always bringing new insights to the audience.

**Tone:** Curious, generous, "look what I found for you."
**Best for:** Newsletter operators, curators, researchers, industry analysts.
**Content style:** Discoveries, analyses, "I tested this so you don't have to," curated insights.
**Risk:** Becoming purely informational with no personal story. Layer in backstory and parables.

#### The Reluctant Hero

Did not want the spotlight. Got pulled in by circumstances. Now here, doing the work.

**Tone:** Humble, self-deprecating, "I can't believe I'm the one saying this, but..."
**Best for:** Introverts, technical founders, anyone whose audience expects modesty.
**Content style:** Personal stories told with surprise at their own success, teaching through "what I learned" rather than "what you should do."
**Risk:** Staying reluctant so long that the audience does not trust your confidence. At some point, own the expertise.

### AC System Checklist

For ongoing content production:

- [ ] **Archetype chosen:** Which of the four are you? (Write it down. Reference it before every piece.)
- [ ] **Backstory documented:** Your origin story is written and can be referenced in fragments across content.
- [ ] **Parable habit:** Every piece of content has at least one daily-life-to-lesson connection.
- [ ] **Flaw shared:** At least one genuine vulnerability per week in your content.
- [ ] **Polarity expressed:** At least one strong stance per week. Something that would make a wrong-fit audience member unsubscribe.
- [ ] **Storyline rotation:** Cycle through the 6 storyline types (Section 3) so content stays varied. Do not default to the same type every time.
