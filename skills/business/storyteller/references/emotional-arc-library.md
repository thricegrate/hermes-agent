# Emotional Arc Library

A reference for selecting the right story type based on the emotional response you want to create, the funnel stage you are writing for, and the message you are trying to land. Use this alongside `storytelling-frameworks.md` -- the frameworks tell you the structure; this file tells you the emotional strategy beneath the structure.

## 1. Six Story Types by Primary Emotion

Each story type is defined by the primary emotion it is engineered to create. Lead with one emotion per piece -- emotional coherence beats emotional variety. Mixing multiple primary emotions in a single story dilutes all of them.

### Aspiration: "The Transformation You Achieved -- or They Could Achieve"

**Primary emotion produced:** Hope, possibility, motivation
**Reader response:** "If they did it, maybe I can too"
**When to use:** When your reader knows the problem but needs to believe the solution is within reach for someone like them. Works at awareness and consideration stages.

**Story pattern:** Before state (recognizable to reader) > Struggle (credible, specific) > Turning point > After state (specific, concrete, earned)

**What makes it land:** The protagonist must resemble the reader closely enough to create projection. If the hero is too far removed (celebrity, outlier, prodigy), the aspiration collapses into envy. Specific struggles build the resemblance.

**Marketing examples:**
- "From 3,000 subscribers to 215,000 -- and the three years of mistakes in between"
- "She was getting $2.50 CPM. Here's how she negotiated her first direct sponsor at $25 CPM"

**Common mistake:** Showing the transformation without the struggle. A story that goes from problem directly to win is not an aspiration story -- it is a claim. The struggle is the credibility.

---

### Redemption: "The Failure That Became Wisdom"

**Primary emotion produced:** Trust, relatability, forgiveness of the reader's own past failures
**Reader response:** "They screwed up and got real -- I can trust this person"
**When to use:** When you need to build parasocial trust quickly. When your audience is skeptical of polished success narratives. Works at all stages but most powerful at awareness and consideration.

**Story pattern:** Failure or mistake (specific, not performatively modest) > The real cost of the mistake > What it forced > What was learned > How that learning applies to the reader

**What makes it land:** The failure must be real enough to be embarrassing. Performative vulnerability ("I once struggled too!") is detected immediately and destroys trust. Real vulnerability shares something the writer would rather not share.

**Marketing examples:**
- "I gave bad advice in my January issue. Three weeks later I found out I was wrong. Here's the correction."
- "I almost shut this newsletter down in month six. Here's what I was thinking -- and what stopped me."

**Common mistake:** Ending on the failure without the lesson. A redemption story without a lesson is just a confession. The reader needs to know what to do with the information.

---

### Cautionary: "The Mistake to Avoid"

**Primary emotion produced:** Fear (constructive), urgency, alertness
**Reader response:** "I need to make sure this doesn't happen to me"
**When to use:** When there is a common mistake in your niche that readers are making without realizing it. When the cost of inaction is concrete and nameable. Works at awareness and consideration stages.

**Story pattern:** Setup (a protagonist doing something recognizable) > The mistake or decision (specific, datable) > The consequence (quantified, real) > The lesson (actionable for the reader)

**What makes it land:** The protagonist must be sympathetic -- they were doing something reasonable, not something stupid. If the reader thinks "I would never do that," the cautionary effect fails. The mistake must feel like one they could make.

**Marketing examples:**
- "She scaled to daily sends. Here's what it cost her over six months."
- "They raised their ad load before fixing their CTR. The revenue calculus that broke their renewal rate."

**Common mistake:** Making the cautionary story about blame rather than education. Readers learn from cautionary stories when they feel empathy for the protagonist. Judgment shuts learning down.

---

### Celebration: "The Win Worth Sharing"

**Primary emotion produced:** Pride, validation, social proof, excitement
**Reader response:** "This works. I want to be associated with this."
**When to use:** When you have a reader win, a client result, or a milestone that proves your method works. Use sparingly -- celebration stories lose credibility if overused. Works at consideration and decision stages.

**Story pattern:** The result (lead with the specific outcome) > The starting point (where they began) > What they did (the specific behavior) > The before/after contrast

**What makes it land:** The result must be specific and verifiable-feeling. "She grew her list by 1,247 subscribers in 30 days" lands. "She grew her list dramatically" does not. Always lead with the result -- if the result is not compelling, the story is not worth telling as a celebration.

**Marketing examples:**
- "He ran his first direct sponsor pitch. Got $800 for one mention. Here's the exact email he sent."
- "She cut sends from 7 to 3 per week. Opens went from 14% to 31%. Revenue went up."

**Common mistake:** Burying the result at the end. Celebration stories should lead with the payoff. Readers who do not know there is a win coming will not invest in the setup.

---

### Discovery: "The Counterintuitive Finding"

**Primary emotion produced:** Surprise, curiosity, intellectual excitement
**Reader response:** "I didn't know that -- and now I need to think differently"
**When to use:** When you have a finding, insight, or data point that contradicts conventional wisdom. When you want to generate shares and discussion. Works at awareness stage and for thought leadership.

**Story pattern:** The conventional assumption (what everyone believes) > The moment of discovery (how you found out this was wrong) > The evidence (specific and quantified) > The implication (what this means for the reader)

**What makes it land:** The conventional assumption must actually be conventional -- if most readers already know the finding, the discovery falls flat. The more common the assumption and the more specific the contradiction, the stronger the story.

**Marketing examples:**
- "Everyone says grow your list. I shrank mine. Here's what happened to revenue."
- "More sends meant less revenue -- and the data behind why"

**Common mistake:** Confusing contrarian positioning with genuine discovery. A discovery story needs evidence (data, experiment, case study). Contrarian positioning ("the gurus are wrong!") without evidence is just noise.

---

### Curiosity: "The Mystery with a Reveal"

**Primary emotion produced:** Compulsion, anticipation, engagement
**Reader response:** "I cannot stop reading until I know how this ends"
**When to use:** When you want maximum retention within a piece of content. When you are writing a multi-part series. When you need readers to read all the way to the CTA.

**Story pattern:** Open an unanswered question (the mystery) > Withhold the resolution > Build toward it with escalating stakes or partial reveals > Deliver the resolution with full payoff

**What makes it land:** The question must be genuinely interesting to the reader, not just to the writer. The resolution must be worth the wait. A curiosity story that builds tension and then delivers a weak payoff is worse than no curiosity gap -- it trains readers to distrust future hooks.

**Marketing examples:**
- "I made a decision that cost me $8,000. It was also the best business decision I've ever made. I'll explain."
- "There is one metric most newsletter operators never look at. It predicted my CTR drop three months before it happened."

**Common mistake:** Never closing the loop. An open loop that stays open is not intrigue -- it is frustration. Close every curiosity gap within the same piece, or explicitly in the next installment with a clear callback.

---

### Story Grid Connection: Value Directions

Each story type maps to a value direction from Story Grid (see `references/story-grid-marketing.md`):

- **Aspiration, Celebration, Discovery** = Prescriptive (values move from negative to positive). The story proves something works.
- **Cautionary** = Cautionary (values move from positive to negative). The story proves something fails.
- **Redemption** = Cautionary-to-Prescriptive (values move down, then back up). The story proves recovery is possible after failure.
- **Curiosity** = The value shift IS the reveal. The direction is hidden until the resolution.

When selecting a story type, you are also selecting a value direction. Use this to write your Controlling Idea: "[Value] is [won/lost] when [specific circumstances]." See `references/story-grid-marketing.md` Section 3 for the full formula and examples.

## 2. Seven Proven Emotional Arc Patterns

These are structural arc patterns -- each one maps to a specific emotional journey over the course of the story. These are complementary to the story types above. A "Redemption" story type can follow a "Man in a Hole" or "Cinderella" arc shape.

| Arc | Shape | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Cinderella** | Rise, fall, rise | Brand recovery stories, comeback narratives, product pivots |
| **Man in a Hole** | Fall, then climb | Founder stories, personal redemption, crisis resolution |
| **Rags to Riches** | Steady climb | Growth stories, cumulative wins, newsletter milestones |
| **Icarus** | Rise, then fall | Cautionary tales, "what not to do" content |
| **Tragedy** | Fall, rise, fall | Industry cautionary stories (use sparingly) |
| **Oedipus** | Fall, rise, fall | Complex cautionary arcs with a final price paid |
| **Riches to Rags** | Downward slide | Very specific cautionary content about overconfidence |

**Practical note:** For most marketing content, you need only three: Man in a Hole (redemption/recovery), Rags to Riches (growth/aspiration), and Cinderella (setback followed by breakthrough). The others are useful for understanding why certain brand stories fail (Icarus) or for analyzing competitors.

## 3. Emotional Arc by Funnel Stage

Match the emotional goal of your story to where the reader is in their relationship with you.

### Awareness Stage: Curiosity / Recognition / Surprise

At awareness, readers do not know or trust you yet. Stories at this stage need to earn attention by creating curiosity or recognition -- "that's exactly my situation" before "here's what to do about it."

**Best story types:** Curiosity (mystery with reveal), Discovery (counterintuitive finding), Cautionary (mistake to avoid)
**Best structures:** Mini-Story (fast hook), In Medias Res opening, Pixar Spine
**Emotional goal:** Surprise them. Make them feel seen. Create an open loop.
**Avoid:** Celebration stories (no trust established yet), heavy aspiration without proof

**Example tone:** "I ran a test that contradicted everything I thought I knew about send frequency. The results surprised even me."

---

### Consideration Stage: Aspiration / Trust / Validation

At consideration, readers know the problem and are evaluating solutions. Stories at this stage need to demonstrate that the solution works for someone like them.

**Best story types:** Aspiration (transformation), Redemption (earned wisdom), Celebration (specific wins)
**Best structures:** TORS, SBI (with positive outcome), Hero's Journey
**Emotional goal:** Build belief. Show the transformation is real and achievable.
**Avoid:** Curiosity stories that never resolve, abstract lessons without proof

**Example tone:** "A newsletter operator with 40,000 subscribers cut her ad load and watched her CPM triple. Here is exactly what she did."

---

### Decision Stage: Confidence / Urgency / Belonging

At decision, readers are close to acting but hesitating. Stories at this stage need to reduce friction, build confidence, and create a mild fear of missing out.

**Best story types:** Celebration (social proof through story), Cautionary (cost of inaction)
**Best structures:** SBI with specific before/after, Mini-Story with transformation result
**Emotional goal:** Remove the last objection. Make acting feel safe and not acting feel costly.
**Avoid:** Long build-up stories, redemption arcs that remind readers of failure

**Example tone:** "Three months ago, she was where you are -- unsure whether to cut send frequency. She cut it. Here is where she is now."

---

### Post-Purchase Stage: Pride / Belonging / Anticipation

Post-purchase stories serve retention and upsell. They celebrate the reader's decision and set up what is coming next.

**Best story types:** Celebration (reinforce their choice), Discovery (new insight that rewards loyalty)
**Best structures:** Mini-Story (quick wins), Pixar Spine (what comes next)
**Emotional goal:** Make them feel smart for choosing you. Build anticipation for what is next.
**Avoid:** Anything that re-introduces doubt, cautionary stories about the product category

**Example tone:** "You made the right call. Here's something most readers don't discover until month three."

## 4. Story Trigger Selection Guide

Given an emotional goal, which story type should you reach for?

| You want to create... | Reach for... | And pair it with... |
|---|---|---|
| Curiosity | Curiosity (mystery/reveal) | In Medias Res opening, curiosity gap device |
| Hope/possibility | Aspiration | TORS structure, specific before/after |
| Trust/authenticity | Redemption | Vulnerability device, specific failure details |
| Urgency/alertness | Cautionary | SBI structure, quantified consequences |
| Excitement/social proof | Celebration | Lead with the result, SBI for proof |
| Intellectual excitement | Discovery | Contrast device, specific data point |
| Inspiration | Aspiration or Redemption | Hero's Journey or TORS |
| Controversy/discussion | Discovery or Cautionary | Contrarian positioning device |

## 5. Fear vs. Aspiration: The Arc Structure

Fear and aspiration are not opposites -- they are a sequence. The most effective marketing stories open with fear (or friction) and transition to aspiration. Fear without aspiration leaves readers stuck. Aspiration without fear feels naive.

### The Fear-to-Aspiration Arc

**Step 1 -- Name the fear (2-3 sentences):** State the specific consequence the reader is trying to avoid. Quantify it. Make the stakes real.
Example: "Your open rate is sitting at 18%. You've normalized it. You shouldn't have. That number is costing you $X in ad revenue every month you leave it there."

**Step 2 -- Acknowledge the reader's current state (1-2 sentences):** Show empathy. Demonstrate that you understand why they are where they are.
Example: "You're sending more because you think presence means frequency. That's the natural assumption, and it's wrong."

**Step 3 -- Introduce the bridge (the insight or turning point):** The specific action or understanding that changes things.
Example: "The metric that matters isn't how often you send. It's how many readers are actively waiting for your next issue."

**Step 4 -- Deliver the aspiration (2-3 sentences):** Paint the specific outcome that is possible. Use concrete numbers.
Example: "When operators fix the quality-frequency ratio, open rates recover to 30-35% within 8 weeks. CTR follows. Ad revenue per send increases because the audience is engaged again."

In Story Grid terms, this is the cautionary-to-prescriptive arc: values shift down (fear beat), then up (aspiration beat). The controlling idea follows the pattern: "[Value] is won when [the protagonist acts despite the fear]."

### When to Use Pure-Fear Stories

Use cautionary stories without the aspiration pivot when:
- Your goal is to disrupt complacency in an audience that does not feel urgency
- You have a specific, documented consequence that the reader can verify
- The fear is actionable (they can do something about it)

Avoid pure-fear stories when:
- The fear is paralyzing rather than motivating (too catastrophic, nothing the reader can do)
- You have no credible solution to offer
- Your audience is already anxious -- amplifying fear without resolution creates avoidance, not action

## 6. The Emotional Resonance Test

Run any story-based piece through these five questions before publishing:

**Question 1: Does the story produce one clear primary emotion?**
If you cannot name the primary emotion in one word, the story is trying to do too many things. Pick one and rebuild around it.

**Question 2: Does the protagonist resemble the reader closely enough?**
If the hero is too famous, too exceptional, or too different from your reader, the aspiration or identification fails. Ask: "Would my reader say 'that could be me'?"

**Question 3: Is the struggle specific enough to be credible?**
Vague struggles ("it was hard") build no trust. Specific struggles ("open rate fell from 37% to 14% over six months") are evidence. Check every struggle statement for specificity.

**Question 4: Does the story connect to a lesson the reader can act on?**
A story that ends with "and that's what happened" has failed. Every story needs a bridge to application. Can you complete this sentence: "The lesson for your situation is..."?

**Question 5: Does the emotion at the end serve the CTA?**
If the story ends on fear but the CTA is "check out this resource," there is a mismatch. The emotional state at the end of the story should make the CTA feel like the obvious next move. Fear should lead to "protect yourself now." Aspiration should lead to "start this." Curiosity should lead to "find out."
