# Story Grid for Marketing

The Story Grid is a narrative analysis methodology created by editor Shawn Coyne after 30+ years in publishing. It provides a systematic framework for understanding why stories work or fail. This reference translates Story Grid's most powerful concepts for marketing content: newsletters, emails, social posts, case studies, ads, and thought leadership.

Story Grid is not a competing framework to TORS, SBI, Hero's Journey, or the other structures in `storytelling-frameworks.md`. It is an analytical layer that works inside any of them. Think of the existing frameworks as the skeleton of a story and Story Grid as the circulatory system that keeps it alive.

## 1. The Five Commandments of Storytelling (Marketing Application)

Every unit of story -- whether a 300-page novel or a 3-paragraph email -- must contain all five commandments. In marketing, this means every email, every social post, every case study needs these five beats to function as narrative rather than information.

### The Five Commandments

**1. Inciting Incident**
Destabilizes the status quo. Something changes, something breaks, something unexpected happens. Without it, there is no reason to keep reading.

**2. Progressive Complications / Turning Point**
The initial approach fails or the situation escalates. New information forces a reassessment. This is where the South Park "but/therefore" rule lives -- each complication connects causally, not additively.

**3. Crisis**
The protagonist faces a binary dilemma with no easy answer:
- **Best Bad Choice:** Two options, both have a cost. Pick the least awful.
- **Irreconcilable Goods:** Two desirable options, but you can only have one.

This is the moment of maximum tension. The reader leans forward because the answer is not obvious.

**4. Climax**
The protagonist's active answer to the crisis. Not what happened to them -- what they chose to do. This reveals character under pressure. In marketing, this is where the insight lands or the key action is taken.

**5. Resolution**
The meaningful consequence tied to what was at stake. The new reality after the choice. This is where the value shift completes -- the reader sees what changed and why it matters.

### Fiction-to-Marketing Translation

| Story Grid Term | Marketing Equivalent | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| Inciting Incident | Hook / Subject line promise / Opening disruption | Earns attention by breaking the status quo |
| Progressive Complications | The "but/therefore" chain / Tension build | Builds stakes and prevents the reader from settling |
| Crisis | The decision point / The tension the reader feels | Creates the moment of maximum engagement |
| Climax | The insight / The reveal / The key action taken | Delivers the payoff the reader has been waiting for |
| Resolution | The takeaway / CTA / New understanding | Completes the value shift and moves reader to action |

### Worked Example 1: Newsletter Email

> **Inciting Incident:** "I cut my newsletter from daily to 4x per week."
>
> **Progressive Complications:** "The first week, 200 people unsubscribed. My ad partner asked if I was 'scaling down.' A competitor started sending daily and bragged about their volume."
>
> **Crisis (Best Bad Choice):** "I could reverse course and protect the subscriber count. Or I could stay the course and trust that engagement would recover -- knowing it might not."
>
> **Climax:** "I stayed. I used the freed-up time to write one deeply researched issue per week instead of five rushed ones."
>
> **Resolution:** "Eight weeks later: open rate back above 31%. CTR at 1.2%. The 200 who left were people who had stopped opening months ago. Revenue per send increased 40%."

### Worked Example 2: Social Post (Compressed)

All five commandments in a LinkedIn post:

> "I deleted 3,000 subscribers from my list last month. [Inciting Incident]
>
> They hadn't opened in 90 days, BUT they were padding my 'total subscribers' number -- the one I bragged about. [Progressive Complication]
>
> Keep the vanity metric or trust the engagement math? [Crisis]
>
> I hit delete. [Climax]
>
> Open rate jumped from 29% to 36% in two weeks. Sponsors noticed. My next deal closed at 2x the CPM. [Resolution]
>
> The list you brag about and the list that pays you are not the same list."

### Worked Example 3: Case Study

> **Inciting Incident:** A SaaS newsletter with 40,000 subscribers was monetizing entirely through network ad fills at $3 CPM.
>
> **Progressive Complications:** Revenue plateaued at $1,440/month. They tried adding more ad slots -- CTR dropped. They tried increasing frequency -- open rates fell. Every obvious lever made things worse.
>
> **Crisis (Irreconcilable Goods):** They could keep the safe, predictable network revenue. Or they could replace two network slots with one direct sponsor slot -- higher upside but requiring sales effort they had never done.
>
> **Climax:** They pitched three companies in their niche. One said yes at $800 per placement, 4x per week.
>
> **Resolution:** Monthly ad revenue went from $1,440 to $4,640 in eight weeks -- a 222% increase -- without adding subscribers or changing content. The direct relationship also gave them leverage to raise network fill rates.

### The Diagnostic Question

After writing any draft, ask: "Can I identify all five commandments?" If you cannot find the crisis (the dilemma), the piece is probably information disguised as story. Add the dilemma. Make the reader feel the tension of the choice before you deliver the answer.

## 2. Value Shifts

The single most powerful diagnostic in Story Grid: every piece of content must shift a value from one pole to another. If no value changes, there is no story -- just information with narrative window dressing.

### The Principle

A "value" is anything that can exist on a spectrum between a positive and negative pole. In fiction, these are Life/Death, Love/Hate, Justice/Injustice. In marketing, the values are different but the principle is identical: something must change polarity over the course of the piece.

### Four Core Values for Marketing Content

| Value Spectrum | Positive Pole | Negative Pole | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Safety / Risk** | Protected, secure, prepared | Exposed, vulnerable, blindsided | Cybersecurity, health, finance, compliance, risk content |
| **Connection / Isolation** | Belonging, community, partnership | Loneliness, irrelevance, being left behind | Community content, social proof, belonging narratives |
| **Insight / Ignorance** | Understanding, clarity, mastery | Confusion, wrong assumptions, blind spots | Education, thought leadership, discovery content |
| **Fairness / Exploitation** | Transparency, fair value, empowerment | Being taken advantage of, hidden costs, manipulation | Consumer advocacy, industry critique, positioning content |

### How to Use Value Shifts

1. **Before writing:** Identify which value your story shifts, and in which direction.
2. **During writing:** Ensure the opening establishes one pole and the ending delivers the opposite.
3. **After writing:** Run the diagnostic: "If I removed the story and just stated the lesson, would anything be lost?" If no, the value didn't shift -- you wrote an argument, not a story.

### Worked Example: Value Shift in a Newsletter Email

**Value:** Insight / Ignorance
**Direction:** Ignorance to Insight (prescriptive)

Opening (Ignorance pole): "I was convinced that send frequency was the growth lever. More sends = more revenue. The math seemed obvious."

Middle (the shift happens): "But the data told a different story. My highest-revenue month came from 16 sends, not 31. The readers who generated 80% of click revenue were opening 3-4 issues per week, not 7."

Close (Insight pole): "Frequency is not a growth lever. Engagement per send is. Every additional send that dilutes engagement is a net negative -- even if the raw numbers look bigger."

The reader starts at one pole (believing frequency = growth) and ends at the other (understanding that engagement per send is the real metric). That shift is the story. Without it, this is just a blog post stating an opinion.

### The No-Shift Test

If a piece of content starts and ends at the same emotional or intellectual position, it has no value shift. Common symptoms:
- "Here are 7 tips for better subject lines" -- information, no shift
- "Subject lines matter" at the start AND "subject lines matter" at the end -- no shift
- The reader nods along but nothing they believed or felt actually changed

Fix: Find the thing your reader believes that is wrong or incomplete. Start there. End at the corrected understanding. That gap is the value shift.

## 3. Controlling Idea

The Controlling Idea is the single sentence that captures what your story proves. It is the sharpest possible version of the "Lesson" field in the story brief templates.

### The Formula

**"[Value at stake] is [won/lost] when [specific circumstances]."**

This forces you to name three things: what value is at play, whether it increases or decreases, and under what conditions. If you cannot write this sentence, you do not yet know what your story is about.

### Marketing Examples

- "Revenue is won when operators prioritize engagement quality over send frequency."
- "Trust is lost when newsletters prioritize ad load over reader experience."
- "Growth is won when content serves one specific reader instead of trying to serve everyone."
- "Clarity is won when founders test assumptions against data instead of scaling what feels right."
- "Credibility is lost when success stories skip the struggle and jump straight to results."

### How to Use

Write the controlling idea BEFORE writing the story. It is the compass. Every paragraph either supports this sentence or should be cut. If you find yourself drifting, re-read the controlling idea and ask: "Does this paragraph prove, illustrate, or advance this idea?"

### Connection to Story Briefs

The Controlling Idea upgrades the existing "Lesson" field in `templates/story-brief.md`. Where the Lesson field asks "what should the reader understand?", the Controlling Idea forces you to also name the value at stake and the condition that determines the outcome. Use both: write the Controlling Idea first, then derive the Lesson from it.

## 4. Prescriptive vs. Cautionary

Every story moves values in one direction. Story Grid calls these two directions prescriptive and cautionary.

### Prescriptive Stories

Values move from negative to positive. The protagonist makes the right choice, applies the right insight, takes the right action -- and the outcome improves. The controlling idea says "[Value] is WON when..."

**Marketing use:** Aspiration stories, celebration stories, discovery stories, case studies with positive outcomes, transformation narratives.

### Cautionary Stories

Values move from positive to negative. The protagonist makes a mistake, ignores a signal, or takes the wrong path -- and pays the price. The controlling idea says "[Value] is LOST when..."

**Marketing use:** Cautionary tales, "what not to do" content, competitive positioning ("here is what happens when you choose the wrong approach"), post-mortem analyses.

### The Power Move: Cautionary-to-Prescriptive

The most effective marketing stories open cautionary and close prescriptive. Values move down first (establishing the fear, the cost, the mistake), then reverse and move up (delivering the insight, the solution, the transformation).

This is the Fear-to-Aspiration Arc from `references/emotional-arc-library.md`, now with Story Grid vocabulary. The "fear" beat is the cautionary movement. The "aspiration" beat is the prescriptive movement.

### Mapping to Existing Story Types

| Story Type (from emotional-arc-library.md) | Story Grid Direction | Controlling Idea Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Aspiration | Prescriptive | "[Value] is won when [protagonist acts]" |
| Celebration | Prescriptive | "[Value] is won when [specific behavior]" |
| Discovery | Prescriptive | "[Insight] is won when [assumption is challenged]" |
| Cautionary | Cautionary | "[Value] is lost when [mistake is made]" |
| Redemption | Cautionary-to-Prescriptive | "[Value] is lost when [mistake], but won back when [lesson applied]" |
| Curiosity | The value shift IS the reveal | "[Value] is [won/lost] when [the mystery resolves]" |

## 5. Objects of Desire

Every protagonist has two desires. Understanding both is what separates a good marketing story from a great one.

### External Object of Desire (What They Want)

The tangible, measurable outcome: more subscribers, higher revenue, better CTR, a direct sponsor deal, a product launch. This is what the protagonist consciously pursues.

In marketing, the external object of desire is the hook. It is the metric, the result, the outcome that gets the reader's attention.

### Internal Object of Desire (What They Need)

The deeper transformation: confidence as a business owner, freedom from imposter syndrome, the identity shift from "hobbyist" to "operator," the courage to charge what they are worth. This is what the protagonist does not consciously pursue but must achieve for the external goal to be meaningful.

In marketing, the internal object of desire is why the story resonates. It is the part readers cannot articulate but feel.

### How They Work Together

- External only: "She grew from 3,000 to 215,000 subscribers." (Impressive but flat.)
- External + Internal: "She grew from 3,000 to 215,000 subscribers -- but the real transformation was the moment she stopped apologizing for charging money for her work." (Resonant.)

The external object drives the plot. The internal object drives the emotional connection. Great marketing stories deliver both.

### Examples for Marketing Content

| Format | External Object (Want) | Internal Object (Need) |
|---|---|---|
| Newsletter case study | "Triple her CPM from $3 to $9" | "Stop undervaluing her audience and own her worth" |
| Founder story | "Build a newsletter to 100K subscribers" | "Become someone who ships imperfect work instead of perfecting forever" |
| Product testimonial | "Cut reporting time from 4 hours to 20 minutes" | "Feel competent and in control instead of drowning in tools" |
| Cautionary tale | "Scale to daily sends for more revenue" | "Learn that more is not always better -- quality of attention is the asset" |

### In Story Briefs

When filling out a story brief, answer both:
- **External:** What does the protagonist/reader want to achieve? (The metric, the outcome)
- **Internal:** What do they need to become or understand? (The identity shift, the belief change)

If you can only identify the external object, the story will inform. If you can identify both, the story will transform.

## 6. Genre Conventions for Marketing

Story Grid's genre system was built for fiction (12 content genres, each with specific conventions and obligatory moments). The full taxonomy is too detailed for marketing content. But the underlying principle is directly applicable: every content format has conventions your audience expects, and violating them without intention breaks trust.

### The Principle

Conventions are the elements your audience expects to find in a given format. They are not rules -- they are reader contracts. Break them consciously (subversion), not accidentally (incompetence).

Obligatory moments are the scenes or beats the audience is specifically waiting for. Miss them and the content feels incomplete, even if the reader cannot articulate what is missing.

### Marketing Format Conventions

**Newsletter case study:**
- Conventions: Named protagonist (or anonymized but specific), quantified before/after, specific timeframe, identifiable behavior change
- Obligatory moments: The problem stated in measurable terms, the specific action taken, the quantified result
- If missing: Feels like a vague endorsement rather than a credible story

**Founder/brand origin story:**
- Conventions: A relatable starting point, a genuine struggle, a near-quit moment, a specific turning point
- Obligatory moments: The "ordinary world" before the business, the moment the problem became personal, the first proof the solution worked
- If missing: Feels like corporate mythology rather than an earned narrative

**How-to / educational content:**
- Conventions: A clear promise of what the reader will learn, step-by-step progression, worked examples
- Obligatory moments: The "why this matters" setup, at least one "common mistake" to avoid, the specific mechanism that makes it work
- If missing: Feels like a list of tips rather than genuine teaching

**Cautionary tale:**
- Conventions: A sympathetic protagonist (not someone the reader can dismiss as foolish), a recognizable mistake, quantified consequences
- Obligatory moments: The moment the protagonist could have changed course but didn't, the cost made specific
- If missing: Feels like judgment rather than education

**Discovery / thought leadership:**
- Conventions: A widely held assumption named explicitly, evidence that contradicts it, a new mental model
- Obligatory moments: The "everyone believes X" setup, the "but actually Y" reveal, the implication for the reader's situation
- If missing: Feels like a contrarian take without substance

### How to Use

Before writing any piece, ask: "What format is this? What does my reader expect to find?" Then check: did you include every convention and obligatory moment? If you intentionally skip one, make sure you know why and that the subversion serves the story.

## 7. Five Commandments Quick-Reference by Format

How each commandment manifests in common marketing formats:

| Commandment | Newsletter Email | Social Post | Case Study | Ad Copy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Inciting Incident** | Opening hook that disrupts the reader's assumption | First line that stops the scroll | The problem the customer faced | The pain point or disruption |
| **Progressive Complications** | The "but/therefore" chain building tension | 2-3 sentences escalating stakes | What the customer tried that failed | The cost of inaction |
| **Crisis** | The dilemma the protagonist faced (or the reader faces) | The implicit choice: old way vs. new way | The decision point before using the product | "You can keep doing X, or..." |
| **Climax** | The insight, action, or decision that changed things | The key reveal or counterintuitive truth | What the customer did (the specific behavior) | The product as the answer to the crisis |
| **Resolution** | The takeaway + CTA | The lesson + invitation to engage | The quantified result + what changed | The transformed state + CTA |

### Compression Rule

In short-form content (social posts, email subject lines, ad headlines), the five commandments compress but never disappear. A single sentence can contain an inciting incident and crisis simultaneously: "I deleted 3,000 subscribers" (inciting incident) + "and revenue went up" (resolution that implies the full arc). The reader's mind fills in the complications, crisis, and climax.

In long-form content, give each commandment room to breathe. The crisis especially benefits from expansion -- the longer the reader sits in the dilemma, the more satisfying the climax.
