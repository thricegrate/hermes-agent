## Ad Assembly Line Process

**Source:** $100M Goated Ads Playbook, pp.5-8

Ads are not created as whole units. They are assembled from interchangeable parts: hooks, meats, and CTAs. This modular approach turns ad production into a factory line where one recording day per week generates hundreds of unique ad variations.

### The Three Parts

1. **Hooks** -- The first 1-5 seconds. The call out + condition for value that makes someone stop scrolling. This is where 80% of your prep time goes.
2. **Meats** -- The ad body. The substance that delivers on the hook's promise. Five major formats (see below). This gets 20% of your prep time.
3. **CTAs** -- The call to action. What to do next. These are formulaic and get almost zero dedicated prep time.

### The Math

- Write **50 hooks** per week
- Record **3-5 meats** per week
- Record **1-3 CTAs** per week
- Mix and match: **50 x 3-5 x 1-3 = 150-750 unique ad variations per week**

### One Recording Day Per Week

Devote one full day per week to ad production (e.g., every Friday). Batch all recording into that single day. The rest of the week is research, hook writing, and analyzing results.

### The Defense Benefit

When you run 150-750 ads per week, no competitor can reverse-engineer what's working. They see hundreds of variations and can't tell which hook-meat-CTA combination is your winner. This is a structural advantage that gets stronger the more ads you produce.

### Why This Works

Most businesses create ads as monolithic units: write one script, shoot it, edit it, ship it. If that ad fails, you lost the whole day. With the assembly line, if a hook fails, the meat and CTA still work with a different hook. You're never starting from scratch.

## Awareness Pyramid (Schwartz Adaptation)

**Source:** $100M Goated Ads Playbook, pp.3-4, 8-11

Hormozi adapts Eugene Schwartz's 5 levels of awareness into a continuous scaling model. Each awareness level maps to a different hook type. The broader (colder) you go, the better your ads must be. This is used to write "expansion hooks" that reach new market segments you've never touched.

### The Five Levels

#### Level 1: Most Aware (Bottom of Pyramid, Smallest Audience)

They know you, know your product, and are ready to buy. They just need the push.

**Hook type:** Offer-driven hooks

**Examples:**
- "50% off our best-selling XYZ"
- "Last chance to get [product] at this price"
- "We just added [feature] to [product]"

**Characteristics:** Direct, transactional, assumes familiarity. Works on existing customers and warm subscribers.

#### Level 2: Product-Aware

They know your product exists but haven't bought yet. They need proof it works.

**Hook type:** Proof-driven hooks

**Examples:**
- "Why 10,000 people chose XYZ"
- "[Name] used [product] and got [result] in [time]"
- "See what [customer type] are saying about [product]"

**Characteristics:** Social proof, case studies, numbers. Removes doubt by showing others already trust you.

#### Level 3: Solution-Aware

They know solutions exist to their problem but don't know your specific product. They need a promise.

**Hook type:** Promise-driven hooks

**Examples:**
- "The fastest way to [result]"
- "Get [result] without [common pain/effort]"
- "What if you could [dream outcome] in [timeframe]?"

**Characteristics:** Leads with outcome. Positions your solution as the best path to something they already want.

#### Level 4: Problem-Aware

They feel the pain but don't know solutions exist. They need empathy first, then a bridge to the solution.

**Hook type:** Pain-driven hooks

**Examples:**
- "Tired of [problem]? There's a better way"
- "Still struggling with [pain]?"
- "[Problem] is costing you more than you think"

**Characteristics:** Validates their experience. Acknowledges the pain before introducing anything else.

#### Level 5: Completely Unaware (Top of Pyramid, Largest Audience)

They don't even know they have a problem. They need curiosity to pull them in.

**Hook type:** Curiosity-driven hooks

**Examples:**
- "The hidden danger in your daily routine"
- "Why everything you've been told about [topic] is wrong"
- "This changes everything"

**Characteristics:** No product mention, no solution mention, no problem mention. Pure intrigue. This is where the widest-reaching hooks live, but conversion is the longest path.

### The Scaling Insight

Most businesses only run Level 1-2 hooks (offer and proof). They exhaust their warm audience and think they've "saturated the platform." They haven't. They've saturated their hook quality. Moving UP the pyramid (to Level 3, 4, 5) unlocks entirely new audience segments at the cost of needing better hooks.

Start at Level 1. When performance plateaus, write hooks for Level 2. When that plateaus, Level 3. And so on. Each level unlocks a bigger audience but requires a longer conversion path. Your hook spreadsheet tells you when you've exhausted a level.

## Five Ad Meat Formats

**Source:** $100M Goated Ads Playbook, pp.12-19

Every ad body ("meat") falls into one of five major format categories. These work across all industries: services, education, physical products, brick-and-mortar, software. They can be mixed and matched with any hook and any CTA.

### 1. Demonstration Ads

Show the product in action. Let the viewer see it working with their own eyes.

**Sub-types:**

- **Live Use** -- Record someone using the product in real time. No cuts, no edits. The viewer watches it work from start to finish.
- **Reactions** -- Capture genuine reactions to first-time use. The surprise, the delight, the "oh wow" moment. These are emotional, not rational.
- **Unboxing** -- Film the opening, the reveal, the first impression. Works for physical products but also for digital (screen-recording an account setup, opening an email with access links).
- **Comparisons** -- Side-by-side with a competitor or the "before" state. Show your product next to the alternative and let the difference speak for itself.
- **Before/Afters** -- The transformation. Before using the product vs. after. Weight loss, revenue growth, website redesign, messy desk vs. clean desk. The visual gap is the proof.
- **Hero Ads** -- High-production ads in the style of Dollar Shave Club or Old Spice. Cinematic, memorable, brand-building. These are expensive but create cultural moments. Use sparingly unless you have budget.

### 2. Testimonial Ads

Let your customers do the talking. Third-party proof is always stronger than self-promotion.

**Sub-types:**

- **UGC (User-Generated Content)** -- Customers film themselves talking about the product on their own phone, in their own space. Raw, unpolished, authentic.
- **Direct-to-Camera** -- Structured testimonials where the customer looks directly into the camera and shares their experience. More intentional than UGC but still personal.
- **Podcast Style** -- Interview format. The customer sits across from an interviewer (often the founder) and tells their story. Feels like eavesdropping on a real conversation.
- **Professional Testimonials** -- Higher production value. Proper lighting, clean background, clear audio. Used for B2B, enterprise, or high-ticket offers where polish signals credibility.
- **Raw iPhone** -- The deliberately unpolished version. Shot on a phone, no editing, no script. The roughness IS the proof. People trust what looks real.
- **Walk-n-Talk** -- The customer walks and talks at the same time. Casual, energetic, feels like a voice memo to a friend. Movement keeps attention.
- **Group / Parade of Proof** -- Multiple customers in rapid succession, each sharing one result or one line. The volume creates overwhelming social proof.
- **Lifecycle Ads** -- Clip before/during/after moments from real customer interactions: sales calls, support calls, milestone celebrations. Shows the full arc.
- **Man-on-the-Street** -- Quick, spontaneous-feeling soundbites captured in public or at events. "Hey, what do you think of [product]?" Short, punchy, feels organic.
- **Influencer Collabs** -- Partner with someone your audience already trusts. Their endorsement carries their existing credibility into your product.

### 3. Education Ads

Teach something useful. The ad itself delivers value, which builds trust and positions you as the expert.

**Sub-types:**

- **Explainer Videos** -- Break down a concept, process, or problem. Clear, structured, informative. The viewer learns something and associates that value with your brand.
- **Whiteboard** -- Draw or diagram the concept while explaining it. Visual learning combined with verbal instruction. Works well for complex or abstract topics.
- **How-To Listicles** -- "5 ways to..." or "3 steps to..." Numbered format with actionable steps. Each step is a mini-lesson. Highly shareable.
- **Repurposed Organic** -- Take your highest-performing organic content (social posts, newsletter clips, YouTube segments) and run it as a paid ad. The content already proved it resonates. Now put money behind it.

### 4. Story Ads

Wrap the message in a narrative. Stories bypass the analytical brain and land in the emotional brain.

**Sub-types:**

- **Storytelling / Narrative** -- Classic story arc: problem, struggle, discovery, transformation. The protagonist is either the founder or a customer. The product is the turning point in the story.
- **Lifestyle** -- Show the life the customer can have after using the product. No hard sell, no explicit pitch. The lifestyle IS the pitch. Aspirational imagery and emotion.
- **Warnings & Opportunities** -- "The hidden danger of..." or "The opportunity nobody is talking about..." Creates urgency through fear or FOMO. The story reveals a risk or a window that's closing.
- **Documentary Style** -- Longer-form. Follows a real person through a real transformation. Raw, detailed, credible. Works best for high-ticket or considered purchases where trust needs more time.
- **Skits** -- Scripted scenarios played for humor or drama. Often exaggerate the problem ("This is what it's like trying to do X without Y") to make the solution feel obvious.
- **Brand Manifestos** -- Big-picture, values-driven storytelling. "This is what we believe. This is why we exist. This is the world we're building." Less about the product, more about the movement.

### 5. Faceless Ads

No human face on camera. The creative itself carries the message. These scale without a spokesperson bottleneck.

**Sub-types:**

- **Screenshot Compilations** -- Walls of customer comments, text messages, DMs, review screenshots. The sheer volume of positive responses is the proof. No face needed.
- **Text Only** -- Bold text on screen with music or voiceover. Simple, cheap to produce, effective for platforms where thumb-stop text works (Instagram Stories, TikTok).
- **Slideshows** -- Multiple images or frames in sequence. Each frame delivers one point. Can include data, before/afters, customer results, process steps.
- **Animations** -- Animated explainers, motion graphics, or product demos. Higher production than text-only but no live action. Good for SaaS, tech, or abstract concepts.
- **Cartoon Ads** -- Illustrated or cartoon-style ads. Stand out visually in a feed full of photo/video content. Can simplify complex ideas through illustration.
- **Visual Effects** -- VFX-enhanced content: split screens, zoom effects, transitions, kinetic typography. The visual treatment IS the hook. The effects make ordinary content feel extraordinary.

### Why Faceless Matters

Hormozi found that 80% of his top 50 performing ads did not have his face in them. They were customer content, screenshots, and faceless formats. This removes the founder as a bottleneck and lets ad production scale without a single person being on camera every day.

## CTA Formula (Show + Tell)

**Source:** $100M Goated Ads Playbook, pp.20-24

A strong call-to-action has five elements. Most CTAs only hit one or two. Hit all five and then demonstrate visually what happens when they act. The core principle: **Clear > Clever**. Nobody ever lost a sale because their CTA was too obvious.

### The Five Elements

1. **What to do:** "Take advantage of this offer by..."
2. **How to do it:** "...tapping the button on the bottom of your screen"
3. **When to do it:** "...before it expires" / "...before enrollment closes Friday"
4. **What they get for doing it:** "...you'll get instant access to the full system plus $1,000 in bonuses"
5. **What happens next (optional):** "...delivered straight to your inbox within 60 seconds"

### The Visual Demonstration Layer

Don't just tell them what to do. Show them. On video: screen-record the click, the checkout, the confirmation page. On a sales page: use arrows, highlighted buttons, and visual cues that make the action physically obvious.

Every step the viewer needs to take should be visible on screen AS you're describing it. The combination of verbal instruction + visual demonstration removes all friction. They see exactly what will happen when they act.

### Application

- Every CTA on a sales page should answer at least 3 of the 5 elements
- Below the button, a single line can handle elements 4 and 5 ("You'll get instant access. First lesson hits your inbox in 60 seconds.")
- In video ads, physically demonstrate the click. Show the screen. Show the form. Show the confirmation.
- Never assume they know what to do. Spell it out.

## Time Allocation

**Source:** $100M Goated Ads Playbook, pp.5-8

The ratio is not intuitive but it's backed by Hormozi's data:

- **80% of prep time on hooks** -- Research, write, test, iterate. The hook determines whether anyone sees the rest of the ad. If someone doesn't make it through the hook, nothing else matters.
- **20% of prep time on meat** -- Pick from the five formats above. The meat is important but secondary. A great hook with decent meat outperforms a decent hook with great meat every time.
- **~0% on CTAs** -- CTAs are formulaic. Use the five-element formula above. Don't reinvent the wheel here. Spend your creative energy on hooks.

### Weekly Rhythm

- One recording day per week (all production batched)
- Remaining days: research hooks, analyze results, plan next batch
- Output: 150-750 unique ad variations per week
- Review performance data before every new production session

## Full Ad Creation Process (5 Steps)

**Source:** $100M Goated Ads Cheat Sheet

This is the operational workflow for creating ads using the assembly line method. Run through these five steps every production cycle.

### Step 1: Figure Out the Awareness Level You're Targeting

Which segment of the market are you reaching for?

- **Completely Unaware** -- needs curiosity hooks
- **Problem-Aware** -- needs pain hooks
- **Solution-Aware** -- needs promise hooks
- **Product-Aware** -- needs proof hooks
- **Most Aware** -- needs offer hooks

Decide before you write a single word. The awareness level dictates the hook type, the meat angle, and the conversion path.

### Step 2: Write 50 Hooks

Divide into awareness buckets or focus the majority broader than your current hooks (to expand reach).

**Five sources for hooks:**
1. Your own prior best-performing ads
2. Top ads from other industries (cross-pollinate)
3. Your own top-performing organic/short content
4. Other people's best organic/short content
5. Saved favorite ads from anywhere

80% of your total prep time goes here. The hooks are the ad.

### Step 3: Write 3-5 Meats

Pick from the five formats:
- Demonstration
- Testimonial
- Education
- Story
- Faceless

Each meat should be self-contained. It works with any hook and any CTA. Don't write meats that depend on a specific hook.

### Step 4: Write 1-3 CTAs

Use the five-element formula:
1. What to do
2. How to do it
3. When to do it
4. What they get
5. What happens next

Show + tell. Demonstrate visually. Clear > Clever.

### Step 5: Find a Winner

Launch the combinations. Monitor performance. Repeat steps 1-4. Double down on winners.

"Don't get bored repeating the same stuff. It'll still be the first time they see it."

When a hook-meat-CTA combination works, run it until performance drops. Then swap individual components (new hook on same meat, same hook on new meat) to extend the life of winning elements.

## Marketing Machine System (9-Node Checklist)

**Source:** $100M Marketing Machine, pp.6-27

A systematic process to generate endless customer-created ad content without the founder being on camera. This is what enabled Hormozi to sell Gym Launch for $46.2M by removing himself as the keyman bottleneck. 80% of his top 50 ads didn't have his face in them. The system turns normal business operations into an ad-generating machine.

### Node 1: Lifecycle Ads

Clip before/during/after moments from recorded business interactions.

**What to capture:**
- Sales calls (the moment someone decides to buy)
- Support calls (the moment a problem gets solved)
- Milestone calls (the moment a customer hits a goal)
- Onboarding sessions (the excitement of getting started)

**How it works:** Record every customer-facing call. Review recordings weekly. Clip the best 10-15 second moments. These become ad creative. The customer's genuine emotion is the ad.

### Node 2: Social Media Scrape

Harvest content your customers are already creating about you.

**What to scrape:**
- Tagged posts and stories mentioning your brand
- Posts using your branded hashtags
- Customer posts featuring your product (even without tags)
- Comments on your content where customers share results

**Process:** Separate studs from duds. The best customer-created content becomes ad creative with their permission. One person's organic post can become your best-performing ad.

### Node 3: In-Person Events

Three sub-types, each generating different kinds of proof:

**3a. Stage Ads**
- Capture the energy of live presentations, workshops, or conferences
- Film audience reactions, engagement, and participation
- The crowd itself is social proof

**3b. Professional Testimonials (Side Room)**
- Set up a filming station at your event (side room, hallway, quiet corner)
- Pull attendees aside and run them through the Six-Point Testimonial Script (see below)
- Professional lighting and audio but conversational tone
- This is your highest-quality testimonial content

**3c. Man-on-the-Street Quick Soundbites**
- 10 core questions, asked rapid-fire to multiple attendees
- Quick, spontaneous, high-energy
- Each answer is 10-30 seconds
- The compilation creates a "parade of proof" effect

### Node 4: Communication & Communities Scrape

Screenshot and collect wins from your own communication channels.

**What to scrape:**
- Support/rep chat transcripts where customers share wins
- Community forum posts celebrating results
- Slack/Discord messages with positive outcomes
- External review site content (Google, Trustpilot, G2, Yelp)

**How to use:** Screenshot the best ones. Compile into screenshot walls for faceless ads. Or use as overlay text on video ads.

### Node 5: Bonus Unlocks

Create a valuable giveaway. Let customers unlock it by submitting a testimonial video.

**How it works:**
1. Create something your customers actually want (template, tool, bonus module, discount)
2. Gate it behind a testimonial submission
3. Give clear instructions: what to say, how long, what format
4. Customer submits a video testimonial -> gets the bonus
5. You get ad creative. They get value. Everyone wins.

**Why it works:** Reciprocity-driven. The customer feels they're trading value for value, not doing you a favor.

### Node 6: Award Testimonials

Create aspirational awards and recognition programs. Each award generates four forms of proof.

**The four forms of proof per award:**
1. **Post Screenshot** -- Customer posts about winning the award on social media. Screenshot it.
2. **Video to Email** -- Customer records a thank-you or celebration video and sends it to you.
3. **Unboxing Video** -- If you send a physical award (plaque, trophy, gift), capture or request the unboxing.
4. **In-Person / Stage Presentation** -- Present the award at a live event. Film the handoff, the speech, the reaction.

**Why it works:** Awards create status. Customers actively WANT to share their award. The proof generation is driven by their pride, not your request.

### Node 7: Testimonial Competition

Annual competition where customers submit raw testimonials. The best-performing ad wins a prize.

**How it works:**
1. Announce the competition to your customer base
2. Set clear rules: submit a video testimonial about your experience
3. Run the submissions as ads
4. The testimonial that performs best as an ad wins a prize (e.g., 6 months free service, a trip, a cash bonus)
5. You get a flood of testimonial content to test

**Why it works:** Gamification + incentive alignment. Customers compete to give you the best ad creative. The winners are selected by the market (ad performance), not by you.

### Node 8: Delivery / Upon-Delivery Ads

Capture raw reactions at the exact moment of delivery.

**What to capture:**
- Unboxing moments (physical products)
- First-time login/setup (digital products)
- "You got the job" / "You hit the goal" / "Your first sale" moments
- The genuine, unscripted emotional reaction at the point of transformation

**Why it works:** Peak emotion is the most persuasive content. The moment someone opens the box, sees the result, or hits the milestone is pure, authentic, impossible-to-fake proof.

### Node 9: Customer Reaction Videos

Ongoing capture of customer experiences at different stages.

**Sub-types:**
- **Unboxing** -- First-time opening/receiving
- **First-Time User** -- Initial experience and reaction
- **Long-Time User** -- Seasoned perspective on sustained results
- **Customer Demonstrations** -- Customers showing how they use the product in their own context

**Why this is separate from Node 8:** Node 8 captures the single peak moment. Node 9 captures the ongoing relationship. A long-time user saying "I've been using this for 2 years and..." is a different kind of proof than a first-time unboxing.

### Implementation Priority Order

If you're starting from scratch, don't try to activate all 9 nodes at once. This is the priority sequence from the playbook, starting with the easiest and highest-ROI:

1. **Competition** -- Announce it, get a flood of content
2. **Chat Scrape** -- You already have these conversations happening
3. **Community Scrape** -- Your community already has wins posted
4. **Reviews Scrape** -- External reviews already exist
5. **Social Media Scrape** -- Customer posts already exist
6. **Bonus Unlocks** -- Build the gate, create the bonus
7. **Award Unlocks** -- Design the awards, set up the program
8. **Events** -- Requires live events (higher effort)
9. **Lifecycle** -- Requires call recording infrastructure

## Six-Point Testimonial Script

**Source:** $100M Marketing Machine, pp.15-16

A structured script for capturing testimonials that hit all emotional and logical proof points. Used at events (Node 3), in bonus unlocks (Node 5), and in testimonial competitions (Node 7). The most important part is the hook: have them say a few different hooks before the story.

### The Six Points

**Point 1: Internal Struggle**

The emotional rock bottom before they found you.

**Question prompts:**
- "What was rock bottom before doing this?"
- "What did you have to do that you hated?"
- "What things did you want to do that you couldn't?"

Let them ramble here. The gold is in the specific, visceral details. "I was lying awake at 3am doing math on whether I could afford both groceries and my kid's field trip" is worth more than "I was stressed about money."

**Point 2: External Struggle**

The measurable, objective reality of where they started.

**Question prompts:**
- "What were your objective metrics?" (revenue, weight, score, subscribers, whatever the measurable thing is)
- "What was the number?"
- "How long had you been stuck there?"

Get the hard numbers. "$2,400 a month." "217 pounds." "Zero clients." Numbers make the transformation real.

**Point 3: Skepticism**

What almost stopped them from buying or joining.

**Question prompts:**
- "What were your main concerns about buying/clicking/joining?"
- "What almost stopped you?"
- "What were you worried would happen?"

This is gold because it mirrors the prospect's current objections. When the viewer hears someone voice the exact same concern they have right now, and then sees that person on the other side of the decision, the objection dissolves.

**Point 4: Overcome**

What made them do it despite the skepticism.

**Question prompts:**
- "What made you do it anyway?"
- "What was the tipping point?"
- "What convinced you to take the leap?"

The answer to this question answers the prospect's unspoken objection. Whatever made THIS person act is the argument that might make the viewer act.

**Point 5: External Victory**

The measurable, objective result after using the product.

**Question prompts:**
- "What are your objective metrics now?"
- "What's the number today?"
- "How long did it take?"

Hard numbers again. The gap between Point 2 and Point 5 is the transformation. "$2,400 to $18,000 a month." "217 to 175 pounds." "Zero to 14 clients."

**Point 6: Internal Victory**

The emotional transformation. What life feels like now.

**Question prompts:**
- "What's been the best moment since?"
- "What can you do now that you couldn't do before?"
- "What things did you hate doing that you don't have to do anymore?"

This closes the emotional loop. Point 1 was the pain. Point 6 is the relief. The viewer sees the full arc: where they were, what they feared, what pushed them through, and where they landed.

### Capture Tips

- Record multiple hooks before the story. Have the customer say their opening line 3-4 different ways. Pick the best one in editing.
- Let them ramble in Points 1 and 6. Cut later.
- For Points 2 and 5, insist on actual numbers. If they say "I was doing okay," push: "What was the actual number?"
- Raw iPhone quality. Don't over-produce. A shaky phone video with genuine emotion outsells a studio shoot.

## Marketing Machine Weekly Checklist

**Source:** $100M Marketing Machine

The operational checklist for running the Marketing Machine system on a weekly basis. This is what the team reviews every week to ensure all nodes are producing content.

- [ ] List of names of people who got great results this week
- [ ] Scraped social media content (tags, customer posts, images, videos)
- [ ] Chat/messaging screenshots from account reps showing customer wins
- [ ] Community post/comments testimonials collected
- [ ] New 5-star reviews from external sites (Google, Trustpilot, G2, etc.)
- [ ] Bonus unlock submissions received (testimonial videos in exchange for bonuses)
- [ ] Award deliveries completed and customer recognition content captured
- [ ] Customer reaction videos collected (unboxing, first-time use, milestones)

Each item feeds directly into the ad assembly line. The weekly checklist outputs raw material. The assembly line process turns that raw material into 150-750 ads.
