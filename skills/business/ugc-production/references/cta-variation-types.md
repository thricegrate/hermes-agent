# CTA Variation Types

The CTA section earns the conversion. Hook earns the watch. Body earns the hold. CTA earns the action. In the Winner Variation Pipeline, generate 10 to 12 CTA variations covering different action types.

Each CTA variation is short, modular, and calibrated to the original CTA timing. The CTA clip is 2-4 seconds. It plugs into the assembled reel after the body and product introduction.

## Why CTA variation matters

The CTA is where most operators leave revenue on the table. They write one CTA, run it across every reel, and never test alternatives. Different CTAs convert different segments of the audience pulled in by the hook and held by the body. The variation set captures this final segmentation.

A reel with strong hook rate, strong hold rate, and weak CTR has a CTA problem, not a content problem. CTA variations diagnose which CTA action types convert which audience segments.

## The 3 primary action types

The source playbook names 3. Generate 3-4 variations within each action type, totaling 10 to 12 CTAs.

### 1. Comment funnel keywords

The CTA asks the viewer to comment a specific keyword. The keyword triggers an automated DM with a free resource.

**Pattern:** "Comment [KEYWORD] and I will send you [specific free resource]."

**Why this works:** Comments rank higher than likes for the algorithm. Each comment also extends reach to the commenter's network. The keyword reply gives you tracking and creates visible social proof ("400 people grabbed this").

**Variation axes within this type:**

- **Different free resources**: same comment funnel structure, different offers
  - Comment SKILL → AI skill setup PDF
  - Comment NEWSLETTER → newsletter bot setup
  - Comment TEMPLATE → Google Doc with the framework
  - Comment CHECKLIST → fill-in checklist version

- **Different keyword tones**
  - Direct: "Comment the product and I will send the link"
  - Hesitant: "Drop the product in the comments if you want this"
  - Curious: "Comment QUESTION if you want to know how this works"

- **Different value framings**
  - Resource framing: "Comment SKILL for the full setup"
  - Promise framing: "Comment YES if you want to do this yourself"
  - Curiosity framing: "Comment HOW and I will show you the bot"

### 2. Specific-person DM share prompts

The CTA asks the viewer to send the reel to a specific person in their life who needs it. The DM share extends reach into network segments the original distribution never touched.

**Pattern:** "Send this to [specific person archetype] who [specific situation]."

**Why this works:** A DM share is a higher-trust signal than a like or save. The receiving person opens the reel because someone they know sent it directly. DM shares extend reach in concentric audience networks rather than algorithmic distribution.

**Variation axes within this type:**

- **Different person archetypes**
  - "Send this to a friend who has tried every diet and given up"
  - "Send this to your sister who keeps complaining about her weight"
  - "Send this to your mom if she has been thinking about getting back in shape"
  - "Send this to your gym buddy who is doing it the hard way"

- **Different situations**
  - "Send this to anyone who is starting over on Monday"
  - "Send this to someone who just hit their breaking point"
  - "Send this to a friend who needs to hear this today"

- **Different relationship types**
  - "Send this to your partner if they need to see it"
  - "Send this to a coworker who is struggling with the same thing"
  - "Send this to your group chat"

### 3. Save prompts (tied to future use cases)

The CTA asks the viewer to save the reel for a specific future use case. The save behavior continues distributing the content for days and weeks beyond the initial posting window.

**Pattern:** "Save this for [specific future moment when the viewer would come back to it]."

**Why this works:** Saves are weighted heavily by the algorithm because they signal high content value. A save is the viewer telling the platform "this is useful enough that I want to come back to it."

**Variation axes within this type:**

- **Different use case timings**
  - "Save this for the next time you feel like quitting"
  - "Save this for tomorrow morning when you wake up"
  - "Save this for the next Sunday night when you are planning the week"
  - "Save this for the next time someone asks you about [topic]"

- **Different reference framings**
  - Reminder framing: "Save this for when you need to remember why you started"
  - Reference framing: "Save this for when you actually want to do it"
  - Coaching framing: "Save this for the next time you talk yourself out of it"

- **Different temporal anchors**
  - Daily: "Save this for tomorrow morning"
  - Weekly: "Save this for Sunday planning"
  - Trigger-based: "Save this for the next time you [specific trigger]"

## How to generate the 10-12 CTA variations

Default split: 4 comment funnel + 4 DM share + 4 save prompt = 12. Adjust based on what the niche responds to. Some niches convert better on comment funnels (most digital products). Some on DM shares (lifestyle, relationships). Some on save prompts (educational, productivity).

If the original reel used one specific action type in its CTA, weight that type higher (5-6 variations within it) and 2-3 in each other type. The original action type is the proven winner. The variations test alternative types.

## What stays locked across all CTA variations

- **The CTA timing**: same length as the original (2-4 seconds)
- **The character reference**: same character
- **The scene baseline**: same setting, same wardrobe family
- **The voice register**: same as the original
- **The transition cue from the body**: the line right before the CTA stays consistent so the CTA plugs in cleanly

Only the action type and the specific wording of the CTA changes.

## Common failure modes

### The CTA is too long

Each CTA clip should be 2-4 seconds. A 7-second CTA breaks the modularity. The viewer mentally moves to scrolling away from the reel, and the long CTA creates dead time. Tight CTAs.

### The CTA stacks multiple action types

"Comment the product, send this to a friend, and save it for later" is three CTAs in one. The viewer does none of them because the ask is fragmented. One action type per CTA. Always.

### The CTA does not match the body's emotional register

A frustration-framed body that ends with "save this for Sunday planning" has a tonal mismatch. Frustration body wants a more emotional CTA ("Send this to anyone who keeps starting over on Monday"). Match CTA register to body register through the compatibility matrix (see [compatibility-matrix.md](compatibility-matrix.md)).

### The free resource in a comment funnel is generic

"Comment YES and I will send you the link" gives the viewer no reason to comment. The resource needs to be specific and named. "Comment the product and I will send the bot setup with prompts and code" gives a clear value exchange.

### The DM share archetype is too broad

"Send this to anyone who needs it" is generic. "Send this to a friend who has tried every diet and given up" is specific. The viewer needs to think of a SPECIFIC person, not a vague category.

### The save prompt has no real future use case

"Save this for later" is a default save prompt that does not earn the save. The viewer needs a specific future moment they will come back to. "Save this for Sunday night when you are planning the week" gives the moment.

## Component-level performance signal

When the variation pipeline deploys 40-50 reels with different CTA variations, watch the CTR per CTA clip. The signal:

- **High CTR on a specific CTA type**: that action type is the winner for this audience. Expand within it.
- **Low CTR across all CTAs of one type but high on another**: the audience does not respond to that action type. Retire it across the library.
- **Variance within one type but not across types**: the action type works, but the specific wording or framing of some CTAs is breaking. Refine the CTA-level wording.

This component-level signal is what makes the pipeline compound. After 8 weeks, you have a map of which CTA action type the audience responds to. New reels start with the winning CTA type baked in.
