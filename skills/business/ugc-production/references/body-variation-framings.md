# Body Variation Framings

The body section of a UGC reel is what keeps the audience watching past the hook. The body's job is to deliver enough emotional resonance that the viewer holds attention through the mechanism bridge and into the product introduction.

In the Winner Variation Pipeline, generate 12 to 15 body variations per winning reel. Each variation explores a different emotional entry point. The mechanism bridge and product introduction structure stay locked in. Only the emotional entry point changes.

## Why body variation works

The hook earns the first 3 seconds. The body earns the next 15 to 30 seconds. Different audience segments respond to different emotional entry points. A viewer pulled in by a specificity hook might hold for a frustration framing but bounce on a wasted-money framing. Different viewer, opposite. The body variation set captures this segmentation.

A 50% hook rate that drops to a 5% hold rate means the hook is winning but the body is losing the audience. Body variations test which framings hold which segments of the audience pulled in by the hook.

## The 6 emotional entry points

The source playbook names 3. In practice 6 entry points cover the full emotional range. Generate body variations across these 6 to find which framing the audience holds longest.

### 1. Frustration framing

The protagonist is fed up with what they have been doing. The body opens with the recent breaking point.

**Pattern:** "I was so tired of [specific pattern]. I had tried [specific list of things]. Nothing worked."

**Why it works:** The viewer recognizes their own frustration. They feel seen. They keep watching to find out what the protagonist did when the frustration peaked.

**Example body open (weight management):**
"I was so tired of starting another diet on Monday. I had tried calorie counting. I had tried Whole30. I had tried intermittent fasting. Each one worked for 6 weeks then I bounced back."

**Holds best for:** Audiences who have already tried multiple solutions. Mid-funnel awareness.

### 2. Wasted money framing

The protagonist tallies what they have spent on things that did not work. The body opens with the financial sting.

**Pattern:** "I added up what I had spent on [category]. The number was [specific dollar amount]."

**Why it works:** Money loss is a clear, measurable pain. The specific dollar amount is concrete. The viewer compares it to their own spending and feels the recognition.

**Example body open (weight management):**
"Last year I added up what I had spent on weight loss. Three gym memberships. Four meal plan apps. Two trainers. Six different supplements. The number was $4,200. None of it worked."

**Holds best for:** Audiences who have spent meaningfully on solutions. Buyers, not lookers.

### 3. Social comparison framing

The protagonist compares themselves to peers and finds themselves behind. The body opens with the moment of comparison.

**Pattern:** "I was at [specific social setting] and I noticed [specific peer behavior or appearance]. I realized [specific gap]."

**Why it works:** Social comparison is a hot psychological trigger. The viewer either has had the same comparison moment or is currently in one.

**Example body open (weight management):**
"At my college reunion last summer I noticed something. The guys who had stayed in shape all looked 5 years younger than me. The ones who had gained weight all looked 10 years older. We were all the same age."

**Holds best for:** Audiences in life stages where peer comparison is acute (reunions, weddings, work events).

### 4. Identity threat framing

The protagonist faces a moment where the situation threatens their sense of who they are. The body opens with the identity-shaking event.

**Pattern:** "[Specific identity-shaking moment]. I realized this was not who I wanted to be."

**Why it works:** Identity is a deeper trigger than frustration or money. When a viewer's identity feels at risk, attention locks in completely.

**Example body open (weight management):**
"My daughter asked me to play tag at her birthday party. I made it 30 seconds before I had to sit down. I watched her play with her cousins for 2 hours. That night I sat in the bathroom and looked at myself."

**Holds best for:** Parents, professionals, anyone with a strong identity tied to a role.

### 5. Failure stack framing

The protagonist lists past failed attempts in rapid succession. The body opens with a montage of failures, each one slightly different.

**Pattern:** "[Failure 1]. [Failure 2]. [Failure 3]. Each one I thought would be the one."

**Why it works:** The cumulative weight of multiple failures creates a sense of "I am the protagonist". The viewer rides the failure stack down with the character, then is ready for the turn when the body pivots.

**Example body open (weight management):**
"Keto. Three weeks. Quit. Atkins. A month. Quit. Beachbody. Six weeks. Quit. F45. Two months. Quit. Each time I thought this would be the one."

**Holds best for:** Long-time strugglers in the niche. Audiences with multiple failed attempts.

### 6. Unfair comparison framing

The protagonist describes a peer or rival who succeeded with what looks like less effort. The body opens with the unfair-feeling observation.

**Pattern:** "[Specific peer] does [less effort] and gets [more result]. I do [more effort] and get [less result]."

**Why it works:** The unfairness is the hook into the body. The viewer recognizes the unfair-feeling pattern in their own life and keeps watching to see what the protagonist figured out about it.

**Example body open (weight management):**
"My sister eats whatever she wants. Pasta, bread, dessert every night. She is two sizes smaller than me. I count calories, do cardio 5 times a week, and have been the same weight for 4 years."

**Holds best for:** Audiences with visible peer comparisons (siblings, coworkers, friends).

## How to pick which framings to generate

When spinning 12-15 body variations, use these defaults:

- **Always include**: frustration, wasted money, social comparison (the source playbook's 3)
- **Add 1-2 of**: identity threat, failure stack, unfair comparison
- **Generate 2-3 variations within each chosen framing** (12 to 15 total)

If the original reel that earned the variation pipeline used one specific framing in its body, weight that framing higher (4-5 variations within it) and 1-2 in each other framing. The original framing is the proven winner. The variations test if other framings hold different audience segments.

## What stays locked across all body variations

The mechanism bridge and product introduction structure stay constant. Specifically:

- **The mechanism bridge**: the line that transitions from the emotional opening into the explanation of how the product/system works
- **The product introduction structure**: the sentence shape that names and frames the product
- **The character reference**: same character as the original
- **The scene baseline**: same setting, same wardrobe family, same lighting profile
- **The voice register**: high energy or calm authority, locked from the original
- **The duration**: same body length as the original

Only the emotional entry point changes. Everything that follows the entry point stays identical structurally.

## Why locking the bridge matters

If the mechanism bridge changes across body variations, the variations test multiple things at once (entry point AND bridge). When data comes back, you cannot tell which variable drove the difference. Lock the bridge so the variations isolate the entry point as the variable.

## Common failure modes

### The framings start blending together

If by variation 8 the bodies all sound like minor remixes of variation 1, the variation set is too narrow. Expand into a different framing category (add identity threat or failure stack) rather than producing more variations within the same framing.

### The framing is too generic

"I was frustrated with my weight" is not a frustration framing. It is just a statement. Frustration framing requires the specific pattern, the specific tried-things, the specific breaking point.

### The framing does not pair with the audience

Wasted money framing on an audience that has not spent meaningfully will not hold. Social comparison on an audience that does not have visible peers will not hold. Match the framing to the audience's actual emotional triggers, not to the framings that sound clever.

### The mechanism bridge gets rewritten

If the bridge changes across variations, you have produced 12 different reels, not 12 body variations of the same reel. Restart with the original bridge locked in.
