# Brunson Funnel & Page Design Frameworks

Russell Brunson's funnel architecture applied to page design. Kennedy tells you what to say on the page. Brunson tells you which page to build, where it sits in the funnel, and how to move people through the sequence. Use both together.

---

## Funnel Type Taxonomy

Each funnel type demands a different page design because the buyer psychology is different. Match the page to the funnel, not the other way around.

### 1. Tripwire Funnel (Low-Ticket Impulse, $1-$47)

**Purpose:** Convert cold or warm traffic into buyers with an irresistible low-price offer. The goal is not profit. The goal is to create a buyer.

**Page structure:**
```
Curiosity headline (outcome-focused, not product-focused)
Short problem agitation (3-5 sentences max)
Product reveal with perceived value stack
Price anchor -> slashed price -> "just $X"
Single CTA button (buy now)
Order bump on checkout page
```

**Design principles:**
- [ ] Page is SHORT. Under 1500 words. Impulse purchases die on long pages.
- [ ] One product image or mockup above the fold. Make it look tangible.
- [ ] Price is visually anchored against a higher "value" number ($297 value, yours for $27).
- [ ] No navigation menu. No footer links. No exits except the CTA.
- [ ] Countdown timer only if the deadline is real. Fake urgency kills trust.
- [ ] Order bump checkbox on the checkout step (adds 30-50% to average order value).
- [ ] Mobile-first. Most impulse buys happen on phones.

### 2. Webinar Registration Funnel

**Purpose:** Get opt-ins for a live or automated webinar that sells a mid-to-high ticket offer ($297-$2,000+).

**Page structure:**
```
"How to [outcome] without [objection]" headline
Date/time with timezone (or "watch instantly" for evergreen)
3-4 bullet points: what they'll discover
Host photo + one-line credibility
Registration form (name + email)
Countdown to event (if live)
```

**Design principles:**
- [ ] Curiosity-driven headline. They register to LEARN something, not to be sold.
- [ ] Show the host's face. Webinars are personal. Stock photos kill registration rates.
- [ ] Maximum 3-4 "you'll discover" bullets. More than that dilutes curiosity.
- [ ] Registration form is visible without scrolling on desktop.
- [ ] Confirmation page immediately shows "add to calendar" and "join our group" CTAs.
- [ ] Reminder sequence starts immediately (confirmation email, 24hr reminder, 1hr reminder, "we're live" email).
- [ ] No mention of price or selling on the registration page. The webinar does the selling.

### 3. Application Funnel (Reverse Squeeze, $2,000+)

**Purpose:** Qualify prospects before letting them buy. Flips the dynamic: they apply to work with you, not the other way around.

**Page structure:**
```
"Is this you?" qualifying headline
Description of ideal client (specific, exclusionary)
What they get if accepted
Application form (5-10 qualifying questions)
"Apply Now" button (not "Buy Now")
Post-application: "Thanks, we'll review and contact you within X hours"
```

**Design principles:**
- [ ] Exclusionary language throughout. "This is NOT for everyone." "We only accept X people per month."
- [ ] No price on the page. Price is discussed on the call after qualification.
- [ ] Application form acts as a commitment device. The more effort they invest, the more likely they close.
- [ ] Questions should qualify on budget, timeline, and seriousness. "What's your current revenue?" "When do you want to start?" "Are you ready to invest $X-$Y?"
- [ ] Design should feel premium: generous whitespace, muted palette, serif typography. Not flashy. Flashy signals mass-market.
- [ ] Social proof focuses on caliber of clients, not volume. "We've worked with [names]" beats "10,000 students."
- [ ] Post-submission page books the call immediately (Calendly embed or similar).

### 4. Book Funnel (Free + Shipping, $0 + $7.95 shipping)

**Purpose:** Acquire buyers at break-even or slight loss. A physical book is the highest-perceived-value lead magnet. Shipping charge qualifies real buyers from freebie seekers.

**Page structure:**
```
Book cover image (professional, 3D mockup)
"Get your FREE copy" headline
Author credibility + what's inside (TOC-style bullets)
"Just cover shipping" price reveal
Shipping form (name, email, address, card for shipping)
Order bump: audiobook or companion guide
OTO page after checkout: course or membership upsell
```

**Design principles:**
- [ ] Book mockup must look professional. Bad cover design kills the entire funnel.
- [ ] "Free" is the headline. Shipping cost is secondary, revealed near the CTA.
- [ ] Include a physical table of contents or chapter preview. Tangibility sells.
- [ ] Testimonials from readers, not generic endorsements. "Chapter 3 alone changed how I..."
- [ ] Checkout page collects shipping address AND email. You now have a buyer with a physical address.
- [ ] Order form bump is critical. "Add the audiobook for just $37" converts at 25-40%.
- [ ] One-Time Offer page immediately after checkout is where the real revenue comes from.

### 5. Challenge Funnel (Daily Engagement, $0-$47 entry)

**Purpose:** Build relationship and demonstrate transformation over 5-7 days. Participants who complete challenges buy the backend offer at 10-30%.

**Page structure:**
```
Challenge name + "Join the X-Day [Outcome] Challenge"
Daily breakdown: Day 1 = X, Day 2 = Y... (brief, curiosity-driven)
What they'll have at the end (tangible deliverable)
Community element: "Join 500+ others doing this together"
Registration form or low-ticket payment
Start date with countdown
```

**Design principles:**
- [ ] Energy and momentum in the design. Bright accent colors, progress indicators, day-by-day visual timeline.
- [ ] Each day's description is ONE outcome, not a curriculum dump.
- [ ] Community proof matters more than expert proof here. "Join 500 others" beats "I have 10 years experience."
- [ ] Daily engagement pages (post-registration) should have progress bars, checkmarks, completion badges.
- [ ] Mobile-first is non-negotiable. Challenge participants check in from their phones.
- [ ] Final day page pivots to the offer. The challenge proved the method works. Now sell the full system.
- [ ] Urgency is built into the structure (it starts on X date, not "whenever you want").

## Seven Funnel Phases

Brunson's funnel isn't just pages. It's a psychological journey. Each phase has a job, and the page design either supports that job or sabotages it.

### Phase 1: Traffic Temperature

**The job:** Recognize that cold, warm, and hot traffic need different entry points.

| Temperature | They know... | Entry page | Design approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cold | Nothing about you | Blog post, ad, free tool | Educate first. No selling. Lead with value. |
| Warm | Your name, maybe your content | Webinar reg, lead magnet, tripwire | Bridge from awareness to offer. Light credibility. |
| Hot | Your work, your reputation | Sales page, application, direct offer | Skip the warmup. Go straight to the offer. |

**Page design implications:**
- [ ] Cold traffic pages have NO jargon. Explain everything. Assume zero context.
- [ ] Warm traffic pages reference the content they already consumed. "You read our guide on X. Here's the next step."
- [ ] Hot traffic pages are shorter. These people already trust you. Don't over-explain.
- [ ] Never send cold traffic to a sales page. They'll bounce. Send them to a bridge page first.

### Phase 2: Pre-Frame Bridge

**The job:** Shape how the prospect perceives you BEFORE they see the offer. The pre-frame determines whether they see you as a trusted authority or just another marketer.

**Design applications:**
- [ ] Blog-to-opt-in bridge: Article gives value, ends with a contextual CTA that feels like the logical next step.
- [ ] Ad-to-landing bridge: The landing page headline matches the ad copy. Visual continuity (same colors, same imagery). "Message match" is non-negotiable.
- [ ] Video-to-sales bridge: A short video (on a separate page) that reframes the problem before they see the offer page.
- [ ] Email-to-page bridge: The email pre-frames the offer. The landing page picks up exactly where the email left off. Same language, same tone.

**The rule:** Whatever they saw LAST before hitting your page is your pre-frame. Control it.

### Phase 3: Qualify Subscribers

**The job:** Turn traffic into leads by offering something valuable enough to trade an email for.

**Design applications:**
- [ ] Lead magnet pages have ONE form field (email) for cold traffic. Name + email for warm.
- [ ] The opt-in promise must be specific and immediate. "Get the checklist" beats "Subscribe to our newsletter."
- [ ] Thank-you page is a selling opportunity, not a dead end. "While you wait for your email, watch this 2-minute video..."
- [ ] Pop-up timing: trigger on exit intent or after 60%+ scroll depth. Not on page load. That's hostile.
- [ ] Quiz funnels qualify AND segment simultaneously. Each answer sorts them into a bucket for personalized follow-up.

### Phase 4: Qualify Buyers

**The job:** Turn subscribers into first-time buyers. The jump from "free" to "paid" is the hardest conversion. Use a low-barrier offer.

**Design applications:**
- [ ] Tripwire page immediately after opt-in (the thank-you page IS the tripwire offer).
- [ ] Price feels insignificant compared to perceived value. Design the value stack to make $7-$27 feel absurd.
- [ ] One-click upsell after purchase (they already entered payment info). No new checkout form.
- [ ] Order confirmation page includes the first upsell offer. They just proved they'll pay. Strike while the wallet is open.
- [ ] Buyer vs. subscriber experience diverges here. Buyers get different emails, different offers, different treatment.

### Phase 5: Identify Hyperactive Buyers

**The job:** Find the 1-5% who will buy everything you offer, right now. These are your most valuable customers. Design the post-purchase sequence to surface them.

**Design applications:**
- [ ] One-Time Offer (OTO) pages appear immediately after the initial purchase. "Wait! Before you go..."
- [ ] OTO page design: short, no navigation, yes/no only. "Add this for $X" or "No thanks, I don't want [benefit]."
- [ ] Stack 2-3 OTOs maximum. After that, diminishing returns and buyer fatigue.
- [ ] Downsell page if they decline the OTO: same offer at a lower price or a payment plan. "Not ready for the full program? Get Module 1 for just $47."
- [ ] Hyperactive buyers self-identify by purchasing 2+ offers in one session. Tag them differently in your email system.

### Phase 6: Ascend and Ascend

**The job:** Move buyers up your value ladder. Each product solves the next problem that the previous product created.

**Design applications:**
- [ ] Each level of the value ladder gets its own page style. Low-ticket = fast, punchy. Mid-ticket = more proof, more detail. High-ticket = premium, exclusive.
- [ ] Ascension pages reference what they already bought. "You completed the Playbook. Here's what's next."
- [ ] Scarcity increases as price increases. $27 product = unlimited. $5K program = 20 spots. $15K DFY = 5 clients/month.
- [ ] Design complexity increases with price point. A $27 product can live on a simple one-page design. A $5K program needs a multi-section sales page with video, testimonials, case studies, and FAQ.
- [ ] Membership/continuity pages focus on what they'll MISS if they leave, not what they'll gain by joining.

### Phase 7: Change the Selling Environment

**The job:** Move from online pages to environments where closing is easier: webinars, phone calls, live events, VSLs. The page's job is to get them INTO that environment.

**Design applications:**
- [ ] Webinar registration pages sell the EVENT, not the offer. The webinar sells the offer.
- [ ] Application pages sell the CALL, not the program. The call sells the program.
- [ ] Live event pages sell the EXPERIENCE, not the ticket price. Photos of past events, attendee testimonials, venue imagery.
- [ ] VSL (Video Sales Letter) pages: the video does all the selling. The page is just a video player + a CTA button that appears at the right moment. Minimal text. No spoilers.
- [ ] Chat/DM funnels: the page collects just enough info to start a conversation. "Tell us about your business" with 3 fields, then a human follows up.

## Brunson Page Design Checklist

Run alongside the Kennedy checklist. They complement, not compete.

### Funnel Architecture
- [ ] Page type matches the funnel type (don't put a long sales page on a tripwire funnel)
- [ ] Traffic temperature matches page complexity (cold = simple, hot = direct)
- [ ] Pre-frame is controlled (ad matches landing page headline, email matches page tone)
- [ ] Post-page destination is planned (what happens after they convert? OTO? Thank you? Webinar?)
- [ ] Value ladder position is clear (what did they buy before? What's next?)

### Conversion Mechanics
- [ ] One CTA per page. One action. No menu, no footer links, no distracting exits.
- [ ] Mobile-first design. Test on phone before desktop.
- [ ] Page load time under 3 seconds. Every second over 3 costs 7% conversion.
- [ ] Order bumps on checkout pages (30-50% AOV increase for zero extra traffic).
- [ ] OTO sequence planned for post-purchase (identify hyperactive buyers).

### Psychological Sequence
- [ ] Headline addresses the prospect's awareness level (cold = problem-aware, hot = solution-aware)
- [ ] Curiosity gaps keep them scrolling (never answer everything above the fold)
- [ ] Value stack makes the price feel small (total value = 10x the price)
- [ ] Scarcity matches the offer tier (low-ticket = urgency, high-ticket = exclusivity)
- [ ] The page earns the right to ask. Value before CTA. Always.
