# Kennedy Landing Page Framework

Dan Kennedy's direct response principles applied to landing page design. A landing page is a sales letter. Same psychology, same structure, same rules. These layer on top of the existing page type templates.

---

## Landing Page = Sales Letter (Framework 5)

Kennedy's core insight: every landing page follows the same structure as a direct mail sales letter. The medium changed. The psychology didn't.

### The Sales Letter Structure for Landing Pages

**Above the fold (the envelope):**
This is your "will they open it?" moment. The headline and first visual must accomplish one thing: make them keep reading.

**Problem section (the hook):**
Agitate the problem they already know they have. Use their language. Not yours. Not industry jargon. The words they use when complaining to a friend over coffee.

**Solution section (the bridge):**
Introduce your product as the bridge from problem to desired outcome. Not features. The transformation.

**Proof section (the evidence):**
Testimonials, case studies, data, screenshots, before/after. Kennedy says: "Proof is the most underused element in marketing."

**Offer section (the stack):**
Everything they get, with values assigned. Bonuses listed. Guarantee prominent.

**Close section (the ask):**
Clear CTA. One action. No options. Tell them exactly what to do.

**P.S. (second most-read element):**
Kennedy's rule: the P.S. is the second most-read part of any sales letter, right after the headline. Restate the offer and the deadline. Many people scroll to the bottom first. The P.S. catches them.

### The Damaging Admission

Kennedy's trust accelerator. Openly list your flaws before the prospect does. When you admit a weakness, credibility on everything else goes up.

**Examples:**
- "This program is not for beginners. If you haven't made your first $1K online, start with [free resource] first."
- "Fair warning: the interface isn't pretty. It's built for results, not screenshots."
- "We don't offer phone support. Everything is handled through our community, which means you get answers from 200+ practitioners, not one support rep."

**Why it works:** The prospect is already looking for reasons not to buy. When you acknowledge the weaknesses first, you take that weapon away and redirect their attention to the strengths.

**Placement:** After the main offer section, before the guarantee. Or in the FAQ section as honest answers.

## The Headline Process (Framework 6)

### Kennedy's Headline Formulas

These are battle-tested over decades of direct mail and still outperform "creative" headlines today.

**"They laughed when I ________, but when I ________"**
Example: "They laughed when I said AI could write my newsletter. But when I showed them my open rates..."

**"How to ________ in [timeframe] without [objection]"**
Example: "How to build an AI automation in 30 minutes without writing a single line of code"

**"Who else wants ________?"**
Example: "Who else wants their newsletter to write itself while they sleep?"

**"Warning: ________"**
Example: "Warning: This tool will make your current workflow feel embarrassingly manual"

**"[Number] reasons why ________"**
Example: "7 reasons your AI tools aren't making you money (and the one fix that changes everything)"

**"The secret of ________"**
Example: "The secret of newsletter operators making $50K/month from a single email list"

### The 50-Headline Process

Kennedy's approach to headlines is brute force, then refinement.

1. **Write 50 headlines.** No editing. No judgment. No stopping to evaluate. Just write. Set a timer for 20 minutes and go.
2. **Walk away.** Don't look at them for at least a few hours. Ideally overnight.
3. **Narrow to 10.** Circle the ones that make YOU want to click, even though you know what's behind them.
4. **Narrow to 3.** Which three would stop a stranger mid-scroll?
5. **Test.** Run all 3 as ad headlines, email subject lines, or split tests. Let data pick the winner.

The first 10-15 headlines are usually garbage. The good ones live in the 25-50 range, after your brain stops recycling cliches.

## Risk Reversal on Landing Pages (Framework 8)

Kennedy's rule: the guarantee should be one of the most prominent elements on the page. Not buried in the FAQ. Not in small text. Front and center.

### Guarantee Placement Rules

1. **Visual prominence:** Use a distinct visual treatment. Badge, seal, bordered box. It should look different from the rest of the page.
2. **Early mention:** Reference the guarantee in the hero section or immediately after the price. "Try it risk-free for 30 days."
3. **Full guarantee section:** Dedicated section below the offer stack. Spell out exactly what happens if they're not satisfied.
4. **P.S. restatement:** Mention the guarantee again in the P.S. section.

### Guarantee Copy Formula

```
[Guarantee Name]: [Duration] [Type]

Try [Product] for [X days]. If [specific condition or "for any reason"],
[exactly what happens: full refund, keep bonuses, no questions].

[Optional: How to request it: "Just email us at X"]
```

**Example:**
"The 'No B.S.' Guarantee: 30-Day Full Refund. Use the entire the product Playbook for 30 days. If it doesn't save you at least 10 hours in your first month, email us and we'll refund every penny. Keep the bonus templates."

## Clear Instructions and Tracking (Rules 1, 3, 4)

### Rule 1: Always Include an Offer

Every page must have a specific offer. Not "learn more." Not "get in touch." A specific thing they get when they take a specific action.

**Weak:** "Sign up for updates"
**Strong:** "Get the 47-piece AI Toolkit (free) when you enter your email below"

### Rule 3: Give Clear Instructions

Tell them exactly what to do. Step by step. Kennedy says: "Never assume they know what to do next."

**On landing pages:**
- "Click the yellow button below"
- "Enter your best email address in the box"
- "Check your inbox in the next 2 minutes for your download link"

This feels over-the-top until you see the conversion lift. People skip steps they're unsure about.

### Rule 4: Track and Measure Everything

Every landing page must have:
- Unique UTM parameters per traffic source
- Conversion pixel (Meta, Google, or both)
- Scroll depth tracking (where do they stop reading?)
- Click tracking on every CTA button
- A/B test capability (at minimum, test the headline)

If you can't measure it, you can't improve it. And you definitely can't scale it.

## Kennedy Landing Page Checklist

Run this before publishing any landing page:

- [ ] Headline follows a proven formula (not "clever" or "creative")
- [ ] 50-headline process completed (or at minimum 20, narrowed to 3)
- [ ] Problem section uses the prospect's language, not yours
- [ ] Damaging admission included (builds trust through honesty)
- [ ] Offer stack is complete (core + bonuses + values + guarantee)
- [ ] Guarantee is visually prominent and mentioned 2+ times
- [ ] P.S. restates the offer and deadline
- [ ] Clear, specific instructions for what to do next
- [ ] Every CTA button has the same action (one ask per page)
- [ ] Tracking pixels and UTMs in place before going live
- [ ] Risk reversal is a selling point, not fine print
