# GTM Outreach Strategy

Plan and execute outreach to a target account or contact. Covers channel selection, sequence design, message construction, and personalization calibration. The goal is outreach that reads like it was written by someone who actually did their homework -- not a template filler. Run gtm-account-research first; outreach without account context produces generic messages that don't convert.

## Context Gathering

Before starting, collect from the user:

- **Your company** -- name, what you sell, value proposition, key differentiators, proof points (customer names + metrics)
- **Your sender** -- name, title, tone/style, relevant background that builds credibility
- **Your buyer** -- primary persona (titles), what they care about most, common objections, what gets their attention
- **Outreach constraints** (optional) -- channels available, sequence length preference, tone guardrails
- **Account context** -- paste summary from gtm-account-research, or describe what you know about this account

## What This Skill Produces

A complete outreach strategy for a specific account: the angle, the sequence, the message for each touch, and a brief summary of the approach.

## Outreach Methodology

### Step 1 -- Angle Selection

Before writing a single word, lock in the angle. The angle is the one reason this person should care about hearing from you, right now, given what's happening in their world.

**A strong angle has three components:**
- **Signal:** A specific, observable thing about them (funding, job posting, LinkedIn post, product launch, news)
- **Bridge:** Why that signal is relevant to the problem you solve
- **Stakes:** What gets better (or worse) for them if they act (or don't)

**Choose the strongest angle type:**

| Angle Type | Description | Use when |
|------------|-------------|----------|
| **Trigger-based** | A recent event creates urgency | Clear signal in the last 60 days |
| **Pain-based** | Lead with the symptom they're experiencing | No strong trigger, but pain is obvious |
| **Outcome-based** | Lead with a result for a similar company | Strong proof point mapping to their situation |
| **Insight-based** | Share a non-obvious observation | Buyer is sophisticated; generic pitches won't land |
| **Peer-based** | Reference a competitor or peer using you | Social proof likely to resonate |

> Choose **one** angle. Do not blend multiple angles into one message. Blending dilutes the hook.

State your chosen angle before drafting: "The angle is: [signal] -> [bridge] -> [stakes]."

### Step 2 -- Channel & Sequence Strategy

Select channels and structure based on account tier, persona seniority, and engagement patterns.

**Channel selection:**

| Situation | Recommended primary channel |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| C-suite or VP, cold | LinkedIn first, then email |
| Director / Manager, cold | Email first, LinkedIn follow-up |
| Already connected on LinkedIn | LinkedIn DM |
| High-priority, no email found | LinkedIn only |
| Warm intro or prior contact | Email, reference the connection |
| Event or content trigger | Email, reference the trigger |

**Sequence structure -- design as a story, not a series of pings:**

| Touch | Channel | Purpose | Timing |
|-------|---------|---------|--------|
| 1 | Primary | Lead with the angle. One ask only. | Day 1 |
| 2 | Secondary | Add new data point or proof point. Don't repeat touch 1. | Day 4-6 |
| 3 | Primary | Shift frame -- different angle or direct question. | Day 10-12 |
| 4 (optional) | Either | Short breakup message. Give easy out. | Day 18-21 |
| 5 (optional) | LinkedIn | Low-commitment: engage with their content or share something relevant. | Ongoing |

**Adjust for account tier:**
- Strategic: Longer sequences, more touches, multi-threaded
- Mid-market: Standard 3-4 touches
- SMB / high volume: Shorter, faster, higher automation tolerance

### Step 3 -- Message Construction

Write each message using this anatomy:

    HOOK (1 sentence)
    Reference the signal, ask a provocative question, or state something
    specific about them. Don't introduce yourself here.

    BRIDGE (1-2 sentences)
    Connect the hook to the problem you solve.
    "We work with [persona type] dealing with [situation]
    and typically see [symptom]."

    CREDIBILITY (1 sentence, optional)
    One proof point. Customer name + result. Not a paragraph of credentials.

    ASK (1 sentence)
    One clear, low-friction ask. Not a demo request on first touch.
    "Worth a 15-min conversation?" or "Happy to share how we approached this."

**Length rules:**
- Email: 75-120 words. Subject line: 3-6 words, no clickbait.
- LinkedIn DM: 50-80 words max.
- Connection request note: 1 sentence, no pitch.
- Follow-up touches: shorter than the first, not longer.

**What to avoid:**
- Opening with "I" or your company name
- "I hope this finds you well" or any equivalent
- Listing features or product capabilities
- Multiple questions in one message
- "Just checking in" follow-ups with no new value
- Vague CTAs ("let me know if you're interested")

### Step 4 -- Multi-threading Strategy

For strategic accounts, never rely on a single contact. Map at least two entry points.

- **Thread 1 -- Champion:** Person who feels pain most acutely. Goal: internal advocacy.
- **Thread 2 -- Economic buyer:** Person who signs off. Goal: top-down awareness.
- **Thread 3 -- Influencer (optional):** Ops, IT, adjacent function. Goal: reduce eval friction.

> Coordinate timing: don't hit all three simultaneously. Stagger by 5-7 days. If one thread responds, pause others.

### Step 5 -- Personalization Calibration

Match effort to account tier.

| Tier | Personalization level | What to customize |
|------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Strategic (top 20) | Deep | Specific signal, relevant proof point, named stakeholders |
| Mid-market | Moderate | Industry/role-specific pain, one relevant signal |
| High-volume / SMB | Light | Persona-level relevance, no individual research |

> For high-volume: personalize the first sentence only. One specific, relevant sentence outperforms a generic paragraph.

### Step 6 -- Objection Pre-emption

Anticipate the most likely reason they don't respond and address it proactively.

| Common failure mode | Pre-emption approach |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| "We already have a solution" | Focus on outcome gap, not product replacement |
| "Not a priority right now" | Urgency framing tied to their strategic moment |
| "Send me more info" | Don't. Redirect to a conversation. Info without context doesn't sell. |
| No response | Don't assume disinterest. Most non-responses are timing, not rejection. |

### Step 7 -- Outreach Summary

Before sending, produce a one-paragraph brief:

> "We're reaching out to [name, title] at [company] using a [trigger/pain/outcome/insight/peer]-based angle. The hook is [one sentence]. The sequence is [N] touches across [channels] over [timeframe]. The primary ask is [CTA]. The key risk is [most likely objection or gap in our intel]."

## Quality Check

- The hook references something specific about this person or company
- Each message has exactly one ask
- Follow-up messages add new value -- not re-sending the first message
- The sequence respects buyer's time -- not too many touches, not too fast
- CTA is calibrated for cold outreach -- low friction, not a 45-min demo request
- You can answer: "Why this person, why this angle, why now?"
