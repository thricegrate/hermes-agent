# Kennedy Coaching Frameworks

Dan Kennedy's direct response principles as coaching tools. These frameworks are modules you can deploy during coaching sessions when the situation calls for them.

## Framework 3: 10 Smart Market Diagnosis Questions (Discovery Tool)

Use these when coaching someone who doesn't deeply understand their market yet. Hand them these 10 questions as homework. The answers become the foundation for every offer, every piece of copy, every ad.

1. **What keeps them awake at 3am?** Not metaphorical. Literally: what thought loops run when they can't sleep?
2. **What are they afraid of?** Fear of failure, fear of missing out, fear of looking stupid. Name the specific fear.
3. **What are they angry about?** Anger is energy. If you can channel their anger toward a solution, they'll buy.
4. **Top three daily frustrations?** The small, recurring annoyances that erode their quality of life.
5. **What trends affect them?** Industry shifts, technology changes, market movements they feel but can't control.
6. **What do they secretly desire most?** The thing they want but won't say out loud in a group setting.
7. **Built-in decision-making bias?** Every group has one. Engineers default to logic. Creatives default to aesthetics. Know the bias, frame accordingly.
8. **Own language/jargon?** Use their words, not yours. If they say "funnel," don't say "customer acquisition pipeline."
9. **Who else sells to them?** Not just competitors. Everyone selling to this person. What's the buying pattern?
10. **Who failed selling to them? Why?** This is gold. The failures tell you what NOT to do and what objections are baked in.

**Coaching application**: Assign these as homework before any offer design, content strategy, or positioning session. Client who can't answer these doesn't know their market well enough to sell to it.

## Framework 13: USP Formula (Coaching Exercise)

**Core question to give the client**: "Why should someone choose you over every other option, including doing nothing at all?"

Most coaching clients answer with vague platitudes. Push until the answer is specific and measurable.

### The USP Workshop (20 minutes)

1. **List competitors**: Write down 5-10 alternatives your customer considers (including "do nothing" and "do it myself")
2. **What do they all claim?** Write the common promises. "We're the best." "We care about quality." "Personalized service."
3. **What can you promise that NONE of them do?** This is the USP.
4. **Make it specific and measurable**: "We respond within 2 hours" not "fast response times." "10K subscribers in 90 days or we work free until you hit it" not "we grow your newsletter."
5. **Test it**: If competitors could copy your USP tomorrow, it's not specific enough.

**Kennedy's rule**: Narrowing your market and owning that slice completely beats trying to appeal to everyone. The client who serves "newsletter operators at 5K-50K subs who want to add product revenue" will outsell the one who serves "anyone who wants to grow their business."

## Framework 11: Product Ascension Ladder (Coaching Module)

Use this when a client has one product and no backend. Or when they're stuck at a revenue ceiling because they only sell one thing.

### The Ladder Exercise

Draw this out with the client:

```
FREE ......... Lead magnet, free tool, demo
LOW ($10-50) .. Entry product, template pack
MID ($100-500) . Full course, playbook, workshop
HIGH ($1-5K) .. Cohort, group coaching, mastermind
ULTRA ($5-15K+)  Done-for-you, 1:1, VIP access
```

**Key questions for the client**:
1. Where are your customers entering the ladder? (If they start at MID, you're leaving money on the table both above and below.)
2. What would a logical next step be after your current offer? (Natural ascension, not a random upsell.)
3. Where does 2/3 of your revenue come from? (If it's all front end, the back end is the opportunity.)

### The Back-End Rule
2/3 to 3/4 of total revenue should come from the back end. Front-end products acquire customers. Back-end products create the business.

A client selling a $47 course with no backend has a hobby, not a business. The $47 course should lead to $197 intensive, which leads to $997 cohort, which leads to $5K DFY.

## Framework 16: "Compete on Economics" (Business Model Coaching)

**Kennedy's golden rule**: The business that can spend the most to acquire a customer wins.

This changes how you coach business strategy. Instead of "how do I lower my CAC," the real question is "how do I increase what a customer is worth so I can outspend everyone?"

### The Economics Diagnostic

Ask the client:
1. What's your customer's lifetime value? (If they don't know, that's problem #1.)
2. How much can you afford to spend to acquire one customer? (Most clients guess low.)
3. If you could spend 2x what you currently spend on acquisition, and still be profitable, what changes?
4. What back-end products or continuity would increase LTV enough to justify higher acquisition costs?

**The shift**: Stop trying to get cheaper leads. Start building a business model where expensive leads are still profitable.

### Revenue Layer Audit

Coach the client through four revenue layers:
1. **Front end**: Customer acquisition (often break-even)
2. **Back end**: Where the real profit lives (upsells, high-ticket)
3. **Continuity**: Recurring revenue (memberships, subscriptions)
4. **Big paydays**: Periodic launches, live events, limited-time offers

If any layer is missing, that's the coaching focus.

## Framework 15: Premium Pricing Mindset (Coaching Tool)

Use when a client is undercharging or afraid to raise prices.

### Nine Reasons Premium Pricing Fails (Diagnostic)

Walk through these with the client. Usually 2-3 apply:

1. **Wrong market**: Selling premium to people who buy on price
2. **No authority**: Haven't built enough credibility to justify premium
3. **Weak positioning**: Look and sound like everyone else
4. **No social proof at the premium level**: Testimonials from $47 buyers don't sell $5K offers
5. **Fear of rejection**: Dropping the price before the customer even pushes back
6. **Commodity framing**: Competing on features instead of transformation
7. **Wrong media**: Premium buyers aren't on discount platforms
8. **No exclusivity**: If anyone can buy it, nobody feels special buying it
9. **No experience design**: Charging premium but delivering commodity

**Coaching question**: "Which of these 9 failures is most true for you right now?"

### Context Controls Price
The same product at a Ritz-Carlton gift shop costs 5x what it costs at Walmart. Context, framing, and environment set price expectations. Help the client design the context around their offer:
- Premium website and branding
- Application process instead of open enrollment
- Limited availability (real, not manufactured)
- Testimonials from aspirational buyers

## Framework 12: Authority Roadmap (Positioning Exercise)

Use when a client needs to build credibility before they can charge what they're worth.

### The Authority Ladder

Where is the client now?
1. **Generalist**: "I do marketing." (Invisible in the market.)
2. **Expert**: "I do email marketing for newsletter operators." (Recognized in a niche.)
3. **Published Authority**: "I wrote the playbook on newsletter monetization." (Content proves expertise.)
4. **Celebrity Expert**: "I'm THE newsletter monetization person." (Known, sought after, premium pricing.)

### Actions Per Level

To move from Generalist to Expert:
- Pick one niche. Commit for 12 months.
- Create content that demonstrates depth, not breadth.
- Get 3-5 case studies with specific results.

To move from Expert to Published Authority:
- Create a signature framework (name it)
- Write a book, course, or comprehensive guide
- Speak at industry events or on podcasts

To move from Published Authority to Celebrity Expert:
- Build omnipresence (show up everywhere your audience looks)
- Use the "WITH" strategy (co-create with bigger names)
- Build a waitlist or application process

## Framework 14: Referral System (Coaching Module)

Use when a client has happy customers but no systematic referral process.

### Quick Referral Audit
1. Do your clients refer people to you? (If yes, how often? If no, why not?)
2. Have you ever asked for a referral? (Most haven't.)
3. Do you make it easy to refer? (Link, script, clear description of who to refer.)
4. Do you recognize referrers publicly? (Recognition drives more referrals than rewards.)

### The Endorsed Mailing Concept
Instead of asking for individual referrals, ask partners to send your offer to their audience with their personal endorsement. One endorsed mailing to a 10K list can produce more results than months of organic referral requests.

## Framework 8: Risk Reversal as an Offer Tool

Use when a client's offer isn't converting and the product is solid.

### The Risk Reversal Conversation
Ask: "What's the biggest risk your customer takes when they buy from you?"
- Risk of wasting money (guarantee needed)
- Risk of wasting time (show speed of results)
- Risk of embarrassment (social proof from similar people)
- Risk it won't work for THEM (conditional guarantee tied to action)

Then design the guarantee that removes that specific risk. Not a generic "money-back guarantee." A guarantee that directly addresses their fear.

## Kennedy Coaching Checkpoint

When using Kennedy frameworks in a session, verify:
1. Did I assign the 10 Market Diagnosis Questions before designing any offer?
2. Does the client have a specific, measurable USP? (Not a tagline.)
3. Is there a product ladder with at least 3 price points?
4. Does the business model allow outspending competitors on acquisition?
5. Is the client charging based on value and transformation, not time and features?
