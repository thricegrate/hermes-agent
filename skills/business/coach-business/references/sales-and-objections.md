## CLOSER Framework

Six-step sales process:

### C, Clarify
- Understand why the prospect is on the call
- They have a problem and want a solution
- Ask: "What made you reach out today?" / "What's the #1 thing you're trying to solve?"

### L, Label
- Make the problem concrete and less abstract
- The prospect must admit they have a problem before you can help solve it
- "So it sounds like the main issue is [specific problem]. Is that right?"

### O, Overview
- Discuss their past attempts to solve the problem
- "What have you tried so far?" / "Why didn't those work?"
- This provides ammunition for later and shows you understand their journey

### S, Sell the Vacation
- Sell the destination, NOT the plane flight
- Focus on outcomes and transformation, not features or process
- "Imagine 6 months from now, you have [dream outcome]. What would that mean for your business?"
- Paint the picture of life AFTER the problem is solved

### E, Explain
- Now explain your solution: how it bridges from their current state to the dream outcome
- Address concerns before they become objections
- Position yourself as the guide, not the hero

### R, Reinforce
- After they pay, reinforce their decision
- Personalized welcome, handwritten notes, branded gifts, immediate onboarding
- The sale doesn't end at payment: post-purchase experience determines LTV

**Key philosophy:** "Sales is about truth. If a product can help someone, the job is to show that clearly."

## AAA Objection Handling

Three-step process for every objection:

### A, Acknowledge
- Repeat the objection in a neutral, understanding tone
- "I totally get that. [Price / timing / need to think] is a real consideration."
- Builds rapport, buys thinking time, shows you're listening

### A, Associate
- Share a success story of someone who had the exact same objection
- "You know, [client name] said the exact same thing. They were worried about [objection]. Here's what happened..."
- Use foils (past clients) or authority appeals

### A, Ask
- Ask for the sale again after addressing the concern
- "Given all that, does it make sense to get started?"
- Don't just answer the objection and wait: loop back to the close

**Additional techniques:**
- **Don't answer trap questions directly**: pivot with questions: "That's a great question. Let me ask you this..."
- **Reframe objections as preferences**: "So you're saying you prefer [X]. Let's see how we can make that work."
- **Maintain control** by asking more questions than you answer

**Common objections and responses:**
| Objection | Reframe |
|-----------|---------|
| "It's too expensive" | "Compared to what? What's it costing you NOT to solve this?" |
| "I need to think about it" | "What specifically do you need to think about? Let's think through it together." |
| "I need to talk to my partner" | "What do you think they'd want to know? Let's address those questions now." |
| "I've been burned before" | "Tell me about that experience. What went wrong? How is this different?" |
| "Now's not the right time" | "When would be the right time? What needs to change?" |

## Pricing Psychology

**Core principles:**
1. **Price = how customers judge value.** Higher price → higher perceived quality.
2. **Premium prices get better customers.** They commit harder, do the work, get results, give testimonials.
3. **The virtuous pricing cycle:** Higher price → better customers → better results → justify even higher prices.
4. **"Fast beats free."** People will always pay for speed.

**Price anchoring:** Introduce an extremely expensive "decoy" option (10-100x more expensive) to make your standard option feel reasonable.

**The pricing test:**
- If 0% of prospects say "too expensive" → you're undercharging
- If 20% say "too expensive" → you're priced right
- If 50%+ say "too expensive" → either overpriced or wrong audience

**Never discount. Instead:**
- Add bonuses (increases perceived value)
- Offer payment plans (changes cash flow, not price)
- Add a guarantee (reduces risk, not price)
- Create a lower-tier option (different product, not discount)

## Reframing Techniques

**Best Case / Worst Case close:**
- "What's the best case if this works? [Paint the dream]"
- "What's the worst case? [You're back where you started, minus the investment]"
- "Is [investment] worth the shot at [dream outcome]?"

**Past / Present / Future:**
- "You've been dealing with [problem] for [time]. Nothing's changed."
- "Right now, it's costing you [cost]."
- "In 6 months, without a change, where will you be? Same place or worse?"

**Cost of inaction:**
- "What's it costing you per month to NOT solve this?"
- "Over the next year, that's [annual cost]. The investment is [price]. The math is clear."

**Tonality and conviction:**
- True belief in your product naturally corrects your tone
- Conviction matters more than script
- If you wouldn't recommend it to your best friend, don't sell it

## Industry Example: Newsletter Business

*The frameworks above apply to ANY business. Below is one industry example to illustrate application.*

### Selling agency services to newsletter operators
**Apply CLOSER:**
- C: "What's your #1 challenge with your newsletter right now?"
- L: "So you're stuck at [X] subscribers and can't get past [Y] revenue."
- O: "What have you tried? Hiring writers? Running ads? Partnerships?"
- S: "Imagine having 100K engaged subscribers generating $5K/mo predictably."
- E: "Here's how we do it: [growth system], [content system], [monetization system]."
- R: Welcome kit with branded dashboard, SOPs, 90-day roadmap

**Retention problem (low agency retention):**
- Problem is likely weak onboarding + vague deliverables + no accountability
- Fix: Concrete KPIs, weekly reporting, 90-day milestones, conditional guarantee
- "We'll add 10K subscribers in 90 days or you don't pay for month 3"

### Selling newsletters ($50K+ flips)
**Apply Grand Slam Offer thinking:**
- Package as "cash-flowing media asset," not "email list"
- Include: subscriber data, revenue history, SOPs, content calendar, ad partner contacts
- Guarantee: Revenue guarantees for first 90 days post-acquisition
- Bonuses: 90-day transition support, content templates, growth playbooks

### Closing ad deals
- Sell outcomes, not impressions: "Our readers buy AI tools. 40% click rate on tool recommendations."
- Use social proof: "Here's what [Brand X] saw from their last campaign with us."
- Offer performance-based pricing for first campaign to reduce risk

## Onion of Blame Diagnostic Taxonomy (Hormozi Closing Playbook)

People avoid buying decisions by casting blame outward in layers. The closer you get to the core (self), the closer you are to a genuine decision. Salespeople should expect prospects to swap objections as you peel layers.

### Layer 1 - External Circumstances
"I can't because of X outside my control"
- **Time:** "I'm too busy right now"
- **Money:** "I can't afford it" / "It's too expensive"
- **Fit:** "It's not quite right for my situation"

### Layer 2 - Other People
"I can't because of someone else"
- **Spouse/partner:** "I need to talk to my wife/husband"
- **Past vendors:** "I've been burned before"
- **Business partner:** "My co-founder won't go for it"

### Layer 3 - Self (Core)
"I can't because of me"
- **Preferences:** "It's not for me"
- **Fear:** "What if it doesn't work?"
- **Avoidance:** "I need to think about it"
- **Rushed decisions:** "I don't want to make a hasty decision"

**Key insight:** When someone swaps objections after you handle one, you're closing correctly -- not failing. Keep peeling. The real decision lives at Layer 3.

**Coaching diagnostic:** When a client says "my customers keep saying it's too expensive," ask: "When you handle the price objection, what do they say NEXT?" If they don't know, they're not peeling the onion.

## 28 Rules of Closing Summary (Hormozi Closing Playbook)

Not scripts but a philosophy for how closing should be conducted. Use as a diagnostic checklist when a client's close rate is low.

**Listening & Rapport:**
1. When in doubt, repeat back what they said
2. Acknowledge or agree, never disagree ("totally understand" before any close)
3. Don't assume objections mean they won't buy (sometimes they're processing out loud)
4. People want to believe you -- help their brains justify the decision

**Tactical:**
5. Stack closes: if first doesn't land, hit from another angle
6. You only need 2-3 closes per obstacle
7. Once they say yes, shut up
8. Never change your price on the spot
9. Only ask for sale when you think you've got them
10. Before getting real, get permission ("Can I be a coach instead of a friend?")

**Mindset:**
11. Yellows are golds: practice on unqualified leads
12. Volume negates luck
13. Selling happens before the ask. Closing happens after.
14. The person who cares the most about the prospect wins the sale.
15. Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust.
16. Belief and trust are a continuum, not binaries.

**Delivery:**
17. Say offensive things about someone like them or your past self, not them directly
18. You don't need to memorize, you need to understand (use your own words)
19. Record all your sales to learn from them.

**Quick diagnostic:** Ask the client "Are your salespeople recording calls?" (Rule 19), "Do they acknowledge before rebutting?" (Rule 2), "Do they keep talking after the yes?" (Rule 7), and "Do they believe in the product?" (Rule 15 -- conviction is the multiplier).

> "Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust."

## Decision-Making Power Model (Hormozi Closing Playbook)

Closing is NOT about getting prospects to buy. It's about giving them enough decision-making power to overcome avoidance.

**The reframe:** People on the fence lack power to decide. The goal is a genuine decision (yes OR no), not a forced yes.

**Three steps:**
1. Understand what they want (from earlier in the conversation)
2. Put what they want next to the options they have
3. Make sure they come to a genuine decision

**Why this matters for coaching clients who hate selling:** Most clients feel "salesy" because they think closing is manipulation. This model reframes it as SERVICE -- you're helping someone make a decision they already want to make but can't pull the trigger on.

**Questions for clients struggling with sales identity:**
- "Do you believe your product genuinely helps people?"
- "If someone needs your help but can't decide, is it more ethical to help them decide or let them stay stuck?"
- "What's the cost to your prospect of NOT making a decision?"

> "The goal of closing isn't to get the prospect to buy. Closing gives the prospect enough power to overcome decision avoidance."
