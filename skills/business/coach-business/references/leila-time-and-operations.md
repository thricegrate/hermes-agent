## 1. Monday Hour One

### Core Principle

> "You're going to spend one hour planning to save 10-12 hours executing."

Every Monday morning (or Sunday night), block out the entire week. Not some of it. All of it. By Monday morning, your calendar should show exactly where every hour is going.

### The 6-Step Process (60 minutes total)

#### Step 1: Brain Dump (20 minutes)

Collect EVERYTHING that needs to happen this week from these sources:
- Last week's calendar (what didn't get done?)
- Project management tool (what's coming up?)
- Sent emails (what's still open?)
- Job description (what are the daily/weekly recurring duties?)
- End-of-week reports (what needs action?)
- Slack/messages (anything unresolved?)

#### Step 2: Prioritize Ruthlessly (5 minutes)

What moves the needle most? That goes first. Everything else queues behind it.

#### Step 3: Block Breaks FIRST (5 minutes)

Lunch, school pickup, walks, personal time. Put them in or they won't happen. Breaks are non-negotiable, not optional.

#### Step 4: Schedule Actual Work (20 minutes)

- No vague "project work." Name the specific project.
- High-priority items get prime time slots (when your energy is highest).
- Email/Slack gets designated windows. Not all day.

#### Step 5: Add Overflow Time (5 minutes)

Buffer for ad-hoc requests, client fires, things taking longer than expected. Without overflow, one surprise derails the week.

#### Step 6: Color Code Everything (5 minutes)

| Color | Category |
|-------|----------|
| Blue | Recurring meetings |
| Red | Admin tasks |
| Yellow | One-off meetings |
| Green | Project/task work |
| Purple | Breaks/food |
| Orange | Personal (optional) |

> "Your calendar should look like Tetris. Every slot filled. Every hour accounted for."

### The Commitment Rule

> "If you block time and don't do what you said you'd do, you're lying to yourself. The calendar is a commitment."

The friction at the beginning of the week leads to freedom the rest of it. Constraints create freedom. When you give yourself too much time, work expands to fill it. When you create boundaries, you get things done.

### Coaching Application

Deploy when client says "I never have enough time," "my week always gets away from me," or "I'm always putting out fires." Monday Hour One is the implementation-level answer to the 91% Rule (Implementation Intentions) from `coach-core/references/productivity-psychology.md`. The 91% Rule says WHEN and WHERE. Monday Hour One makes that a weekly system.

Also pairs with weekly planning template (`coach-core/templates/weekly-planning.md`). The template is for coaching sessions. Monday Hour One is for the client's own weekly practice.

## 2. Makers vs Managers

### The Research

Task-switching costs up to 40% of productive time (Meyer, Evans, Rubinstein). Even brief mental blocks from shifting between tasks destroy productivity.

At Acquisition.com, that translated to $1.6 million per year in lost productivity. $133,000 per month. $5,000 per day.

### Two Types of Workers

| | Managers | Makers |
|---|---------|--------|
| **Work in** | 30-60 minute blocks | Half-day or full-day blocks |
| **Calendar** | Filled with meetings, comms, trainings | Open = maximum productivity |
| **Free time is** | A lost opportunity | The whole point |
| **One meeting** | Costs one time slot | Kills half their productive time (thinking "I have to finish before XX") |
| **Role** | Direct, collect data, lead, train, decide | Create content, designs, copy, systems |

### The 10x Cost Rule

> "When managers schedule meetings with makers, it costs the maker 10x what it costs the manager."

Managers often prevent makers from doing the work they need done from them. The meeting that takes 30 minutes of a manager's day takes 4 hours of a maker's day (the meeting + the destroyed focus blocks on either side).

### Solutions by Type

**For Managers:**
- Every meeting with a maker costs them 10x what it costs you. Make sure it's worth it.
- Respect when makers say no to meetings.
- Use async communication (project tools, documented updates) instead of sync meetings.

**For Makers:**
- Block ONE work unit (half day) for admin/meetings. Eat up one unit, not five.
- Set expectations with away messages. Retrain teammates.
- Work when you say you'll work. If you block time and make nothing, you hurt all makers.

**For Hybrids (most founders):**
- Theme your days rather than mixing tasks.
- See each day as 2 chunks: 4 hours AM for maker work, 4 hours PM for manager work.
- Or block 1 full day per week with NO meetings.
- Communication is more important for hybrids than anyone. Set clear expectations about which mode you're in.

### The Diagnostic Question

> "Are you a maker, a manager, or something in between? Once you know, protect your time accordingly."

### Coaching Application

Deploy when client has creative/maker roles but is drowning in meetings, or when building teams with both types. The Maker/Manager diagnosis often explains why a founder who "works 12 hours a day" still feels unproductive. They're in manager mode all day while their maker work (the stuff that actually moves the business) never gets focused time.
