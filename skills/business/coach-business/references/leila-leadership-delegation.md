## 1. Unavailability as Leadership Tool

### Core Principle

> "If a leader being unavailable causes everything to break, that isn't leadership; that's dependency."

### The 7-Day Unavailability Test

Leila was forced offline for 7 days after surgery. While she was out, the team handled: Head of Product quitting, Facebook account getting shut down, a major client issue. All without her. The team handled them well.

The lesson: being constantly needed is not the goal. Building people and systems that operate without you is.

### 3 Practices to Reduce Dependency

**1. Pause Before Answering**

When someone brings a question, resist the instinct to solve it. Ask one of these:
- "What do you think the right move is?"
- "What options have you considered?"
- "If I weren't here, what would you do?"

If they can't answer, that signals missing clarity or training. Not a reason to take over.

**2. Identify One "Default Task" to Hand Off**

Look at your week and ask:
- What decision am I making by default?
- What problem keeps coming back to me?
- What am I involved in simply because I've always been involved?

Pick one. Intentionally hand it off. Even if it feels slower at first.

**3. Notice Where You're Rewarded for Being Needed**

Pay attention to moments where being indispensable feels good: saving the day, fixing something fast, being the hero. Those moments are traps.

Ask: "Does this build capability downstream, or does it reinforce dependency?"

### Coaching Application

Deploy when founder says "I can't step away" or "everything falls apart without me." Pair with the Phone Test and Delegation Method from `operations-and-hiring.md`. The Phone Test identifies what CAN be delegated. This framework addresses WHY the founder hasn't delegated it yet.

## 2. People Strategy & Team Leveling

### Core Principle

> "If a single person on your team is not growing or performing at the level needed, they are now your team's ceiling."

People strategy is often overlooked. The best companies look at the people first. Business strategy without people strategy is a plan without an army.

### 8 Reflection Questions for Leaders

1. Is my team rising at the same rate I am? Or am I outpacing them in a way that creates drag?
2. Have I given my team the tools, systems, and clarity they need to grow?
3. Am I developing leaders or just managing doers? Do I have successors?
4. Am I delegating at the level that matches the leader I want to become? If not, why isn't my bench ready?
5. Do I have the right people for the NEXT version of the company, not just the current version?
6. Is my team getting better because of my leadership or in spite of it?
7. If every person on my team performed at the level of my top performer, how different would outcomes be? What am I doing to close that gap?
8. If someone cannot meet expectations even with clarity, support, and development, am I willing to make the hard call?

### The Team Ceiling Principle

Growth requires alignment. Alignment requires intention. A single underperforming team member becomes the ceiling for the entire team.

Part of leadership is giving people every reasonable chance to rise. Part of leadership is knowing when the role has outgrown the person. Both are acts of stewardship.

### Coaching Application

Deploy during quarterly planning, growth roadmap reviews, or when a client says "my team isn't keeping up." Pair with the Scorecards and First 5 Hires frameworks from `operations-and-hiring.md`. Those give the WHAT (roles, metrics). This gives the HOW (reflection, leveling, hard calls).

## 3. Calendar Availability Audit

### Core Principle

A calendar full by DEFAULT is rarely a sign of effectiveness. It's a sign of one of three things:
1. Missing key people
2. Haven't delegated fully or set clear expectations
3. Letting other people's urgency override your priorities

### Seasonal Availability (When Full Calendar IS Appropriate)

Some seasons naturally require more from leaders:
- Onboarding a new exec or leader
- Building or rebuilding a system
- Making a major strategic decision
- Resetting expectations or aligning on direction
- Real crisis (not a perceived one)

During these seasons, more communication is part of stabilizing the foundation.

### The Red Flag

> "The red flag isn't a full calendar. It's a full calendar that never changes, regardless of the season."

Sustained overload means you're operating reactively rather than architecting proactively.

### 5 Audit Questions

1. Which meetings still require my presence, and which only require my leadership through someone else?
2. What calls exist because I haven't documented a decision or expectation clearly?
3. Where am I being a crutch instead of developing the person who should own this?
4. Which meetings energize me (high-leverage) and which drain me (filling a gap in structure)?
5. Is this week a "full calendar season" for a strategic reason, or just habit?

### Coaching Application

Deploy when client says "my calendar is packed" or "I have no time to think." Pair with Think Day (`sustainable-performance.md`) as the antidote and Monday Hour One (`leila-time-and-operations.md`) for the fix. Also connects to sustainable performance recovery: a leader with no white space has no cognitive capacity for judgment.

Cross-reference: `sustainable-performance.md` (Recovery, Full Day Off Rule)

## 4. 6-Inch Putt Decision Protocol

### The Problem

> "My team was outsourcing their thinking to me."

A 6-inch putt decision is one that's simplified, well thought out, and should be executed quickly without unnecessary back-and-forth. Most questions that come up the chain are NOT 6-inch putts. They require more thought, context, and problem-solving. The person bringing the question is outsourcing thinking to you.

### 5-Step Protocol (Turn Any Decision Into a 6-Inch Putt)

1. **Get the right information.** What are the key facts? What problem are we solving? What's the GOAL?
2. **Consider your options.** What are 2-3 possible solutions? Pros and cons of each?
3. **Identify the risks.** What could go wrong? Worst-case scenario?
4. **Ask a colleague.** Get a different perspective before escalating.
5. **Make a recommendation.** If you had to decide right now, what would you do and WHY?

### 3 Decision Rules

| Scenario | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Decision is already a 6-inch putt | Just make the call. No need to check in. |
| Not a 6-inch putt yet | Don't bring an open-ended question. Bring a recommendation with reasoning. |
| Still stuck after protocol | Say: "Here's the situation, here are the options, and this is what I'd do if I were making the call." |

### Core Insight

> "Most people don't need your permission. They need your confidence."

When you make people turn problems into 6-inch putts before bringing them to you, you teach them to think like owners. When everyone thinks like an owner, the whole company moves faster.

### Coaching Application

Deploy when founder is the bottleneck on every decision, team constantly asks for permission, or meetings are consumed by "what should we do about X?" questions. This is the operational companion to Who Not How (`coach-core/references/who-not-how.md`). Who Not How asks "who should own this?" 6-Inch Putt asks "how should they bring decisions to me?"

## 5. Five Leadership Shifts

Year-end reflection on five shifts that transformed Leila's leadership. Each shift includes a lesson and behavior change.

### Shift 1: Constant Pushing -> Strategic Rest

Most people don't quit because the work is wrong. They quit because they are depleted. Physical overtraining is widely understood. Cognitive and emotional overtraining are mislabeled as weakness.

**Lesson:** Quitting while depleted does not bring clarity. It allows exhaustion to make decisions that should be made with a clear head.

**Behavior:** Rest is not something to apologize for when it improves decision quality. Leaders manage energy the same way they manage capital.

### Shift 2: Personal Involvement -> Systems

In early stages, proximity and speed are advantages. Over time, those same traits become constraints. "I became too many of the systems because it was faster than hiring, documenting, and building correctly."

**Lesson:** Any organization that depends on one person's presence, judgment, or energy is fragile. What limited us was infrastructure, not talent.

**Behavior:** If something requires heroics to function, it is either new or unfinished. Leaders build systems that absorb pressure, not become the system themselves.

### Shift 3: Constant Availability -> Predictable Cadence

Constant availability creates dependency. It teaches others they can't navigate problems on their own. Predictable cadence creates capability. When people know how and when decisions are made, escalation decreases and ownership increases.

**Lesson:** Leaders don't owe everyone access. They owe the organization clarity, decisions, and direction. Availability creates bottlenecks and task doers. Cadence builds leaders.

**Behavior:** Urgency should be earned, not assumed. Cadence helps separate true priorities from emotion-driven escalation.

### Shift 4: Niceness -> Kindness

Niceness avoids discomfort and delays truth. Kindness requires honesty, timely feedback, and decisions that protect the organization's long-term health.

**Lesson:** Withholding clarity to spare feelings may feel compassionate. It erodes trust over time. People sense uncertainty even when it's not named. High standards delivered with respect create stability. Avoidance creates instability.

**Behavior:** Choose clarity early, even when uncomfortable. The kindest leaders protect clarity, dignity, and the team's future. Not comfort.

### Shift 5: Raw Effort -> Judgment

At scale, the company depends less on stamina and more on judgment. One poor decision costs more than a week of hard work can make up for. Protecting cognitive bandwidth is not optional.

**Lesson:** Judgment compounds over time. Fatigue degrades it long before it affects visible productivity. Emotional regulation is a requirement for clear thinking, not a soft skill.

**Behavior:** Protect cognitive bandwidth as aggressively as execution time. Exhaustion is not evidence of commitment. It is a liability at scale. Work creates progress. Judgment creates direction.

### Coaching Application

Deploy during year-end or quarterly strategic reviews. Use as a diagnostic: "Which of these five shifts have you made? Which are you still resisting?" Each shift maps to existing frameworks:
- Shift 1 -> `sustainable-performance.md` (Stress + Rest = Growth)
- Shift 2 -> `operations-and-hiring.md` (Delegation, SOPs)
- Shift 3 -> Calendar Availability Audit (above)
- Shift 4 -> Feedback Formula (below)
- Shift 5 -> `coach-core/references/performance-equation.md` (Cognitive Capacity)

## 6. Feedback Formula

### The Formula

> "I observed you do X, next time do Y."

That's it. Describe what you observed. Tell them what to do instead.

### Insult vs. Feedback

**An insult** compares someone to something negative. It makes people defend their character.

**Feedback** informs them of a discrepancy between where they are and the ideal. It asks them to change a behavior.

| Insult (Don't Do This) | Feedback (Do This) |
|------------------------|-------------------|
| "You're being difficult" | "I noticed you interrupted Sarah three times in the meeting. Next time, let her finish before responding." |
| "You're not a team player" | "You sent that client email without the pricing attachment. Next time, double-check all attachments before sending." |
| "You need to get your shit together" | "You came to the standup without your numbers prepared. Next time, have them ready before the meeting starts." |
| "You're dropping the ball" | [Specify which ball and what "not dropping it" looks like] |

### 3 Pre-Checks Before Giving Feedback

1. Am I describing what I observed, or am I labeling the person?
2. Am I telling them what TO do next time, or just what NOT to do?
3. Would I want to receive this feedback the way I'm about to deliver it?

> "If you can't articulate what you observed and what you want instead, you're not ready to give feedback. You're just venting."

### The Mistake Protocol (Complementary Framework)

When a team member makes a costly mistake:

1. **Acknowledge without emotion.** "It happens." Not "it's fine" (because it isn't). Not "how could you?" (not helpful).
2. **Skip the blame.** They're already punishing themselves. Adding disappointment won't un-spend the money. It will make them hide the next mistake.
3. **Focus forward.** "What's the plan for next time?" This is where learning happens. Not in the shame spiral.
4. **Document the learning.** Turn the mistake into a system improvement so nobody else makes the same one.

> "Your job as a leader isn't to punish mistakes. It's to point out the gap between where they are and where they need to be."

### Coaching Application

Deploy when client says "my team doesn't listen," "I don't know how to have hard conversations," or "I keep avoiding feedback." Also deploy when client describes blowing up at a team member. Pair with BE NEUTRAL (`coach-personal/references/leila-emotional-regulation.md`) for managing your own state before giving feedback, and Shift 4 (Niceness -> Kindness) above for the mindset shift.
