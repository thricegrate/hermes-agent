# Kennedy Ad Rules

Dan Kennedy's direct response advertising principles applied to paid media. Every ad is a sales letter in miniature.

## Framework 1: Every Ad = Offer + Urgency + Instructions + Tracking

No exceptions. Every ad you run must have all four elements or it's burning money.

### 1. The Offer
Your ad is not an announcement. It's not "brand awareness." It's an offer. Something specific the viewer can get right now.

Bad: "We help businesses grow with AI."
Good: "Free AI audit shows you 3 automations that save 10+ hours/week. Grab yours before Friday."

The offer is the reason someone stops scrolling. If your ad doesn't have one, it's a billboard pretending to be direct response.

### 2. Urgency
Why should they click NOW instead of later? "Later" means never. Every ad needs a deadline, a limited quantity, or a consequence of waiting.

- "Only 50 spots available this month"
- "Price goes up Monday"
- "This audit tool closes to new users Friday"
- "First 100 signups get the bonus template pack"

### 3. Clear Instructions
Don't make them think. Tell them exactly what to do.

- "Click the button below"
- "Enter your email on the next page"
- "Watch the 3-minute demo, then claim your free audit"

One action. One click. One page. Clarity converts. Confusion kills.

### 4. Tracking
If you can't measure it, don't run it. Every ad gets:
- UTM parameters on every link
- Conversion pixel on the destination
- Revenue attribution back to the specific ad
- Cost per acquisition calculated weekly

Kennedy's rule: "Half my advertising works. I just need to know which half." Tracking tells you which half.

## Framework 8: Model Mail-Order Advertising

The best ad copywriters study direct mail, not ad blogs. Direct mail companies spent millions testing what works. Here's what they proved:

### Long Copy Outsells Short Copy (When the Market Is Right)
For considered purchases, long-form ads convert better. Facebook carousel ads, multi-slide Instagram stories, long YouTube pre-rolls. Don't fear length. Fear boredom.

### Testimonials Beat Claims
"This tool saved me 15 hours last week" from a real person beats "Save hours with our tool" from your brand. Every ad should include at least one specific testimonial or proof point.

### Specifics Beat Generalities
"Save 10 hours and 47 minutes per week" beats "save time." "2,847 newsletter operators use this" beats "thousands of users." The more specific the number, the more believable the claim.

### The Two-Step Approach
Don't try to sell in the ad. Get the lead first, sell in the follow-up. Ad > free resource > email sequence > sale. This is the mail-order model translated to digital.

### Test Before Scaling
Mail-order companies tested small before rolling out. Same rule: $50-100/day test budget for 5-7 days. Only scale what proves profitable. Never scale hope.

## Framework 6: Headline-First Advertising

The headline does 80% of the selling. If the headline doesn't stop the scroll, the body copy never gets read.

### Proven Headline Formulas for Ads

**"They Laughed When I..."**
"They laughed when I said AI could write my newsletter. Then I showed them my open rates."

**"How to [Result] in [Time] Without [Objection]"**
"How to grow your newsletter to 10K subscribers in 90 days without spending on ads."

**"Who Else Wants..."**
"Who else wants their AI tools to actually make money instead of just looking cool?"

**"Warning:"**
"Warning: If you're still writing every newsletter from scratch, you're working 10x harder than you need to."

**"Do You Make These Mistakes..."**
"Do you make these 3 mistakes with your email subject lines? (Most newsletter operators do.)"

**"The Lazy Person's Way To..."**
"The lazy newsletter operator's way to 40%+ open rates."

### The 50-3 Process
1. Write 50 headlines. Don't judge. Just generate.
2. Walk away for at least an hour.
3. Come back and pick your top 3.
4. Run those 3 as separate ad variations.
5. Let the data pick the winner.

Why 50? Because headlines 1-10 are obvious. Headlines 11-30 are interesting. Headlines 31-50 are where the breakthrough usually hides.

## Framework 17: Direct Mail to Digital Translation

The medium is different. The psychology is identical.

### Email = Direct Mail Letter
- Same structure: headline, opening story, body with proof, offer, PS
- Same rule: the subject line is the envelope teaser. If it doesn't get opened, nothing else matters.
- Advantage: faster testing, instant delivery, measurable opens and clicks

### Facebook/Instagram Ad = Print Display Ad
- Same elements: headline, image, body copy, CTA
- Same rule: the image is the "stopper" and the headline does the selling
- Advantage: targeting precision, instant feedback, lower minimum spend

### Retargeting = Follow-Up Mailing
- Same principle: the first touch rarely converts, follow-up does the heavy lifting
- Same rule: 5-12 touches before purchase. Retargeting delivers these automatically.
- Advantage: fraction of the cost per impression, behavioral targeting

### Landing Page = Long-Form Sales Letter
- Same structure: headline, problem, agitation, solution, proof, offer, guarantee, CTA
- Same rule: one offer per page, one action per page, no navigation links
- Advantage: split-testing, dynamic content, pixel tracking

### Webinar = Seminar Selling
- Same flow: teach for 45 min, transition to pitch, present offer with urgency
- Same rule: value first, then offer. But the "value" must create desire for the offer.
- Advantage: no venue costs, unlimited capacity, replay capability

## Kennedy Ad Checkpoint (Run Before Every Campaign)

1. Does every ad include a specific offer? (Not "learn more." A tangible thing they get.)
2. Is there a real reason to act now? (Deadline, limited quantity, disappearing bonus.)
3. Are the instructions crystal clear? (One CTA, one action, one destination.)
4. Can I track results to the individual ad level? (UTMs, pixels, attribution.)
5. Have I written at least 20 headline variations? (If not, you haven't found the best one yet.)
6. Am I testing fundamentally different messages, not just different images?
7. Does the landing page match the ad's promise exactly? (Message match = conversion.)
