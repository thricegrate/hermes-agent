# Emotional Triggers in Marketing: Complete Reference

Synthesized from 17 psychology transcript files. Covers Clayton Makepeace's 16 dominant emotions, the Life Force 8 (Drew Eric Whitman/Cashvertising), 8 psychological motivations with copy templates, emotional arc by funnel stage, fear vs. aspiration strategy, and the emotional resonance test.

Core principle from all 17 transcripts: **people buy because of emotion and justify with logic**. Target the emotion first. Provide the logic second. Copy that only delivers logical arguments fails because it enters at the wrong cognitive layer.

---

## Part 1: Clayton Makepeace's 16 Dominant Emotions

Clayton Makepeace was one of the highest-paid copywriters in direct response history. His framework identifies 16 human emotions that drive purchasing decisions. These emotions operate before rational evaluation begins. Identifying which emotion your offer should target -- and writing directly to it -- is the foundation of high-converting copy.

For each emotion: the core driver, when it motivates purchase, and how to trigger it in copy.

---

### 1. Greed

**Core driver**: The desire for more -- resources, money, luxury, or better circumstances.

**When it drives purchase**: When the offer promises measurable financial gain, access to luxury, or a significantly better outcome than the current situation. Greed responds to specific numbers ("earn $X," "save $Y," "gain Z in 30 days").

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Lead with the specific financial or material benefit
- Use real numbers, not vague promises
- Show the lifestyle or outcome gap between now and after
- Highlight exclusivity or access to something only those who act will receive

**Copy angle**: "Here is exactly how much [product] can improve [financial/material situation] for someone in your position."

**Warning**: Greed-driven copy requires credibility. Wild claims trigger skepticism. Ground every gain claim in specific proof.

---

### 2. Fear

**Core driver**: The desire to avoid negative outcomes, harm, failure, or loss.

**When it drives purchase**: When the reader can vividly imagine a negative consequence they want to prevent. Fear is most effective when the threat feels real, near, and specific. Generic fears ("what if things go wrong?") are less motivating than specific ones ("what if your [specific valuable thing] disappears in the next 90 days?").

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Identify the specific, real consequence the buyer fears most
- Make it vivid: describe the outcome in concrete, imaginable terms
- Show how it is already happening (or could be)
- Position your product as the prevention or protection

**Copy angle**: "If [specific fear-triggering scenario] happens, here is what that means for [buyer's specific situation]. [Product] prevents this."

**Transition**: Fear opens a door; aspiration closes the sale. Use fear to create urgency and identify the problem, then transition to the solution and its positive outcome. Do not leave the reader in fear -- lead them through it.

**Warning**: Fear copy that exaggerates or invents threats is manipulative and destroys credibility. The fear must be real and the product's protection must be genuine.

---

### 3. Vanity

**Core driver**: The desire for admiration and attention from others.

**When it drives purchase**: When the offer visibly signals the buyer's sophistication, taste, or status to others. Vanity is activated by products that are publicly visible or that change how others perceive the buyer.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Paint a picture of how the buyer will be seen by others after using the product
- Use aspirational imagery and language that mirrors the desired image
- Feature people the target audience wants to be perceived as similar to

**Copy angle**: "Here is how [product] changes how [specific audience -- peers, colleagues, competitors] see you."

**Distinction from Pride**: Vanity is about external perception (how others see you). Pride is about internal self-worth (how you see yourself). The same product may activate both, but they require different copy angles.

---

### 4. Love

**Core driver**: Connection, care, and the desire to protect or nurture others.

**When it drives purchase**: When the product helps the buyer express love, protect loved ones, or strengthen relationships. Love is particularly powerful for gifts, family products, health products where the buyer's wellbeing affects others, and anything framed around legacy.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Frame the product as an expression of care for others
- Reference the specific people in the buyer's life who benefit
- Show the impact on relationships, not just on the individual

**Copy angle**: "The people who depend on you deserve [outcome]. [Product] is how you provide that."

---

### 5. Pride

**Core driver**: Self-worth, accomplishment, and the need to feel good about one's choices and character.

**When it drives purchase**: When the offer validates the buyer's identity as a certain type of person -- a professional, a parent, a craftsperson, an entrepreneur. Pride is activated by products that are associated with the buyer's self-concept.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Identify the specific identity the buyer aspires to and is proud of
- Frame the product as what "people like them" use or do
- Show the accomplishment that using the product represents

**Copy angle**: "People who [identity statement] use [product] because [specific reason that connects to that identity]."

---

### 6. Lust

**Core driver**: The desire for pleasure, sensuality, and indulgence.

**When it drives purchase**: When the offer promises an experience that is inherently pleasurable, desirable, or gratifying. Lust applies beyond its obvious uses -- it activates for any product that promises intense, immediate pleasure.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Sensory language that evokes the experience of the product
- "Indulgence" framing: give yourself permission to want this
- Remove guilt from the desire: this is something you deserve

**Copy angle**: "Imagine [specific sensory experience of the product]. That is what [product] feels like every time."

---

### 7. Revenge

**Core driver**: The desire to prevail over those who have doubted, dismissed, or harmed you.

**When it drives purchase**: When the buyer has been told they cannot achieve something, been dismissed, or has failed before and wants to prove the doubters wrong. Revenge is the underdog emotion -- it activates specifically for people who feel overlooked or underestimated.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Reference the specific people or systems that have dismissed your reader
- Frame the product as the tool that enables them to prove those people wrong
- Position success as the ultimate response to prior failure or dismissal

**Copy angle**: "For everyone who told you that [specific limitation or dismissal]. Here is your answer."

**Use case**: This emotion is particularly effective in the entrepreneur, creator, and career development markets. "They said it couldn't be done" is a revenge hook.

---

### 8. Health

**Core driver**: The desire for physical wellbeing, energy, longevity, and the ability to function at full capacity.

**When it drives purchase**: When the offer addresses a real or feared health limitation. Health motivations are particularly strong when they are connected to a deeper Life Force drive (survival, care of loved ones, sexual attractiveness, comfortable living).

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Connect health outcomes to the deeper things health enables (energy to play with kids, confidence from appearance, longevity to see grandchildren)
- Use specific, measurable health claims where verifiable
- Address the fear of losing health as well as the desire to gain it

**Copy angle**: "When you have [health outcome], you can [specific thing that matters to buyer beyond the health itself]."

---

### 9. Security

**Core driver**: The desire for safety from ongoing or hidden threats.

**When it drives purchase**: When the product protects against something the buyer has not fully reckoned with or has been ignoring. Security emotions are activated by awareness of a risk the buyer did not previously perceive.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Surface the hidden risk the buyer has been ignoring
- Quantify the risk (how likely, how severe, how soon)
- Position the product as the protection

**Copy angle**: "Most [buyers' category] do not know about [specific risk]. Here is what it means if you ignore it, and here is how [product] addresses it."

**Distinction from Survival**: Security is about ongoing, hidden, or chronic threats. Survival is about immediate, acute danger. Security copy is educational; survival copy is urgent.

---

### 10. Power

**Core driver**: The desire to control circumstances, people, or outcomes.

**When it drives purchase**: When the buyer feels powerless or overwhelmed and wants a product that returns control to them. Power emotions are particularly relevant for software, systems, and tools that give the buyer command over something they currently feel at the mercy of.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Identify the specific situation where the buyer feels out of control
- Name the frustration of not being the one who decides
- Show how the product makes them the person in charge

**Copy angle**: "Stop letting [external force -- algorithm, boss, market, system] decide your [outcome]. [Product] puts you back in control."

---

### 11. Recognition

**Core driver**: The need for acknowledgment of one's talents, contributions, and effort.

**When it drives purchase**: When the buyer has been working hard without acknowledgment and wants a product that either generates recognition or validates that their work has been worthy of it.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Acknowledge the reader's efforts and competence directly
- Show how the product produces visible, external results that others notice
- Frame the product as a tool that helps the buyer's work finally be seen

**Copy angle**: "You have been putting in the work. [Product] is what makes sure that work gets the recognition it deserves."

---

### 12. Fame

**Core driver**: The desire to be widely known, envied, and admired.

**When it drives purchase**: When the buyer aspires to a public profile, a larger audience, or status that extends beyond their immediate circle. Fame is distinct from Recognition (which is about acknowledgment from people who already know the buyer) -- Fame is about reaching people who do not know the buyer yet.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Reference the specific platforms, stages, or audiences the buyer wants to reach
- Show what "being known" looks like in specific terms for this buyer's market
- Position the product as what the people they admire use

**Copy angle**: "Here is how [specific person or type of person the buyer admires] built [specific public profile]. [Product] is how you do the same."

---

### 13. Fortune

**Core driver**: The desire to accumulate wealth over time, not just earn income.

**When it drives purchase**: When the offer connects to wealth-building, investment return, or financial security over a longer time horizon. Fortune is distinct from Greed (which is about immediate gain) -- Fortune is about building something lasting.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Frame the product as an investment with a return
- Show the long-term compounding value
- Reference financial security and what it enables

**Copy angle**: "At [your price], this is not a cost. It is an investment that returns [specific calculation] over [timeframe]."

---

### 14. Survival

**Core driver**: The primal instinct to avoid catastrophic harm, loss, or destruction.

**When it drives purchase**: When the threat is severe, immediate, and undeniable. Survival copy is for genuine emergencies -- products that address serious risk, significant loss, or real threats to important things. It is the most powerful emotion but should be reserved for situations where it is genuinely applicable.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Name the specific, severe consequence in plain language
- Establish the immediacy (this is happening now or could happen soon)
- Make the path from inaction to consequence feel short and clear

**Copy angle**: "If [specific action is not taken] in the next [timeframe], [specific serious consequence]. [Product] is how you prevent that."

**Warning**: Survival copy is the most emotionally manipulative category. Reserve for genuine threats. Manufactured survival urgency is immediately recognized as manipulative and irreparably damages trust.

---

### 15. Acceptance

**Core driver**: The need to belong, to be included, and to be seen as a legitimate member of a group.

**When it drives purchase**: When the buyer wants to be part of a community, movement, or group they identify with. Acceptance is the emotion behind community products, professional certifications, and anything that grants membership in a desirable group.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Describe the community and its members in aspirational terms
- Make it clear who belongs and who this is for
- Show the community as active and valuable

**Copy angle**: "The people inside [community/product] are [specific compelling description]. If you see yourself in that description, you belong here."

---

### 16. Happiness

**Core driver**: The desire for sustained emotional wellbeing -- not just moments of joy, but the reduction of ongoing anxiety, frustration, and negativity.

**When it drives purchase**: When the buyer is in a chronic state of stress, frustration, or overwhelm and wants relief. Happiness in this context is as much about what is removed (anxiety, complexity, difficulty) as what is added.

**How to trigger it in copy**:
- Name the specific ongoing frustration the buyer lives with
- Show what life looks and feels like without it
- Frame the product as relief and simplification, not just addition

**Copy angle**: "What would your [day/week/work/relationship] feel like if [specific frustration] was no longer there? [Product] removes it."

## Part 2: Life Force 8 (The Cashvertising Framework)

Drew Eric Whitman's Cashvertising framework distinguishes between two categories of human desire: primal drives that cannot be conditioned away, and learned wants that can be satisfied by cultural alternatives.

**The most powerful copy connects to Life Force 8.** Learned Wants are real but secondary -- they rationalize a purchase; they rarely initiate one.

### Life Force 8: Primal, Hardwired Drives

| Drive | Marketing Application | Highest-Leverage Category |
|---|---|---|
| Survival, enjoyment of life, life extension | Products that extend health, energy, or longevity | Health, fitness, insurance |
| Enjoyment of food and beverages | Sensory pleasure, taste, indulgence | Food and beverage, lifestyle |
| Freedom from fear, pain, and danger | Safety, relief, protection | Security, health, financial |
| Sexual companionship | Attractiveness, desirability, intimacy | Personal care, dating, fitness |
| Comfortable living conditions | Ease, warmth, convenience, reduced friction | Home, software, services |
| To be superior and winning | Status relative to peers | Luxury, professional tools, vehicles |
| Care and protection of loved ones | Family safety and wellbeing | Insurance, health, education |
| Social approval | Belonging, respect, being liked | Community, personal development |

"Care and protection of loved ones" (#7) is among the strongest emotional triggers available across all categories. Health, insurance, and family products that fail to activate this drive are underperforming.

**Status (#6) and Social Approval (#8)** are the highest-leverage drives in commercial marketing for most non-health, non-family products. Most purchasing decisions in professional, luxury, and personal development categories map to these two drives.

### 9 Learned Human Wants: Secondary Motivators

These rationalize purchase decisions. They rarely initiate them. Copy that leads with learned wants often performs below potential. Use them to justify the purchase after the primal drive has been activated.

1. To be informed (education, knowledge, staying current)
2. Curiosity (the pull of the novel and unknown)
3. Cleanliness of body and surroundings
4. Efficiency (more output with less input)
5. Convenience (lower friction, faster access)
6. Dependability and quality (reliability over time)
7. Expression of beauty and style
8. Economy and profit (smart use of money)
9. Bargains (paying less than something is worth)

**Bargains** are a Learned Want, not a primal drive. Competing on price alone is fighting on the weakest terrain. A product that connects to Life Force 8 can charge a premium over one that only satisfies Learned Wants.

## Part 3: The 8 Psychological Motivations (with Copy Templates)

These 8 motivations, from across the transcript set, represent the bridge between the emotional drives above and the copy templates that activate them.

### 1. Control
**What it is**: The desire to stop feeling powerless over a specific problem.
**Copy templates**:
- "Take back control of your [specific problem] with [product]."
- "Here is how [product] puts you in charge of your [desired outcome]."

### 2. Belonging
**What it is**: The desire to be part of a community that shares your values and understands you.
**Copy templates**:
- "Be part of the [community name] and connect with people who share your values."
- "Join [number] others who have already made the switch and are [specific outcome]."

### 3. Status
**What it is**: The desire to be seen differently -- as more successful, sophisticated, or capable.
**Copy templates**:
- "Upgrade your [specific context] with [product] and stand apart from [peer group]."
- "Here is how [product] can help you reach the next level of [specific thing]."

### 4. Security
**What it is**: The need to feel that a purchase decision is safe and protected.
**Copy templates**:
- "With [product], you are making the smart, secure choice for your [specific concern]."
- "[Product] has been trusted by [number] customers for [timeframe]. Your decision is safe."

### 5. Habit
**What it is**: The desire to upgrade or improve a behavior the buyer already practices.
**Copy templates**:
- "Already doing [common behavior]? Here is how [product] makes it even better."
- "Make [desired outcome] part of your daily routine with [product]."

### 6. Instant Gratification
**What it is**: The desire for fast results. Speed of outcome is a selling point.
**Copy templates**:
- "See results with [product] in just [short timeframe]."
- "Get [specific benefit] today. Start using [product] immediately."

### 7. Regret Aversion
**What it is**: The anticipatory fear of looking back and wishing you had acted.
**Copy templates**:
- "Do not be the person who looks back in [timeframe] wishing they had started today."
- "You are making the best choice for [specific problem] today and in the future."

### 8. Validation
**What it is**: The need for external confirmation from peers or authorities before committing.
**Copy templates**:
- "Join [number] satisfied customers who trust [product] for [specific benefit]."
- "Here is why [influential person or respected group] recommends [product]."

## Part 4: Emotional Arc by Funnel Stage

Different emotions motivate action at different stages of the buyer's journey. Mismatching the emotion to the stage is one of the most common conversion failures.

### Awareness Stage
**Emotional state**: Largely unaware of a solution, or dimly aware of a problem. The buyer is not looking for you.

**Target emotions**: Curiosity, Surprise, Resonance (the "that's exactly me" feeling)

**Approach**: Surface the problem in terms the buyer already experiences. Use the language of their daily frustration, not your product's terminology. The goal is a pattern interrupt that makes them think "wait, that's my situation."

**Example emotion-to-angle**: "If you feel overwhelmed by [specific common situation], this is why -- and it is not your fault."

---

### Consideration Stage
**Emotional state**: Problem-aware, solution-scanning. The buyer is looking but has not committed to a category yet.

**Target emotions**: Hope, Curiosity, Mild Fear (of choosing wrong), Trust-building

**Approach**: Show that you understand their specific problem better than anyone else. Build trust before building desire. The buyer at this stage is evaluating whether to trust you, not whether to trust the category.

**Example emotion-to-angle**: "Here is exactly what is causing [specific problem], and the three approaches that get tried -- and why only one of them actually works."

---

### Decision Stage
**Emotional state**: Solution-aware and evaluating your specific product against alternatives.

**Target emotions**: Confidence, Loss Aversion, Fear (of making the wrong choice), Regret Aversion

**Approach**: Remove the risk of the decision. Guarantee. Proof. Social validation. Address the specific objections that create hesitation. Make the no-action path feel more costly than the action path.

**Example emotion-to-angle**: "Here is what happens if you choose [product]: [outcome]. Here is what happens if you don't: [specific ongoing cost of inaction]."

---

### Post-Purchase Stage
**Emotional state**: Potentially experiencing buyer's remorse or uncertain whether the product will deliver on its promise.

**Target emotions**: Pride (they made a smart choice), Excitement (anticipation of outcomes), Belonging (they are now part of something)

**Approach**: Validate the decision immediately. Welcome them. Show them what others inside have achieved. Give them a quick win that proves value early. The first 14 days determine whether a buyer stays or churns.

**Example emotion-to-angle**: "Welcome to [product/community]. Here is your first win: [specific quick action with a fast, tangible result]."

## Part 5: The Emotion-to-Copy-Angle Translation Guide

Use this when you know what your customer is feeling and need to identify the right copy angle.

| If your customer feels... | Lead with this copy angle |
|---|---|
| Overwhelmed and out of control | Power/Control: "Here is how [product] puts you back in charge" |
| Afraid of a specific consequence | Fear + Relief: "Here is what happens if [threat], and here is how to prevent it" |
| Frustrated that others don't recognize their effort | Recognition/Revenge: "Here is how you make your work impossible to ignore" |
| Jealous of others who have what they want | FOMO/Status: "Here is exactly what they are doing that you are not -- yet" |
| Embarrassed by a problem they have not fixed | Security + Non-judgment: "Most [buyers] feel this way. Here is the fix." |
| Skeptical that anything works | Story Bias + Social Proof: Lead with a story from someone exactly like them |
| Excited about a possibility but uncertain about risk | Confidence + Risk Reversal: "Here is the proof, and here is the guarantee" |
| Ready to invest and committed | Fortune + Pride: "Here is what this looks like when it compounds" |
| Lonely or disconnected | Belonging + Acceptance: "Here is the community of people who understand this" |
| Guilty about not having taken action sooner | Regret Reframe: "Starting now is better than having started then. Here is your first step." |

## Part 6: Fear vs. Aspiration -- When to Use Each

### Fear Copy: Use When
- The prospect does not yet feel urgency about the problem
- The consequences of inaction are real and specific
- The buyer is more motivated by avoiding loss than by achieving gain
- The threat is underappreciated or ignored

Fear opens a door. It creates urgency where none existed. It motivates people who would not otherwise act.

**Fear copy structure**:
1. Identify the specific threat in vivid, specific terms
2. Show it is real, happening now, and affects people like them
3. Show the consequence of ignoring it
4. Pivot to the solution as protection/prevention
5. Close on aspiration: the protected, improved state

### Aspiration Copy: Use When
- The prospect is already motivated and looking for direction
- The buyer is more energized by positive outcomes than by avoiding negative ones
- The purchase is associated with positive identity or achievement
- Fear framing would feel tone-deaf or condescending in context

**Aspiration copy structure**:
1. Paint the desired future state in specific, vivid terms
2. Show who is living that state already
3. Show the specific path from here to there
4. Address risk briefly (guarantee, proof)
5. Close with the action that starts the journey

### The Fear-to-Aspiration Arc
The most effective single-piece copy moves through fear into aspiration:

1. **Open with the problem** (activates fear and urgency)
2. **Empathize with the situation** (builds trust, removes isolation)
3. **Agitate the consequences** (deepens urgency)
4. **Introduce the solution** (relief begins)
5. **Show the aspirational outcome** (close on hope and possibility)
6. **Prove it and remove risk** (conviction)
7. **CTA** (action from positive emotional state)

The reader enters through fear (this is my problem) and exits through aspiration (this is my future). The journey from one to the other is the emotional arc of the copy.

## Part 7: The Emotional Resonance Test

Before publishing any piece of copy, run this five-question test:

**1. Does it feel like "they wrote this for me"?**
Read the copy as the target buyer. Does the opening paragraph mirror their specific situation -- their specific frustration, their specific context, their specific language? If it feels generic, it needs more specificity.

**2. Does it create a visceral response in the first 30 seconds?**
Read the first three sentences aloud. Do they create any feeling -- recognition, curiosity, urgency, relief? If the first 30 seconds create no emotional response, the opening is not working.

**3. Does it name the specific emotion the buyer is feeling?**
Review the copy for specific emotion words and emotion-activating situations. Does it name what the buyer feels ("frustrated that," "afraid that," "proud of," "excited about")? Copy that talks around emotions rather than naming them creates less resonance than copy that names them directly.

**4. Does it make inaction feel more costly than action?**
Read the copy as a skeptical buyer who defaults to no. Is there a specific, calculable cost to not buying? Or does the copy only describe the benefits of buying? Both sides of the decision need to be present for the emotional arc to complete.

**5. Does it end on a positive emotional state?**
The emotional state the reader is in when they reach the CTA significantly affects conversion. Ending on fear (the last emotion is anxiety) is less effective than ending on confidence or aspiration (the last emotion is excitement or conviction). The CTA should feel like the logical next step of a positive journey, not the escape hatch from a threatening situation.
