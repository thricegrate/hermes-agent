# Kennedy High-Ticket Sales Framework

Dan Kennedy's direct response principles for premium pricing, authority positioning, and high-ticket closing. These layer on top of the existing Diagnose-Prescribe-Close methodology.

---

## Premium Pricing: The 9 Failures (Framework 15)

Nine ways businesses destroy their ability to charge premium prices. Every one of these is a revenue ceiling in disguise.

### 1. Obsessing Over Competitors' Prices
Their cost structure, their positioning, their margins are all different from yours. Looking at what others charge tells you nothing about what YOU should charge.

### 2. Attracting Price-Only Buyers
If the main reason they bought was price, the main reason they'll leave is price. Discount buyers are the most expensive customers: highest refund rates, most support tickets, least likely to buy the back end.

### 3. No Premium Option
Always offer a top tier. Some buyers want the best available, period. If your highest option is $3K and someone would have paid $15K, you just left $12K on the table because you didn't ask.

### 4. Discounting Without Reason
Random discounts train your audience to wait. Every surprise sale teaches them "the real price is lower, I just need to be patient." Only discount with a clear, time-bound reason tied to a specific action.

### 5. Not Showing Value Before Price
If they see "$5,000" before they understand what they're getting, they anchor on cost. If they understand the transformation first, they anchor on value. Sequence matters enormously.

### 6. Assuming Price Sensitivity
Most markets have a segment that will pay 3-5x more for the premium version. You haven't found them because you haven't offered them anything worth 3-5x more.

### 7. Competing on Price
The race to the bottom has one winner, and they barely make money. Every dollar you cut from the price comes directly from your ability to deliver results, which creates a death spiral.

### 8. Apologizing for Price
If you present the price like it's bad news, the prospect will treat it like bad news. State the price with confidence. Then shut up. The first person to speak after the price is revealed loses.

### 9. Linking Price to Time, Not Value
"$200/hour" caps your earnings at your available hours. "$5,000 for the outcome" doesn't. Price the transformation, not the time.

### Context Controls Price

Kennedy's insight: the same thing sells for dramatically different prices depending on context.

**A PDF** for $7. **An "Implementation Kit"** with the same PDF plus templates, a checklist, and a video walkthrough for $197. Same core content. 28x the price. The difference is context, packaging, and perceived value.

For high-ticket:
- Premium environments (private Slack, curated community, exclusive events)
- Premium delivery (1-on-1 calls, personalized audits, custom strategies)
- Premium positioning (application required, limited spots, interview process)

### Marketing to Affluent Buyers

Affluent buyers have different psychology. They don't respond to "save money" messaging. They respond to:

- **Aspiration:** "Join the founders who..."
- **Status:** "By invitation only"
- **Exclusivity:** "Limited to 12 members per cohort"
- **Speed:** "Skip the line. Get results in weeks, not months."
- **Proof of peers:** "Here's who else is in the room" (names, companies, results)

The difficulty of access itself becomes a selling point. Being hard to reach increases perceived value. Easy access = commodity. Restricted access = premium.

## Authority & Celebrity Expert Positioning (Framework 12)

Kennedy's authority hierarchy determines how much you can charge and how easily you can close.

### The 4 Levels

**Level 1: Generalist.** "I help businesses grow." No differentiation. Commodity pricing. You compete on price.

**Level 2: Expert.** "I help newsletter operators monetize their lists." Narrower positioning. You can charge more because you're solving a specific problem for a specific audience.

**Level 3: Published Author.** "I wrote the book on newsletter monetization." A book (physical, digital, or both) is still the single most powerful authority accelerator. It doesn't need to be a bestseller. It needs to exist.

**Level 4: Celebrity + Expert.** "I'm the AI automation guy. You've seen me at [conferences], read my book, and used my free tools." This is where you can charge 5-10x what a generalist charges for the same outcome.

### Moving Up the Hierarchy

Each level unlocks higher prices and easier closes:

| Level | Close Difficulty | Typical Pricing Power |
|-------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Generalist | Hard (selling against everyone) | Market rate |
| Expert | Moderate (niche credibility) | 2-3x market rate |
| Published Author | Easier (instant credibility) | 3-5x market rate |
| Celebrity Expert | Easiest (they come to you) | 5-10x+ market rate |

### The "WITH" Strategy

Proximity transfers credibility. If you can't yet claim celebrity expert status, borrow it.

- **Speak at events** where your target market gathers
- **Guest on podcasts** your prospects listen to
- **Get endorsed** by people your market already trusts
- **Co-create content** with recognized names

Each "WITH" moment accelerates your climb up the hierarchy.

### Difficulty of Access

Kennedy's counterintuitive principle: being hard to reach increases perceived value.

**Apply this to your sales process:**
- Application required before booking a call
- Qualifying questions that filter out bad fits
- Limited call slots per week (real limits)
- Waitlist for programs at capacity
- "We only take on X new clients per quarter"

This isn't pretending to be busy. This is being selective about who you work with. And that selectivity makes the people who DO qualify feel special.

## The 9 Trust Questions Prospects Silently Ask

Every prospect on a high-ticket call is silently evaluating you against these 9 questions. Address each one proactively, don't wait for them to ask.

1. **"Can I trust you?"** Build rapport. Be genuine. Share a damaging admission early.

2. **"Do you understand my specific situation?"** Repeat their situation back to them more clearly than they described it. This is the single most powerful trust move on a call.

3. **"Do you have proof this works?"** Case studies that mirror their situation. Not generic testimonials. Specific, relevant results.

4. **"What makes you different from everyone else selling this?"** Your unique mechanism. Your proprietary process. Your specific advantage.

5. **"Is this worth the money?"** Value build before price. ROI math. Cost of inaction.

6. **"What if it doesn't work for me?"** Guarantee. Risk reversal. "What's the worst that happens?"

7. **"What will other people think?"** Social proof. "Who else has done this?" Names, companies, logos.

8. **"Will this be too hard for me?"** Simplify the process. "Here's exactly what happens in Week 1, Week 2, Week 3." Make it feel doable.

9. **"Do I really need this right now?"** Cost of inaction. "What happens if nothing changes in 6 months?" Create temporal urgency.

**On the call:** Use the discovery phase to answer questions 1-3. Use the prescription phase to answer 4-8. Use the close to answer 9.

## Take-Away Selling for High-Ticket (Framework 7)

Kennedy's take-away selling is the opposite of begging for the sale. You position the offer as something they might not qualify for.

### How to Use It

**Before the call:** Application process. Qualifying questions. "We only accept clients who [criteria]."

**On the call:** "Based on what you've told me, I need to be honest. This program isn't for everyone. Let me ask a few more questions to see if it's actually a fit for your situation."

**During the close:** "I want to make sure this is right for you. If it's not, I'd rather tell you now than have you invest and not get results."

**After hesitation:** "Look, this might not be the right time for you. And that's completely fine. I'd rather work with someone when they're 100% ready."

### Why It Works

The moment you're willing to walk away from the sale, the prospect's psychology shifts. You stop being a salesperson and start being an advisor. Advisors are trusted. Salespeople are resisted.

Take-away selling also filters out bad clients. The person who responds to "this might not be for you" with "no, I want in" is a better client than the one you had to convince.

## Kennedy High-Ticket Checklist

Add to every high-ticket sales process review:

- [ ] Authority level identified (generalist through celebrity expert)
- [ ] Plan to move up at least one level in next 90 days
- [ ] Pricing based on value/outcome, not time
- [ ] Premium context created (application, limited spots, exclusive access)
- [ ] All 9 trust questions addressed in the sales process
- [ ] Take-away selling language in the script
- [ ] Value build happens before price reveal
- [ ] No price apologizing (stated with confidence, then silence)
- [ ] Guarantee is a selling tool, not fine print
