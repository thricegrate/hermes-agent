# High-Ticket Closing Framework

Based on the Automatic Clients methodology (Sultanic & Neckelius) and consultative
selling principles. This framework treats the sales call as a diagnosis, not a pitch.

---

## The Diagnose-Prescribe-Close Model

The core principle: You are a doctor, not a salesperson. Doctors don't convince
patients they're sick. They ask questions, identify the problem, and prescribe
a solution. If the patient doesn't follow the prescription, that's their choice.

### Why this works for high-ticket:
- Removes "selling" pressure from both sides
- Positions you as the expert (authority frame)
- Makes the prospect sell themselves on the solution
- Naturally disqualifies bad fits before the close

---

## The 3 Levels of Customers

Every prospect falls into one of three categories. Your backend should ideally
serve all three at different price points:

### Level 1: DIY (Do It Yourself)
- **Profile**: Has time, limited budget, wants to learn
- **What they buy**: Courses, templates, tools ($97-$997)
- **Your role**: Provide information and resources
- **Served by**: Frontend products (already purchased)

### Level 2: DWY (Done With You)
- **Profile**: Has some time and budget, wants guidance
- **What they buy**: Group coaching, masterminds, mentorship ($3K-$10K)
- **Your role**: Guide implementation, answer questions, provide accountability
- **Served by**: Group programs, coaching containers

### Level 3: DFY (Done For You)
- **Profile**: Has budget, limited time, wants results fast
- **What they buy**: Agency services, 1:1 implementation ($10K-$35K+)
- **Your role**: Execute the work on their behalf
- **Served by**: Service packages, retainers, project engagements

### Key insight from AC:
Your frontend product naturally segments customers into these levels. The ones
who buy the frontend, get results, and want MORE are your Level 2/3 prospects.
The ones who buy and never use it are not your target for backend (and that's fine).

## Objection Response Framework

For every objection, follow the **LAER** model:

1. **Listen**: Let them finish completely. Don't interrupt or get defensive.
2. **Acknowledge**: "I totally understand. A lot of our clients felt the same way before they started."
3. **Explore**: "Can I ask what specifically concerns you about [the objection]?"
4. **Respond**: Address the ROOT concern, not the surface objection.

### The 7 Universal High-Ticket Objections

#### 1. "It's too expensive" / "I can't afford it"
**Real concern**: They don't see the ROI clearly enough.
**Reframe**: "I understand. Let me ask you this: if we get you [specific result]
in [timeframe], what would that be worth to your business? ... So the question
isn't whether you can afford [price], it's whether you can afford NOT to solve
[their problem] for another 6-12 months."
**Proof**: Share a case study with specific ROI numbers.
**Payment plan**: Offer a split payment if budget is genuinely the barrier.

#### 2. "I need to think about it"
**Real concern**: They haven't been given enough information, or there's an
unspoken objection.
**Reframe**: "Of course. What specifically do you need to think about? Is it
the investment, the time commitment, or something else?" (This surfaces the
real objection.)
**If genuine**: "Totally fair. What would help you make this decision? I can
send you [specific resource]. Let's schedule a 10-minute follow-up for [2 days
from now] so I can answer any questions that come up."

#### 3. "I need to talk to my partner/spouse"
**Real concern**: Either genuine (respect this) or a stalling tactic.
**Reframe**: "Completely understand. Would it help if we scheduled a quick call
with both of you so I can answer their questions directly?"
**Alternative**: "What do you think their main concern will be? Let me help you
address that so you can have a productive conversation."

#### 4. "How do I know this will work for ME?"
**Real concern**: Self-doubt or past failures with similar programs.
**Reframe**: "Great question. Let me share [client name]'s story, who was in
almost the exact same situation as you... [specific case study]. The difference
isn't the program, it's the implementation support. That's exactly why we do
[specific support element]."
**Proof**: Match case study to their specific situation.

#### 5. "I've seen similar programs for less"
**Real concern**: They don't understand the differentiation.
**Reframe**: "You probably have. The question is whether those programs got
their students [specific result]. What happened when you tried them?" (Most
have either tried cheaper options that didn't work, or they're just comparing
price without comparing outcomes.)
**Key point**: Compete on economics, not price. The ROI matters, not the cost.

#### 6. "I don't think I can do this"
**Real concern**: Imposter syndrome, fear of failure.
**Reframe**: "I appreciate your honesty. Can I share something? [Client name]
said the exact same thing on their first call. Here's what happened... The reason
I'm not worried is because you've already [specific achievement from their
frontend experience]. That tells me you have what it takes."
**Key point**: Reflect their own evidence of capability back to them.

#### 7. "I've tried something like this before and it didn't work"
**Real concern**: They're protecting themselves from being burned again.
**Reframe**: "I'm glad you brought that up. What specifically didn't work about
it? ... That's actually a really common problem with [competitor approach]. Here's
how what we do is different: [specific differentiator]. And here's our guarantee:
[guarantee details]."
**Key point**: Diagnose WHY it failed, then show how your approach addresses that specific failure point.

## Red Flag Screening Guide

### Hard disqualifiers (do NOT close these people):

1. **Blame deflectors**: "Every program I've tried was a scam" / "My last coach
   was terrible" / "The market is rigged." If they never take responsibility,
   they'll blame you too. Guaranteed refund request or chargeback.

2. **Can't-afford-it closers**: They want to put it on a credit card they can't
   pay off, borrow from family, or skip essential bills. Selling to someone who
   genuinely can't afford it creates a lose-lose: they'll resent you and demand
   a refund.

3. **No concrete plan**: They want the program but can't articulate what they'd
   actually DO with it. "I just want to make money online" without any direction
   is a red flag. They need at minimum a niche, a skill, or a service.

4. **Price negotiators**: "Can you do it for less?" / "What if I just do half
   the program?" Constantly trying to change your terms signals they don't value
   what you offer.

5. **Chronic consumers**: They've bought 10+ courses/programs and completed none.
   The issue isn't information, it's implementation. Unless your program has
   heavy accountability (which adds cost), they'll do the same thing.

### Soft yellow flags (proceed with caution):

- Unrealistic timeline expectations ("I want $10K/month in 30 days")
- Comparing you to free YouTube content
- Asking for guarantees that aren't possible ("guarantee I'll make $X")
- Spouse/partner opposition
- Currently in another coaching program

### How to decline gracefully:
"Based on what you've shared, I don't think this is the right fit for you right
now, and I wouldn't feel right taking your money. Here's what I'd recommend
instead: [genuinely helpful alternative]. If things change in the future,
my door is always open."

This actually INCREASES your authority and generates referrals. People respect
being turned away when it's genuine.

## Follow-Up Cadence

### Post-call (didn't close):

| Day | Channel | Content |
|-----|---------|---------|
| 0 (2hrs) | Email | Personal recap of their situation + "one thing I kept thinking about after our call" |
| 1 | Email | Value bomb addressing their #1 pain point |
| 3 | Email | Case study of someone in a similar situation |
| 5 | Email/SMS | "Checking in" + address the specific objection they raised |
| 7 | Email | Final direct pitch with deadline (if applicable) |
| 14+ | Email | Weekly value, no pitch for 30 days |
| 45 | Email | Soft re-engagement ("A lot has changed since we spoke...") |

### No-show cadence:

| Timing | Channel | Content |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Call time | SMS | "Hey [name], I'm on the call. Want me to wait a few?" |
| +1 hour | Email | "No worries if something came up. Rebook link" |
| +1 day | Email | Value piece + rebook link |
| +3 days | SMS | "Still interested? Here's what we would've covered" |
| +7 days | Email | Final attempt, then move to general nurture |

### Key principles:
- Never guilt-trip or pressure. "Kill them with kindness and value."
- Every follow-up delivers genuine value, not just "checking in"
- If they go cold after 7 days, stop direct outreach and move to nurture
- Some will buy 30, 60, 90 days later. Stay top-of-mind with value.

## Conversion Velocity in High-Ticket

The speed at which a prospect moves from "interested" to "closed" depends on:

1. **Pre-call education**: How much awareness bridge content have they consumed?
   More educated prospects close faster and with fewer objections.

2. **Proof density**: How many relevant case studies can you share? The more
   "people like me" success stories, the faster the close.

3. **Urgency**: Is there a genuine reason to decide now? (Limited spots,
   cohort start date, price increase, their business pain is time-sensitive)

4. **Trust gap**: How much do they trust you already from frontend + content?
   Prospects who've consumed 5+ value bombs close 2-3x faster than cold leads.

The AC model is designed to MAXIMIZE pre-call education so the sales call
is a formality, not a persuasion session. If you're spending most of the call
convincing, the awareness bridge needs work.
