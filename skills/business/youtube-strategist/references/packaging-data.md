# Packaging Data: Titles + Thumbnails

Complete data reference for YouTube packaging decisions. All data from Richard YTS 300K+ outlier study, 1of10media blog, Jake Thomas (Creator Hooks), and 10xCreator 3M video study.

---

## Title Data

### Length
- 5-6 words / 30-40 characters = maximum median views (Richard, 300K study)
- 10+ words: median views "fall off a cliff"
- ~30 characters: ~65K median views
- ~70 characters: ~40K median views (62% drop)
- 70-100 characters = highest absolute performance (10xCreator, 3M study)
- Resolution: front-load hook in first 5-6 words/40 chars, total can be longer if needed

### 3 Drivers of Attention
1. **Curiosity** (appears in 61% of top titles): create information gap the brain must close
2. **Desire** (46%): sell the result so they want the process
3. **Fear** (40%): help avoid threats, ease concerns
- Most effective titles combine 2 drivers (curiosity + fear, curiosity + desire)

### Sentiment and Framing
- Negative framing = +22% more views than positive
- High readability (Flesch score) = +20% view boost
- Active verbs > passive: Snuck, Controlled, Challenged, Trapped > Reviewing, Discussing, Playing
- Open loops > complete thoughts

### Numbers
- 35% of viral titles use numbers
- But no-number titles average 11% more views overall
- When using numbers: odd numbers > round numbers
- Extreme specificity adds credibility: "$47,000" > "a lot of money"

### Power Words
- Qualifier adjectives raise stakes: "7 Dangerous Mistakes" > "7 Mistakes"
- Emotional triggers that lift CTR: SHOCKING, BRUTAL, MINDBLOWING, SUBTLE, EXTREME, TERRIFYING
- Case: Jesse Enkamp discovered "TERRIFYING" via 1of10. Every video with it wins A/B tests, hits 1.2M-1.3M views

### Unique Angles / Trending Power Words (Sam Goday)
- Certain words catch fire on YouTube then saturate. Ride the wave early, stop before oversaturation
- Recent wave examples: "illegal" ("so productive it feels illegal"), "brutal" ("40 brutal truths"), "dangerously" ("dangerously motivated"), "cheating" ("feels like cheating")
- "Brutal" outperformed "harsh" in Dan Martell's 40 truths video. More visceral = more clickable
- Always look for the NEXT version of these words. Once everyone copies a word, it loses power
- The word must be authentic to the creator. Dan swears naturally, so "brutal" fits. Don't force words that clash with your brand

### Censored Swear Words in Thumbnails
- Censored swear words (e.g., "F*CK IT" or blurred text) draw the eye in thumbnails
- Works when authentic to the creator's brand. Use with taste, not on every video
- Sam Goday: "It's also what resonates with your brand. Dan swears a lot, so we show it."

### 3 Title Variation Methods
1. Question <> Strong Statement flip
2. Change the words: "I tested" > "I tried", "$1 vs $1,000" > "Level 1 vs Level 1000"
3. Add a power word: shocking, brutal, mind-blowing, subtle, extreme

### Richard's 12 Things to Consider (Title Infographic)
1. Fewer than 50 characters
2. Front-load the most impactful information
3. Easy to read: avoid complex word stacks; break with punctuation; use capitalization for vibe; low reading level
4. Hook validation: does your hook deliver on the title?
5. Does content deliver on the promise? (Viewers punish exaggeration in titles more than thumbnails)
6. Matches audience style (casual vs serious)
7. Build trust/authority (especially small education channels)
8. Sell the result, not the process (education)
9. Retention-based structures ("Level 1 vs Level 1000")
10. Title + thumbnail synergy: build on each other
11. Open loops: does the combo make viewers ask a question?
12. Maximize desired emotions (fear, intrigue, desire)

### Ethical Clickbait
- Zone 1 (Vanilla): truthful but boring
- Zone 2 (Sweet Spot): most extreme truthful version. Target this
- Zone 3 (Danger): stretching truth. Damages trust
- Zone 4 (Fraud): outright lies. Destroys channel

4 stackable attention frameworks: Negative Bias (+22%), Urgency, Authority, Extreme Specificity

Super Stimulus:
- 1 lever = ~1K views
- 2 levers = ~50K views
- 3 levers = 500K+ views

### AI-Era Title Trends (2025+, Ed Lawrence / Film Booth)

Educational viewers now arrive pre-informed by AI. These title patterns work because they signal NEW information, not the same advice AI already gave them:

**Dying title patterns:**
- "X Mistakes You're Making" (used to be guaranteed views, now flop consistently)
- "Why You Can't [do thing]" (viewers already know why from AI)
- Generic how-to that AI answers faster

**Rising title patterns (with view data from screenshots):**
- **Personal transformation:** "How I [Past Tense Verb] Myself to [Result]"
  - "How I Transformed Myself and Built My Dream Life" (1.1M, 1 year ago)
  - "How I Brainwashed Myself to Success" (1.9M, 3 years ago, still trending format)
- **Age-based urgency:** "I'm [age]. If you're in your [decade]s, [watch/read] this:"
  - "I'm 45. If you're in your 30s watch this:" (2.7M, 6 months ago)
- **Time-bound promises:** "[X] Months/Days Is All You Need"
  - "6 Months Is All You Need" (1.1M, 1 year ago)
- **Provocative/contrarian:** Direct challenge to common beliefs
  - "F*ck Your Feelings" (1.9M, framed as pattern interruption)
  - "Stop Wasting Your Life" (2.8M, 1 year ago)
- **Quit/stop lists:** "X Things I Quit to Go From [Bad State] to [Good State]"
  - "Stop Wasting Your Life - 7 Things I Quit to Go From Broke to Millionaire" (2.8M)
- **Authority + lesson:** "[Credential/Experience]. Here's what I learned."
  - "Everything I Learned at MIT (That Made Me a Multimillionaire)" (969K, 4 months ago)

**Pattern:** All top performers are personal stories or contrarian takes. None are generic how-to.

### Title Change Case Studies
- "The dopamine ladder trick that gets viewers addicted" > "How to become a storytelling genius" = 10K to 150K views (title change only)
- One channel: packaging refresh = 5x total views in 1 month

## Thumbnail Data

### Core Principles
- 3 elements max, understood in 0.5 seconds (the glance test)
- Left-to-right story structure: Before > Arrow > After (brain reads thumbnails like a story)
- 0.5-second rule: simple > pretty/detailed/cinematic
- Thumbnail comes FIRST in production (not last). It dictates what to shoot

### Composition + Elements Framework
- **Composition**: structure and layout (person + weapon + object being destroyed)
- **Elements**: specific things within that composition
- Steal composition from outliers. Swap elements for your niche
- Can take composition from anywhere: anime scenes, movie posters, ads

### The Complement Rule (Sam Goday)
- The thumbnail must COMPLEMENT the title, not REPEAT it
- **Bad:** Title says "How to get rich in 2026" + thumbnail text says "GET RICH" = lazy repetition
- **Good:** Title says "How to get rich in 2026" + thumbnail shows person with specific visual metaphor that adds context
- The viewer should understand the concept MORE deeply by seeing the thumbnail, not just read the title again
- Mr. Beast and Dan Martell thumbnails always add information beyond the title
- Sam's formula: 3 elements for educational space = big face + big text + icon/element (background or prop)
- But the text should never just restate the title. It should add a new angle or emotion

### Color Data
- Cyan: +36% views (strongest performing color)
- Green, yellow/orange: perform well
- Brightness peak: 100-110 grayscale
- Bold colors including black and white
- High contrast between elements (color, texture, background)

### Face Data
- Multiple faces > single face: +35-50% CTR
- Sad faces: highest average views (2.3M)
- Surprise expression: +49% CTR
- Faces + emotion = trust/authority + curiosity

### Text Data
- 84% of viral thumbnails include text
- But text reduces views by ~19% on average
- When using text: under 10 characters, less than 7% of thumbnail area
- Only use text if it adds new information (not restating the title)
- Legible at all sizes. 5th-grade reading level

### Dark Mode
- 80% of YouTube users use dark mode
- Always test thumbnails against dark backgrounds

### 10 Iconic Thumbnail Types
1. Big Bold Colorful: bright colors, large text, high energy
2. Raw: unfiltered, authentic, documentary feel
3. Before/After: transformation structure
4. Products: product lineup or showcase
5. Numbers: large number as focal point
6. Scary: horror, danger, shock
7. Reactions: facial expressions as the story
8. Nostalgic: retro feel, throwback
9. Quotes: text-heavy with a compelling quote
10. Comments: featuring audience comments

### Richard's 15 Rules for Better Thumbnails
1. Thumbnail comes first (not last)
2. Pretty doesn't win clicks. Understandability does
3. Steal from outliers
4. Design for instant understanding
5. Complexity kills clicks
6. Remove until obvious
7. Limit to ~3 core elements
8. Thumbnail and title: same story, not same words
9. Use contrast to stop the scroll
10. If it looks fake, it feels fake
11. Only use text if it adds new information
12. Assume viewer never reads the title
13. CTR doesn't really matter long-term (drops naturally)
14. Judge by reach and views gained
15. Test, swap, repeat

### 23 Things to Consider
**Basics** (5): 3 main elements; bold colors; bright/high contrast; big/clear elements; high-quality assets
**Text** (2): legible at all sizes; 5th-grade reading level
**Other**: timestamp placement; mood/channel alignment; squint/glance test; pattern-break on homepage; open curiosity loop with title; clearer/smarter alternative?; don't spoil payoff; show action/movement; novelty; build authority/trust; check negative data

### AI-Era Thumbnail Trends (2025+)

Current high-performers in education thumbnails based on 1M+ view videos:

**Dominant patterns:**
- **Bold text overlays with high contrast:** Large, provocative statements in white/yellow on dark backgrounds
  - "STOP WASTING TIME" (2.7M), "DON'T WASTE YOUR LIFE" (2.8M), "F*CK YOUR FEELINGS" (1.9M)
  - Text is BIG, takes up 30-50% of the thumbnail (breaks the "under 7% area" rule but works because the text IS the hook)
- **Split bold text wrapping face:** Text split above and below the face
  - "DON'T [top half] WASTE [bottom half] YOUR LIFE" with face in center
  - "THINK LIKE [top] THE TOP 1% [bottom, highlighted]" with face offset
- **Face + strong emotion is non-negotiable** for education thumbnails. Every single 1M+ education video uses it
- **Single topic word + visual cue:** One word (e.g., "TESTOSTERONE") with an arrow or visual indicator overlaid on the face
- **Authority backdrops:** Recognizable institutions (MIT campus), impressive settings used to build instant credibility
- **Red, white, yellow/orange dominant** color palettes. Dark backgrounds with bright text
- **Color highlighting for emphasis:** Key words in different color (purple "THE TOP 1%" vs white surrounding text)

**What's NOT working:**
- Overly designed, polished thumbnails (too "professional" feels inauthentic)
- Thumbnails without faces (education niche specifically)
- Subtle, quiet thumbnails that don't interrupt the scroll
- Text that merely repeats the title

**Key shift:** Education thumbnails are borrowing from entertainment/motivation. Bold, provocative, high-energy text overlays that would have felt "clickbaity" 2 years ago are now standard for 1M+ education videos.

### 5-Phase Thumbnail Blueprint
1. Inspiration: brainstorm, research niches, external sources
2. Analyze: why did it work?
3. Remix: adapt for your channel
4. Apply: 23 Things checklist
5. Test: relentlessly on back catalog

### A/B Testing
- YouTube native Test and Compare: use watch time per impression (not CTR) as the metric
- TubeBuddy: alternative A/B testing tool
- Always test 3 radical variations (not subtle changes)

### Thumbnail Swap Case Studies
- JackSucksAtLife: thumbnail swap = 978% view increase
- 35K to 320K+ from thumbnail/title change
- 3K to 500K+ from retitling/re-thumbnailing
- 1K to 100K+
- 10s views/day to 2,000+/day from one swap
- One creator: 3M+ extra views from client packaging refresh

## Video Duration Data

| Niche | Optimal Duration |
|-------|-----------------|
| General | 20-25 min |
| Tech | 28 min |
| Engineering/DIY | 26 min |
| Gaming | 21 min |
| Vlogs | 14 min |
| 80+ min | Picks back up (YouTube TV push) |

Longer > shorter in general. YouTube rewards sustained attention, not shortness. "Value per minute" concept: as long as it stays high, longer videos = more total watch time = stronger recommendations.
