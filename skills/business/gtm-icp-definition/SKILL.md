# GTM ICP Definition

Build an Ideal Customer Profile from scratch, pressure-test an existing one, or sharpen targeting criteria that have drifted from reality. The ICP is the foundation everything else in GTM is built on -- if it's wrong, everything downstream is wrong too. Rerun every 6 months or after any significant product, pricing, or market change.

## Context Gathering

Before starting, collect from the user:

- **Current customer list** -- even a rough one. 10-20 existing customers is enough. If zero customers, ask for 5-10 dream-fit companies and why.
- **Best and worst customers** -- which are happiest, growing, easy to work with? Which churned or were painful?
- **What they sell** -- product/service, core use case, the problem solved.
- **Stage** -- pre-PMF, early traction, scaling, or mature? ICP definition looks different at each.

Without this, the output is a generic template. With it, you build a profile calibrated to actual customer reality.

## What This Skill Produces

A documented ICP with firmographic criteria, situational criteria, buying triggers, buying committee roles, explicit disqualifiers, observable signals, and a confidence level -- in a format every revenue team member can use and reference quickly.

## ICP Methodology

### Step 1 -- Establish the Purpose

An ICP is not a wish list. It is a pattern extracted from reality.

An ICP is used to:
- Prioritize which companies to pursue (and which to ignore)
- Guide outbound targeting and list building
- Score and qualify inbound leads quickly
- Align sales, marketing, and product on who the customer is
- Make resource allocation decisions

An ICP is not:
- A persona (that is a person, not a company)
- A TAM exercise (ICP is about fit quality, not market size)
- A permanent document (it should evolve as you learn)
- A consensus document (it should reflect reality, not make everyone feel included)

If the team has multiple conflicting views, that conflict should be surfaced and resolved -- not papered over.

### Step 2 -- Analyze Existing Customers

The fastest path to an accurate ICP is backward -- look at who already bought and succeeded.

For each current or past customer, collect:
- Company size (headcount and revenue if available)
- Industry / vertical
- Geography
- Business model (B2B, B2C, marketplace, SaaS, services, etc.)
- Tech stack (relevant tools they use)
- How they found you or why they bought
- Time to close
- Expansion or contraction since signing
- Health score or NPS if available
- Why they churned (if applicable)

Sort into three tiers:

**Tier A -- Best customers.** Bought fast, adopted fully, expanded, refer others, easy to support, clearly get value.
**Tier B -- Average customers.** Fine. Paid. Not churned. Not excited. Require normal effort.
**Tier C -- Bad customers.** Churned, struggling, never adopted, required disproportionate support, or the relationship is difficult.

Your ICP is built from Tier A only. Not from the average. Optimizing for the average means optimizing for mediocrity.

> If fewer than 5 Tier A customers, acknowledge that the ICP is a hypothesis -- not a validated pattern.

### Step 3 -- Find the Pattern in Tier A

Look across Tier A customers and identify what they have in common. Signal, not coincidence.

**Firmographic patterns (the "who"):**
- Consistent size range? (e.g., 50-200 employees, Series A-B)
- Concentrated in specific industries?
- Shared geography or language/market?
- Same business model or revenue structure?

**Situational patterns (the "when"):**
- What was happening when they bought? (rapid growth, leadership change, scaling pains)
- Specific stage of their own lifecycle?
- Consistent purchase catalyst?

**Behavioral patterns (the "how they buy"):**
- How did they find you? (inbound, outbound, referral, event)
- How long to buy?
- Who was involved in the decision?
- What made them choose you over alternatives?

**Value patterns (the "why it works"):**
- What outcome did they hire you to achieve?
- What metric improved?
- How do they describe the value to colleagues?

Look for 3-5 attributes appearing in 80%+ of Tier A. Those are your ICP criteria. Under 50% = nice-to-haves, not requirements.

### Step 4 -- Identify the Negative ICP

Knowing who is NOT your ICP is equally important. Prevents wasted cycles on companies that look right but are not.

From Tier C customers, extract:
- What firmographic traits did bad customers share?
- What was their situation when they bought? (oversold to, wrong use case, too early, too late)
- What was the tell visible before the sale -- that you ignored?
- What objections should have been disqualifying?

Common negative ICP signals:
- Too small to get real value (can't afford implementation, team too thin)
- Too large for current product maturity (needs enterprise features you don't have)
- Wrong vertical -- use case doesn't translate cleanly
- Wrong buying stage -- needed something before they could use you
- Wrong internal champion -- bought by someone without authority or budget

Document the negative ICP explicitly. "We do not sell to X" is a policy, not a failure.

### Step 5 -- Define the Buying Trigger

A company that fits your ICP perfectly but has no reason to buy right now is a future prospect, not a current one. The buying trigger separates the two.

A buying trigger is the specific condition that creates urgency. Situational, not firmographic.

**Types of buying triggers:**

| Trigger Type | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| Growth trigger | Scaling faster than current tools/processes can handle |
| Pain trigger | Problem has become acute enough that status quo is unacceptable |
| Event trigger | Funding, acquisition, leadership change, product launch, new market |
| Competitive trigger | Competitor gaining ground, need to respond |
| Compliance trigger | Regulatory or legal deadline forcing action |
| Budget trigger | New fiscal year, budget approved, or previous tool contract expiring |

Identify:
- Primary buying trigger (most common in Tier A)
- 1-2 secondary triggers
- What the trigger looks like from the outside -- the observable signal

> The observable signal turns your ICP into a targetable list. "Series B company in logistics that just hired a VP of Operations" is actionable. "Series B company in logistics" is a list.

### Step 6 -- Define the Buying Committee

ICP operates at the company level -- the buying committee operates at the person level.

For each deal, identify:

**Economic buyer** -- who approves budget? Their priorities? What business outcome do they care about?

**Champion** -- who wants this internally? Role, motivation, ability to influence?

**End user / evaluator** -- who uses it day-to-day? What does success look like for them?

**Influencer** -- who has input but not final authority? (IT, legal, procurement, a peer)

**Blocker** -- who might slow or kill the deal? What's their concern and what resolves it?

For each role, document:
- Typical title(s) at ICP companies
- What they care about (their job-to-be-done)
- What messaging resonates
- How they typically behave in an evaluation

The buying committee should reflect Tier A reality -- not org chart theory.

### Step 7 -- Pressure-Test the ICP

Before committing, validate.

**Test 1 -- The exclusion test**
Apply ICP criteria to current pipeline. Does it cleanly separate deals you're excited about from ones you're grinding on? If not, criteria aren't sharp enough.

**Test 2 -- The prediction test**
Pick 10 companies you haven't sold to. Apply criteria and predict which would be Tier A. If criteria don't produce confident predictions, they're too vague.

**Test 3 -- The consensus test**
Share ICP with sales, marketing, and CS. Do they all recognize it as accurate? If marketing says "yes" and sales says "that's not who we close," there's a gap. Resolve the conflict -- don't split the difference.

**Test 4 -- The list test**
Build a prospect list using only ICP criteria. 50-100 companies = specific enough and broad enough. 10,000 = too loose. 12 = too narrow.

### Step 8 -- Document the ICP

Write it up in a form every team member can use quickly:

    PROFILE NAME: [a name the team will use, e.g., "Mid-market SaaS scaler"]

    FIRMOGRAPHIC CRITERIA
    Industry / vertical:
    Company size (headcount and/or revenue):
    Geography:
    Business model:
    Tech stack signals (if relevant):

    SITUATIONAL CRITERIA
    Stage of growth or lifecycle:
    Primary buying trigger:
    Secondary triggers (1-2):

    BUYING COMMITTEE
    Primary buyer: [title, priorities, messaging angle]
    Champion profile: [title, motivation]
    Other key stakeholders:

    NEGATIVE ICP
    Explicit disqualifiers:

    OBSERVABLE SIGNALS
    How you identify that a company fits the ICP and the trigger has fired --
    what you can actually see from the outside:

    CONFIDENCE LEVEL
    [ ] Low -- fewer than 5 Tier A customers. ICP is a hypothesis.
    [ ] Medium -- 5-15 Tier A customers. Directionally right, some gaps.
    [ ] High -- 15+ Tier A customers. Validated pattern.

    LAST UPDATED: [date]
    NEXT REVIEW: [date -- 6 months out, or sooner if major change]

### Step 9 -- Turn the ICP Into a Working System

An ICP document that lives in a folder is worthless. It needs to be operationalized.

| GTM Function | How ICP Connects |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Scoring | Use ICP criteria as the Fit dimension in qualification scoring |
| Targeting | Build prospect lists using ICP firmographics + observable trigger signals |
| Outreach | Use buying triggers and committee roles to personalize messaging |
| Qualification calls | Use ICP criteria as a discovery checklist to qualify/disqualify fast |
| Forecasting | Flag deals not matching ICP as higher risk |
| Product | Share ICP with product team so they build for the right user |

The ICP should influence every decision in the revenue org. If it doesn't, it's decoration.

## Quick Reference

| Layer | What to Define | Source |
|-------|---------------|--------|
| Firmographic | Industry, size, geography, business model | Customer data + enrichment |

| Value | What outcome they hire you for, how they measure it | CS + customer interviews |
| Negative | Hard disqualifiers, bad-fit patterns | Churn analysis + Tier C customers |
