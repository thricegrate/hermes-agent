# YC Framework: How to Talk to Users (Eric Migicovsky)

Source: Y Combinator Startup School 2020 -- Eric Migicovsky

## Core Philosophy

Talk to users before building. It saves months of wasted effort. The biggest mistake founders make is building something nobody wants because they never asked.

## The Interview Framework

Three phases, in order. Never skip Phase 2 (Problem Exploration).

### Phase 1: Context Gathering (2-3 min)
Set the stage. Understand who they are and their relationship to the problem space.

- "What do you do?" / "Tell me about your role"
- "How did you first get into [topic]?"
- "How much time do you spend on [relevant activity]?"

Purpose: Build rapport and understand their world before diving into problems.

### Phase 2: Problem Exploration (10-15 min)
The meat of the interview. Understand their pain deeply.

**The Golden Questions:**
1. "Tell me about the last time you [did X]. Walk me through exactly what happened."
2. "What's the hardest part about [doing X]?"
3. "Why is that hard?" (then keep asking why -- Five Whys technique)
4. "How often do you encounter this problem?"
5. "How do you currently deal with it?"
6. "What don't you love about your current solution?"
7. "Have you tried anything else? What happened?"

**The Five Whys:**
Surface answers hide root causes. Keep asking "why" until you hit bedrock.

Example:
- "I can't keep up with AI tools" (surface)
- Why? "There are too many to track" (symptom)
- Why does that matter? "I don't know which ones actually help" (closer)
- Why is that a problem? "I waste hours trying things that don't work" (real pain)
- Why do you keep trying? "Because my competitors are using AI and I'm falling behind" (root cause = FEAR OF BEING LEFT BEHIND)

That root cause ("fear of being left behind") is 10x more powerful for copy than "too many AI tools."

### Phase 3: Solution Discovery (5-10 min)
Only AFTER you deeply understand their problem.

- "If you could wave a magic wand, what would be different?"
- "Would you pay for a solution? How much?"
- "What would it need to do for you to switch from your current approach?"
- "Who else has this problem that you know of?"

## Key Principles

### 1. Actions > Words > Intentions
- What people DO (past behavior) is the most reliable signal
- What people SAY (current description) is somewhat reliable
- What people SAY THEY'LL DO (future intention) is the least reliable

Always ask about the PAST: "Tell me about the last time..." not "Would you ever..."

### 2. Specifics Over Generalities
Bad: "Do you have trouble with AI?"
Good: "Tell me about the last time you tried to use ChatGPT for work. What happened?"

Specifics force people to recall real experiences rather than give you polished answers.

### 3. Talk Less, Listen More
80/20 rule: They talk 80%, you talk 20%. If you're explaining your idea, you're doing it wrong.

Your job is to EXTRACT their experience, not to PITCH your solution.

### 4. Follow the Energy
When someone's voice changes -- they speed up, get frustrated, laugh nervously, sigh -- that's where the real insight lives. Go deeper.

"Tell me more about that."
"Why did that frustrate you?"
"What did you do next?"

### 5. Language Mining
Listen for the EXACT words people use. Don't translate into marketing-speak.

If 5 users say "I'm drowning in AI tools" -- your headline is "Stop drowning in AI tools," not "Streamline your AI workflow."

Their words > your words. Always.

## Analysis Method

### Pattern Recognition (after 5+ interviews)
- Track problems mentioned by 3+ people = validated problem
- Track language used by 3+ people = copy gold
- Track solutions they've tried = competitive landscape
- Track emotional intensity = priority ranking

### The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)
Three rules to avoid false positives:
1. Talk about THEIR life, not YOUR idea
2. Ask about the PAST, not hypothetical futures
3. Talk LESS and listen MORE

People want to be nice. They'll say your idea is great even when it's not. The only defense is to never reveal your idea until you understand their problem.

### Sample Size Guidelines
- 5 interviews: spot obvious patterns, enough for quick gut-check
- 10 interviews: confident directional decisions
- 20 interviews: nuanced segmentation and prioritization
- 30+: diminishing returns unless you're doing formal research

### Red Flags in Interviews
- "That sounds cool" with no follow-up energy = politeness, not interest
- "I would definitely use that" but they haven't tried existing solutions = lukewarm
- Compliments about YOU rather than excitement about the SOLUTION = social pressure
- Vague answers to specific questions = they don't actually have this problem

### Green Flags
- They describe the problem before you ask = top of mind
- They've already tried multiple solutions = active problem-solver (your best customer)
- They get emotional describing the pain = high severity
- They ask "When can I get this?" = validated demand
- They offer to pay or pre-order without being asked = strongest signal

## Practical Tips

1. **Record interviews** (with permission) so you can focus on listening, not note-taking
2. **Interview in pairs** if possible -- one asks, one takes notes
3. **Debrief immediately** after each interview while details are fresh
4. **Don't batch analysis** -- review after every 3-5 interviews to adjust questions
5. **Recruit from multiple channels** to avoid selection bias (email + social + community)
6. **Offer a real incentive** -- early access, gift card, free consultation. Respect their time.
7. **20-30 minutes is the sweet spot** -- enough depth without fatigue
