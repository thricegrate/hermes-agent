# Email Optimizer

Polish any email draft by applying 15 proven copywriting rules AND generate high-converting subject lines from 100 battle-tested frameworks, all matched to your email's specific intent, emotion, and hook.

---

## How to Use This Skill

When triggered, ask the user for:

> To optimize your email, I need:
>
> 1. **Your email draft** (or describe what it's about)
> 2. **Type of email** - Newsletter, sales, nurture, launch, promotional, welcome?
> 3. **Goal** - What action should readers take?
> 4. **Target audience** - Who's reading this?
> 5. **Audience tone** - Casual/friendly, professional, edgy, motivational?
> 6. **Any words to avoid?** - Overused phrases, brand restrictions, etc.

If the user just pastes an email without context, analyze it and optimize immediately.

## Part 1: The 15 Email Copy Rules

### Rule 1: Write at Least 10 Subject Line Variations

Your email doesn't matter if nobody opens it. Always brainstorm 10+ subject line variations using different psychological angles. Keep all subject lines under 42 characters (mobile cutoff).

**Action:** Generate 10 subject line variations using the framework library below.

### Rule 2: Write the Intro Like a LinkedIn Hook

Skip long greetings. Capture attention immediately. The first 1-2 lines should make someone want to keep reading.

**Bad:** "Hey there! Hope you're having a great week. I've been thinking a lot lately about..."
**Good:** "I make a living writing emails for 7, 8, and 9 figure founders. Here are 10 tips..."

**Action:** Identify if the intro is weak and rewrite it as a hook.

### Rule 3: Speak Directly to the Reader

The best emails feel like a 1:1 conversation. Replace "Hey everyone" with "Hey" or "Hey [name]." Aim for 80%+ second person ("you") vs. first person ("I").

**Action:** Flag any crowd-speak and rewrite to direct address.

### Rule 4: Edit for Mobile

Most readers are on mobile. Break any paragraph with 2-3 sentences into 1-sentence paragraphs with line breaks.

**Action:** Identify blocky paragraphs and suggest mobile-friendly formatting.

### Rule 5: Read It Aloud

Flag any sections that would sound clunky when read aloud. Awkward phrasing, sentences that don't flow, or sections that feel boring.

**Action:** Identify clunky sections and suggest smoother alternatives.

### Rule 6: Use Sub-Heads for Skimmability

Most readers skim, not read. Sub-heads give signposts.

Rules of thumb:
- Listicles: each item gets its own sub-head
- Non-listicles: aim for 3-5 sub-heads minimum
- Find key sentences that drive a point home and turn them into sub-heads

**Action:** Suggest where to add sub-heads if the email is missing them.

### Rule 7: Always Include a PS

The PS is one of the most-read sections of any email. Prime real estate.

**3 uses:**
- Value newsletters with no body CTA: soft, contextual CTA in PS
- Sales emails: address a common objection in PS
- Sales emails: reinforce urgency or deadline in PS

**Action:** Write a PS if missing, or improve the existing one.

### Rule 8: Make CTAs Contextual

CTAs should feel natural where they're placed, not bolted on.

**Two levers:**
- **Placement** - Most relevant section of the email for this CTA
- **Phrasing** - Framed congruently with the surrounding copy

**Action:** Evaluate CTA placement and phrasing; suggest improvements.

### Rule 9: Turn Blocky Paragraphs into Bulleted Lists

Bullets make information feel 10x easier to consume. Only 1-2 bulleted lists per section. Always include 1-2 intro sentences before a bulleted list.

**Action:** Identify paragraphs that would work better as bullets.

### Rule 10: Break the Consensus

If this email says the same thing every other email in the space says, it will get ignored. Check: what is the consensus advice on this topic? Does this email contradict, reframe, or add something novel?

**Action:** Identify if the email repeats standard advice. Suggest a contrarian angle or unexpected reframe.

### Rule 11: Include a Personal Story or Proof

Generic tips get skimmed. Personal stories get read. Every email should include at least one specific personal experience, customer result, or documented proof.

**Checks:**
- Is there a real story with specific details (names, numbers, what went wrong)?
- Are claims backed by proof (screenshots, data, results)?

**Action:** If no personal story, flag it and help the user surface one.

### Rule 12: Every Email Needs a Clickable Tool CTA

Every email must include a link to a free demo, artifact, or bot the reader can try RIGHT NOW. The CTA is experiential, not informational. "Try this bot" beats "Read this article." If there's no tool to link, the email shouldn't go out until one is built.

**Action:** Check for experiential CTA. Flag if only informational CTAs exist.

### Rule 13: Reduce 3rd-Party Ad Density

Prioritize own product CTAs over paid sponsor slots. Reserve the best placement for our tools. Keep enough sponsor revenue to cover costs, but own product CTA goes above the fold.

**Action:** If sponsor placements exist, check whether own product CTAs have the best positioning.

### Rule 14: Survey-Style Engagement

Simple "click to answer" emails outperform content emails 3x on clicks. When engagement drops, send a poll or survey instead of more content.

**Action:** If engagement is declining, suggest replacing the next content email with a survey or poll. Provide 3 poll question options.

### Rule 15: Copywork on the Greats

(Educational tip: Regularly hand-copy great emails from your swipe file to internalize patterns. Mention to user if relevant.)

## Cold Email Optimization (Different Rules)

When optimizing **cold outreach emails** (not newsletters), several rules above change or don't apply. Cold emails must look like personal messages from a peer, not marketing.

**Rules that apply differently for cold email:**

- **Rule 6 (Sub-Heads):** SKIP. Cold emails never use sub-heads. They must look like a personal message, not a formatted newsletter.
- **Rule 7 (PS Section):** SKIP. PS sections signal marketing. In cold email, everything goes in the body.
- **Rule 9 (Bulleted Lists):** MAX 3-4 items. More than that looks like a pitch deck. Keep each item under 5 words.
- **Rule 12 (Tool CTA):** SKIP. Cold email CTAs are reply-based ("Mind if I send more info?") or meeting-based ("Have 10 minutes?"), never click-based.
- **Rule 13 (Ad Density):** N/A. Cold emails have no sponsor slots.
- **Rule 14 (Survey Engagement):** N/A. Cold prospects won't take surveys.

**Cold email-specific rules:**

- **Brevity is king:** 25-75 words optimal. If a sentence doesn't move toward a reply, cut it.
- **Subject lines:** 2-4 words, lowercase, boring. "Quick question" outperforms creative subjects. See `email-outreach` skill for full subject line data.
- **CTA:** One ask. Interest-based CTAs ("Worth exploring?" / "Mind if I send more info?") beat meeting requests ("Have 15 minutes?") on first touch.
- **No HTML, no images, no links** (except maybe your calendar link on follow-ups). Plain text only.
- **Personalization:** Must connect to the problem, not just attention-hack. If you remove the personalized line and the email still makes sense, the personalization isn't working.

**Action:** When the user's draft is a cold outreach email, apply the cold email rules above instead of Rules 6, 7, 9, 12, 13, 14. Flag the email type in your optimization report.

## Part 2: Subject Line Generation

### Step 1: Analyze Email Intent

Read the email content and identify:
- **Primary goal:** Sale, engagement, nurture, announcement, content delivery
- **Core emotion:** Map to emotional trigger: WOW, WTF, OMG, LOL, UGH, WHOA, NAH, YEP, OOF, HMM, DAMN, SMH. The subject line must trigger this emotion.
- **Key hook elements:** Numbers, results, pain points, authority figures, timeframes

### Step 2: Match Frameworks

Select 3-5 framework categories that align with the email's intent:

- Sales/promo: Urgency, FOMO, Results, Value, Contrarian
- Educational: Curiosity, Quick Win, Contrarian, Story
- Story-based: Story, Pain Point, Transformation, Contrarian
- Authority-building: Authority, Social Proof, Results, Story
- Challenge/launch: Action, Empowerment, Urgency, Contrarian
- Newsletter/content: Curiosity, Value, Quick Win, Trend, Story, Contrarian

**AI-era priority:** In every batch, ensure Contrarian and Story categories are represented.

### Step 3: Generate 5-10 Subject Lines

For each, provide:
```
Subject line: [subject line]
Framework: [which framework it's based on]
Suggested preview text: [preview text that complements the subject line]
```

### Style Rules

- Keep under 50 characters when possible (mobile optimization)
- Use specific numbers over vague claims
- Match the sender's voice
- Lowercase often outperforms Title Case
- Questions outperform statements for cold audiences
- One clear idea per subject line
- **Avoid:** "Unlock," "Discover," "Revolutionary," "Game-changing," "Don't miss"
- At least 2-3 variations should use pattern interruption or contrarian framing
- At least 1-2 should tease personal stories

### Preview Text Guidelines

Preview text should:
- **Complement, not repeat** the subject line
- Add specificity or intrigue the subject line lacks
- Be 40-90 characters
- Create a 1-2 punch with the subject line

**For the full 100-framework library**: See [references/subject-line-frameworks.md](references/subject-line-frameworks.md)

### Storytelling-Driven Subject Lines

- **The Question Framework:** Frame as questions, not topics. Creates tension the reader must resolve.
- **The Hidden Thesis Tease:** Tease a surprising conclusion without revealing it.
- **The Full-Circle Callback:** Reference a previous email's opening or promise.
- **The "But" Connector:** "But" creates instant tension. "I tried the #1 AI tool... but something went wrong."
- **Naming the Abstract:** Give the concept a name. "Meet Eve: the AI that controlled my life."

## Kennedy Direct Response Filter

Run this as the final check on every optimized email. These are non-negotiable.

1. **Offer check.** Does the email contain a specific, clear offer with a specific ask? If not, add one.
2. **Urgency check.** Is there a real reason to act today? Deadline, limited quantity, disappearing bonus, rising price, or social scarcity. At least one must be present in sales/promo emails.
3. **Instructions check.** Are the next steps spelled out clearly? "Click the button below, enter your email, get instant access." Not just "check it out."
4. **P.S. check.** Does the P.S. restate the offer and deadline, OR address the #1 objection? If the P.S. is just a throwaway line, rewrite it.
5. **PAS structure check.** Does the body follow Problem > Agitate > Solution? If the email jumps straight to the solution without naming the pain, restructure.
6. **Response check.** Would this email produce a measurable response, not just "awareness"? If you can't trace it to a click or reply, it's not direct response. Fix it.

See `references/kennedy-email-rules.md` for the full Kennedy framework, P.S. strategy, and direct mail to digital translation guide.

## Quality Criteria

The optimization must:
- **Be specific** - Not "make it better" but "change X to Y because Z"
- **Preserve the author's voice** - Improve, don't rewrite from scratch
- **Prioritize high-impact changes** - Subject line and intro first, formatting second
- **Include the full optimized email** - Not just feedback, but the finished product
- **Generate 10 real subject line options** - Different angles, all under 42 characters
- **Break the consensus** - At least 2-3 subject lines should use pattern interruption
- **Match the email's actual content** - No clickbait that doesn't deliver
