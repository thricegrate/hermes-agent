# Kennedy Direct Response Rules for Email

Dan Kennedy's direct mail principles translated to email. These rules have made billions in direct response. They work because they treat every email like a sales letter, not a "touch."

---

## Rule 1: Every Email Must Have an Offer

No exceptions. Every email you send needs a specific, clear offer with a specific ask. "Check out our blog" is not an offer. "Download this free tool that finds your best subject lines in 30 seconds" is an offer.

**The test:** Can the reader complete the action in under 60 seconds? If not, simplify the ask.

**What counts as an offer in email:**
- Free tool/demo with a specific outcome
- Limited access to something valuable
- A product with a price and a reason to act now
- A reply-based ask ("Hit reply and tell me X")

**What does NOT count:**
- "Stay tuned for more"
- Links to a homepage
- "Learn more" with no specific destination
- Vague invitations with no clear next step

---

## Rule 2: Create Urgency to Respond NOW

If there's no reason to act today, they won't act today. Or tomorrow. Or ever.

**5 urgency types that work in email:**

1. **Deadline.** "This closes Friday at midnight." Must be real.
2. **Limited quantity.** "Only 47 spots." Must be real.
3. **Disappearing bonus.** "First 25 buyers get the extra training."
4. **Rising price.** "Price goes up $200 on Monday."
5. **Social scarcity.** "Not everyone qualifies. Here's who this is for."

**The rule:** Pick at least one. Layer two or three for high-stakes sends. Never fake it. Once you fake scarcity, trust is gone forever.

---

## Rule 3: Give Clear Instructions

Tell them exactly what to do. Step by step. Like you're talking to someone who's never clicked a link before.

**Bad:** "Check it out here."
**Good:** "Click the blue button below. You'll land on a page that asks for your email. Enter it. You'll get instant access."

Most marketers assume the reader knows what to do. They don't. Or they're distracted. Spell it out.

---

## Rule 4: Track and Measure Everything

Every email campaign needs its own tracking. Unique UTM parameters, unique landing pages, unique codes.

If you can't tell which email drove which sale, you're guessing. And guessing is expensive.

**Minimum tracking per email:**
- Open rate (subject line effectiveness)
- Click rate (body copy + CTA effectiveness)
- Conversion rate (offer + landing page effectiveness)
- Revenue per email sent (the number that actually matters)

---

## Rule 5: Brand-Building Is a Side Effect

Never send an email "for awareness." Every email must be designed to produce a measurable response. Brand recognition happens as a byproduct of sending great direct response emails consistently.

**Kennedy's line:** "If you can't trace it to a sale, you can't afford it."

---

## The P.S. Strategy (from the Ultimate Sales Letter)

The P.S. is the second most-read element of any email, right after the subject line. Most readers scroll to the bottom before reading the middle.

**3 P.S. plays:**

1. **Restate the offer and deadline.** "P.S. Remember, the $200 discount disappears Friday at midnight. [Link]"
2. **Address the #1 objection.** "P.S. Worried it won't work for you? Here's what Sarah said after week 2: [testimonial]"
3. **Add a bonus reveal.** "P.S. I almost forgot. Everyone who joins this week also gets [bonus]. I wasn't planning to include it, but it fits perfectly."

**The SECOND NOTICE pattern:** When following up on an offer, stamp it. "SECOND NOTICE: Your invitation expires in 48 hours." This replicates the power of physical "SECOND NOTICE" mailings. People who ignored the first email often respond to the second simply because the format changed.

## Direct Mail to Digital Translation (Framework 17)

Kennedy built his empire on direct mail. Here's how those principles map to email:

| Direct Mail Element | Email Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Outer envelope teaser | Subject line + preview text |
| Sales letter body | Email body (PAS formula) |
| Response device | CTA button/link |
| Return envelope | Reply mechanism |
| P.S. on the letter | P.S. in the email |

**The structure that's worked for decades:**

1. **Problem.** Name the pain they're feeling right now.
2. **Agitate.** Make them feel it. Tell a story about what happens if they don't fix it.
3. **Solution.** Present your offer as the fix. Benefits, not features.
4. **P.S.** Restate the offer and the deadline.

**The one thing that changed:** Speed. Direct mail took weeks to test. Email lets you test in days. Use that speed. Split-test subject lines. Split-test P.S. copy. Split-test urgency types. But keep the underlying structure.

**The one thing that didn't change:** People. They still read the headline first, skip to the P.S., then decide whether to read the middle. Write accordingly.

---

## Quick Reference: The Kennedy Email Checklist

Before sending any email, run through this:

- [ ] Does it have a specific offer with a specific ask?
- [ ] Is there a real reason to act today (deadline, quantity, bonus)?
- [ ] Are the instructions crystal clear (click here, do this, get that)?
- [ ] Can I track which email drove which result?
- [ ] Does the P.S. restate the offer or handle the top objection?
- [ ] Would this email produce a measurable response, not just "awareness"?
- [ ] Is the copy structured as Problem > Agitate > Solution?

If any answer is "no," fix it before sending.
