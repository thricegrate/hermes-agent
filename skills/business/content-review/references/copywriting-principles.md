# Copywriting Principles

Compiled from Gary Halbert (The Boron Letters, newsletters), Bond Halbert (The Halbert Copywriting Method), and Eddie Shleyner (VeryGoodCopy). These three share a core philosophy but each brings unique techniques.

---

## Shared Principles (All Three Agree)

### 1. Market > Copy (40/40/20 Rule)
40% success = audience, 40% = offer, 20% = text. Gary calls it the "starving crowd." No amount of brilliant writing saves a wrong audience or weak offer.

### 2. Editing > Writing
- **Gary:** Read aloud, rewrite rough spots, repeat
- **Bond:** Dedicated an entire book to editing sales copy. First book about editing, not writing
- **Eddie:** "The editing process is much longer and more intense than the writing process"

### 3. Copywork (Rewriting by Hand)
All three recommend taking proven sales letters and copying them by hand. Trains subconscious rhythm and persuasion instincts.

### 4. Short Paragraphs + Eye Relief
- **Gary:** Max 3 sentences, wide margins, white space
- **Bond:** Systematized into rules (indents, spacing, fonts)
- **Eddie:** 1-3 sentences per paragraph, double line breaks

### 5. Simple Language
- **Gary:** Cut "-ly" words, cut "that", write like you talk
- **Bond:** "Be compelling, not poetic." Break grammar if it helps grip
- **Eddie:** "A big word will never impress the reader as much as a big idea, clearly expressed"

### 6. Slippery Slide
Every sentence exists only to make the reader read the next one. Joe Sugarman's term, but all three use it as a core principle.

### 7. Emotion > Logic
Hook emotionally first. Logic supports, doesn't lead. Eddie: "95% of purchase decisions are unconscious and emotional."

## Gary Halbert -- Storyteller and Showman

The father of direct response copywriting. His techniques center on intimacy, storytelling, and physical engagement.

### Bucket Brigades

Short transitional phrases that break pattern and re-engage:

| Phrase | When to use |
|--------|-------------|
| "Anyway..." | After a digression, pulling back to main thread |
| "Look." | Before a direct, important statement |
| "Now." | Shifting to next point |
| "Think about it." | After dropping a surprising fact |
| "Here's the thing." | Before revealing a key insight |
| "But wait." | Building anticipation |
| "And get this..." | Escalating a sequence |
| "Now, here's where it gets interesting." | Transitioning to the meat |

Gary used "anyway" 33 times across 18 Boron Letters. These aren't filler -- they're momentum devices.

**Anti-pattern:** "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally" -- these are academic, not conversational.

### Physical / Sensory Details

Gary describes weather, clothing, location, sounds. This creates intimacy:

> "I'm sitting here in the prison, looking out through these dirty windows at the guard tower."

The reader is there. Not reading about it -- experiencing it.

**Application:** When writing about a product, an experience, or a story -- include one sensory detail per paragraph. What does it look like, sound like, feel like?

### Ellipsis (...)

Gary's signature: dramatic pauses through three dots.

> "And then something happened... something that changed everything."

Creates suspense. Forces the reader to pause and lean in. Use sparingly (1-2 per piece max) or it becomes a tic.

### Grab Devices

Physical elements attached to letters: dollar bills, coins, photos. In digital: unexpected opening lines, pattern interrupts, visual elements that break expectation.

### Read Fiction, Not Marketing

Gary recommended the Travis McGee series over business books. Storytelling > theory. The best copywriters read novels, not copywriting books.

### "Motion Beats Meditation"

Stuck? Move. Run, walk, write anything. The first draft is always bad -- and that's normal. Don't wait for perfection. Write, then fix.

## Bond Halbert -- Systematic Editor

Gary's son. Took his father's instinctive genius and turned it into repeatable systems.

### Complete Sentences, Incomplete Thoughts

Bond's single biggest contribution. The sentence is grammatically complete, but the thought isn't. The reader must continue:

> "I finally figured out why my ads weren't working." (Complete sentence. But what did he figure out? Incomplete thought. You must read on.)

vs.

> "My ads weren't working because the targeting was wrong." (Complete sentence AND complete thought. Reader can stop.)

**How to create them:**
- End paragraphs with a tease, not a conclusion
- Open a loop, close it in the next paragraph
- Promise information, deliver it later

### 4 Reader Types

Every piece must work for all four:

| Type | Behavior | What they need |
|------|----------|---------------|
| Skimmers | Scan headlines, bold, bullets | Clear visual hierarchy, subheads that tell the story |
| Sequential | Read top to bottom | Logical flow, smooth transitions |
| Cherry-pickers | Jump to what interests them | Self-contained sections, clear labels |
| Evaluators | Assess quality before committing | Strong opening, credibility signals early |

### Hunt "Ands"

Found an "and" joining two ideas? Split into two sentences. Almost always stronger:

> Before: "She launched the product and sales doubled within a week."
> After: "She launched the product. Sales doubled within a week."

The period creates emphasis. The second sentence lands harder.

### 6-Step Editing Formula

Bond's systematic editing process, in order:

| Step | Focus | Action |
|------|-------|--------|
| 1 | Eye relief | Fix paragraph length, add white space, check subheads |
| 2 | Clarity | Remove ambiguity, simplify complex sentences |
| 3 | Momentum | Check slippery slide, fix dead spots, add bucket brigades |
| 4 | Punch up | Sharpen word choice, kill adverbs, strengthen verbs |
| 5 | Hidden psychology | Add open loops, curiosity gaps, incomplete thoughts |
| 6 | Read aloud | Final pass for rhythm, stumbles, unnatural phrasing |

### Bullet Matrix

Bond's system for writing bullets (list items in sales copy):

- Lead with the benefit, not the feature
- Each bullet is a "desire amplifier" -- it should make the reader want the thing more
- Alternate bullet styles: question bullets, "how to" bullets, "secret of" bullets, "warning" bullets
- Never list more than 7 bullets without a subhead break

### AI Workflow

Bond is the only one of the three who addresses AI: use it for research, but hooks and psychological editing must be human. AI can draft; only a human can feel whether the copy grips.

## Eddie Shleyner -- Minimalist and Craftsman

Founder of VeryGoodCopy. His philosophy: less is more, precision is everything.

### Fewer Syllables Test

"Can I say this with fewer syllables?" The answer is almost always yes:

| Before | After | Syllables saved |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| Utilize | Use | 2 |
| In order to | To | 3 |
| At this point in time | Now | 5 |
| Implement | Do / Build / Run | 2 |
| Leverage (verb) | Use | 2 |
| Facilitate | Help / Run | 3 |
| Methodology | Method | 3 |
| Approximately | About | 3 |

**Rule:** If the simpler word carries the same meaning, always use it. Complexity is not sophistication.

### Necessity Test

"If I remove this sentence, does the piece suffer?"

- If no: kill it
- If yes: keep it
- If maybe: it's a no

Apply to: sentences, paragraphs, entire sections, individual words. Be ruthless. The reader's attention is the most expensive thing you'll ever buy.

### Clean Windows

"You don't notice a clean window. You see through it. That's what good copy does."

The reader should see the idea, not the writing. Signs of dirty windows:
- You admire the sentence but forget the point
- Clever wordplay that slows comprehension
- Unusual vocabulary that sends readers to a dictionary
- Metaphors that call attention to themselves

### Strong Verbs (Hemingway Rules)

Replace adverb + weak verb with one strong verb:

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| Quickly ran | Sprinted |
| Slowly walked | Crept |
| Significantly reduced | Slashed |
| Carefully examined | Scrutinized |
| Completely destroyed | Obliterated |
| Gently touched | Caressed |
| Loudly shouted | Bellowed |

**Rule:** If you see an adverb, the verb is probably too weak. Find a verb that doesn't need the crutch.

### Micro-Article Format

Eddie's signature blog format:
- 200-400 words
- One idea per post
- Opens with a hook (often a story or surprising fact)
- Delivers the principle in 2-3 paragraphs
- Ends with a sharp closer -- no summary, no CTA fluff

This format has extreme information density. Every word earns its place.

### Hemingway's 6 Rules (as curated by Eddie)

1. Use short sentences
2. Use short paragraphs
3. Use vigorous English (active voice, strong verbs)
4. Be positive, not negative ("He was silent" not "He didn't say anything")
5. Never use jargon when a common word will do
6. Cut every word that isn't pulling weight

## Quick Reference: When to Apply What

| Situation | Who to channel | Technique |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| Text feels monotone | Gary | Vary sentence length, add bucket brigades |
| Paragraphs are walls | Bond | Eye relief pass, subheading audit |
| Reader can stop mid-piece | Bond | Incomplete thoughts, open loops |
| Too many fancy words | Eddie | Fewer syllables test |
| Sentences with adverbs | Eddie | Strong verb replacement |
| Writing feels "writerly" | Eddie | Clean windows check |
| No personality/voice | Gary | Sensory details, personal stories |
| Weak transitions | Gary | Bucket brigades |
| Bullets don't excite | Bond | Bullet matrix (desire amplification) |
| Can't tell what's important | Bond | 4 reader types audit |
| Piece feels bloated | Eddie | Necessity test, kill every line that doesn't suffer |
