# Sheridan Narrative Framework for Newsletters

How to apply Taylor Sheridan's screenwriting principles to newsletter writing. This is the application guide -- for the full principle set, see `skills/storyteller/references/sheridan-framework.md`. For the template, see `templates/newsletter-sheridan-narrative.md`.

---

## When to Use Sheridan vs Other Templates

| Your Situation | Template | Why |
|---|---|---|
| You have a personal story with a clear lesson | Welsh Narrative | Welsh is story-to-lesson. The 8-beat arc needs a bottom line. |
| You have a story that haunts you but you don't know why | **Sheridan Narrative** | Sheridan is scene-to-question. No lesson required. |
| You want to teach a framework or process | Deep Dive | Teaching content needs structure, not mystery. |
| You have a before/after with clear numbers | Case Study Metrics Arc | Metrics stories need the number sequence. |
| You want to show a trade-off, a cost, a hard choice | **Sheridan Narrative** | Cost and moral ambiguity are Sheridan's DNA. |
| You want to build trust through real vulnerability | **Sheridan Narrative** | Sheridan vulnerability is observational, not performative. |
| You have a tip list or resource roundup | Listicle or Curation | Wrong format for narrative. |
| Build-in-public content about something that didn't work | **Sheridan Narrative** | Failed experiments are perfect Sheridan material. |
| Build-in-public content about something that DID work | Case Study Metrics Arc or Welsh | Success stories need either numbers or lessons. |

**The decisive question:** Do you have a neat answer? Use Welsh. Are you still sitting with the question? Use Sheridan.

## The "Simple but Deep" Newsletter Formula

Sheridan's secret: absurdly simple premise + infinite character depth. Here's how that translates to newsletters:

### Step 1: Strip the Premise to One Sentence

Before writing, state what happened in one sentence. If you need two, simplify.

- "I automated my newsletter and felt nothing." (one sentence)
- "A client asked me to do the thing I tell other people not to do." (one sentence)
- "I looked at my revenue dashboard and closed the laptop." (one sentence)

If your one-sentence premise doesn't make you want to know more, pick a different story.

### Step 2: Go Deep on the Person, Not the Topic

The topic is the container. The person is the content. Don't write about "newsletter automation." Write about the moment you realized automating everything meant you had nothing left to do at 6 AM. The feeling. The silence. The question.

### Step 3: Withhold the Lesson

This is the hardest part for marketing writers. Every instinct says: give the takeaway. Wrap it up. Here's what I learned.

Sheridan doesn't do that. Sicario ends with a soccer game resuming after gunfire. 1883 ends with a dead girl reaching Montana. The question is louder than any answer could be.

In a newsletter: describe what happened. Describe the cost. Ask the question. Stop.

The reader will supply their own lesson. And their lesson will be more powerful than yours, because it's theirs.

## Sheridan-Style Subject Lines

### The Subtext Principle

Sheridan's titles tell you nothing and everything: "Sicario" (hitman). "Wind River" (a place). "1883" (a year). "Landman" (a job). They're windows, not summaries.

### Patterns

**The Detail** -- a specific object or moment from inside the story:
- "The parking lot"
- "11:47 PM"
- "Row 14, seat C"
- "The second email"

**The Absence** -- what's missing or not said:
- "Nobody said anything"
- "The call I didn't make"
- "Silence"
- "What I didn't tell them"

**The Cost** -- the price, stated directly:
- "I lost the account"
- "Three months"
- "$40K"
- "Everything before Tuesday"

**The Question Fragment** -- incomplete, forcing the open:
- "What would you have..."
- "Was it worth"
- "Would you still..."

### A/B Testing Recommendation

Always test a Sheridan-style subject line against a standard emotional hook. Track which drives higher open rates AND which drives higher reply rates. Sheridan subjects may get lower opens but higher engagement (more replies, more forwards, more saves).

## Example Outlines: the product Build-in-Public Content

### Example 1: "The automation that made me feel empty"

1. **Window:** 6:14 AM, coffee's ready, laptop open. Dashboard says everything's running. Newsletter went out at 5. Social posted at 5:30. DMs are being handled. Revenue report's in my inbox. I have nothing to do.
2. **Surrogate:** Me. The guy who built the system specifically so I'd have nothing to do at 6 AM.
3. **Simple Engine:** I automated my entire business and got exactly what I asked for.
4. **Gap:** But something felt wrong. Not broken-wrong. Empty-wrong. The kind of wrong you can't troubleshoot because nothing's actually broken.
5. **Reveal:** I opened a blank doc and started writing a new skill. Not because I needed to. Because I needed to feel like I was building something. The system worked. I was the part that didn't.
6. **Cost:** I built the product to free up my time. It did. What I didn't account for: what happens when the thing you love doing gets done without you.
7. **Question:** What do you do when you get exactly what you wanted and it's not enough?
8. **Tool:** I'm packaging this system. [Link]. But I'm still figuring out the answer to that question myself.

### Example 2: "The client who asked me to lie"

1. **Window:** Wednesday, 2 PM. Zoom call. The client's screen is shared. He's pointing at a metric I know is wrong.
2. **Surrogate:** Me. Watching him build a pitch deck around a number I told him not to use.
3. **Simple Engine:** A client wanted me to make the dashboard say something it didn't say.
4. **Gap:** He wasn't asking me to fabricate data. He was asking me to present real data in a way that told a different story. Which is technically fine. Which is technically how everyone does it.
5. **Reveal:** I showed him both versions. The one that tells the story he wants. The one that tells the story the data wants. He picked the first one. I would have too, three years ago.
6. **Cost:** I didn't lose the client. I lost the version of myself that would have said something. I just sent the dashboard and closed the laptop.
7. **Question:** Where's the line between presenting data and performing it?

### Example 3: "Nobody clapped"

1. **Window:** Team meeting, Tuesday. I'm sharing my screen. The slide says "Product Pivot." Three faces on Zoom. Nobody's camera is off, which means they're paying attention. Which makes what happens next worse.
2. **Surrogate:** Me. The founder about to tell his team everything changes.
3. **Simple Engine:** I told my team we're pivoting from ads to products.
4. **Gap:** I'd rehearsed this. The vision. The numbers. The opportunity. I hit every point. Finished talking. Silence. Not awkward silence. Processing silence. The kind where people are deciding if they believe you.
5. **Reveal:** Max said "OK." Julia nodded. Oleg asked one question about the Meta ads budget. That was it. No pushback. No excitement. Just... OK. They'd been waiting for me to say it.
6. **Cost:** I spent three weeks agonizing over the pivot announcement. They'd already pivoted in their heads. The only person who needed convincing was me.
7. **Question:** How often do you agonize over a decision your team already made without you?

## Combining Sheridan with Existing Techniques

### Sheridan + South Park But/Therefore Rule
Already aligned. Sheridan never uses "and then." Every beat in his work is connected by conflict ("but") or consequence ("therefore"). Apply this to every transition in the Sheridan template.

### Sheridan + Kennedy's Damaging Admission
Sheridan's vulnerability IS the damaging admission. The cost beat is where this lives. "I lost the account." "I closed the laptop." "I didn't say anything." Use Kennedy's principle but let it land through Sheridan's showing, not Kennedy's telling.

### Sheridan + Hormozi Hooks
Use a Hormozi hook type for the subject line, then switch to Sheridan structure for the body. The hook gets the open. Sheridan keeps them reading. This is the "Views vs Sales" dual strategy in action.

### Sheridan + Foreshadow-Forget-Deliver (Multi-Issue Arc)
For serialized newsletter content:
- **Issue 1:** Drop a detail or scene that hints at something coming. Don't explain it. (The foreshadow.)
- **Issues 2-4:** Different stories. The reader forgets the hint.
- **Issue 5:** Deliver the thing you foreshadowed. The reader goes "wait -- that's what that was about."

This is 1883's opening flash-forward applied to a newsletter series.
