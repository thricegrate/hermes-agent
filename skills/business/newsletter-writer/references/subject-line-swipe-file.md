# Subject Line Swipe File & Hooks

Your subject line is the most important line in your entire newsletter. If nobody opens it, nobody reads it. This file gives you a proven system for writing subject lines that get clicks.

## Cyber Corsairs Performance Data (452 emails, Sept 2023 - Mar 2026)

These insights come from statistical analysis of our own newsletter at scale (215K+ subscribers). They override generic advice wherever they conflict. Full analysis: `private project analytics/newsletter-subject-line-analysis.md`.

**Top 5 Subject Lines (mature list, 50K+ sends):**
1. 44.6% "New ChatGPT feels like cheating" (108K sent)
2. 44.5% "Use ChatGPT as a free speaking coach" (207K sent)
3. 44.1% "AI in 2027" (110K sent)
4. 43.3% "V7 is insane" (110K sent)
5. 43.0% "Deep Research unlocked, yay" (115K sent)

**Bottom patterns (all under 28% OR):** Generic listicle format + vague benefit. "ChatGPT's diet tricks" (22.3%), "Meet 10 AI tools" (24.3%), "5 prompts for new skills" (24.6%). The "N ways to X" template with no specificity or emotion is the worst performer.

**What our data proves:**
- Product name + emotional claim = best formula (+3.9%)
- Metaphors/analogies = strongest single signal (+4.6%)
- "ChatGPT" keyword reliably boosts OR (+1.2%); "Claude" hurts it (-4.5%)
- Numbers in subject lines DON'T help (-0.6%). Exception: numbers inside bold claims.
- Questions outperform statements (+1.5%)
- Saturday + Tuesday = best days; Wednesday = worst
- 2-3x/week beats daily by 2-3 OR points

## The 4 Proven Hooks

Every high-performing subject line uses at least one of these hooks. They tap into fundamental human motivations:

### Hook 1: Value for Minimal Time
Promise a meaningful outcome in a short timeframe. Readers want results without long commitments.

- "Master AI image generation in 5 minutes"
- "The 3-minute prompt that writes your weekly report"
- "Learn this Claude trick in 60 seconds"

### Hook 2: Value for Minimal Cost
Show readers how to get premium results for free or cheap. Cost savings always grab attention.

- "The free tool that replaces $500/mo software"
- "Stop paying for AI tools: this free setup does more"
- "How I built a $10K/mo business with $0 in AI costs"

### Hook 3: Solve Problem Without Effort
Remove the friction. Show readers an easier path to something they're already trying to do.

- "Stop writing prompts from scratch: steal mine"
- "The lazy person's guide to AI automation"
- "You don't need to learn coding to automate this"

### Hook 4: Unlock Outcome Instantly
Promise immediate access to a result, template, or transformation.

- "Copy this template and 2x your output today"
- "The exact prompt I used to write a 3,000-word post in 20 minutes"
- "Steal my AI workflow: it's all here"

## The 6 Golden Rules

Follow these rules for every subject line you write:

### Rule 1: 4-7 Words, 25-34 Characters (after emoji)
Our data (452 emails) shows 25-34 characters is the sweet spot (35.7% OR). Under 15 chars can work for curiosity ("V7 is insane" = 43.3%) but risks being vague. Over 44 chars drops off. Aim for 4-7 words. If you can say it in 4 words, don't use 10.

### Rule 2: Sentence-Case
Write subject lines like sentences, not headlines. "How I saved 10 hours with AI" reads more naturally than "How I Saved 10 Hours With AI." Sentence-case feels personal, like a message from a friend.

### Rule 3: Use 1 of the 4 Hooks
Every subject line should clearly employ one of the four hooks above. If you can't identify which hook you're using, the subject line probably isn't strong enough.

### Rule 4: Visceral Language (Without Triggering Spam Filters)
Use words that create an emotional or physical reaction. But be careful: some visceral words are also spam triggers. The line between "attention-grabbing" and "spam-flagged" is thin.

**Safe visceral words (use these):**
- Seriously...
- Confession:
- Unpopular opinion:
- This is embarrassing, but...
- I can't believe...
- Steal this (in context, not as a command)

**Use sparingly (1x max, never in ALL CAPS):**
- Warning: (OK lowercase, spam risk in CAPS)
- Free (OK in natural context: "your free template." NOT in hype: "FREE!!!")

**Avoid in subject lines (spam triggers):**
- URGENT! (spam filter red flag)
- ACT NOW, BUY NOW, CLICK HERE
- GUARANTEED, RISK-FREE, LIMITED TIME
- Excessive punctuation: !!! or ??? or $$$
- ALL CAPS words of any kind

One visceral word per subject line maximum. Sentence case always. Overuse kills both the effect AND deliverability.

### Rule 5: Name & Claim Ideas
Give your frameworks, methods, and ideas catchy names. Named concepts feel more valuable and memorable.

- "The 60-Minute Newsletter Method"
- "The Lazy Genius AI Workflow"
- "The Mirror Framework for stealing newsletter formats"
- "The 3-3-3 Content System"

### Rule 6: Be SUPER Specific
Specificity builds credibility. Vague promises feel like spam. Specific claims feel like insider knowledge.

- Vague: "How to use AI to write better"
- Specific: "How I used Claude to write 47 LinkedIn posts in one sitting"
- Vague: "AI tools you should try"
- Specific: "The 3 AI tools I use daily that cost $0 total"

### Rule 7: Pass the Spam Filter
Every subject line must pass deliverability checks before it passes the creativity check. A brilliant subject line that lands in spam helps nobody.

- **Never use these words:** act now, buy now, click here, guaranteed, risk-free, limited time, urgent, 100% free, earn cash, double your money, congratulations, winner, no obligation, once in a lifetime, exclusive deal
- **No ALL CAPS** -- not even one word. "INSANE deal" = flag. "This is insane" = fine.
- **No excessive punctuation** -- !!! ??? $$$ are all red flags. One ! max, and rarely.
- **Emojis:** 1 max, at the end. Avoid 💰🔞🏆🎰. Rotate styles (pirate flag every send = banner blindness AND looks like a template).
- **"Free" is context-dependent:** "your free template" is fine. "FREE GIFT" is spam. Always lowercase, always in a natural sentence.
- Run subject lines through mail-tester.com before A/B testing if unsure.

Full spam word categories (349+ words by category): `email-optimizer/references/subject-line-frameworks.md` (Spam section).

## The 4 Whisper Types (Preview Text)

Preview text is the secondary line that appears after the subject line in email clients. Use it to reinforce the open with a "whisper", a quiet, persuasive nudge.

### Trust Whisper
Build credibility and reduce skepticism.
- "I tested this for 30 days before sharing it with you."
- "Based on data from 500+ newsletter creators."
- "I wouldn't recommend this if I hadn't used it myself."

### Obstacle Whisper
Name the reader's hesitation and dismiss it.
- "No, you don't need to be technical."
- "Even if you've tried AI tools before and gave up."
- "This works whether you have 100 subscribers or 100,000."

### Benefit Whisper
Expand on the promised value.
- "Plus: the exact prompts I used."
- "Including a template you can copy today."
- "And the one mistake that almost cost me 200 subscribers."

### Outcome Whisper
Paint a picture of the result.
- "Imagine having next month's content done by Friday."
- "Your mornings are about to get a lot easier."
- "This is the system that let me go from weekly to daily."

## A/B Testing on Beehiiv

### How to Set Up A/B Tests
1. In Beehiiv's email composer, click the A/B test toggle for subject lines
2. Write two subject lines: each using a DIFFERENT hook (don't just swap one word)
3. Set your test audience percentage (recommended: 20% of list)
4. Set the testing window (recommended: 1-2 hours for lists over 1,000; 4 hours for smaller lists)
5. Beehiiv automatically sends the winner to the remaining 80%

### Testing Best Practices
- **Test different IDEAS, not just word order.** "5 AI tips for beginners" vs "Stop making these AI mistakes" tests two hooks. "5 AI tips for beginners" vs "5 tips for AI beginners" tests nothing useful.
- **Track results over time.** Keep a spreadsheet of which hooks and subject line types get the best open rates for YOUR audience. Patterns will emerge.
- **Test one variable at a time.** If both subject lines are completely different in every way, you won't know what drove the difference.
- **Minimum 3 tests before drawing conclusions.** One test is noise. Three tests with the same pattern is a signal.

### What to Track
| Metric | What It Tells You |
|---|---|
| Open rate by hook type | Which motivation resonates with your audience |
| Open rate by subject line length | Whether your readers prefer short or detailed |
| Open rate by day/time | When your audience is most active |
| Click rate (not just open rate) | Whether the subject line attracted the RIGHT readers |

## Welsh Fragment Style (Narrative Issues)

For Welsh Narrative template issues, use ultra-short provocative fragments as subject lines. These create maximum curiosity with minimum words.

### The Pattern

2-5 words. Provocative statement or noun phrase. Period at the end. Creates curiosity gap without revealing the topic. Reads like a text message from a friend, not a newsletter subject.

### Examples (from Justin Welsh's The Saturday Solopreneur, 200K+ subs)

- "Thief of joy."
- "Rock bottom."
- "Get out."
- "Bored to death."
- "You've changed."
- "One person away."
- "He was only 25."
- "I'm a crazy person."
- "Small by design."
- "Being a purple squirrel."
- "Nobody's even trying."
- "The addiction to relevance."
- "Nobody is coming to save you."
- "The case for anti-scale."
- "Hustling your way to nowhere."
- "We all remember insults."
- "You're missing perspective."
- "Missing someone who doesn't exist."

### Rules

1. **2-5 words max.** If you need more, you're explaining. Don't explain.
2. **End with a period.** This makes it feel like a statement, not a headline. Periods feel final and confident.
3. **Never reveal the topic.** "Rock bottom." makes you open. "How I recovered from failure." doesn't.
4. **Emotional charge required.** The fragment must create one of: curiosity, recognition, discomfort, or intrigue.
5. **Sentence case only.** "Thief of joy." not "Thief Of Joy."
6. **No hooks, no promises, no numbers.** This is the opposite of "5 tips to..." -- it's a vibe, not an offer.
7. **Test against standard hooks.** A/B test a Welsh Fragment vs a hook-driven subject line. Track which one performs with YOUR audience.

### When to Use

- Welsh Narrative template issues (personal story essays)
- Build-in-public content
- Deeply personal or reflective issues
- Any issue where the story IS the value, not the framework

### When NOT to Use

- Educational deep dives (readers need to know the topic)
- Curation roundups (readers need to know what's inside)
- Product launch emails (need clarity, not mystery)
- Re-engagement campaigns (cold readers need explicit value)

## Cross-Pollination: What Cold Email Teaches Us

Cold email data shows that ultra-short, boring, internal-looking subject lines dramatically outperform creative ones in prospecting contexts (2-word subjects get 60% more opens than 5-word). For newsletters, this doesn't apply directly because your readers opted in and expect content.

But the underlying principle transfers:

- **Subject lines that look like personal messages outperform subject lines that look like marketing.** This aligns with Rule 2 (Sentence-Case) above. "question for you" feels more like a text from a friend than "7 Proven Strategies to Boost Your AI Workflow."
- **The most-opened cold email subjects contain zero value propositions.** For newsletters, consider testing ultra-simple subjects like "this is wild" or "quick question" alongside your hook-driven subjects. These work as occasional pattern interrupts, not as your default style.
- **Ambiguity creates curiosity differently than specificity.** Hook 4 (Unlock Outcome Instantly) and Rule 6 (Be SUPER Specific) are your bread and butter. But once every 5-10 issues, an ambiguous subject ("I need to tell you something") can spike opens by breaking the pattern.

**When to use this:** As an occasional A/B test variant. Not as a replacement for your proven hook-driven system. Test one "cold email style" subject per month against your normal style and track results.
