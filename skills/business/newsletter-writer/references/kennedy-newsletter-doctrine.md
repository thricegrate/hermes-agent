# Kennedy Newsletter Doctrine

Dan Kennedy's direct response principles applied to newsletter writing. These aren't theory. They're the mechanics behind newsletters that actually make money.

---

## The Herd Concept (Framework 10)

Your subscriber list is a herd you own. The newsletter is the fence that keeps them in and competitors out.

**Core math that changes everything:**
- Acquiring a new subscriber costs 5-7x more than keeping one
- Existing subscribers spend 214% more than new ones
- A subscriber who stays 12 months is worth 5-10x what they paid in month one

**The fence is frequency.** Frequency begets longevity. The more often you show up in their inbox, the harder it is for competitors to poach your readers. Monthly newsletter = weak fence. Weekly = solid. 4-5x per week = fortress.

Every issue you skip is a gap in the fence. Your competitors don't skip.

### The 40/60 Content Rule

Kennedy's split for newsletters that retain AND sell:
- **40% about THEM.** Their wins, their struggles, their questions, their stories. Reader spotlights. Survey results. Community highlights. Poll results shared back. Make them feel seen.
- **60% soft promotion.** Not hard pitching. Stories about your tools, behind-the-scenes of building, case studies of results, "here's what I'm working on" updates that naturally point to your offers. Every story has a product woven in.

Most creators do 90% education / 10% promotion and wonder why nobody buys. Kennedy's ratio keeps readers engaged AND moving toward a purchase.

**How to implement the 40/60 in practice:**
- Start every issue with something about the reader (poll result, reader question, community win)
- Main content: your story + tool demo + results (this is the 60%, but it reads as value)
- End with a CTA that feels like a natural next step, not a pitch

## Every Issue Has an Offer (Framework 1, Rule #1)

Kennedy's #1 rule of direct marketing: always include an offer. Every single issue. No exceptions.

An "offer" doesn't mean a hard sell. It means a clear, specific ask with a clear, specific next step.

**The offer spectrum for newsletters:**

| Offer Type | Example | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Free tool demo | "Try my AI coach bot free" | Building product awareness |
| Lead magnet | "Grab the prompt pack (free)" | Growing segmented lists |
| Low-ticket product | "$27 Skill Pack, this week only" | Revenue + buyer identification |
| Webinar/workshop | "Join me live Thursday" | Mid-ticket warm-up |
| Application | "Apply for the cohort (12 spots)" | High-ticket qualification |
| Reply CTA | "Hit reply and tell me..." | Engagement + research |
| Referral ask | "Forward this to one friend who..." | Growth |

**Rules for the offer:**
1. One primary offer per issue. Not three. Not five. One.
2. Make the CTA stupid clear. "Click this button." "Reply with one word." "Forward this email."
3. Tell them exactly what happens next. "You'll get the bot link in 10 seconds."
4. Add urgency when real. "I'm closing this Friday" works. Fake deadlines don't.

**Where to place the offer:**
- Best spot: after the main value section, as a natural next step
- Second best: the P.S. (second most-read element after the subject line, per Kennedy)
- Backup: woven into the story itself ("the tool that did this is available here")

## Newsletter as Business Backbone (Framework 16)

Kennedy's info-business revenue model puts the newsletter at the center of everything.

**The newsletter isn't the product. It's the engine.**

Here's how the money actually flows:

1. **Front end (newsletter):** Free content that builds trust and identifies buyers. Breaking even on subscriber acquisition is fine. The newsletter's job is to create customers, not revenue directly.

2. **Back end (products/services):** This is where 2/3 to 3/4 of total revenue comes from. The newsletter warms people up. Products close the deal.

3. **Continuity (membership):** Predictable monthly income from a paid tier, community, or subscription. The newsletter feeds new members in constantly.

4. **Periodic big paydays:** Launches, cohorts, intensives. The newsletter builds anticipation and drives enrollment.

**The business that can spend the most to acquire a subscriber wins.** If your newsletter feeds a product ladder, you can afford to spend more on growth than competitors who only monetize through ads.

### Practical Application for Each Issue

Every newsletter issue should do at least two of these:
- Build trust (personal story, vulnerability, proof)
- Identify buyers (click tracking on product links, poll responses)
- Feed the back end (soft CTA to a product or application)
- Create anticipation (tease upcoming launch, cohort, or tool)

If an issue only educates without doing any of these four things, it's a missed opportunity. Education alone is a hobby. Education that moves people toward buying is a business.

## Kennedy Offer Checklist (Quick Reference)

Before hitting send on any issue, run through this:

- [ ] Does this issue contain at least one clear offer?
- [ ] Is there a specific CTA telling readers exactly what to do?
- [ ] Is the offer placed after value has been delivered (not before)?
- [ ] Does the P.S. reinforce or add a second offer?
- [ ] Is there any urgency or reason to act now (if applicable)?
- [ ] Does at least 40% of this issue focus on the reader (their wins, questions, struggles)?
- [ ] Does the content naturally connect to a product or service (even softly)?

If you can't check at least 5 of 7, revise before sending.
