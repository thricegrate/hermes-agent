# Sponsor Block Anatomy

Detailed reference for writing sponsored content blocks that feel native, serve readers, and justify premium CPMs. Used by all newsletter templates.

## The 5-Part Sponsor Block Structure

Based on Nathan May's analysis of top-performing newsletter sponsor integrations (The Rundown, Morning Brew, Chasing Creative).

### 1. Clickable Heading

Bold, hyperlinked to the sponsor's landing page. Written in YOUR voice, not the sponsor's marketing copy.

**Good:** "This tool cuts my newsletter production in half"
**Good:** "Check out [Product] (I've been using it for 3 months)"
**Bad:** "[SPONSOR NAME] ADVERTISEMENT"
**Bad:** "A message from our sponsors"

The heading should feel like a recommendation, not an interruption.

### 2. Simple Visual

One clean image: product screenshot, logo lockup, or hero image. NOT a banner ad. It should feel editorial, not promotional.

- Sponsor provides the image
- If they send a low-quality banner, request a product screenshot instead
- Same visual style as the rest of your newsletter (not a jarring design break)

### 3. Brief Summary in YOUR Voice

1-2 sentences describing what the product does. Write it the way you'd describe it to a friend at a coffee shop.

**Good:** "I started using [Product] last month and it honestly saves me about 2 hours every week on email scheduling."
**Bad:** "[Product] is a revolutionary AI-powered platform that streamlines enterprise workflow optimization and drives measurable ROI."

Rules:
- Never paste the advertiser's press release or marketing copy verbatim
- Include what makes it relevant to YOUR specific readers
- If you've actually used the product, say so (credibility boost)
- If you haven't used it, write from the reader's perspective

### 4. Bulleted Benefits (3-4 max)

Specific outcomes, not vague claims.

**Good:**
- Cuts email production time from 4 hours to 45 minutes
- Free plan handles up to 5,000 subscribers
- Built-in A/B testing (no extra tool needed)

**Bad:**
- Streamlines your workflow
- Best-in-class technology
- Trusted by thousands

Frame benefits as reader advantages, not product features.

### 5. Clear CTA

One link, one action. Make the CTA specific to the offer, not generic.

**Good:** "Try [Product] free for 14 days"
**Good:** "Get 20% off with code CORSAIRS"
**Bad:** "Learn more"
**Bad:** "Click here"

If there's a special offer for your readers, lead with that.

## Placement Rules by Template

Where the sponsor block goes depends on which template you're using:

- **Roundup:** IMMEDIATELY after Headline Story #1. This is prime attention real estate. Readers are engaged but haven't hit fatigue yet.
- **Deep Dive / Listicle:** After Section 2 (the main content), before the conclusion. Reader has received value; the sponsor block is a natural pause.
- **Welsh Narrative:** Physically separated from editorial content. After the sign-off in Beat 8. Never embedded in the story.
- **Trends Report:** Between Trend #3 and Trend #4, or before the resource/download section.
- **Curation:** After the first 3 curated items, before Tools & Resources.

One sponsor block per issue. Two blocks = reader fatigue and lower CTR for both.

---

## CPM Guidance

- **Main sponsor block:** $25-50+ CPMs depending on list size and niche engagement
- **Quick Hits sponsor slots:** $5-15 CPMs (lower friction, higher volume, one line each)
- Native feel = higher CTR = justifies premium pricing over banner-style ads
- Write the ad in your own language when you can. You know your audience better than the advertiser.

---

## Anti-Patterns

- Pasting sponsor's marketing copy verbatim (readers smell it instantly)
- Multiple sponsor blocks in one issue (attention fatigue)
- Sponsor content longer than editorial sections (imbalance kills trust)
- Generic CTAs like "Learn more" (no urgency, no specificity)
- Banner-ad-style images (screams "ad," tanks engagement)
- Hiding the sponsor block at the very bottom (low visibility = low CPMs = sponsors don't renew)

## Own Product Promotion (Non-Sponsor Issues)

When no external sponsor, use the same 5-part block structure for your own products:

- Same anatomy (heading, visual, summary, benefits, CTA)
- Same placement rules
- Frame it as "This Week's Pick" or "Tool Spotlight" or just weave it naturally
- Link to your demo, artifact, or product page
- This is where bot-per-email demos go for Cyber Corsairs issues
