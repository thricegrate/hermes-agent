# Newsletter as Sales Engine (Cole/Bush)

Source: Nicolas Cole & Dickie Bush (Ship 30 for 30), Low-Ticket Product M&M Course, Module 7

---

## Core Philosophy

- The weekly newsletter is the **engine** that powers all evergreen digital product sales
- Your social content strategy IS your newsletter content strategy -- just "more" of it
- Whatever performs well on social, expand into 800-1,500 words for the newsletter
- Newsletters are less frequent (1x/week), more in-depth, and longer form than social posts
- Ship 30 benchmark: $30K-$100K+/month in evergreen sales from a single newsletter

## 10 Biggest Problems Framework

Frame every newsletter around 1 of the 10 biggest problems your target customer faces.

- First 10 newsletters = address each problem one by one (Problem 1 = Newsletter 1, etc.)
- After the first 10, revisit the same problems with new angles:
  - "Here's ANOTHER way to solve that problem."
  - "Here's ANOTHER framework you can use."
  - "Here's a DIFFERENT way of thinking about this."
  - "Here's a NEW strategy you can use."
- You never run out of content because you cycle through the same 10 problems endlessly

## 4 Non-Negotiables (Weekly Newsletter Checklist)

### 1. Word Count: 800-1,500 words
- Under 800 words = feels "thin"
- Over 1,500 words = feels like too big of a lift
- This range is the sweet spot after thousands of articles and newsletters

### 2. 1 Topic Per Newsletter
- Most common mistake = trying to tackle too many different ideas in a single newsletter
- Each newsletter should be its own standalone asset
- The more topics you cram in, the less helpful it becomes

### 3. Problem or Outcome Oriented
- Each newsletter speaks to ONE of two things:
  - 1 of the 10 Biggest Problems for your target reader/customer
  - 1 of the 10 Most Desirable Outcomes for your target reader/customer
- Help them solve problems and unlock outcomes FOR FREE
- The more you help for free, the more likely they become a customer later

### 4. Organic Mention of Digital Product
- Every newsletter includes at least 1 casual, organic mention of your product
- Three forms the mention can take:
  1. **Direct parenthetical** -- quick aside mentioning the product almost as an afterthought
  2. **Example reference** -- "For example, a framework we teach inside [Product] is..."
  3. **Natural recommendation** -- "If you want guidance on this, I recommend checking out [Product] because..."
- The mention should be barely noticeable -- casual, not promotional

## PS Hard-Pitch Strategy

**Frequency:** 1 out of every 4 newsletters (25%). This is separate from the organic mention inside the newsletter body.

### 5 PS Types

1. **Question/Answer** -- Surface a hidden objection as a question the reader is asking themselves. Answer it. Then explain how/why the product successfully addresses it.

2. **Direct Objection** -- State the objection head-on ("Worried you're not qualified enough?", "Think it's too late to start?"). Then help the reader overcome it.

3. **Testimonials** -- Direct people to social proof. Options:
   - Link to a master testimonial library on your website
   - Screenshot of multiple testimonials together
   - Single testimonial/success story most relevant to the newsletter topic

4. **Sneak Peek** -- Preview 1 module, asset, or template from the product. Let people "try" and experience the product. Dramatically increases buy likelihood because they understand quality and how it works.

5. **Waitlist CTA** -- CTA to a waitlist for:
   - A product before it goes live
   - A new product launching at some future date (open/close cart)
   - A product idea, cohort, or group coaching you are considering
   - Builds a segment of potential buyers long before you put something up for sale

## "Why Buy?" Framework

Answer the question: "Why would someone buy if you cover the same topics for free?"

**4 reasons people pay for products over free content:**

1. **Compiled** -- Everything they need about a specific topic, in one place
2. **Organized** -- Easy-to-follow structure, somewhere it "won't get lost"
3. **Compressed** -- Condensed form, learn 10x faster than teaching themselves
4. **Different** -- Explained differently, more in-depth, different examples, new assets

**Key insight:** 80% of the content inside a paid product exists elsewhere on the internet for free. People don't pay for the information -- they pay for the packaging.

**"Play the hits"** -- Your audience WANTS to hear you talk about the same problems and outcomes over and over again. Repetition is welcomed, not penalized. Just like listening to a favorite creator revisit the same ideas on YouTube or podcasts.

## The Mantra

> Share free value > Remind people you have something for sale > Repeat forever

This is the entire weekly newsletter strategy in one line.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Newsletter as hard-pitch only** -- The newsletter must be primarily free value. Hard pitches belong in the PS, and only 25% of the time.
- **Multiple topics per newsletter** -- Must be ONE topic. Cramming multiple ideas dilutes the value.
- **No product mentions** -- Leaving money on the table. Every newsletter should have at least 1 organic mention.
- **Fear of unsubscribes from product mentions** -- Faulty belief. Casual, organic mentions are barely noticeable. You are NOT hard-pitching in every email.
- **Making newsletter content completely separate from product content** -- Overlap is the point. The newsletter and the product should address the same 10 problems. The product packages it better (compiled, organized, compressed, different).

## Application Notes

- Start with 10 newsletters = 10 biggest problems, one per newsletter
- After the first 10, rotate through the same problems with fresh angles/frameworks
- Organic product mention goes INSIDE the newsletter body (every issue)
- PS hard-pitch goes at the END of the newsletter (1 in 4 issues only)
- Social content feeds newsletter topics -- expand what works on social into newsletter depth
