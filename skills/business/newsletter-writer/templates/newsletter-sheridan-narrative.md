# Sheridan Narrative Newsletter Template

A scene-driven newsletter template based on Taylor Sheridan's storytelling craft. One scene, one person, one question. No lessons. No listicles. No neat conclusions. The reader leaves thinking, not clicking.

Full framework reference: `skills/storyteller/references/sheridan-framework.md`

**How this differs from Welsh Narrative:** Welsh is story-to-lesson (8 beats ending with a bottom line + reply question). Sheridan is scene-to-question (7 beats ending with an open question + soft tool CTA). Use Welsh when you HAVE a lesson. Use Sheridan when you have a story that won't let go and you're still sitting with the question yourself.

---

## Voice Toggle (Pick One Per Issue)

**[ ] Captain YAR Voice** -- Sheridan structure + short sentences, blunt, self-deprecating, simple words. Read private voice-reference notes before writing. Sounds like: a buddy describing a scene he can't get out of his head.

**[ ] Sheridan Tone** -- Sheridan structure + spare, cinematic, observational. Sounds like: someone who saw something and is still processing it. Shorter than Welsh. Less reflective, more visceral.

---

## Pre-Writing Checklist

Before writing, answer these:

1. **What's the scene?** (Specific moment, place, sensory detail. What did you see/hear/feel?)
2. **Whose eyes?** (Who are we looking through? You? A client? A team member? A stranger?)
3. **What's missing?** (What information is withheld? What doesn't add up? Where's the gap?)
4. **What was the cost?** (What was lost, sacrificed, or can never be undone?)
5. **What's the question you're still sitting with?** (If you have a neat answer, use Welsh Narrative instead.)

## The 7-Beat Template

### Beat 1: THE WINDOW
<!-- 1-3 sentences. Drop reader into a specific moment in a specific world. -->
<!-- No context. No setup. No "let me tell you about..." -->
<!-- The reader is looking through a window before they know what they're seeing. -->
<!-- Include at least two: place name, time, sensory detail, specific action, weather, sound -->

[SCENE: Where are you? What are you seeing/hearing/doing? What's the first thing you notice?]


### Beat 2: THE SURROGATE
<!-- 1-2 sentences. Introduce the person we see through. -->
<!-- This person doesn't have to be the hero. They have to be the one experiencing what we need to feel. -->
<!-- Reveal one telling detail about them -- something that shows who they are without explaining it. -->

[WHO: Who are we following? What do they want? What one detail tells us who they are?]


### Beat 3: THE SIMPLE ENGINE
<!-- 1-2 sentences. The situation in the simplest possible terms. -->
<!-- If your premise needs more than two sentences to explain, simplify it. -->
<!-- The simpler the engine, the more room for what matters. -->

[SITUATION: What's happening? One sentence. Maybe two. That's it.]


### Beat 4: THE GAP
<!-- 2-4 sentences. Build tension through what's missing. -->
<!-- Something doesn't add up. Information is withheld. The reader leans in. -->
<!-- Use "but" and "therefore" transitions. Never "and then." -->
<!-- Don't resolve the tension. Let it build. -->

[TENSION: What doesn't add up? What's the reader leaning in to find out? What do you NOT explain here?]


### Beat 5: THE REVEAL
<!-- 2-4 sentences. Let behavior show truth. -->
<!-- No explanation. No "here's what I realized." -->
<!-- Show what happened. Show what someone DID. The meaning arrives through action. -->
<!-- This is the Sheridan principle: camera shows, character doesn't tell. -->

[ACTION: What happened? What did someone DO that showed the truth? Don't explain it. Just show it.]


### Beat 6: THE COST
<!-- 1-3 sentences. Name the price. -->
<!-- What was lost? What was sacrificed? What can never be undone? -->
<!-- Nothing in a good story comes free. The cost is what makes it true. -->

[PRICE: What did this cost? What was given up? What changed permanently?]


### Beat 7: THE QUESTION
<!-- 1-2 sentences. End with a question, not a lesson. -->
<!-- This is NOT "What do you think?" or "Let me know your thoughts." -->
<!-- This is the specific, uncomfortable question you can't stop thinking about. -->
<!-- The reader should close the email and still be sitting with this. -->

[OPEN QUESTION: What are you still wondering? What has no neat answer? What would you ask the reader that makes them pause?]

**[TOOL CTA -- separated from editorial, thematic connection to the question, not the answer]**

## Post-Writing Quality Checklist

Run through these before publishing:

- [ ] **Window test**: Does it open with a scene? (Not a tip, not a claim, not a question, not a statistic)
- [ ] **Surrogate test**: Is there ONE person whose eyes we see through the whole piece?
- [ ] **Simplicity test**: Can you state the situation in one sentence?
- [ ] **Gap test**: Is there a moment where information is withheld and the reader leans in?
- [ ] **Show test**: Does the reveal come through behavior/action, not explanation?
- [ ] **Cost test**: Is there a real price named? (Not "I learned a valuable lesson" -- what was LOST?)
- [ ] **Question test**: Does it end with a question you genuinely don't have a neat answer to?
- [ ] **No-preaching test**: Is the writer an observer, not a teacher? (Delete any sentence that starts with "The lesson here is...")
- [ ] **Silence test**: Is there at least one moment where what's NOT said carries more weight than what is?
- [ ] **CTA separation**: Is the product CTA physically and tonally separate from the editorial?
- [ ] **Word count**: 600-900 words? (Sheridan runs leaner than Welsh -- fewer beats, more impact per word)
- [ ] **Voice consistency**: Does the writing match the selected voice toggle?
- [ ] **But/therefore**: All transitions use conflict/consequence, never "and then"?
- [ ] **Banned words check**: Run against `references/banned-words.md`?
- [ ] **Coffee shop rule**: Would you tell this story exactly this way to a friend at a cafe?

## Subject Line Guidance

Sheridan subject lines use subtext. The subject line hints at the scene without revealing the story. The reader opens because they need to see what's behind the window.

Patterns:
- **The Detail**: A specific object or moment from the scene. "The parking lot." "Tuesday at 3 PM." "Two missed calls."
- **The Absence**: What's missing. "Nobody said anything." "The email I never sent." "Silence."
- **The Cost**: The price, named directly. "I lost the account." "Three months." "$40K."
- **The Question Fragment**: An incomplete question. "What would you have..." "Was it worth..."

A/B test against a standard hook-style subject line.
