# Conversion Psychology Reference

Frameworks from the Automatic Clients methodology for writing copy that converts
at every stage of the funnel.

---

## The 5 Awareness Levels

Every prospect sits at one of five awareness levels. Your copy must match their level.
Writing for the wrong level kills conversion.

### Level 1: Unaware
- **State**: They don't know they have a problem
- **Where**: Cold traffic, interruption-based ads (social media scrolling)
- **Copy approach**: Lead with curiosity or a pattern interrupt. Don't mention your product.
- **Example headline**: "Why 90% of agency owners never break $20K/month"

### Level 2: Problem Aware
- **State**: They know the problem, but not the solution
- **Where**: People who clicked an ad, searched a pain-point keyword
- **Copy approach**: Lead with the problem. Agitate it. Show you understand.
- **Example headline**: "Tired of chasing clients? There's a better way."

### Level 3: Solution Aware
- **State**: They know solutions exist, but not YOUR solution
- **Where**: People comparing options, researching approaches
- **Copy approach**: Lead with your unique mechanism. Why YOUR approach is different.
- **Example headline**: "The 3-step system that gets 8+ clients/month on autopilot"

### Level 4: Product Aware
- **State**: They know YOUR product, evaluating whether to buy
- **Where**: People on your sales page, reading your emails, consumed your content
- **Copy approach**: Lead with proof, social proof, guarantees, and bonuses. They need
  reasons to buy NOW.
- **Example headline**: "[Product Name]: Join 2,000+ agency owners who..."

### Level 5: Most Aware
- **State**: They know your product and are ready to buy, just need a push
- **Where**: People who visited your sales page multiple times, cart abandoners
- **Copy approach**: Lead with the offer. Price, deal, urgency, guarantee.
- **Example headline**: "Last chance: [Product Name] - $47 (price goes up Friday)"

### Matching Copy to Awareness:
| Page Type | Target Awareness | Lead With |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Ad | Unaware / Problem Aware | Curiosity / Pain |
| Main sales page | Problem Aware / Solution Aware | Problem then unique mechanism |
| OTO page | Product Aware (just bought) | Complementary offer, urgency |
| Retargeting ad | Product Aware | Social proof, urgency |
| Call booking page | Product Aware / Most Aware | Results, qualification |
| Email sequence | Solution Aware -> Product Aware | Education, case studies, proof |

## "Salesmanship in Print"

Advertising is salesmanship in print (John E. Kennedy, 1905). Every piece of copy
should work the way a great salesperson works:

1. **Get attention** (headline = opening line)
2. **Identify the problem** (problem section = discovery questions)
3. **Present the solution** (solution section = recommendation)
4. **Prove it works** (social proof = references and case studies)
5. **Handle objections** (FAQ = objection handling)
6. **Ask for the sale** (CTA = the close)

The copy is not creative writing. It is a sales conversation in written form.
Judge every line by: "Does this advance the sale?" If not, cut it.

## Ad-to-Page Congruence

The #1 conversion killer: disconnect between the ad and the landing page.

### The Rule:
Whatever the ad promises, the landing page must deliver IMMEDIATELY in the
headline and above-the-fold content. The visitor should never think "wait,
is this what I clicked on?"

### Congruence checklist:
- [ ] Same language/phrasing used in ad appears on the page
- [ ] Same visual style (colors, imagery) carries through
- [ ] The promise in the ad is the headline on the page
- [ ] If the ad mentions a specific problem, the page leads with that problem
- [ ] If the ad uses a specific number, the page references that number

### Why this matters for optimization:
When your sales page isn't converting, check congruence FIRST before rewriting
the page. Often the issue is that the ad attracted the wrong audience or set
the wrong expectations, not that the page copy is bad.

## Proof Hierarchy by Price Point

Different price points require different types and amounts of proof:

### Low-ticket ($5-97):
- **Minimum proof**: Personal credibility statement, 1-2 social proof elements
- **Best proof types**: Screenshots of results, "as seen in" logos, audience size
- **Risk is low**: Buyer doesn't need much convincing. Remove friction, keep it simple.

### Mid-ticket ($99-350):
- **Minimum proof**: 3-5 testimonials with specific results
- **Best proof types**: Before/after case studies, student results, video testimonials
- **Risk is moderate**: Buyer needs to believe in the method AND your ability to teach it.

### High-ticket ($3K+):
- **Minimum proof**: Deep case studies, verifiable results, credentials, guarantee
- **Best proof types**: Detailed client stories (problem, process, result), ROI calculations,
  video testimonials with real names and businesses, media features
- **Risk is high**: Buyer needs to trust you with significant investment. Every proof element
  should answer: "Can I trust this person with $X?"

### Proof escalation strategy:
Use more proof as the price goes up. A $5 book needs 1 proof element. A $10,000
coaching program needs 10+ proof elements across multiple formats.

## The Price Anchor Framework

Never present the price in isolation. Always anchor it against something more expensive:

### Anchor patterns:
1. **Value anchor**: "Total value: $1,247. Your price: $47." (most common, works well for low-ticket)
2. **Comparison anchor**: "Other programs charge $2,000+ for this. You get it for $97." (use sparingly)
3. **ROI anchor**: "If this helps you land just ONE client worth $3,000, your $47 investment pays for itself 63x over." (best for any price point)
4. **Time anchor**: "This replaces 40+ hours of trial and error. At $100/hour, that's $4,000 in saved time." (good for tool/template offers)
5. **Alternative anchor**: "You could hire a consultant for $500/hour. Or get their exact framework for $47." (great for methodology-based products)

### Rules:
- Pick ONE primary anchor and use it consistently
- The anchor must be believable (don't claim $10,000 value for a $27 ebook)
- The ROI anchor is the strongest because it's about THEIR money, not your pricing
