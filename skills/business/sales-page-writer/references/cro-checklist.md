# CRO Checklist Reference

Conversion rate optimization checklists, common mistakes with fixes, CTA formulas, and trust signal hierarchy. Synthesized from SplitBase's 24-point framework, Conversion Alchemy, and copywriting transcript analysis.

Use this as a pre-launch audit, a post-launch optimization queue, and a diagnostic tool when a page is not converting.

---

## The 24-Point CRO Success Checklist (SplitBase, 2025)

Run this before sending any traffic to a page. Each unchecked item is a known conversion leak. Fix all 24 before launch. If you cannot fix them all before launch, prioritize by category in the order listed.

### CATEGORY 1: Above the Fold (Priority: Critical)

These five determine whether any visitor stays past the first 3-5 seconds. Failures here lose visitors who never see the rest of the page.

- [ ] **Clear, benefit-focused headline**
  - Pass: Headline states a specific outcome or names a specific problem. A stranger can understand what this page is about and why they should care in under 5 seconds.
  - Fail: Headline is a product name, company name, or vague claim ("The best solution for your business").
  - Why it matters: The headline is the first thing every visitor reads. If it fails, nothing else gets read.

- [ ] **Supporting sub-headline that builds on the headline**
  - Pass: Sub-headline adds information -- who this is for, a key detail about the offer, or a proof statement. It does not restate the headline.
  - Fail: Sub-headline repeats the same information with different words, or is absent entirely.
  - Why it matters: Visitors who are on the fence after the headline make their decision based on the sub-headline.

- [ ] **Hero image or video that supports the offer**
  - Pass: Hero visual shows the product in use, the result of using it, or the person the visitor could become. It adds context to the headline rather than competing with it.
  - Fail: Generic stock photo of smiling people in an office. Abstract design that communicates nothing about the product.
  - Why it matters: Visuals are processed faster than copy. A mismatched or irrelevant hero visual creates immediate cognitive friction.

- [ ] **Primary CTA visible without scrolling**
  - Pass: CTA button is fully visible on the screen without any scrolling, on both desktop and mobile (test both).
  - Fail: CTA is anywhere below the fold on the first view.
  - Why it matters: Visitors who need to scroll to find the CTA -- whether from high intent or impulse -- have to work for it. They often do not.

- [ ] **Social proof elements visible above the fold**
  - Pass: At minimum one of: customer count ("12,000+ customers"), star rating ("4.9 from 3,200 reviews"), recognizable client logos, or a brief testimonial snippet.
  - Fail: Page opens with no social proof signal above the fold.
  - Why it matters: Social proof is a trust signal that makes visitors more willing to continue. Leading without it forces visitors to take the brand's word for everything.

---

### CATEGORY 2: Copy and Messaging (Priority: High)

- [ ] **Copy addresses customer pain points and desires directly**
  - Pass: The copy describes specific problems the reader has in language they would use themselves. Visitors should think "this is exactly me."
  - Fail: Copy describes the product's features or the company's history. No pain points named.
  - Why it matters: Visitors do not connect with products. They connect with their own problems being understood.

- [ ] **Benefits stated clearly, not just features**
  - Pass: Every feature on the page is paired with what it means for the customer's life. "Automated follow-up" is not a benefit. "Your follow-up runs automatically while you sleep" is a benefit.
  - Fail: Feature lists with no benefit translation.
  - Why it matters: Features describe the product. Benefits describe the change in the customer's life. People buy the change, not the product.

- [ ] **Value proposition is unique and differentiated**
  - Pass: The page communicates at least one dimension in which this offer is specifically better, faster, or different from alternatives -- with evidence, not adjectives.
  - Fail: Could be describing any product in the category. No differentiation claimed or proven.
  - Why it matters: In any category with competition, "we're great" does not stand out. Specific contrast does.

- [ ] **Objections addressed throughout the page, not just at the bottom**
  - Pass: Major objections (trust, price, fit, risk, time) are handled at the natural point in the page where they arise, not saved for the FAQ section.
  - Fail: The entire page is positive claims followed by an FAQ section where all doubt is addressed. This leaves objections unhandled for the duration of the page.
  - Why it matters: Objections form progressively as a visitor reads. Address them when they form, not after.

- [ ] **Urgency or scarcity used where genuine and appropriate**
  - Pass: If there is a real deadline, limited quantity, or expiring offer, it is stated clearly. If not, none is invented.
  - Fail: "Limited time offer!" with no deadline. "Only X left!" for a digital product with unlimited supply.
  - Why it matters: Fake scarcity gets detected by savvy buyers and destroys trust. Real scarcity is a legitimate conversion driver.

### CATEGORY 3: Trust and Credibility (Priority: High)

- [ ] **Customer testimonials include real photos and full names**
  - Pass: Testimonials show full first and last name plus a photo. If possible, include job title, location, or other identifying detail.
  - Fail: "J.T. from California." Generic first name with no photo. Or testimonials with photos that look like stock images.
  - Why it matters: Unverifiable testimonials are indistinguishable from made-up ones. Real details make them checkable, and checkability makes them credible.

- [ ] **Trust badges, certifications, or media mentions visible**
  - Pass: Security badges (SSL, payment processor logos), industry certifications, or "as seen in" media logos are visible -- especially near the purchase area.
  - Fail: Trust badges buried in the footer. No trust badges at all. Fake or unrecognizable badges.
  - Why it matters: Trust badges provide visual confirmation that a transaction is safe. Absence of them triggers security anxiety near payment.

- [ ] **Money-back guarantee or risk reversal clearly stated**
  - Pass: Guarantee is stated in specific terms: "30-day full refund, no questions asked" or "Complete all modules. If you don't see results, we'll refund every penny." The guarantee is prominently visible, not in fine print.
  - Fail: Vague "satisfaction guaranteed" language. Guarantee buried below the fold or in terms-of-service text.
  - Why it matters: A specific, visible guarantee removes the primary financial risk objection before it stops the purchase.

- [ ] **Real product images used, not stock photography**
  - Pass: Product photos show the actual product, results, or real customers. Screenshots or screencasts for digital products show the real interface.
  - Fail: Generic stock photos substituting for product images.
  - Why it matters: Real product images increase perceived product quality. Stock images signal that no real product exists or that the seller cannot afford to show it.

### CATEGORY 4: User Experience (Priority: High)

- [ ] **Page loads in under 3 seconds**
  - Pass: Page load time is 3 seconds or under on a standard mobile connection (test with Google PageSpeed Insights or GTmetrix).
  - Fail: Page takes more than 3 seconds to load.
  - Why it matters: Google research shows that 53% of mobile visitors abandon pages that take more than 3 seconds to load. Every second of delay costs conversions before a visitor reads a word.

- [ ] **Mobile design is fully optimized**
  - Pass: Page is tested on an actual phone, not just a browser emulator. All text is readable. Buttons are large enough to tap. No horizontal scrolling. CTAs are visible above the fold on mobile.
  - Fail: Desktop page scaled down for mobile. Elements overflow on small screens. Buttons require precise tapping.
  - Why it matters: More than 60% of landing page traffic arrives on mobile. Desktop-first pages consistently underperform on the primary traffic format.

- [ ] **Navigation is minimal -- distractions removed**
  - Pass: No main navigation menu on the landing page. No links that take visitors away from the page before they convert.
  - Fail: Full site navigation visible at the top. Internal links throughout the copy. Header menus with dropdown options.
  - Why it matters: Every navigation option is an escape route. Landing pages exist to convert one specific visitor to one specific action. Navigation undermines that.

- [ ] **Form fields are minimal**
  - Pass: The form asks only for information essential to the conversion: email for a lead magnet, name + email + payment for a purchase. No unnecessary fields.
  - Fail: Long forms asking for phone number, company name, job title, or annual revenue for a simple opt-in.
  - Why it matters: Each additional form field reduces completion rate. Ask only for what you need.

- [ ] **CTA button stands out visually**
  - Pass: The CTA button is a high-contrast color against the page background. It is large enough to be immediately identifiable as the primary action. No other element competes with it visually.
  - Fail: CTA button color matches the page color scheme (no contrast). CTA is the same size and weight as other text elements on the page.
  - Why it matters: Visitors who intend to convert must be able to find the CTA instantly. Visual prominence is the difference between a conversion and an abandoned intent.

### CATEGORY 5: Technical and Testing (Priority: Medium -- Fix After Launch)

- [ ] **Analytics tracking is set up and verified**
  - Pass: Google Analytics (or equivalent) is installed and verified. Conversion events are firing correctly.
  - Fail: No analytics. Analytics installed but not verified. Conversion events not configured.
  - Why it matters: Without conversion tracking, you cannot measure performance, identify improvement opportunities, or justify optimization work.

- [ ] **Heatmaps and session recordings are active**
  - Pass: Hotjar, Microsoft Clarity, or equivalent is installed. Session recordings are collecting. Heatmaps are set up on the primary landing page.
  - Fail: No heatmaps. No session recordings.
  - Why it matters: Heatmaps and recordings show where visitors scroll to, what they click on, and where they leave. This is the diagnostic layer that reveals WHY a page is underperforming.

- [ ] **A/B tests running on key elements**
  - Pass: An active A/B test is running on at minimum the headline, CTA copy, or hero image.
  - Fail: No active tests. "We tried two headlines once" does not count as an optimization program.
  - Why it matters: Optimization without testing is opinion. Testing converts opinions into data.

- [ ] **Page tested across devices and major browsers**
  - Pass: Page is tested on Chrome, Safari, and Firefox on both desktop and mobile before any traffic is sent.
  - Fail: Tested only on the developer's machine.
  - Why it matters: Browser-specific rendering issues cause invisible conversion leaks. A broken page on Safari or Chrome mobile loses a material percentage of visitors before they can convert.

- [ ] **Exit-intent mechanisms in place**
  - Pass: An exit-intent popup or mechanism captures visitors who are about to leave. The exit offer is relevant (a second chance at the same offer, a downgrade offer, or a lead magnet).
  - Fail: No exit mechanism. Visitors who decide to leave are permanently lost.
  - Why it matters: Exit-intent mechanisms can recover 10-15% of visitors who would otherwise leave without converting. They are among the highest-ROI additions to a landing page.

## Pre-Launch Validation Checklist

Run this immediately before sending any traffic. These are the checks most likely to catch problems that kill first-impression conversions.

**Copy clarity test:**
- [ ] Can someone who has never heard of this product read the page and accurately explain the offer, who it is for, and what they need to do in under 60 seconds? If not, the headline or structure needs work.

**Trust signal inventory:**
- [ ] At least one social proof element is visible without scrolling
- [ ] The guarantee is stated specifically (dollar amount, timeframe, conditions)
- [ ] Testimonials include real names and photos
- [ ] Payment security signals are visible near the form

**CTA visibility test:**
- [ ] CTA is visible above the fold on a standard phone screen
- [ ] CTA button is high-contrast against background
- [ ] CTA copy states what the visitor gets, not what they do

**Mobile experience test:**
- [ ] Page loaded on an actual phone, not a simulator
- [ ] All text is legible without zooming
- [ ] Buttons are large enough to tap with a thumb
- [ ] No horizontal overflow or scroll

**Load speed check:**
- [ ] Page scores 80+ on Google PageSpeed mobile test
- [ ] All images are compressed
- [ ] No unoptimized video autoplay on mobile

## Post-Launch Optimization Priority Queue

When a page is live and underperforming, test in this order. Each item has progressively more complex implementation -- start simple.

### Priority 1: Headline (Test First)
The headline has the highest impact per word of any element on the page. A headline change can move conversion rate by 20-50%. Run this test first because the result informs everything else.

What to test: Benefit-first vs. problem-first headline. Specific claim vs. open question. Outcome-focused vs. transformation-focused.

### Priority 2: CTA Copy
CTA copy is the second-highest impact element and the easiest to change. One-word changes to button text have produced 10-30% conversion lifts in documented A/B tests.

What to test: Generic action word ("Buy Now") vs. outcome statement ("Lose 5 Pounds of Fat"). Brand voice ("Yes, I Want Access") vs. direct command ("Get Instant Access"). Short copy vs. long copy.

### Priority 3: Hero Visual
The hero image communicates before copy is read. A mismatched or irrelevant visual creates friction that copy cannot overcome.

What to test: Product-in-use vs. result/transformation image. Person-facing-camera vs. person-in-context. Lifestyle image vs. product mockup.

### Priority 4: Social Proof Placement
If proof is siloed in one section, test distributing it. A testimonial adjacent to the CTA, proof signals in the hero, and a case study near the guarantee can each lift conversion.

What to test: Move strongest testimonial to directly above CTA. Add customer count to hero. Test specific number vs. vague volume language.

### Priority 5: Guarantee Prominence
If the guarantee is present but not prominent, move it. Test placing it directly above the order form. Test stating it in a visual callout box instead of running text.

### Priority 6: Page Length
Long-form and short-form pages each win in different contexts. Test a condensed version (hero + proof + CTA + short FAQ) against the full page for high-awareness traffic.

## 10 Common Landing Page Mistakes: Before/After Fixes

### Mistake 1: Weak or Vague Headline

Before: "The Ultimate Business Growth System"
After: "Add 3 New Clients Every Month Using a 20-Minute Daily Prospecting Routine"

What changed: Removed abstract claim. Added specifics: frequency (monthly), number (3 clients), and mechanism (20-minute daily routine).

Fix process: Replace every adjective ("ultimate," "powerful," "innovative") with a specific number or concrete claim. If you cannot make it specific, you do not understand your offer well enough to write for it.

---

### Mistake 2: Too Many CTAs

Before: Five different buttons: "Learn More," "Download the Guide," "Book a Call," "See Pricing," and "Watch the Demo"
After: One primary CTA: "Get Instant Access" (and one secondary "Book a Demo Call" for enterprise visitors)

What changed: Eliminated decision paralysis. The Rule of 1: one offer, one action, one CTA on any page. Multiple CTAs make visitors choose between actions instead of choosing to act.

---

### Mistake 3: Missing Social Proof or Weak Social Proof

Before: "Thousands of satisfied customers love our product!"
After: "14,217 marketers have used this system to reduce their content creation time by 60%." Plus three testimonials with full names, photos, and specific results.

What changed: Vague volume language replaced with specific number. Generic praise replaced with results-based testimonials from identifiable real people.

---

### Mistake 4: No Risk Reversal

Before: Page makes the offer with no mention of what happens if the product doesn't work.
After: Visible guarantee box: "Try it for 30 days. If you're not satisfied for any reason, email us once and we'll refund every dollar. No questions asked, no hoops to jump through."

What changed: Added explicit risk removal at the point of purchase. The guarantee converts fence-sitters by eliminating the financial risk of being wrong.

---

### Mistake 5: Long-Form FAQ in the Wrong Position

Before: Detailed FAQ section placed above social proof and the order form.
After: FAQ section placed after the order form, after social proof. FAQs are the last resort -- for visitors who almost converted but have one remaining question. They should not appear before the primary conversion elements.

What changed: Repositioned FAQ to resolve final objections, not introduce doubt early.

---

### Mistake 6: Mismatched Message from Ad to Page

Before: Ad promises "50% off your first order." Landing page headline: "Premium Dog Food for Healthy, Happy Dogs."
After: Landing page headline mirrors the ad exactly: "Get 50% Off Your First Box of Fresh, Human-Grade Dog Food."

What changed: The ad created an expectation. The page now confirms it immediately. Zero cognitive friction. (See Farmers Dog case study in `landing-page-architecture.md`)

---

### Mistake 7: No Mobile Optimization

Before: Desktop page scaled to mobile. Text too small to read. Buttons too small to tap. CTA below the fold.
After: Mobile-first design. Headline readable at 16px minimum. Buttons 44px height minimum. CTA visible in the top 500px on mobile.

What changed: The page was tested on an actual phone before launch. More than 60% of paid traffic arrives on mobile -- an unoptimized mobile page fails the majority of visitors.

---

### Mistake 8: Slow Load Time Killing Conversions

Before: Page loads in 7 seconds due to unoptimized images and uncompressed CSS.
After: Images compressed, CSS minified, page loads in 2.1 seconds.

What changed: Every second above 3 seconds costs approximately 7% of conversions (Google research). A 7-second page may be losing 30-40% of visitors before they read a word.

---

### Mistake 9: Buried CTA

Before: CTA button appears once, at the bottom of a 2,000-word page.
After: CTA appears in the hero section (above fold), after the benefits section, after social proof, and at the bottom. High-intent visitors can convert without reading every word.

What changed: CTAs appear at each natural decision point. Multiple CTA placements do not split focus -- they catch visitors at multiple readiness levels.

---

### Mistake 10: Feature-Focused Instead of Benefit-Focused

Before: "Includes a 12-module curriculum covering funnel architecture, email automation, split testing, retargeting, and cross-sell sequence optimization."
After: "You'll have a complete, running client acquisition funnel by the end of Module 3 -- and a full automated email sequence by Module 7 -- without needing a developer or agency."

What changed: Features (curriculum modules, topics) replaced with outcomes (what the visitor will have built and by when). Every feature was translated into a specific, tangible result.

## CTA Copy Formulas

CTAs are where conversion happens or does not. The difference between a 2% click rate and a 6% click rate can be one word.

### The Four Elements of a High-Converting CTA
1. **Specificity**: States what the visitor gets, not what they do. "Get the SEO Checklist" beats "Download Now."
2. **Benefit**: Contains the outcome or value in the button text itself. "Start Losing Weight Today" beats "Sign Up."
3. **Zero friction language**: Removes the last objection in the micro-copy beneath the button. "No credit card needed." "Cancel anytime." "Instant access."
4. **Active voice**: Command structure with a strong action verb first. Start with: Get, Start, Claim, Unlock, Download, Join, Build, Discover, Access.

### CTA Formula Templates by Context

**For lead magnets (free offer):**
- "Send Me the [Name] Blueprint"
- "Show Me the [Outcome] Framework"
- "Get My Free [Specific Name]"
- "Yes, I Want [Specific Benefit]"

**For low-ticket purchases ($7-97):**
- "Get Instant Access for Just $[Price]"
- "Claim My Spot (Only $[Price])"
- "Start [Achieving Outcome] Today -- $[Price]"
- "Give Me the [Name] System"

**For mid-ticket purchases ($99-500):**
- "Join [Number]+ [Audience Type] Inside [Product Name]"
- "Yes, I Want to [Achieve Outcome] -- $[Price]"
- "Enroll in [Product Name] Now"
- "Get [Specific Deliverable] + [Bonus] for $[Price]"

**For high-ticket / call booking:**
- "Book My Free [Outcome] Call"
- "Apply for [Program Name]"
- "Schedule My Strategy Session"

**Customer-voice alternatives (first person):**
- "I Need This" (replaces "Buy Now")
- "Count Me In" (replaces "Subscribe")
- "Show Me How It Works" (replaces "Learn More")
- "Send It Over" (replaces "Submit")

### What Makes a CTA Fail
- Passive voice: "Enrollment is open" -- no urgency, no command
- Generic action verbs with no context: "Click here" / "Submit" / "Register"
- Describing what the visitor does instead of what they get: "Purchase the Course" vs. "Get Instant Access to the Full Course"
- No friction-reducing micro-copy beneath the button
- Visual camouflage -- button same color as background

## Trust Signal Hierarchy

Not all trust signals are equal. Place the most powerful signals at the most critical moments.

### Tier 1: Highest-Impact Trust Signals
These signals have the greatest effect on conversion and should be placed in the hero section and near the order form.

1. **Specific customer testimonials with real photos, full names, and measurable results** -- "I went from 0 to 4 clients in 6 weeks using this exact system. -- Jamie Torres, freelance designer, Austin TX." Real name. Real photo. Specific result. Specific timeframe. Specific detail.

2. **Specific customer volume with a number** -- "14,217 students enrolled" beats "thousands of students." The more specific, the more credible. Update regularly.

3. **Media logos ("As seen in...")** -- Only use logos from outlets the reader will recognize. An unknown blog logo provides zero trust value.

### Tier 2: Important Supporting Signals
Place these in the social proof section and near the guarantee.

4. **Case studies with named individuals and verifiable outcomes** -- Before situation, what they did, measurable after result. More powerful than testimonials because they tell a story with context.

5. **Guarantee (specific terms, not vague language)** -- "30-day full refund, no questions asked" is a trust signal. "Satisfaction guaranteed" is noise.

6. **Third-party reviews or ratings** -- Star ratings with a review count from a recognized platform (Google, Trustpilot, App Store) carry more weight than self-reported ratings.

### Tier 3: Baseline Signals
Present but low-impact on their own. They do not convert fence-sitters but their absence signals something is wrong.

7. **SSL/security badges** -- Essential near payment forms. Their absence triggers anxiety. Their presence is expected (not differentiating).

8. **Payment logos** -- Visa/Mastercard/PayPal logos confirm familiar payment methods are accepted.

9. **Privacy assurance** -- "Your information is never sold or shared." Standard, but visitors notice its absence.

### Signal Placement Map

| Location | Required Signals |
|---|---|
| Hero section | Customer volume, star rating or review count |
| Benefits section | Relevant testimonial matching the benefit |
| Social proof section | 3+ full testimonials, case study, media logos |
| Adjacent to CTA | Short testimonial, guarantee reminder |
| Order form | SSL badge, payment logos, guarantee, testimonial |
| FAQ section | Volume proof, relevant case study |
