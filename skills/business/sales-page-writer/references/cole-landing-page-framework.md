# 13-Section Landing Page Framework (Cole/Bush)

Source: Nicolas Cole & Dickie Bush (Ship 30 for 30), Low-Ticket Product M&M Course, Module 4

---

## Overview

This is the proven landing page framework Cole and Bush use for every low-ticket digital product launch. It generated ~$468K for their Full Stack Writer product ($438K in the first week + ~$30K from 2-payment plans hitting 30 days later). The product was priced at $350.

The framework has 13 sections in a specific order. The ordering is not arbitrary -- it follows a psychological sequence where education and problem awareness come BEFORE any product discussion.

**The Critical Ordering Principle:** Sections 1-5 must happen BEFORE you talk about the product. The customer cannot care about the solution until they first care about the problem. Repeat that until it sticks. You should not even start talking about product features/benefits until you have:

1. Made an Offer
2. Named 3 customer archetypes
3. Quickly introduced yourself
4. Listed 10 Biggest Problems
5. Educated on 3 Biggest Mistakes

Only THEN do you earn the right to talk about your digital product, what it does, how it works.

---

## The 13 Sections

### Section 1: The Offer (Hero)

The very first section is arguably the most important. This is the FIRST thing the customer sees. This is the POSITIONING of the product. This is where you present the OFFER and value proposition.

The goal: get as many of the 10-Point Offer Creation Checklist items into this section as possible. The more you can speak to, the more the value becomes obvious.

**What goes here:**
- Strong headline communicating the value proposition
- Subheadline expanding on the offer
- Optionally a VSL or high-quality product mockup
- As many of the 10 Offer Checklist points as you can fit

**There is no one "right" answer.** The game is simply to speak to as many checklist pieces as possible in this opening section.

### Section 2: Quiz (Customer Archetypes)

Not actually a quiz. It is a section where you name ~3 different archetypes of people who would benefit from your product.

**What this section does:** Frames the value slightly differently for 3 types of people who are most likely to get the most value. The product itself does not change -- the framing changes per archetype.

**Full Stack Writer example archetypes:**
- Someone brand new to writing online
- Someone who has been writing online but struggling to see results
- Someone who feels their career is at-risk from AI and wants to acquire digital writing and monetization skills

**Bonus points:** Throw a testimonial somewhere in this section (proof!).

### Section 3: Your Instructors (Credibility)

Introduce yourself. But notice the ordering -- YOU are not the very first thing on the landing page. The OFFER is the first thing. Who "you" are matters significantly less. Once the customer understands what you are offering, and whether it is for them (Quiz), THEN you introduce yourself.

**Recommendations:**
- Professional headshot
- Short bio -- anything you have "done" that is relevant credibility to this product
- Short bulleted list of other metrics/accomplishments that give additional context as to why you are "worth listening to" on this topic

### Section 4: Problems, Solved!

The golden rule: The customer cannot care about the SOLUTION until they first care about the PROBLEM.

This is where you talk about the 10 Biggest Problems you identified in your niche research.

**Format:**
- Present the 10 Big Problems
- Give a 1-sentence explanation next to each one about what it is / why the customer has it
- At the end, simply say something like: "I show you how to demolish all of these, one by one."

**Note:** Full Stack Writer focused on 7 problems because the entire product was positioned around "7 skills, 7 revenue streams, 7-figure potential" -- but the same logic applies whether you use 7 or 10.

### Section 5: Mistakes (x3)

Now educate the customer EVEN MORE. People want to know what to DO, but they also want to know what NOT TO DO to avoid wasting time, making mistakes, and getting embarrassed.

**Structure:** Spend 3 entire sections (subsections within this area) educating the customer on the 3 biggest mistakes people just like them make.

**This accomplishes 2 things:**
1. Educates the customer on what will happen if they remain uneducated (i.e., they do not buy your product)
2. Educates the customer on where so many other people go wrong (i.e., how they will immediately be "further ahead" if they buy your product)

Each mistake gets its own visual section with explanation.

### Section 6: How It Works

This is the prelude to the product sections that follow. You are just beginning the introduction of "how the product works."

**Remember:** Notice how much education needs to be done BEFORE you even start talking about your product. A lot. This section is the transition point between the education/problem segments and the product segments.

### Section 7: Curriculum Overview

NOW it is time to talk about your digital product. The goal is to make each part of the product feel as tangible as possible.

**Do NOT just say "Here's what each module is about." Instead:**
- Break each module (each "action step") out as its own section
- Give a little explanation about why each module is so important/valuable
- Share 3-5 bullets/checkmarks of TANGIBLE assets included in each module
- Include some sort of screenshot so the customer can "see" this part of the product (making it tangible)

**The end goal:** After the reader has gone through individual sections for EVERY module, they should feel like they are getting TEN products -- because each module has been made to feel incredibly valuable and tangible on its own.

### Section 8: Total Perceived Value (Price Anchoring)

A pretty advanced but very effective price anchoring tactic.

If you followed the instructions and made each individual module FEEL tangible, you can now put a price/value ON each module. The customer cannot feel and internalize that value unless you put a number on it.

**The tactic:**
1. Make each module tangible (done in Section 7)
2. Associate a price tag with each module (as if selling each module standalone, or what you feel the information is "worth")
3. Add all the price tags up

**What this does:** Price anchors the customer to the TOTAL perceived value of everything included.

**Full Stack Writer example:** All the information inside was easily worth $4,000+. "But we're giving you access for $350."

**Price Anchoring defined:** You say a bigger number first -- so that the real number sounds small. This is Business 101. Grocery stores price anchor you all the time. Why do you think the really expensive salmon is right next to the cheap farm-raised half-frozen salmon?

### Section 9: Bonuses

Even though price anchoring makes your product seem like a steal, what else can you do to make the price feel even more like a bargain? Bonuses.

**The same idea, except you are doing 2 things:**
1. Adding in "more, but different" free TANGIBLE assets
2. Associating price tags with each bonus -- so the perceived value keeps going up

**Guidelines:**
- Aim for 2-4 additional bonuses per product
- To maximize the tactic, the collective price of the bonuses should exceed the cost of the product itself
- Result: It feels like they are getting multiple products for the price of one

### Section 10: Final Pitch (Last Nudge)

End your landing page (as far as sales copy goes) with a Final Pitch.

**Formula:** Some version of "Ready to get started... [Offer]?"

There is no one right way to end here. It is a final Call To Action (CTA) reminding people of:
- The benefits of taking action
- And/or what they are going to miss out on if they do not

### Section 11: FAQ (Objection Handler)

A critical section near the end. This is where you cover logistical questions AND -- more importantly -- buying objections disguised as questions.

**Logistical questions to cover:**
- "Is this a self-paced course?"
- "Can I join at a later time?"
- "What time commitment should I expect?"

**Pro Tip -- The real power of the FAQ section:** This is an amazing opportunity to answer questions in the form of addressing objections.

**Full Stack Writer example objections (disguised as questions):**
- Objection: "I've never written online before. I don't think this is for me."
  - FAQ version: "What if I've never written anything online before?"
- Objection: "I've already started writing online on my own. I don't think this is for me."
  - FAQ version: "Will this help if I've already started writing online?"

**Key insight: All questions are really objections in disguise.**

When someone is on the fence, the FAQ is one of the first sections they skip to. If you do not do a good enough job answering their questions, they are much less likely to buy -- because they do not have all the information they need to make an educated purchase.

Most people will NOT email you to ask their question directly. Most people just want to make a buying decision on their own, with as little interaction with others as possible. The FAQ solves that problem.

### Section 12: Refund Policy

It is usually a good idea to have some sort of refund policy.

**Default recommendation: 14 Days, No Questions Asked.**

Reality check:
- Very few people take you up on it
- A small % of people will abuse it (buy the product, copy all materials, then request a refund)
- You cannot be afraid of this happening
- Having a refund policy helps get a lot of people over the fence, especially first-time digital product buyers
- It is just part of the game -- accept there will be a few bad actors

**Alternative: The Justin Welsh No-Refund Approach**

Another option is to deliberately NOT have a refund policy. Welsh executed this well for one of his recent launches. But notice -- it works because it is addressed HEAD-ON.

**Key distinction:** This is NOT the same as "not having a refund policy" and not speaking to it at all. If you take this route, you must address it directly with your customers. Silence on refunds is not a strategy -- it is a gap.

**Decision tree:**
- If you want maximum fence-sitters to convert: 14-day no-questions-asked refund
- If you have strong brand authority and want to filter for committed buyers: No refund, but address it explicitly and confidently on the page
- If you do nothing and say nothing about refunds: You lose both audiences

### Section 13: Payment

All that is left is to collect payment. The big 2 payment methods are Credit Cards and PayPal. As long as you accept those 2, you are fine. Everything else (ApplePay, etc.) is a bonus.

**Payment Plan Strategy:**

Standard procedure: Charge full-price, one-time payment for your product.

But you can also offer a payment plan to make it easier for customers to "cash flow" the purchase. For a low-ticket product, do not go beyond a 2-Pay (e.g., $175 today, $175 in 30 days). There is no reason to create a 6-payment plan for a low-ticket product.

**Critical timing rule:** Do NOT roll out a payment plan right away. Wait until after the first surge of customers comes through. Reason: You would rather capture 100% of the revenue up front. If you offer a payment plan from day one, a bunch of people will default to it just because it is "easier" -- and you have to wait 30 days to collect the other 50% of your cash.

## 10-Point Offer Creation Checklist

This checklist drives Section 1 (The Offer). The more of these you can speak to, the more the value of your product becomes obvious.

1. **Specific Problem** -- What exact problem does your product solve?
2. **Solved For A Specific Person** -- Who exactly is this for?
3. **Solved In A Specific Way** -- What is unique about your approach/method?
4. **Achieving An Objective Outcome** -- What measurable or binary result do they get?
5. **In A Specific Time Frame** -- How long until they see results?
6. **Price-Anchored To Upside 5x-10x** -- Can you anchor to the revenue/value they could generate?
7. **Or Price-Anchored To Downside 5x-10x** -- Can you anchor to the cost of NOT solving this?
8. **Named Something Tangible** -- Does the product name represent an identity or tangible thing?
9. **Positioned In A Niche Where You Have Experience** -- Do you have credibility here?
10. **And Proof It Works** -- Can you show evidence?

### Full Stack Writer Worked Example

Product: Full Stack Writer, $350, standalone digital product.

1. **Specific Problem:** Writers do not know all the different skills they need to acquire to successfully build an audience and monetize their writing online. Most "writing courses" are fragmented -- selling individual skills but not explaining how they all connect.
2. **Solved For A Specific Person:** Beginner digital writers / writers who have not monetized their talents yet (or not monetized very well).
3. **Solved In A Specific Way:** Instead of "just" talking about 1 individual skill, they give you ALL 7 of the skills you need to become a Full-Stack Writer -- build an audience and monetize as a beginner.
4. **Achieving An Objective Outcome:** When you have these 7 skills and 7 different revenue streams, you become a Full-Stack Writer. It is binary: do you have these 7 skills, yes or no?
5. **In A Specific Time Frame:** No specific time frame used here.
6. **Price-Anchored To Upside:** "7 skills, 7 revenue streams, 7-figure potential."
7. **Price-Anchored To Downside:** Nothing used here.
8. **Named Something Tangible:** Full-Stack Writer -- an "identity" you can embody.
9. **Positioned In A Niche With Experience:** Yes, they have tremendous experience and 7+ figures using all of these skills.
10. **Proof It Works:** No proof shown in the header specifically.

**Checklist score: 7 out of 10 addressed.** There is no "one right answer" -- the game is simply to check as many boxes as possible.

## Full Stack Writer Case Study

- **Product:** Full Stack Writer (standalone digital product)
- **Price:** $350
- **Total revenue:** ~$468K (~$438K in the first week + ~$30K from 2-payment plans hitting a month later)
- **Landing page:** fullstackwriter.com
- **Positioning:** "7 skills, 7 revenue streams, 7-figure potential"
- **Key tactics used:** All 13 sections of this framework, price anchoring (total perceived value $4,000+ for a $350 product), 2-Pay option rolled out after initial surge, FAQ as objection handler, 14-day refund policy

So, here's what we're going to do:

I'm going to provide you with key details about my product & target audience, including:

• The name of my product
• The main problem(s) the product solves
• The main benefits/outcomes I'm aiming to help people unlock
• The target audience of my product

• A credibility statement listing out some of the tangible milestones or results that make me an expert/authority on this topic

That way, you can have a complete understanding of what the product is about, what we're going to cover, and the different outcomes/problems we're going to help people solve.

Now, here's the specific structure I'd like you to follow when writing the sales page copy.

SALES PAGE STRUCTURE

Our high-converting sales pages typically include the following 11 sections:

The "Hero" Section: A strong headline and sub-headline that clearly communicate the product's value proposition. Optionally, a video sales letter (VSL) or a high-quality product mockup.

The "Quiz" Section: A breakdown of 2-3 key archetypes of people who would benefit from this product. The more specific, the better.

The "Your Instructor" Section: A credibility-building section introducing the creator(s) behind the product to establish authority and trust.

The "Proof" Section: A series of "tangible" bullets showing objective results or milestones I've been able to achieve (to show my competence/authority/expertise on this niche/topic).

The "Problems" Section: A quick list of the 7-10 major pain points and challenges my target audience faces, which the product is designed to solve.

The "Mistakes" Section: A deep dive into the 3 biggest mistakes people make when trying to solve these problems on their own, followed by how the product helps avoid them.

The "Everything Included" Section: A detailed breakdown of the product's content, such as modules or lessons, highlighting key takeaways. Optionally, include assigned value estimates for each component.

The "Bonuses" Section: A compelling breakdown of any additional bonuses included in the offer.

The "Last Nudge" Section: A persuasive closing section reinforcing the value of the offer and creating urgency with a deadline (if there's a deadline, if not you can skip that last part)

The "FAQ" Section: Answers to common objections and questions potential buyers may have.

The "Guarantee" Section: A reminder of the risk-free nature of the offer, given I'll give people a 14-day, no-questions-asked guarantee.
