# Kennedy Sales Letter Frameworks

Dan Kennedy's direct response sales letter methodology. These frameworks map directly to sales page sections. Use them alongside the existing conversion-psychology.md and landing-page-architecture.md references.

## The 28-Step Ultimate Sales Letter (Mapped to Sales Page Sections)

Kennedy's full sales letter process, translated into modern sales page structure.

### Stage 1: Know Your Customer (Steps 1-2)
Before writing anything, answer the 10 Market Diagnosis Questions (see `skills/copy-writing/references/kennedy-copy-foundations.md`). Then list every feature and translate each into a benefit.

This maps to **Step 1: Gather Core Messaging Inputs** in the sales-page-writer workflow. Don't skip it.

### Stage 2: Build Credibility (Step 3) = Damaging Admission
Openly list flaws and objections before the prospect does. When you admit a weakness, your credibility on everything else goes up dramatically.

Place the damaging admission in **Section 2 (Problem Agitation)** or early in **Section 3 (Solution Reveal)**.

Examples:
- "This isn't for everyone. If you want done-for-you, this isn't it."
- "The interface isn't fancy. It works, though."
- "I'm not a developer. I'm a newsletter operator who figured this out the hard way."

### Stage 3: Get the Letter Read (Steps 4-6) = Headline + Subheadlines
- Write 50 headlines, test top 3
- Add subheadlines every 2-3 paragraphs for skimmers
- Most people scan first, read second. Subheadlines are your second chance to hook them

This maps to **Section 1 (Headline + Subheadline)** and the subheads throughout every section.

### Stage 4: Handle Price (Step 7) = Value Before Price
Never present price before building value. Three techniques:

1. **Comparison:** "A single coaching call costs $500. This entire system is less than that."
2. **ROI framing:** "If this brings you even ONE extra client, it pays for itself 10x over."
3. **Minimization:** "That's less than $2 a day. Less than your morning coffee."

This maps to **Section 5 (Bonus Stack)** and **Section 9 (Final CTA)**. Always show the value math before the price reveal.

### Stage 5: Write the Copy (Steps 8-16)
- Write emotional, conversational, enthusiastic
- Write at 6th-grade reading level
- Short sentences. Short paragraphs.
- Answer every objection before they ask it
- Use stories, not claims

This applies to ALL sections. Every paragraph should pass the "would I say this to a friend?" test.

### Stage 6: Drive Action (Steps 17+)
- Create real scarcity and deadline (see Scarcity Mechanics below)
- The P.S. is the second most-read element after the headline
- Restate the offer and deadline in the P.S.
- Send 2nd and 3rd follow-up notices after initial send

This maps to **Section 9 (Final CTA)** and any follow-up email sequences.

## Irresistible Offer Stack (7 Elements + Thud Factor)

Every sales page needs all 7 elements. If any are missing, conversion drops. Check this list before you publish.

### 1. Core Product Clearly Defined
One sentence: what is this thing? No jargon. No abstraction. "A 6-module video course that teaches you to build an AI-powered newsletter from scratch."

### 2. Price Justification (Value Build Before Reveal)
Stack the value of each component BEFORE showing the price. The reader should be thinking "this must cost $2,000" before you reveal $197.

Format:
- Module 1: [Name] (Value: $X)
- Module 2: [Name] (Value: $X)
- Total Value: $X,XXX
- Your Price Today: $XXX

### 3. Bonuses (Minimum 2-3)
Each bonus should address a remaining objection or remove a specific friction point.

Rules for bonuses:
- Each bonus needs its own value assigned
- "Refund the product but keep the bonuses" removes all buying risk
- Best bonuses solve the "what do I do AFTER I buy?" question
- Name them specifically: "The Quick-Start Template Pack" not "bonus materials"

### 4. Deadline (Real, Specific Date)
"Enrollment closes Friday, March 20th at 11:59pm EST." Not "limited time." Not "act now." A real date they can put on their calendar.

### 5. Incentive for Fast Response
A bonus that disappears for early buyers. "The first 50 buyers get a private 1-on-1 strategy call (value: $500)." This rewards action, not just interest.

### 6. Penalty for Late Response
Price goes up after the deadline. "After Friday, the price increases to $297." This creates real cost for procrastination.

### 7. Guarantee
See the Guarantee Strategy section below. Pure guarantee outperforms conditional in most markets.

### 8. "Thud Factor"
The physical or visual weight of what they receive. When someone sees a stack of 6 modules, 4 bonuses, 3 templates, and 2 checklists, it FEELS like more value than "access to our course."

Show mockup images, course screenshots, PDF thumbnails, video thumbnails. Make the digital feel tangible. Stack the visual. The buyer's brain equates visual volume with value.

### Take-Away Selling
"Only serious marketers qualify." Making something harder to get increases desire. Use application forms, qualifying questions, or "this isn't for everyone" language. When you push away unqualified buyers, qualified buyers lean in.

## Risk Reversal and Guarantee Strategy

Most businesses treat guarantees as fine print. Kennedy treats them as marketing weapons.

### Types of Guarantees

**Pure Guarantee:** "30 days. Don't like it for any reason. Full refund. No questions." Simplest and usually most effective. Works best for products under $500.

**Conditional Guarantee:** "Complete all 6 modules and implement the system. If you don't see results in 90 days, full refund." Protects against tire-kickers. Works well for courses and coaching.

**Performance Guarantee:** "Get 500 new subscribers in 60 days or your money back." Highest risk for you, highest conversion. Only use when you're extremely confident in outcomes.

**Anti-Guarantee:** "No refunds. This is for serious people only." Works for high-ticket, exclusive offers. The lack of guarantee IS the positioning. "We don't need to guarantee it. Our results speak."

**Bonus Retention:** "If you refund, keep all the bonuses." Removes the last shred of risk. They literally cannot lose money. This is a conversion weapon.

### How to Use Guarantees Offensively
- Put the guarantee ABOVE the price, not below it
- Make the guarantee part of the headline if it's strong enough
- Name your guarantee: "The Double-Your-Money Promise" not "our refund policy"
- Bold, large font. Not fine print. Not hidden in FAQ.
- Reference the guarantee in the P.S.

## Scarcity and Deadline Mechanics (5 Types)

All scarcity must be REAL. Fake scarcity destroys trust permanently. If you say "only 50 spots," there better be only 50 spots.

### 1. Deadline Scarcity
Specific date. "Cart closes March 20th at midnight." The most common and most effective. Everyone procrastinates. A deadline forces a decision.

### 2. Limited Quantity
"Only 50 spots available." Must be genuinely limited. Works best when you can explain WHY it's limited: "I can only handle 50 people in the private community without diluting the experience."

### 3. Disappearing Bonuses
"The first 25 buyers get the private audit template." Creates urgency within urgency. Early buyers get rewarded.

### 4. Rising Price
"Price goes up $50 each day this week." Creates daily urgency. Each day of delay has a real cost. Best for launches, not evergreen.

### 5. Social Scarcity
"Not everyone qualifies." Application-based. Waitlist-based. "We review every application and only accept people we believe we can help." Works exceptionally well for high-ticket offers.

### Follow-Up Escalation Sequence
1. **Full offer** (Day 1): Complete sales page, all details
2. **Reminder** (Day 3-4): "Have you seen this?" Re-share key benefits
3. **24 hours left** (Day 6): Urgency ramps. Testimonial-heavy
4. **Last chance** (Day 7 morning): "Doors close tonight"
5. **Closed** (Day 8): "The offer is closed. Here's what you missed." FOMO for next launch

## Premium Pricing: 9 Failures to Avoid

If you're undercharging, one of these is the reason.

1. **Obsessing about competitors' lower prices.** Their price is their problem. Your value is your conversation.

2. **Attracting price-only customers.** If price is how they found you, price is how they'll leave you. Build your marketing around value, not discounts.

3. **Never offering a premium option.** Some buyers WANT the expensive option. Give it to them. A $5K tier makes the $500 tier feel reasonable.

4. **Discounting without reason.** Every discount trains customers to wait for the next discount. If you discount, tie it to a real event: launch, anniversary, bundle.

5. **Not demonstrating value before showing price.** If you lead with price, you're competing on price. Lead with transformation, results, and proof. Price becomes secondary.

6. **Assuming your market is price-sensitive.** They're not price-sensitive. They're value-uncertain. Show them the ROI and the sensitivity disappears.

7. **Competing on price as strategy.** There's always someone cheaper. You cannot win a race to the bottom. Compete on outcome, speed, exclusivity, or experience.

8. **Apologizing for your price.** "I know it's a lot, but..." kills the sale. If you don't believe your price is fair, the buyer won't either. State the price with confidence. Then shut up.

9. **Linking price to time or effort, not value.** "It took me 100 hours to build this" is not a pricing argument. "This will save you $50K in the next 12 months" is. Price anchors to the value they receive, not the work you put in.

## Quick Reference: Kennedy Offer Stack Checklist

Before publishing any sales page, verify all elements:

- [ ] Core product clearly defined in one sentence
- [ ] Value stacked before price reveal
- [ ] 2-3 bonuses, each solving a remaining objection
- [ ] Real deadline with specific date
- [ ] Fast-action incentive (disappearing bonus or early-bird price)
- [ ] Late-response penalty (price increase after deadline)
- [ ] Guarantee prominently displayed (not in fine print)
- [ ] Visual "thud factor" (mockups, screenshots, stacked visuals)
- [ ] Damaging admission placed early for credibility
- [ ] P.S. restating offer + deadline
- [ ] Price justified with comparison, ROI, or minimization
