# Cole's Tangibility & Proof Principles (Low-Ticket Launchpad)

> Source: Nicolas Cole, Low-Ticket Launchpad course
> Modules: 3 (Offer Creation) + 7 (Product Design)
> Context: Principles for making sales page copy and visuals TANGIBLE. Use when writing sales pages to ensure offers feel concrete, outcomes are objective, and design reinforces perceived value.

---

## 1. Tangible vs Intangible Copy

The single biggest mistake in digital product marketing: making offers intangible.

### Intangible (BAD) -- vague, unmeasurable, no "object"

- "I'll help you up-level your sales skills."
- "I'll show you how to become the person you were meant to be."
- "I'll give you what you need to be a successful change-maker in the world."

Intangible offers are things you can't hold, can't measure, can't feel, and can't "collect." They're loose phrases with vague, implied meaning.

### Tangible (GOOD) -- specific, holdable, collectible

- "Build a Notion Time-Tracking Dashboard for your cold outreach."
- "Write 30 pieces of published writing in public."
- "Create your first 10 automations with AI in 72 hours."

### The Tangibility Test

Can the customer...
- **Hold** it? (even digitally)
- **Measure** it? (count, track, compare)
- **Feel** it? (sense of ownership/collection)

If no to all three, the offer is intangible. Rewrite it.

### How to Make the "Specific Way" Tangible

Anchor the delivery method to a concrete digital asset:

- Roadmap
- Blueprint
- Checklist
- Excel Template
- Notion Dashboard
- Keynote Presentation
- Data Analysis Whitepaper
- X Pieces of Published Content

The secret: it has to feel like they're getting an OBJECT. Without an object, the customer can't evaluate why it's valuable or how to measure success.

### Making BOTH the Way and the Outcome Tangible

You want either the WAY you help (the deliverable) or the OUTCOME to be tangible. Bonus points if both.

Ship 30 for 30 example:
- Way: daily writing exercises + accountability
- Outcome: "You'll create 30 pieces of published writing in public."

30 pieces of writing = tangible (holdable, measurable, collectible) AND objective (you either wrote 30 pieces or you didn't).

**"The Tangible x Objective combination is, in my opinion, the most important part of the entire game."** -- Cole

## 2. Objective vs Subjective Outcomes in Sales Copy

### Subjective (BAD) -- 10 people = 10 different interpretations

- "I'll help you live the life of your dreams."
- "I'll help you finally be the person you want to be."
- "I'll help you make that transition and be your best self."

If everyone gives a different answer about what the outcome means, nobody actually knows what's being promised.

### Objective (GOOD) -- binary, measurable, it happened or it didn't

- "Land your first $5,000 ghostwriting client."
- "Buy your first single-family rental property."
- "Go on your first 5 dates."

### CRITICAL Nuance for Low-Ticket Products

There is a cataclysmic difference between these two promises:

**BAD:** "If you use this strategy, I guarantee you will attract your first 10,000 followers."

**GOOD:** "If you use this strategy, you will have everything you need to attract your first 10,000 followers."

Why this matters:
- **Low-ticket sells information (DIY).** You don't control implementation. You HOPE they follow through, but you can't guarantee it.
- **High-ticket sells information + implementation (DWY/DFY).** You have more control over the outcome because you're involved in execution.

**Sales page language rule:** Promise "everything you need to achieve X" -- never "I guarantee you will achieve X." The information is the product. Implementation is on them.

This is what separates ethical digital product marketing from the stuff that gives online education a bad name.

## 3. The 5 Proof Levels (Testimonial Progression)

Use these levels to structure your testimonial/social proof sections. Each level unlocks wider audiences and dismantles more objections.

### Level 1: "This worked for me."

Your own case study. Easiest proof because you control all variables. Just share what you did and the impact on your own life/business.

**Sales page use:** Founder story section, "here's my result" screenshot, before/after.

### Level 2: "This worked for people like me."

People with similar background, education, starting point, skills, and limiting beliefs who used your approach and achieved similar results.

**Sales page use:** 3-5 testimonials from people matching your target buyer profile.

### Level 3: "This also worked for people NOT like me."

True social proof territory. Your offer carries completely different weight when you prove it works for people who are nothing like you.

**Sales page use:** Diverse testimonials showing different demographics, backgrounds, experience levels.

### Level 4: "This has also worked across different industries."

Dismantles the objection: "OK I see how that worked for you in that niche, but I'm in a different niche so I don't think it will work for me."

**Sales page use:** Industry/niche-specific testimonial clusters. "Works for coaches, freelancers, agency owners, SaaS founders..."

### Level 5: "This has also worked for a long time."

Tested by time. The longer you stay in business, the more compelling your offer becomes. This is the compounding game.

**Sales page use:** "Since [year]" timestamps, multi-year case studies, cohort/batch numbers.

### Bootstrapping Proof from Zero

If you have no testimonials yet:
1. You should be your own case study (Level 1 is always available).
2. Give 10-20 people free access as "beta users" in exchange for a testimonial at the end.
3. Power-level your first 10-20 testimonials and get the proof train rolling.

## 4. Three Product Design Styles (Visual Sales Page Elements)

Design matters, but content comes first. Pick a style, execute it, don't let design hold up the launch.

### Style 1: Minimalist (Jack Butcher model)

- Black background, white font, simple visual
- 1-2 colors max, one clean font
- Best for: tight budgets, no design eye, or intentionally "premium simple" brand
- Effect: leaves the reader wondering "if the cover is this simple, what's inside?"

### Style 2: Operating System (Justin Welsh model)

- Treat the digital product like a physical software bundle you'd buy at Best Buy
- Creates the signal: "There's a LOT here!"
- Best for: multi-module courses, template bundles, system-style products
- Welsh calls his products "Operating Systems" -- the design matches the name

### Style 3: Stock Photo Professional (Michael McGill model)

- Stock photography + Canva or basic graphic design
- Lowest barrier to entry, still looks professional and purchase-ready
- Best for: first-time product creators, fast launches
- Platforms: Canva, or a cheap Fiverr designer

### Rule: Don't let design block the launch. You can always upgrade later.

## 5. Three Design Assets Every Sales Page Needs

Create these three assets for every digital product. They make the product FEEL tangible (reinforcing the copy principle above).

### Asset 1: Product Box

- The single most valuable design asset
- Creates the feeling that a digital product is a physical object
- **Requirements:** 3D version (not flat/head-on), .png with transparent background so you can overlay it on landing pages, slides, emails
- This is different from the social image -- you need both

### Asset 2: Social Image

- Product box overlaid on a solid color background (black, white, or brand color)
- Perfect square dimensions
- .jpg file type
- Used for: social media posts, email headers, ad creatives

### Asset 3: Module Header Template

- Google Slides template with product box, product font, and header layout
- Used for: creating consistent headers inside your course platform (Skool, Circle, etc.)
- Not required, but instantly makes the product feel high-quality and cohesive

## 6. Design Budgeting for Sales Pages

### The 10% Rule

Don't invest more than 10% of anticipated first-year product revenue into design.

- Expect $100K in year one? Budget up to $10K for design.
- Expect $10K in year one? Budget up to $1K.
- Expect $1K? Spend $0 and use Canva.

### Three Investment Levels

**Level 1: Beginner ($0)**
- Very small audience, first product, testing the market
- DIY with Canva, stock photos, screenshots of the platform
- Ship 30 for 30 used a ship emoji and Slack screenshots for 2 years and made millions

**Level 2: $10K-$50K in revenue ($1K-$5K budget)**
- Existing business or side hustle generating revenue
- Invest in professional design to increase perceived value
- Find a freelance designer, not an agency

**Level 3: $50K+ in revenue ($2K-$6K per product)**
- Commit to professional design for every future product
- Hire incredible freelance designers for 2-4 weeks of work
- This tier lasts a long time -- Cole's team spends $2K-$6K per product while generating millions

### Where to Find Designers

1. **Dribbble** -- best for talented "general" product designers, also great for inspiration
2. **Upwork** -- cost-effective, look for portfolios showing work similar to what you need
3. **I Need A Book Cover** -- niche site for book covers, many designers also do product design
4. **X/LinkedIn** -- search "graphic designer," browse portfolios

### Key Advice: Hire a talented freelancer or solopreneur. Skip the agency. You don't need a "brand strategy" or "design roadmap." You need someone with a good eye to make a product mock-up.

## Integration Notes

- **Cole's tangibility principle extends Kennedy's "thud factor"** (see `kennedy-sales-letter.md`) to digital products. Kennedy says make the physical package thick and heavy. Cole says make the DIGITAL offer feel like a physical object through naming, design, and measurable deliverables.
- **Cole's 5 Proof Levels map to Hormozi's 13-Point Proof Checklist** (see `hormozi-proof-checklist.md`) as a simplified progression. Use Cole's levels to structure the testimonial section, then apply Hormozi's granular checklist for each individual proof element.
- **The design assets section fills a gap** in our sales-page-writer skill. We have copy frameworks (Brunson, Kennedy, Hormozi) but no visual/design guidance. Use Cole's 3-asset checklist + budgeting tiers when advising on sales page visuals.
- **Tangible x Objective is the quality gate** for all sales page headlines and offer descriptions. Before finalizing any sales page, run every promise through: Is it tangible? Is it objective? If not, rewrite.
- **Low-ticket language rule** ("everything you need to achieve X" vs "I guarantee X") applies to all the product product sales pages. We sell information products. Never guarantee outcomes we can't control.
