# Frontend Offer Strategist

Your frontend offer is not a product. It is a conversion event. Its sole purpose is to transform a stranger into someone who trusts you enough to pay for your backend DFY service. The format you choose determines HOW that trust transfer happens.

This skill follows the **Automatic Clients ECA Model** (Educate-Convert-Ascend):
1. **Educate**: The frontend offer educates buyers on your method and proves your competence
2. **Convert**: One-time offers (OTOs) after purchase maximize immediate revenue and deepen commitment
3. **Ascend**: Value bombs + awareness bridge content move buyers toward your high-ticket backend

See `references/ac-formula.md` for the full Automatic Clients methodology.

## Lead Magnet Templates

A collection of prompt templates for brainstorming, naming, and generating lead magnets lives in `templates/lead-magnet/`. These cover content analysis for lead magnet ideas, 30-day content series, curation lead magnets, AI-powered ideas, hyper-relevant ideas, collaboration ideas, money-making ideas, viral giveaways, email course value propositions, name generation, mockup images, copywriting legend prompts, and welcome email writing.

### Choosing Your Lead Magnet Type

Before brainstorming specific ideas, choose which of the 3 Hormozi lead magnet types fits your frontend-to-backend strategy:

- **Type 1: Reveal a Problem** -- Best when your backend solves a problem buyers don't know they have. The lead magnet shows them the problem (audit, scorecard, grader), then the frontend offer educates on the solution. Feeds naturally into **Fast Start Masterclass** and **Paid Case Study** formats.
- **Type 2: Trial of the Solution** -- Best when your backend is service-based (DFY). Give a small taste of the actual result for free. Maps directly to the **DFY Personalized** frontend format. When someone experiences the result firsthand, selling becomes easy.
- **Type 3: One Step of a Multi-Step** -- Best when your backend is complex/technical. Pull one automated piece of the full offer and give it away. Maps to the **Tools & Templates** frontend format. Way more compelling than "request a quote."

Higher-cost-to-deliver lead magnets convert better. For a frontend funnel, investing more in the lead magnet pays off through backend ascension.

For the full taxonomy, tool type mapping, and ideation approaches, see `free-tool-strategy` SKILL.md.

This skill acts as a strategic advisor. It diagnoses before prescribing. You do not pick a format because it sounds cool. You pick the format that dissolves the specific objections standing between your buyer and your backend DFY offer.

## Prerequisites

- A backend DFY offer (clearly defined, or at minimum a description of the service you sell/want to sell)
- Target customer profile (from `niche-finder`, `coach-business`, or user-provided)
- Knowledge of: what the backend costs, who buys it, what objections they face
- Optional: strategic context from `coach-business` diagnostic, existing products or content

## Workflow

### Step 1: Define the Backend DFY Offer (The Destination)

Before selecting a frontend format, crystallize the backend. You cannot choose a frontend without knowing what it leads to.

**Ask the user:**
- What is the DFY service you want people to buy?
- What does it cost? (price or price range)
- What does it deliver? (specific outcome)
- What are the top 3 objections buyers have before purchasing?
- How many clients can you serve per month?

**Output: Backend Offer Statement**
```
Service: [What you deliver]
For: [Who you serve]
Outcome: [What they get]
Price: $[backend price]
Top 3 Objections:
1. [Objection 1]
2. [Objection 2]
3. [Objection 3]
Monthly Capacity: [X] clients
```

If the user does not have a clearly defined backend, route to `coach-business` for strategic clarity before continuing.

### Step 1b: Quick Pitch Round (Rapid Ideation)

Before the heavyweight format scoring, generate 3-5 quick product pitches based on the validated problem from `niche-finder`. This gives the user a fast visual comparison before committing to a format.

Each pitch gets exactly this structure (2-3 lines max per idea):

```
**[Product Name]** -- specific and outcome-focused. The buyer should immediately think "that's what I need." No generic names like "Social Media Masterclass."
Format: [Template Pack / Mini-Course / Workshop / Challenge / Toolkit / Case Study / Book]
Price: $[X]
Build time: [Weekend / 2-3 days / 1 week]
Transformation: "You go from _____ to _____."
```

**Build-time mapping:**
- **Weekend** = single-asset deliverable (template pack, swipe file, checklist, cheat sheet)
- **2-3 days** = recorded training, paid case study, or workshop
- **1 week** = multi-part challenge, mini-course, or interactive tool

Lean toward products that are fast to build. For a first product, something shippable in a weekend beats something that takes 2 months. Speed to market matters more than comprehensiveness.

Ask: **"Which 1-2 of these grab you? I'll flesh out the winner."**

Once the user picks, proceed to Step 2 (format scoring) with the selected concept to validate the format choice, then Step 3 to build it out fully.

### Step 2: Score the 6 Formats (Decision Matrix)

Use `templates/format-scorecard.md` to score each format on 6 criteria. Load `references/offer-formats.md` for deep details on each format to inform scoring (includes Format 6: Authority Book).

| Criteria | What It Measures |
|----------|-----------------|
| Backend Alignment | Does this format naturally dissolve the backend's top objections? |
| Buyer Sophistication | Does it match the buyer's awareness level? (Unaware needs education. Aware needs proof or tools.) |
| Market Differentiation | Does this format stand out in the niche, or is the market flooded with it? |
| Fulfillment Capacity | Can you deliver at the volume you need? |
| Education Required | Does the buyer need to understand your method first? |
| Speed to Revenue | How fast can you launch and get paid? |

Score each format 1-5 on each criterion. Highest total score wins.

**Format selection shortcuts:**
- Buyer is action-oriented + backend is complex/technical = **Tools & Templates**
- Buyer needs a quick win + backend requires ongoing engagement = **Evergreen Challenge**
- Buyer is unaware of your method + needs a belief shift = **Fast Start Masterclass**
- Market is saturated with how-to content + trust is the barrier = **Paid Case Study**
- Buyer wants a sample of backend quality + backend is service-based = **DFY Personalized**
- You want maximum buyer volume at lowest CPA + you have deep expertise to share = **Authority Book** (the Automatic Clients model)

### Step 3: Structure the Selected Format

Once a format is selected, load `references/offer-formats.md` for the format-specific structure and build out:

- **Core deliverable**: What the buyer gets
- **Price point**: Within the format's range (see Quick Reference below)
- **Delivery method**: How they receive it (digital download, drip, video platform, custom delivery)
- **Timeline**: How long to consume/complete
- **The complexity reveal moment**: The specific point where the buyer realizes they need DFY help

Each format has a different structure. See the reference file for complete outlines:
- Tools & Templates: 3-7 assets + quick-start guide + video walkthrough
- Evergreen Challenge: Day-by-day protocol (7/14/21 days) + daily video + daily task
- Fast Start Masterclass: 60-min training (Setup 10 min, Method 30 min, Implementation 15 min, Next Steps 5 min)
- Paid Case Study: Context + Problem + Process + Results + Lessons (60 min total)
- DFY Personalized: Intake form + customized deliverable + quality control checklist

### Step 3b: Design OTO Stack (Optional but Recommended)

One-Time Offers are presented immediately after the frontend purchase, before the buyer reaches the thank-you page. OTOs maximize immediate revenue and deepen buyer commitment. Hand off to `product-offer` for detailed OTO pricing/structure using `templates/oto-brief.md`.

**OTO design principles:**
- **OTO 1**: Complements the frontend. "You just bought [frontend]. Want the [enhancement] too?" Priced at 1.5-3x the frontend price.
- **OTO 2**: Addresses a related but different need. "While you're here..." Priced at 0.5-1x the frontend price (downsell).
- Each OTO must deliver standalone value (buyer should not regret buying even without the frontend)
- OTO pages are urgency-driven: single CTA, countdown timer, "this offer disappears when you leave this page"
- Have `sales-page-writer` create OTO page copy

**Why OTOs matter for the AC model:**
The frontend is designed to break even on ad spend. OTOs turn the frontend from break-even to profitable on Day 1, giving you more budget to outspend competitors on advertising.

### Step 4: Design the Ascension Path

Load `references/ascension-mechanics.md` and design the specific bridge from frontend to backend:

1. **Belief shift**: What does the buyer believe BEFORE vs. AFTER consuming the frontend?
2. **The "I need help" moment**: Where in the frontend does the buyer hit the wall?
3. **Transition CTA**: What is the specific call-to-action at that moment?
4. **Backend offer at transition**: How is the DFY service presented?
5. **Ascension conversion target**: Benchmark 5-20% of frontend buyers inquire about backend (varies by format; see `references/ascension-mechanics.md` for per-format benchmarks)

**The full ascension journey (AC model):**
```
Frontend Purchase -> OTOs -> Product Delivery -> Value Bombs (3+) -> Awareness Bridge -> Call Booking -> High-Ticket Close
```

Each stage moves the buyer up in awareness:
- **Unaware** -> **Problem Aware** (content/ads)
- **Problem Aware** -> **Solution Aware** (frontend product)
- **Solution Aware** -> **Product Aware** (value bombs + awareness bridge)
- **Product Aware** -> **Most Aware** (call booking + close)

Also design the post-purchase ascension email sequence (3-5 emails):
- Email 1: Deliver value, set expectations, no pitch
- Email 2: Share a result/case study from a backend client
- Email 3: Soft introduction: "Most people who bought [frontend] also asked about..."
- Email 4: Direct invitation to learn about the backend service
- Email 5: Scarcity on backend availability (only if authentic)

For the value bomb and awareness bridge email sequences, hand off to `email-sequence` (value bomb series, awareness bridge, and high-ticket nurture).

### Step 5: Output the Offer Brief

Use `templates/offer-brief.md` to compile the complete frontend offer brief. The brief includes:
- Backend Offer Statement (from Step 1)
- Quick Pitch summary (from Step 1b, if used)
- Selected Format + scorecard summary (from Step 2)
- Offer structure (from Step 3)
- Ascension path design (from Step 4)
- **Who it's for** -- one sentence using language the buyer would recognize themselves in
- **Who it's NOT for** -- one sentence. This builds trust and sharpens positioning. Saying who shouldn't buy is as important as saying who should.
- Fulfillment plan
- Revenue model (frontend revenue + backend ascension math)
- Downstream handoff notes

Save the completed brief to `private project notes/offer-briefs/[offer-name].md`.

## Format-Specific Downstream Handoff Notes

### For product-offer
- The $350 default does NOT apply to frontend offers. Pass the format's price range ($27-97).
- Bonus stack for a $47 offer looks different than for a $350 course. Keep bonuses small: 1-2 max, total bonus value 1.5-2x product price.
- Value justification is different: "For $47, you get a preview of what our $[backend price] service delivers" not "For $350, you'll make $3,500."

### For product-architect
- Only needed for Evergreen Challenge and Fast Start Masterclass formats.
- For Tools & Templates, Paid Case Study, and DFY Personalized, skip `product-architect` and go directly to `product-offer`.
- NOTE: The transformation arc ends at a point that naturally leads to backend inquiry, not full transformation.

### For sales-page-writer
- Each format requires a different hero section angle and proof structure.
- The sales page sells the FRONTEND offer only. Do not sell the backend on the frontend sales page.
- The offer brief includes format-specific copy direction.

### For email-sequence
- The standard 5-day EEC may not apply. Templates and DFY Personalized may use a shorter 3-email showcase sequence.
- Add a NEW ascension sequence type (3-5 post-purchase emails bridging to backend inquiry). See `references/ascension-mechanics.md` for structure.
