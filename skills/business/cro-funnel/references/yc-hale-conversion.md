# YC Framework: How to Improve Conversion Rates (Kevin Hale)

Source: Y Combinator Startup School 2020 -- Kevin Hale

## Core Principle: Most Conversion Problems Are Clarity Problems

Before optimizing buttons and colors, ask: does the visitor immediately understand what this is, how it helps them, and why they should trust you? If the answer is no to any of these, that's your bottleneck. Not the button color.

## The Conversion Psychology Pyramid

Fix from bottom to top. Each level must be solid before the next matters.

```
        /\
       /  \  4. ACCELERATE ACTION
      /    \     (urgency, scarcity, incentives)
     /------\
    /        \ 3. REMOVE OBJECTIONS
   /          \    (guarantees, social proof, FAQ)
  /------------\
 /              \ 2. REDUCE FRICTION
/                \    (fewer steps, simpler forms, faster loads)
/------------------\
|                    | 1. CLARIFY VALUE
|  What is this?     |    (headline, subheadline, hero section)
|  How does it help? |
|  Why trust you?    |
+--------------------+
```

**Level 1: Clarify Value (fix this first)**
- 5-second test: Can a stranger understand your value prop in 5 seconds?
- Headline = what you do + for whom
- Subheadline = the specific outcome or transformation
- Hero image/demo = proof it works

**Level 2: Reduce Friction**
- Every additional step multiplies drop-off (not adds -- multiplies)
- Remove form fields. Each field = 5-10% fewer completions.
- One CTA per page. Multiple CTAs = decision paralysis.
- Page speed matters: every 1 second of load time = 7% conversion drop.

**Level 3: Remove Objections**
- Social proof (testimonials, logos, user counts, ratings)
- Guarantee (remove risk from the buyer)
- FAQ section (answer the questions they're afraid to ask)
- Authority signals (credentials, media mentions, "as seen in")

**Level 4: Accelerate Action**
- Urgency (deadline, limited enrollment, price increase)
- Scarcity (limited spots, closing soon)
- Incentive (bonus for acting now)
- Only add urgency AFTER levels 1-3 are solid. Urgency on a confusing page just makes people leave faster.

## The Three Questions Every Visitor Asks

In this order:

1. **"What is this?"** -- If unclear, they bounce immediately. No amount of social proof saves a confusing headline.
2. **"How will it help me?"** -- They understood what it is, now they need to see themselves using it. Specific outcomes > feature lists.
3. **"Why should I trust you?"** -- They want it, now they need proof. Testimonials, track record, guarantees.

Miss any one of these = conversion drops.

## The Friction Audit Framework

Walk through your funnel as a first-time visitor. At each step, ask:

1. **Do I know what to do next?** (If not = navigation friction)
2. **Do I want to do it?** (If not = motivation friction)
3. **Can I do it easily?** (If not = execution friction)

**Common friction sources:**
- Registration required before seeing value (show value first, ask for info after)
- Too many form fields (name + email is enough for most lead magnets)
- Confusing navigation (one path, one action per page)
- Slow page loads (compress images, minimize scripts)
- Asking for payment info before showing the product
- Dense text blocks (nobody reads paragraphs on landing pages)

**The Bottleneck Principle:**
Your conversion rate is limited by your WEAKEST step. A 90% opt-in rate means nothing if your landing page converts at 0.5%. Fix the weakest link first -- it has the highest ROI.

## Trust Building Components

**Social proof types (ranked by strength):**
1. Specific results with numbers ("Increased my open rate from 22% to 38%")
2. Video testimonials (hardest to fake)
3. Written testimonials with full name + photo
4. User count / subscriber count
5. Brand logos ("As used by...")
6. Star ratings
7. Generic endorsements (weakest)

**Authority signals:**
- Industry experience ("Built newsletters reaching 500K+ subscribers")
- Media mentions
- Certifications / credentials
- Association with respected brands or people

**Commitment/consistency:**
- Small yes before big yes (free tool -> email signup -> paid product)
- Micro-commitments reduce resistance (quiz funnel = series of small yeses)
- Foot-in-the-door: people who've taken one action are more likely to take the next

## Conversion Rate Formula

**Conversion Rate = Clarity x (1 - Friction) x Trust**

All three are multipliers. If any one is zero, conversion is zero.

- Perfect trust + perfect clarity + high friction = low conversion
- Perfect clarity + low friction + no trust = low conversion
- High trust + low friction + confusing value = low conversion

## Practical Application

**For newsletter signup pages:**
- Headline: What the reader gets (not what the newsletter is about)
- Proof: subscriber count, open rate, testimonial
- Form: email only (add name later if needed)
- One CTA: "Join [X] readers" or "Get [specific thing]"

**For product sales pages:**
- Follow the pyramid bottom-to-top
- Lead with the transformation, not features
- Stack proof throughout (not just at the bottom)
- Single CTA repeated 3-5 times as they scroll
- Guarantee near the price (removes objection at decision point)

**For app/tool landing pages:**
- Show the tool in action (screenshot, GIF, demo video)
- "Try it free" > "Sign up" (action-oriented, lower commitment)
- Social proof near the CTA
- Speed: if the tool is fast, make the page fast too (congruence)
