# Newsletter Positioner

Positions and brands a category newsletter on Beehiiv. Takes a broad topic area (AI for productivity/business/side hustles) and outputs a complete positioning brief: newsletter name, 1-liner description, About page copy, visual brand direction, and Beehiiv setup checklist.

This skill does NOT find a niche or choose a platform. The topic is already set. The platform is already Beehiiv. This skill focuses on positioning within that context.

## Prerequisites

- **Newsletter topic area**: The broad category is already decided (e.g., AI for productivity/business/side hustles). This skill narrows it further.
- **Target audience**: General sense of who you're writing for. This skill sharpens that into a specific reader profile.
- **Beehiiv account**: Free tier works to start. Paid tiers unlock premium subscriptions, automations, and custom domains.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Decide Free vs Paid Model

Choose your monetization model before naming or positioning. The model shapes everything downstream.

**Decision tree:**

- **Free newsletter**: The newsletter is an engine that drives revenue elsewhere (courses, consulting, affiliate, products, services). You grow the list, build trust, and sell through the newsletter. This is the most common model for creators who sell their own stuff.
- **Paid newsletter**: The newsletter IS the product. Subscribers pay for the content itself. Works when your expertise is deep enough that people will pay for access. Beehiiv supports premium tiers natively.
- **Freemium**: Free tier for growth + paid tier for premium content. Best of both worlds but requires enough content volume to justify the split. Beehiiv handles this with its premium subscription feature.

**How to decide:**
1. Do you have (or plan to have) products/services to sell? → Free model, newsletter is the engine
2. Is your expertise so specialized that content alone has value? → Paid model
3. Do you want maximum reach AND recurring revenue? → Freemium (but commit to the content volume)

**Paid Newsletter Readiness Check (Cole Prerequisites):**

Paid newsletters are 10x harder than free newsletters. Before choosing paid, confirm you meet ALL three prerequisites:

1. **10,000+ loyal followers** on a single platform (not scattered across 5 platforms)
2. **1+ year of consistent online writing** (proven you can sustain the cadence)
3. **Launched 1+ digital product generating $100K+ revenue** (proven people will pay you for knowledge)

If you don't meet all three, start with a free newsletter. Walk before you run.

**Revenue proof (real-world paid newsletters):**
- Write With AI (Cole/Bush): 50K free subs, 1,400 paying at $20/mo = **$317K/yr** subscription revenue + $67K webinar + $100K+ product drops
- Category Pirates (Cole/Bush, exited 2023): 30K free subs = **$150K+/yr** subscriptions + $100K+/yr book royalties + $5M+ consulting
- Start Writing Online (free model): 149K subs, powers Ship 30 for 30 = **$2M/yr** business + $3M+ incremental revenue

The pattern: free newsletters powering product businesses generate more total revenue than paid newsletters alone. The newsletter is the engine, not the product.

Document your choice. It affects your 1-liner, About page framing, and Beehiiv setup.

### Step 2: Define Sub-Niche Angle

**Optional: Information Advantage Framework (if niche isn't fully set)**

If you're still validating whether your topic is right, run Cole's 3-step Information Advantage exercise before narrowing further. Skip this if your topic is already decided.

Your "information advantage" is knowledge gained through experience that feels ordinary to you but holds real value for others. Moving across the country, building a business, recovering from burnout, managing a team -- any life experience counts.

1. **List all careers + personal interests.** Every job you've held (all experience counts, not just relevant ones) plus genuine interests (topics you prioritize learning about without being asked).
2. **Stack-rank by knowledge depth.** Careers: most to least experience. Interests: deepest to shallowest knowledge.
3. **Ask: "What do I want to keep learning about?"** The #1 reason newsletters fail is the founder stops. 35% of newsletters never reach issue 2. Top 10% on Substack requires just 1x/week for 3 months. Consistency beats everything.

**Your niche = largest information advantage x most genuine interest.** Not the most lucrative-sounding topic. Not the trendiest. The one you'll actually sustain.

Common objections: "I'm not an expert" -- readers only care if you can help them. A 6th grader teaching a 5th grader is perfectly fine. "What if it's not lucrative?" -- YOU make niches work, niches don't make you work. "What if I know nothing?" -- not recommended. Double learning curve (writing + topic) is a recipe for quitting.

---

"AI for productivity" is too broad. Narrow it to an intersection of three things:

1. **Your expertise**: What do you actually know deeply enough to write about consistently?
2. **Your interest**: What could you write about every week for 2+ years without burning out?
3. **Audience demand**: What are people actively searching for, paying for, or complaining about?

The sub-niche angle sits at the intersection of all three.

**Use the 9 Ways to Niche Down Further** (see `references/naming-frameworks.md`):
- By experience level (AI for beginners vs AI for power users)
- By revenue level (AI for solopreneurs under $100K vs AI for 7-figure businesses)
- By platform/tool (AI workflows in Notion, AI for ChatGPT power users)
- By location (digital or analog context)
- By demographic (AI for Gen X professionals, AI for stay-at-home parents)
- By life event/status (AI for career changers, AI for new freelancers)
- By career path/job title (AI for marketers, AI for real estate agents)
- By problem (AI to eliminate busywork, AI to replace your VA)
- By desired outcome (AI to build a $10K/month side hustle, AI to save 10 hours/week)

Pick ONE niche-down method. Write it down. This becomes part of your 1-liner.

### Step 3: Create 1-Liner Positioning

Your 1-liner is the most important sentence in your entire newsletter. It appears on your landing page, in social bios, in podcast intros, everywhere.

**Pre-Step: The "DOING Not Learning" Test**

Before writing your 1-liner, internalize this: your newsletter should help readers DO something, not learn about something. "Learning" is intangible. "Doing" is tangible and measurable.

- "Learn how to lift weights" → "Lose 10 pounds by summer"
- "Learn what makes NYC special" → "Have a great weekend with friends in specific venues"
- "Learn Microsoft Excel" → "Make yourself more productive and save 5 hours a week"
- "Learn about AI" → "Replace your first VA with an AI workflow this month"

If your 1-liner starts with "learn," rewrite it around the outcome the reader achieves.

**Pre-Step: Top 10 Problems + Top 10 Outcomes Exercise**

Before picking your 1 problem and 1 outcome for the formula, brainstorm broadly:

1. List **10 problems** your target audience has (within your niche). Be specific. Not "struggling with business" but "can't get past $5K/month because they're doing everything manually."
2. List **10 outcomes** your target audience wants. Be tangible. Not "success" but "publish content daily without burnout."
3. Circle the problems you **know how to solve** AND **are excited to solve repeatedly**.
4. Circle the outcomes you **know how to unlock** AND **want to help others unlock**.
5. Pick ONE problem and ONE outcome from your circled items. These feed into the formula below.

This exercise prevents the common mistake of picking the most impressive-sounding problem/outcome instead of the one you can actually deliver on consistently.

**Formula:**

> Topic + niche-down-further + 1 problem + 1 outcome

Or flipped:

> Topic + niche-down-further + 1 outcome + 1 problem

**Rules:**
1. Use TANGIBLE language, not intangible (see `references/naming-frameworks.md` for the tangible vs intangible framework)
2. Use OBJECTIVE language, not subjective: if 10 people could interpret it 10 different ways, rewrite it
3. Be specific about who it's for
4. Include a measurable or concrete outcome
5. Keep it to one sentence

**Examples:**
- Bad: "Helping entrepreneurs leverage AI to achieve success" (intangible, subjective, vague)
- Better: "Weekly AI workflows that save solopreneurs 10+ hours on client work"
- Bad: "Your guide to the AI revolution" (grandiose, no specificity)
- Better: "One AI tool breakdown every Tuesday for freelancers who hate admin work"

Stress-test your 1-liner: Read it to someone unfamiliar with your newsletter. Can they tell you exactly who it's for and what they'll get? If not, rewrite.

### Step 4: Name the Newsletter

The name should be CLEAR, not clever. A reader should get what the newsletter is about from the name alone (or name + 1-liner combo).

**10 Naming Conventions** (see `references/naming-frameworks.md` for 5 examples of each):

1. **Topic + Tangible Objects**: Combine your topic with a real-world object (The AI Toolbox, The Prompt Playbook)
2. **With/For Platform**: Name includes who it's for (AI for the Rest of Us, Prompts for Freelancers)
3. **Time-Based**: References cadence or time savings (The 5-Minute AI Brief, Tuesday Tools)
4. **Outcome-Oriented**: Names the result readers get (The Automated Inbox, The 10-Hour Week)
5. **Listicle/Numerical**: Uses numbers (3 AI Tools, The Top 1%)
6. **Audience Callout**: Names the reader directly (The Solopreneur's AI, Busy Founder Briefing)
7. **Journey/Process**: Implies progression (From Zero to Automated, The AI Learning Curve)
8. **Action-Oriented**: Starts with a verb (Build With AI, Automate Everything)
9. **Metaphor-Driven**: Uses a metaphor for the value (The AI Compass, Signal & Noise)
10. **Toolkit/Collection**: Implies curated resources (The AI Stack, Prompt Vault)

**Naming criteria checklist:**
- [ ] Makes a clear promise
- [ ] Uses tangible, objective language
- [ ] 7 words or fewer
- [ ] Speaks to a specific audience
- [ ] Flows naturally when spoken aloud
- [ ] Available on Beehiiv (check subdomain)
- [ ] Not already taken by a major newsletter

Generate 10-20 candidates across multiple conventions. Narrow to 3. Pick the one that passes the most checklist items.

### Step 5: Write About Section for Beehiiv Landing Page

The About section has five parts. Each has a specific job.

**Section 1: Welcome**
- Open with a warm, direct greeting
- Repeat your 1-liner within the first two sentences
- Set the tone (professional but human, not corporate)
- 2-3 sentences max

**Section 2: Who Is [Newsletter Name] For?**
- List 3-5 bullet points describing your ideal reader
- Use "You..." statements ("You're a freelancer who...")
- Be specific enough that wrong-fit readers self-select out
- Include both identity markers AND pain points

**Section 3: Who Am I / Who Are We?**
- Brief credibility statement (not a resume)
- Why you're qualified to write this specific newsletter
- Personal connection to the topic
- Keep it to 3-5 sentences
- Include one specific proof point (number of years, specific result, relevant experience)

**Section 4: Best Issues**
- Link to 2-3 of your best newsletter editions
- If you haven't published yet, describe 2-3 planned topics with working titles
- This gives new visitors a taste of what they'll get
- Pick issues that showcase different types of content you'll produce

**Section 5: My Promise To You**
- State your commitment in concrete terms
- What they'll get, how often, what quality bar you hold
- End with a CTA to subscribe
- Make the promise specific: "Every Tuesday, one AI workflow you can implement in under 15 minutes"

**Important:** Run the completed About section through the `humanizer` skill before publishing. The About page is outward-facing copy and must sound natural.

### Step 6: Generate Visual Brand Direction

Your brand doesn't need to be perfect on Day 1. It needs to be consistent and not embarrassing.

**What you need:**
- **Logo**: Simple wordmark or icon. Generate options with Looka, Canva, or AI image tools.
- **Primary color**: One dominant color that appears in headers, buttons, links
- **Secondary color**: One accent color for highlights and CTAs
- **Font style direction**: Modern sans-serif (clean/tech), serif (authoritative/editorial), or mixed

**Brand direction principles:**
- Look at the top 10 newsletters in your sub-niche. Note their visual patterns.
- Don't copy, but don't be wildly different either. Readers have visual expectations by category.
- AI/tech newsletters tend toward: dark backgrounds, blue/purple accents, sans-serif fonts
- Business/productivity newsletters tend toward: white backgrounds, bold accent colors, clean layouts
- Pick what fits your positioning and audience, not what you personally like

**Tools:**
- Looka: AI logo generator, outputs full brand kits
- Canva: Templates for newsletter headers and social assets
- Gemini/DALL-E: Generate logo concepts from text prompts

Document your brand direction in the positioning brief. Upload assets to Beehiiv.

### Step 7: Set Up Beehiiv Publication

With positioning, name, About copy, and brand direction done, configure Beehiiv:

1. **Create publication**: Use your newsletter name, upload logo
2. **Configure landing page**: Add 1-liner as headline, paste About section, set brand colors
3. **Set up subscribe forms**: Embedded form, popup, and landing page form. Customize button text to match your voice.
4. **Connect custom domain**: If you have one. Otherwise use the Beehiiv subdomain (yourname.beehiiv.com)
5. **Configure welcome email**: First thing new subscribers see. Repeat your promise. Set expectations for cadence and content.
6. **Upload brand assets**: Logo, favicon, social sharing image, email header image

**Beehiiv-specific notes:**
- Free plan supports up to 2,500 subscribers
- Scale plan ($39/mo) unlocks premium subscriptions, automations, and custom domains
- The landing page builder is drag-and-drop: good enough for launch
- If you need a custom landing page beyond Beehiiv's builder, use the `design-page` skill

## Kennedy USP Checkpoint (MANDATORY)

Before finalizing any positioning brief, run Kennedy's USP test. Your 1-liner must answer: "Why should I read YOUR newsletter over every other option?"

1. **Build the USP.** List features, convert to benefits, find the ONE unique benefit, make it specific, frame it as a promise. Not a slogan. A promise.
2. **Acid test.** Is it specific (verifiable)? Is it unique (no competitor makes the same claim)? Is it desirable (audience actually wants it)? All three must pass.
3. **Authority level.** Where are you on Kennedy's hierarchy: Generalist, Specialist, Published Authority, or Celebrity Expert? Your positioning brief should target at least Specialist level. Include a plan to move up one level.
4. **9 trust questions.** Your About page must answer: Are you authentic? Truthful? Competent? Right for me? Will you listen? Can I rely on you? Do you understand me? Better than alternatives? Fair price? If any go unanswered, revise.
5. **Damaging admission.** Include at least one honest limitation in your About page. "This newsletter isn't for everyone. If you want corporate AI strategy, look elsewhere." Credibility on everything else goes up.
6. **"WITH" strategy.** Who are you associated with that transfers credibility? List any collaborators, endorsements, or recognized names in your positioning brief.

See `references/kennedy-positioning.md` for the full USP formula, authority ladder, and all 9 trust questions with implementation guidance.

## Hormozi Brand & Authority Checkpoint

After the Kennedy USP checkpoint, layer Hormozi's brand frameworks:

- **Brand Building 4-Step:** (1) Identify what your audience already likes, (2) figure out what specifically they like about it, (3) associate your newsletter with those things, (4) optimize based on feedback. Your About page and 1-liner should reflect what they already trust. See `content-strategy/references/hormozi-marketing-machine.md`.
- **Levels of Authority (Brand Measurement):** Brand = Reach x Influence x Direction. Your positioning brief should map where you stand on each axis and which one to grow first. See `content-strategy/references/hormozi-marketing-machine.md`.

## Brunson Movement & Positioning Checkpoint

After the Kennedy USP checkpoint, run the Brunson layer. Use `references/brunson-frameworks.md` for the Secret Formula (dream reader, congregation, bait, result), New Opportunity framing (vehicle, not improvement), and Movement Building (tribal name, "I Am" statement, manifesto, milestones, social mission). The Secret Formula sharpens Steps 2-3; the Movement elements feed into the About page and welcome email.
