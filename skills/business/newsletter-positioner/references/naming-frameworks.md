# Newsletter Naming & Positioning Frameworks

Reference material for the newsletter-positioner skill. Contains the frameworks, formulas, and examples used during Steps 2-4 of the positioning workflow.

---

## Tangible vs Intangible Language

The single biggest mistake in newsletter positioning is using intangible language. Intangible language sounds impressive but communicates nothing specific. Tangible language creates a clear picture in the reader's mind.

**Rule:** If a reader can't visualize it, measure it, or point to it in the real world, it's intangible. Rewrite it.

### 20 Ways to Convert Intangible to Tangible

| # | Method | Intangible | Tangible |
|---|--------|-----------|----------|
| 1 | Specify numbers | "Save time" | "Save 10 hours per week" |
| 2 | Add timeframes | "Grow your business" | "Add $2K/month in 90 days" |
| 3 | Define the end result | "Achieve success" | "Land your first 3 paying clients" |
| 4 | Mention specific tools | "Use AI effectively" | "Build automations in Zapier + ChatGPT" |
| 5 | Include real-world examples | "Improve your workflow" | "Process 50 invoices in 10 minutes instead of 3 hours" |
| 6 | Paint a scenario | "Unlock your potential" | "Wake up to 5 new leads in your inbox every morning" |
| 7 | Focus on actions | "Leverage technology" | "Set up one AI automation every Friday" |
| 8 | Call out specific groups | "For ambitious professionals" | "For freelance copywriters billing $5K+/month" |
| 9 | Quantify effort | "Easy to implement" | "Takes 15 minutes or less to set up" |
| 10 | Use step-by-step | "Learn the process" | "Follow a 4-step workflow from prompt to published post" |
| 11 | Name specific skills | "Develop expertise" | "Write email sequences that convert at 3%+" |
| 12 | Use comparisons | "Better than the competition" | "2x the output of a $50/hour VA" |
| 13 | Provide metrics | "Increase engagement" | "Get 40%+ open rates on every send" |
| 14 | Describe physical results | "Create content" | "Publish 3 LinkedIn posts and 1 newsletter per week" |
| 15 | Use specific locations | "Grow your online presence" | "Rank on page 1 of Google for your target keyword" |
| 16 | Highlight deliverables | "Get valuable resources" | "Download a Notion template, 3 prompt chains, and a Loom walkthrough" |
| 17 | Use exact measurements | "Improve performance" | "Cut response time from 24 hours to 2 hours" |
| 18 | Describe before/after | "Transform your business" | "Go from 0 to 1,000 email subscribers in 60 days" |
| 19 | Use brand names | "Use the best tools" | "Build your stack with ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Claude" |
| 20 | Define frequency and tie to goal | "Stay updated" | "Every Tuesday: one AI tool breakdown you can implement before lunch" |

### Quick Test

Read your sentence out loud. Ask: "Can I take a photo of this outcome?" If yes, it's tangible. If no, rewrite.

## Objective vs Subjective Language Stress-Test

**Subjective language** can be interpreted differently by different people. **Objective language** has one clear interpretation.

**The 10-Person Test:** Read your 1-liner to 10 people. If they describe 10 different things when asked "what does this newsletter give you?", the language is subjective.

| Subjective (Fail) | Objective (Pass) |
|---|---|
| "Valuable AI insights" | "One AI workflow you can copy-paste into your business" |
| "Cutting-edge strategies" | "The exact prompt chains I use to write 5 blog posts per week" |
| "Empowering entrepreneurs" | "Showing solopreneurs how to replace their first hire with AI" |
| "Next-level productivity" | "Eliminate 10 hours of admin work per week using AI tools" |
| "Premium content" | "Step-by-step teardowns of real AI automations that save $2K+/month" |
| "Actionable advice" | "Every issue includes one workflow you can implement in under 15 minutes" |
| "Thought leadership" | "What's actually working in AI right now, tested on my own business" |
| "Best-in-class resources" | "A Notion template, 3 prompts, and a video walkthrough every week" |

**Red flag words** (almost always subjective): valuable, powerful, cutting-edge, next-level, game-changing, revolutionary, premium, best-in-class, world-class, transformative, innovative, empowering, unlock, leverage, optimize, elevate, holistic, synergy.

**Fix pattern:** Replace the adjective with a specific noun, number, or action.

## 1-Liner Formula

The 1-liner is the most important sentence in your newsletter. It does the heavy lifting on your landing page, social bios, podcast intros, and everywhere you describe the newsletter.

### Formula

> **Topic** + **niche-down-further** + **1 problem** + **1 outcome**

Or flipped:

> **Topic** + **niche-down-further** + **1 outcome** + **1 problem**

### Components

- **Topic:** Your broad subject area (AI, productivity, business)
- **Niche-down-further:** The specific slice (see 9 Ways below)
- **1 Problem:** The single pain point your reader feels most (be specific)
- **1 Outcome:** The single result your reader wants most (be tangible)

### Examples

| Topic | Niche-Down | Problem | Outcome | 1-Liner |
|-------|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| AI tools | For freelancers | Drowning in admin work | Save 10+ hours/week | "Weekly AI workflows that save freelancers 10+ hours on admin work" |
| AI productivity | For solopreneurs under $100K | Can't afford to hire | Replace their first employee | "How solopreneurs replace their first hire with AI, one tool at a time" |
| AI for business | For non-technical founders | Don't know where to start with AI | Implement AI in their business | "One no-code AI automation every Tuesday for founders who hate tech" |
| AI side hustles | For 9-to-5 workers | No time to build on the side | Build a $1K/month side income | "AI-powered side hustles you can build in 5 hours a week while keeping your day job" |

### Quality Checklist

- [ ] Contains tangible language (passes the "can I photograph it?" test)
- [ ] Contains objective language (passes the 10-person test)
- [ ] Identifies a specific reader
- [ ] Names one concrete problem OR one concrete outcome
- [ ] Is one sentence
- [ ] Could be understood by someone who has never heard of your newsletter

## 9 Ways to Niche Down Further

Start with your broad topic (e.g., "AI for productivity"). Pick ONE of these methods to narrow it further. The narrower you go, the faster you grow, counterintuitively, specificity attracts more subscribers than breadth.

### 1. By Experience Level
Narrow by how much the reader already knows.
- AI for complete beginners (never used ChatGPT)
- AI for intermediate users (use it daily, want to go deeper)
- AI for power users (building automations, custom GPTs, API integrations)
- AI for developers transitioning to AI-first workflows
- AI for executives who need to understand AI, not build with it

### 2. By Revenue Level
Narrow by the reader's business size.
- AI for pre-revenue side projects
- AI for solopreneurs under $100K/year
- AI for small businesses ($100K-$500K)
- AI for agencies ($500K-$5M)
- AI for enterprise teams ($5M+)

### 3. By Platform/Tool
Narrow by the specific tools or platforms the reader uses.
- AI workflows built on Notion
- ChatGPT power-user techniques
- Claude for professional writers
- AI automations in Zapier + Make
- AI for Shopify store owners

### 4. By Location (Digital or Analog)
Narrow by where the reader operates.
- AI for remote teams
- AI for brick-and-mortar businesses going digital
- AI for the creator economy (Twitter/X, YouTube, newsletters)
- AI for Amazon FBA sellers
- AI for local service businesses

### 5. By Gender/Age/Demographic
Narrow by who the reader is.
- AI for Gen X professionals re-entering the job market
- AI for stay-at-home parents building income
- AI for retired professionals starting consulting
- AI for college students building portfolios
- AI for women in tech leadership

### 6. By Life Event/Status/Identity
Narrow by what's happening in the reader's life right now.
- AI for career changers (leaving corporate)
- AI for new freelancers (first 90 days)
- AI for people who just got laid off
- AI for new parents with no free time
- AI for digital nomads optimizing for location independence

### 7. By Career Path/Job Title
Narrow by what the reader does for work.
- AI for real estate agents
- AI for marketing managers
- AI for freelance copywriters
- AI for accountants and bookkeepers
- AI for HR professionals

### 8. By Problem
Narrow by the specific pain the reader wants to solve.
- AI to eliminate email overwhelm
- AI to replace your virtual assistant
- AI to stop wasting time on social media content
- AI to automate client onboarding
- AI to handle customer support without hiring

### 9. By Desired Outcome
Narrow by what the reader wants to achieve.
- AI to build a $10K/month side income
- AI to save 10+ hours per week
- AI to publish content daily without burnout
- AI to land your first 1,000 subscribers
- AI to close more sales with less effort

### Combination Strategy

The strongest positions combine 2 methods:
- **Problem + Revenue Level:** "AI to replace your first hire: for solopreneurs under $100K"
- **Tool + Career Path:** "ChatGPT workflows for freelance copywriters"
- **Outcome + Experience Level:** "Build a $5K/month AI side hustle: no coding required"

Pick one primary method. Optionally add a secondary. More than two makes it confusing.

## 10 Naming Conventions with Examples

### 1. Topic + Tangible Objects
Combine your subject with a real-world object that implies value or utility.

- The AI Toolbox
- The Prompt Playbook
- The Automation Blueprint
- The AI Workbench
- The Productivity Stack

### 2. With/For Platform
Include "for" or "with" to name the audience directly.

- AI for the Rest of Us
- Prompts for Freelancers
- Automation for Solopreneurs
- AI with No Code
- Tools for Builders

### 3. Time-Based
Reference cadence, speed, or time savings.

- The 5-Minute AI Brief
- Tuesday Tools
- The Weekly Workflow
- AI in 10 Minutes
- The Morning Prompt

### 4. Outcome-Oriented
Name the result readers will achieve.

- The Automated Inbox
- The 10-Hour Week
- Inbox to Income
- The One-Person Business
- Zero Busywork

### 5. Listicle/Numerical
Use numbers to set clear expectations.

- 3 AI Tools
- The Top 1% Toolkit
- 7 Prompts
- One Workflow Weekly
- 5 Automations

### 6. Audience Callout
Name the reader directly in the title.

- The Solopreneur's AI
- Busy Founder Briefing
- The Freelancer's Edge
- The Non-Technical Guide
- Side Hustler's Signal

### 7. Journey/Process
Imply progression or transformation.

- From Zero to Automated
- The AI Learning Curve
- Build Then Scale
- Beginner to Builder
- The Automation Journey

### 8. Action-Oriented
Start with a verb that implies doing.

- Build With AI
- Automate Everything
- Ship Faster
- Prompt Better
- Work Smarter Daily

### 9. Metaphor-Driven
Use a metaphor that captures the newsletter's value.

- The AI Compass
- Signal & Noise
- The Cutting Room
- The Lab Report
- Current & Voltage

### 10. Toolkit/Collection
Imply curated, organized resources.

- The AI Stack
- Prompt Vault
- The Tool Shelf
- Workflow Library
- The AI Arsenal

## AI Naming Prompt

Use this prompt to generate newsletter name candidates. Paste into Claude or ChatGPT with your specifics filled in.

```
I'm launching a newsletter about [TOPIC] for [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE].

My 1-liner positioning is: "[YOUR 1-LINER]"

The newsletter will [CADENCE, e.g., "go out every Tuesday"] and each issue will [FORMAT, e.g., "break down one AI tool with a step-by-step workflow"].

Generate 20 newsletter name options across these 10 naming conventions (2 per convention):
1. Topic + Tangible Objects
2. With/For Platform
3. Time-Based
4. Outcome-Oriented
5. Listicle/Numerical
6. Audience Callout
7. Journey/Process
8. Action-Oriented
9. Metaphor-Driven
10. Toolkit/Collection

For each name, score it against these criteria (Yes/No):
- Makes a clear promise about what the reader gets
- Uses tangible, objective language (not vague or subjective)
- Is 7 words or fewer
- Speaks to a specific audience
- Flows naturally when spoken aloud (say it out loud test)

Only include names that score Yes on at least 4 of 5 criteria. For any name that scores No on "tangible, objective language", provide a revised version that passes.

Format as a table: Name | Convention | Promise | Tangible | ≤7 Words | Specific Audience | Natural Flow
```

### How to Use This Prompt

1. Fill in your specifics (topic, audience, 1-liner, cadence, format)
2. Run the prompt
3. Review the table: focus on names that score 5/5
4. Take your top 5-10 and run them through the naming criteria checklist from SKILL.md Step 4
5. Check Beehiiv subdomain availability for your top 3
6. Pick the winner

### Naming Red Flags

Reject any name that:
- Requires explanation ("What does that mean?")
- Could apply to any newsletter in any niche (too generic)
- Uses jargon your target reader wouldn't know
- Is longer than 7 words
- Tries to be clever at the expense of clarity
- Uses a pun that only works in text, not spoken aloud
- Is already the name of a well-known newsletter, podcast, or publication

## Quick Competitive Analysis (15-20 Minutes Max)

Before finalizing your name and positioning, do a quick scan of what already exists in your niche. This is NOT an MBA-level market research exercise. Don't spend months on it. Don't build spreadsheets. Don't use analysis as procrastination. 15-20 minutes of internet exploration, then move on.

### Where to Look

1. **Substack Explore:** Browse your category. Check the leaderboard (top free and paid). Click the top 10 newsletters. Read their 1-liners and About pages. Note what value propositions they lead with.
2. **Beehiiv Explore:** Go to "Beehiiv For" and select your category. Same drill -- scan the top players.
3. **Google:** Search "{your niche} newsletter." Check the first 2 pages of results. Note who shows up.
4. **Social platforms:** Search your niche keyword on X/Twitter, LinkedIn. Who's talking about their newsletter? What angle are they using?
5. **AI shortcut:** Ask Claude or ChatGPT: "List 50 newsletters about {your niche}. For each, give me the name, 1-liner, and estimated subscriber count." Not perfectly accurate, but gives you a fast landscape view.

### What to Look For

- **Positioning gaps:** What angle is nobody taking? If every AI newsletter targets beginners, there's a gap for intermediate users. If everyone writes weekly, there's a gap for daily.
- **Naming patterns:** What naming conventions dominate? If everything is "The AI [Object]," go with a different convention.
- **1-liner quality:** How tangible are their promises? Most will be vague. That's your advantage.
- **What's working:** Which newsletters in your niche have the most subscribers? What are they doing that you can learn from (not copy)?

### The Rule

Competitive analysis answers ONE question: "How is my positioning different from what already exists?" If you can't answer that after 15-20 minutes, your niche-down isn't specific enough. Go back to the 9 Ways to Niche Down Further.

## Traffic Engine Strategy

A newsletter is not a standalone publication. It's the nucleus of an entire business ecosystem. The pattern: one topic drives content across all platforms, the newsletter captures and nurtures the audience, and products/services monetize the relationship.

**Topic → Newsletter → Products/Services**

This is the overarching strategy. Everything feeds the newsletter, the newsletter feeds everything else.

### Proof Patterns

- **Ryan Holiday:** Stoicism → Daily Stoic newsletter → books, courses, medallions, journals ($XX M/yr business)
- **Nicolas Cole:** Digital writing → Ship 30 for 30 newsletter → courses, ghostwriting academy, consulting ($2M+/yr)
- **Codie Sanchez:** Boring businesses → Contrarian Thinking newsletter → courses, deal flow, community ($XX M/yr)

The common thread: none of them just "write a newsletter." They build an ecosystem around one topic. The newsletter is the engine at the center, not the business itself.

### How This Applies

When positioning your newsletter, think beyond the newsletter:
- What products could this topic support? (Templates, courses, coaching, software)
- What content platforms feed subscribers into the newsletter? (Social, podcast, YouTube, SEO)
- What's the full value chain from free reader to highest-ticket buyer?

Your 1-liner and About page should hint at this ecosystem without overwhelming. The newsletter is the entry point, not the ceiling.
