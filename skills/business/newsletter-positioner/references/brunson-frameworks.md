# Brunson Positioning Frameworks for Newsletters

Russell Brunson's positioning and movement-building frameworks applied to newsletter identity. These go beyond USP and naming into building something people want to belong to.

---

## The Secret Formula (4-Step Audience Clarity)

Before you position anything, nail these four questions in order. Skip one and the whole thing wobbles.

### 1. Dream Reader (Who)

Not a demographic. A specific person with a specific problem at a specific moment.

**Build the avatar:**
- [ ] Name them (real or fictional). "Sarah, 34, freelance designer, just lost her biggest client to a Canva-using intern."
- [ ] What do they search for at 2am? (This reveals real pain, not survey pain.)
- [ ] What have they already tried that failed? (This shapes how you frame your newsletter as different.)
- [ ] What would they brag about to their friends if your newsletter helped them achieve it?
- [ ] What language do they use to describe their problem? (Use THEIR words, not yours.)

**Depth check:** If your dream reader could be anyone in your niche, you haven't gone deep enough. "Solopreneurs" is too broad. "Solopreneurs who've been using ChatGPT for 6 months but still can't turn it into revenue" is a dream reader.

### 2. Where They Congregate (Where)

Your dream readers are already gathered somewhere. Find the watering holes.

**Digital congregations:**
- [ ] Which subreddits do they lurk in? (List 3-5 specific ones.)
- [ ] Which YouTube channels do they binge? (Not categories. Specific channels.)
- [ ] Which newsletters do they already read? (These are your direct competitors AND collaboration targets.)
- [ ] Which Twitter/X accounts do they follow?
- [ ] Which Facebook groups or Discord servers are they active in?
- [ ] Which podcasts do they listen to on their commute?

**Physical congregations:**
- [ ] Which conferences or meetups do they attend?
- [ ] Which courses or cohorts have they joined?
- [ ] Which tools or platforms are they paying for? (Tool communities are goldmines.)

**Action step:** Pick 3 congregations. Lurk for a week. Write down the exact questions they ask and the exact frustrations they voice. This language goes directly into your 1-liner and About page.

### 3. Bait (What Attracts Them)

The thing that makes your dream reader stop scrolling and say "I need this."

**Bait criteria:**
- [ ] Solves an immediate, specific problem (not a vague "get better at AI")
- [ ] Delivers value before they subscribe (the landing page itself teaches something)
- [ ] Uses tangible language (see naming-frameworks.md for tangible vs intangible)
- [ ] Creates curiosity about what comes next (the bait is a taste, not the full meal)

**Bait types for newsletters:**
- **The Free Tool:** A working demo, calculator, or template they can use right now. (Strongest bait for AI/tech newsletters.)
- **The Breakdown:** A teardown of something they already use, showing them what they're missing.
- **The Shortcut:** "I spent 200 hours testing AI tools so you don't have to. Here are the 3 that actually work."
- **The Proof:** "Here's how I automated my entire business. Subscribe and I'll show you each piece."
- **The Challenge:** "Join 5,000 others doing the 7-Day AI Automation Challenge. Free via email."

**Test your bait:** Would your dream reader share this with a friend without being asked? If not, the bait isn't strong enough.

### 4. Result You Deliver (Why)

The transformation the reader gets over time. Not what you send them. What changes in their life.

**Result formula:**
- [ ] Before state: Where is your reader RIGHT NOW? (Specific situation, not "struggling.")
- [ ] After state: Where will they be after 90 days of reading? (Specific, measurable.)
- [ ] The gap: What bridges before and after? That's your newsletter's actual job.

**Examples:**
- Before: "Spending 3 hours/day on admin tasks." After: "Spending 30 minutes. AI handles the rest." Gap: Weekly tool breakdowns + implementation guides.
- Before: "Making $0 from AI knowledge." After: "Earning $2K/month from an AI-powered side hustle." Gap: Build-in-public series showing each step.

**Integration with 1-liner:** Your result statement feeds directly into the "outcome" part of the 1-liner formula from naming-frameworks.md. The Secret Formula gives you the depth; the 1-liner formula gives you the compression.

## New Opportunity Positioning

This is the difference between a newsletter people tolerate and one they champion.

### The Core Concept

People don't want improvement. They want something NEW. Improvement reminds them of past failures. A new opportunity gives them a clean slate.

**Improvement offer:** "A better AI newsletter with curated links and tips."
**New opportunity:** "The first newsletter that gives you a working AI tool in every issue. Try it before you read about it."

### Improvement vs New Opportunity Checklist

Score your positioning. Every "Improvement" answer means you need to reframe.

| Question | Improvement (weak) | New Opportunity (strong) |
|----------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Does your 1-liner compare you to other newsletters? | "Better than other AI newsletters" | "Something no other newsletter does" |
| Does your About page reference what readers tried before? | "Tired of newsletters that just list tools?" | "What if every newsletter came with a tool you could try right now?" |
| Does your name imply iteration? | "AI Newsletter 2.0", "Better AI Brief" | "The AI Workbench" (entirely new frame) |
| Does your positioning ask readers to change behavior? | "Read more carefully, implement what we share" | "Just click the tool. No reading required to get value." |
| Does your promise sound like something they've heard? | "Curated AI news delivered weekly" | "One AI skill packaged as a tool, delivered Tuesday" |

### How to Find Your New Opportunity Angle

1. **List what readers hate about current newsletters in your niche.** (Too long, too many links, no practical value, generic tips, sounds like everyone else.)
2. **For each complaint, ask: "What's the opposite?"** ("Too long" -> "Fits in 5 minutes." "No practical value" -> "Includes a working tool.")
3. **Pick the opposite that you can ACTUALLY deliver consistently.** Don't promise what you can't sustain for 100+ issues.
4. **Frame it as a vehicle, not a destination.** "This newsletter is a new WAY to learn AI" not "This newsletter teaches AI." The vehicle is the differentiator.

### The Vehicle Frame

Brunson's key insight: people buy vehicles, not destinations. Everyone wants the destination (make money, save time, grow audience). The vehicle is what's new.

**Destination (everyone says this):** "Learn to use AI in your business."
**Vehicle (only you say this):** "Every week, I build an AI tool live and ship it to you. You learn by using what I build."

**Apply to your newsletter:**
- [ ] What is your unique vehicle? (HOW you deliver value, not WHAT value you deliver.)
- [ ] Can you name it? (A named vehicle becomes ownable. "The Tool-First Method." "The Build-in-Public Playbook.")
- [ ] Does your vehicle make old approaches feel outdated? (Not by attacking them. By being obviously better.)

### Status Elevation

A new opportunity must also raise the reader's perceived status. If reading your newsletter makes them feel smarter, more capable, or part of something exclusive, they'll stick.

- [ ] Does subscribing signal something about the reader? ("I read The AI Workbench" should feel like a badge.)
- [ ] Can readers reference your newsletter in conversations to look informed?
- [ ] Does your content give them language and frameworks they can use with peers?

## Movement Building for Newsletter Identity

A newsletter with subscribers is a list. A newsletter with a movement is a business. This section turns your positioning into something people identify with.

### The 5 Movement Elements

Build all five. Skip one and you have a newsletter. Build all five and you have a tribe.

#### 1. Tribal Name

What do your readers call themselves?

**Criteria:**
- [ ] One or two words max
- [ ] Something readers would say out loud without cringing
- [ ] Implies membership, not just subscription
- [ ] Creates an us-vs-them without being negative

**Examples:**
- "Corsairs" (adventurers, not conformists)
- "Builders" (people who make things, not just consume)
- "The Automated" (people who've escaped manual work)

**Test:** Would a reader put this in their Twitter bio? If yes, you have a tribal name. If no, keep iterating.

**How to find yours:**
1. What identity does your reader WANT to have? (Not what they are now. What they aspire to.)
2. What single word captures that identity?
3. Add "The" or make it plural. Test both.

#### 2. "I Am" Statement

A one-sentence declaration that members can say to define what the movement stands for.

**Formula:** "I am a [tribal name] who [core belief/behavior]."

**Examples:**
- "I am a Corsair who builds AI systems instead of consuming AI content."
- "I am a Builder who ships one automation every week."
- "I am part of The Automated, and I don't do manually what a machine can do better."

**Rules:**
- [ ] Must feel aspirational but achievable
- [ ] Must create separation from "everyone else"
- [ ] Must connect to your newsletter's core promise
- [ ] Reader should feel a small surge of pride when they say it

**Use it:** In welcome emails. In social posts. On the landing page. Repeat it until it sticks.

#### 3. Manifesto (The Belief Document)

A short declaration of what your movement believes and what it rejects. Not a mission statement. A line in the sand.

**Structure (keep it to 10-15 lines max):**

1. **The World As It Is** (2-3 sentences): What's broken about the current state? What frustrates your dream reader?
2. **What We Believe** (3-5 bullet points): Your core principles. Each one should be slightly contrarian or at least distinct from mainstream thinking.
3. **What We Reject** (2-3 bullet points): What does your movement stand AGAINST? This is what creates the boundary between members and non-members.
4. **The Promise** (1-2 sentences): Where this movement is headed. The future state you're building together.

**Checklist:**
- [ ] Can you read it in under 60 seconds?
- [ ] Does at least one belief make some people uncomfortable? (If everyone agrees, it's not a manifesto. It's wallpaper.)
- [ ] Does it use "we" language, not "I" language? (The manifesto belongs to the movement, not the leader.)
- [ ] Could a member share it and have someone instantly understand what this is about?

**Where to use it:** Pin it to your About page. Include a condensed version in your welcome email. Reference individual beliefs in newsletter issues.

#### 4. Milestone Awards

People stay when they feel progress. Create visible milestones that mark a reader's journey.

**Newsletter milestone ideas:**
- [ ] **Welcome Badge:** First-issue milestone. "You're officially a [Tribal Name]." (Welcome email.)
- [ ] **Streak Recognition:** "You've read 10 consecutive issues." (Beehiiv engagement tracking.)
- [ ] **Implementation Award:** Reader replies with proof they used a tool from an issue. Feature them.
- [ ] **Referral Tiers:** Beehiiv's referral program lets you set reward tiers. Name them after movement milestones, not generic rewards.
- [ ] **Founding Member Status:** Early subscribers get a permanent label. Creates urgency + loyalty.

**Implementation on Beehiiv:**
- Use referral rewards for tangible milestones (3 referrals = exclusive resource, 10 = name in the newsletter, 25 = 1-on-1 call)
- Tag engaged readers (high open rate, frequent replies) and reference them by tribal name in issues
- Create a "Hall of Fame" or "Builder Spotlight" section for members who implement

#### 5. Social Mission (The Cause Bigger Than Content)

A movement needs a "why" that goes beyond information delivery. What are you collectively building or changing?

**Finding your social mission:**
- [ ] What injustice or inefficiency does your movement exist to fix?
- [ ] If your newsletter disappeared tomorrow, what would be lost beyond the content?
- [ ] What would 10,000 activated members of your movement change about the world?

**Examples:**
- "We believe everyone deserves AI-powered leverage, not just funded startups. We're democratizing the tools."
- "We're proving that one person with AI can outperform a team of 10. Every issue is another proof point."
- "We're building the open-source playbook for AI-powered businesses. Every member's win is a chapter."

**Rules:**
- [ ] Must be genuine (readers detect manufactured causes instantly)
- [ ] Must connect to your newsletter's content (not a random charity bolt-on)
- [ ] Must be something members can participate in, not just observe
- [ ] Should make members feel like they're part of something that matters

## Integration: How These Frameworks Connect

The three Brunson frameworks feed into each other and into the existing positioning workflow:

**Secret Formula** answers WHO you serve, WHERE to find them, WHAT hooks them, and WHY they stay. This sharpens Steps 2-3 of the main SKILL.md workflow (sub-niche angle and 1-liner).

**New Opportunity Positioning** ensures your newsletter isn't "another one of those." This directly improves the USP Acid Test from kennedy-positioning.md by adding the vehicle frame on top of the unique benefit.

**Movement Building** transforms subscribers into members. This feeds into the About page (Step 5 in SKILL.md), the welcome email, and long-term retention via newsletter-grower.

### Quick Audit Checklist

Before finalizing your positioning brief, verify:

- [ ] Secret Formula: Can you fill in all 4 boxes (dream reader, congregation, bait, result) with specifics, not generalities?
- [ ] New Opportunity: Is your newsletter framed as a new vehicle, not an improvement on existing newsletters?
- [ ] Movement - Tribal Name: Do you have a name your readers would use to describe themselves?
- [ ] Movement - "I Am" Statement: Can members say it out loud and feel something?
- [ ] Movement - Manifesto: Do you have a belief document with at least one contrarian stance?
- [ ] Movement - Milestones: Are there at least 3 visible progress markers for readers?
- [ ] Movement - Social Mission: Is there a cause bigger than content that members can participate in?
