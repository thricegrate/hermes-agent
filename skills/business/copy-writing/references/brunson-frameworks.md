# Brunson Copy Frameworks

Russell Brunson's persuasion frameworks. These are structural principles that sit on TOP of the formulas in `formula-decision-tree.md` and `kennedy-copy-foundations.md`. Use them to architect the persuasion arc before you write a single line.

---

## 1. Hook, Story, Offer (HSO)

The atomic unit of all persuasion. Every ad, email, funnel page, social post, and webinar section is built from this three-part structure. If your copy is not converting, one of these three pieces is broken.

### Hook

The hook has ONE job: stop the scroll. Create a pattern interrupt that opens a curiosity gap the reader must close.

**The core formula:** "How to [awesome result] without [awful thing they expect]"

**What makes a hook work:**
- Pattern interrupt. Say something that violates expectation. Not louder, not bolder. Different.
- Curiosity gap. The reader needs to know "how?" or "why?" before they can move on.
- Slightly odd specificity. "How a 3am email to my dentist turned into $47K" beats "How I made money online." Weird details signal real stories.
- Pair with a slightly odd image when possible. A hook + unexpected visual = double pattern interrupt.

**Hook diagnostic:** If your hook could appear on any competitor's page, it is not a hook. It is a headline. Hooks are specific, strange, and incomplete. They create tension that only the story resolves.

**Quick hook templates (Brunson-specific):**
- "How to [result] without [pain]" + odd image
- "I was [embarrassing situation] when I discovered..."
- "The [number]-second trick that [result]"
- "[Authority figure] told me I was crazy. Then I showed them [proof]."

### Story (The Epiphany Bridge)

The story is NOT a testimonial. Not a case study. Not a feature walkthrough. It is a narrative that creates an epiphany in the reader's mind so they arrive at the same belief you hold.

**The Epiphany Bridge structure:**

1. **Backstory.** Where you (or the character) were before. Make the starting point match the reader's current situation. "I was broke, overwhelmed, and had tried three other tools that all promised the same thing."

2. **The wall.** What stopped you. The external event or internal frustration that made the old way impossible. This is the conflict. Without conflict, there is no story. "I hit a wall. I was working 14-hour days and my revenue was going backwards."

3. **The villain.** Name the thing (not a person, unless appropriate) that was keeping you stuck. Bad advice, a broken system, a false belief, the industry consensus. The villain gives the reader someone to root against. "Everyone said you needed to post daily on 5 platforms. That advice was killing me."

4. **The epiphany.** The moment everything changed. This is the emotional peak. Make it specific: when, where, what triggered it. "I was sitting in a parking lot, listening to a podcast, and I realized I had been optimizing the wrong metric the entire time."

5. **The transformation.** What happened after. Specific results, specific timeline. "Within 6 weeks, I went from $2K/mo to $11K/mo. Same product. Same list size. Different system."

**Why this works:** People do not buy products. They buy the belief that the product will work for them. The Epiphany Bridge does not sell the product. It sells the epiphany. Once the reader has the same "aha" moment you had, the offer is a logical next step. You are not convincing them. You are helping them convince themselves.

**Critical rule:** The story must create an emotional experience, not deliver information. If you strip out all the facts and the story still moves the reader, it is working. If you strip out the emotion and there is nothing left, it is a feature list dressed up as a story.

**Story diagnostic questions:**
- Does this story have a specific villain?
- Does the reader feel the wall (not just understand it)?
- Is the epiphany a single moment, or a vague "I realized over time"?
- Would the reader say "that is exactly what happened to me" about the backstory?

### Offer

The offer only works AFTER the story has aligned the reader's beliefs. If the story did its job, the offer feels like the natural next step, not a pitch.

**Offer mechanics:**
- Restate the transformation from the story as a promise: "This is exactly what [product] does for you."
- Specific action: what to click, what to fill out, what happens next.
- Specific benefit: what they get and when they get it.
- Remove the last objection: guarantee, risk reversal, or proof point.

**Where to apply HSO:**
- Funnel pages (each page gets its own HSO)
- Individual emails (hook = subject line + first line, story = body, offer = CTA)
- Social media posts (hook = first line, story = middle, offer = last line)
- Webinar sections (each teaching section opens with a hook, delivers via story, transitions to an offer or next section)
- Ads (compressed HSO: hook in headline, story in body, offer in CTA)

## 2. Pre-Frame Bridge Copy

Different traffic temperatures need different copy. Sending cold traffic to a sales page built for fans is the most common funnel mistake. The pre-frame bridge matches copy length, tone, and trust-building to the reader's starting temperature.

### Hot Traffic (Fans and Customers)

**Who they are:** People who already know you, trust you, and have bought from you before. Your email list. Your repeat customers. Your community members.

**Pre-frame needed:** Minimal. They already believe. You just need to tell them what is new.

**Copy approach:**
- Short. Direct. No lengthy story needed.
- Email or social post linking straight to the offer page.
- Tone: casual, insider, "hey, I made something new for you."
- Skip the big backstory. They lived through it with you.

**Example flow:** Email ("Hey, I just finished building [thing]. Here is why I think you will love it.") -> Direct to offer page.

**Mistake to avoid:** Over-explaining to people who already trust you. It signals you do not trust the relationship.

### Warm Traffic (Referred or Aware)

**Who they are:** People who know OF you but have not bought. They follow you on social media, read your content, or were referred by someone they trust. They are interested but not committed.

**Pre-frame needed:** Medium. Leverage the existing relationship (their trust in the referrer, or their familiarity with your content).

**Copy approach:**
- Medium length. Enough to build specific credibility but not a full sales pitch.
- Bridge page or dedicated landing page that establishes YOUR authority (not just the referrer's).
- Address "why should I listen to you specifically?" with a compressed story or proof stack.
- Tone: professional but warm. Prove competence without performing.

**Example flow:** Social post or referral link -> Bridge page (your story + key proof points + "here is why this matters for you") -> Offer page.

**Mistake to avoid:** Treating warm traffic like hot traffic. They know your name but have not experienced your value. The bridge closes that gap.

### Cold Traffic (Strangers)

**Who they are:** People who have never heard of you. They clicked an ad, stumbled on a search result, or landed on your page from a link with zero context.

**Pre-frame needed:** Maximum. You must earn the right to be heard before you pitch anything. Cold traffic without a pre-frame bridge converts at near zero.

**Copy approach:**
- Full bridge page before they see any offer. Educate, qualify, and warm them to your worldview.
- Long-form if needed. These people have zero trust. You are building it from scratch.
- Lead with the problem (they came because something hurts). Prove you understand it better than they do.
- Establish credibility through story, not credentials. Cold readers do not care about your resume. They care about proof that you have been where they are.
- Qualify them: "This is for people who [specific situation]. If that is not you, no hard feelings."
- THEN move them to the offer page, where they arrive with context, trust, and self-qualification.

**Example flow:** Ad -> Bridge page (problem + story + proof + qualification) -> Offer page.

**Mistake to avoid:** Sending cold ad traffic directly to a sales page. The sales page assumes a level of trust that does not exist yet. The bridge page builds that trust.

### Traffic Temperature Decision Matrix

Before writing copy, answer: "Where is this reader coming from, and how much do they already trust me?"

- **Hot:** Skip the bridge. Email/post -> offer.
- **Warm:** Short bridge. Landing page with compressed proof -> offer.
- **Cold:** Full bridge. Ad -> bridge page -> offer.

The copy length, tone, and trust-building intensity all change based on this one question. Get it wrong and even perfect copy underperforms.

## 3. Secret Formula (4 Questions Before Writing ANY Copy)

These four questions are the strategic foundation. Answer them before you pick a framework, write a headline, or open a doc. They come before Kennedy's 10 Market Diagnosis Questions (which go deeper into the psychology). These four set the strategic direction.

### Question 1: Who is your dream client?

Not a demographic. Not "women 25-45 who like fitness." A specific person with a specific name, a specific frustration, and a specific desire.

**How to find them:**
- Think of your single best customer. The one who got the best result, was easiest to work with, and would buy again tomorrow.
- Describe that person in detail. Age, role, what they were struggling with when they found you, what they said in their first message.
- That person is your dream client. Write all copy as if you are talking to them.

**Why this matters for copy:** Vague audiences produce vague copy. A specific dream client produces copy that reads like a personal letter. The more specific your audience definition, the more magnetic your copy becomes.

### Question 2: Where do you find them?

Not "on the internet." Specifically.

- What platforms are they active on? (Specific subreddits, Facebook groups, YouTube channels, podcasts, newsletters they read)
- What events do they attend? (Conferences, meetups, webinars)
- What keywords do they search? (Exact phrases, not categories)
- Whose content do they already consume and trust?

**Why this matters for copy:** The platform shapes the copy format, length, and tone. LinkedIn copy is not Twitter copy is not email copy. And the trusted voices in their space tell you what language, proof types, and positioning will resonate.

### Question 3: What bait attracts them?

What free thing would your dream client grab without hesitation? This is your lead magnet, your hook, your conversation starter.

**The test:** If you put this offer in front of your dream client, would they give you their email in under 5 seconds? If the answer is "maybe" or "after reading the landing page," the bait is not strong enough.

**Bait criteria:**
- Solves a specific, immediate problem (not a general educational resource)
- Delivers value in under 10 minutes (not a 47-page ebook they will never read)
- Directly relates to your paid offer (the bait is a taste of the transformation, not a random freebie)

### Question 4: What result do you deliver?

Not what your product IS. What transformation does the customer experience? Before state -> After state.

**How to articulate it:**
- Before: "[Dream client] is [specific painful situation]."
- After: "[Dream client] is [specific desired situation]."
- Your product is the bridge.

**Why this matters for copy:** Every headline, every CTA, every story should point at this transformation. If your copy does not clearly communicate "you are HERE, you want to be THERE, this gets you there," it is not doing its job.

### Using the Secret Formula

Write out all four answers on one page. Pin it above your desk (or at the top of your copy doc). Every piece of copy you write should be addressed to Answer 1, distributed where Answer 2 says, baited with Answer 3, and promising Answer 4.

If any of the four answers is vague, your copy will be vague. Sharpen the answers first.

## 4. Blair Warren's 5 Persuasion Levers

These five emotional levers bypass rational resistance. Brunson teaches them as the most powerful opening paragraphs you can write. Weave one or more into the first 2-3 sentences of any copy piece to create instant emotional alignment.

### The 5 Levers

**1. Encourage their dreams.**
Tell them the thing they secretly want is possible. Not in a vague "you can do anything" way. In a specific "here is proof that someone like you achieved this exact thing" way.

Copy application: "You are right to want [their dream]. And here is someone who was exactly where you are 6 months ago..."

**2. Justify their failures.**
Tell them it is not their fault. The thing they tried before did not work because the approach was wrong, not because they are wrong. This is not manipulation. Most people genuinely failed because of bad advice, bad tools, or bad timing, not personal deficiency.

Copy application: "If [common approach] did not work for you, it is not because you did not try hard enough. It is because [specific reason the approach fails]."

**3. Allay their fears.**
Name the fear they have not spoken out loud and then neutralize it. The unnamed fear is the most powerful objection because the reader cannot articulate why they are hesitating.

Copy application: "You might be thinking: what if I invest time in this and it does not work? Here is exactly why that will not happen: [specific mechanism or guarantee]."

**4. Throw rocks at their enemies.**
Identify the thing (industry, bad advice, a common frustration source) that your reader blames for their situation. Validate that blame. Then position your product as the alternative.

Copy application: "The [industry/guru/old approach] told you [bad advice]. They were wrong. And here is why: [specific evidence]."

**5. Confirm their suspicions.**
Your reader already suspects something is true but has not had it confirmed. When you say it out loud, they feel seen. Instant trust.

Copy application: "You have probably suspected for a while that [thing they suspect]. You are right. And here is the data: [proof]."

### How to Use the 5 Levers

- Pick 1-2 levers per piece of copy. Do not force all 5 into one opening.
- The strongest lever depends on your audience's emotional state. Angry audiences respond to #4 and #5. Discouraged audiences respond to #1 and #2. Anxious audiences respond to #3.
- Place them in the opening paragraphs, before any pitch or product mention. They create emotional buy-in that makes everything after more persuasive.
- These levers work in any format: email, ad, landing page, social post, webinar intro, video script.

### Lever Selection by Audience State

| Audience state | Primary lever | Secondary lever |
|---|---|---|
| Discouraged / given up | Encourage dreams (#1) | Justify failures (#2) |
| Angry / frustrated | Throw rocks (#4) | Confirm suspicions (#5) |
| Anxious / risk-averse | Allay fears (#3) | Justify failures (#2) |
| Skeptical / burned before | Confirm suspicions (#5) | Throw rocks (#4) |
| Hopeful but unsure | Encourage dreams (#1) | Allay fears (#3) |

### 5 Levers + HSO Integration

The 5 levers slot directly into the Hook and Story phases of HSO:

- **Hook:** Use a lever to create emotional resonance in the first 1-2 sentences. "You have probably suspected that [suspicion]. You are right." (Lever #5 as hook.)
- **Story:** Weave levers into the Epiphany Bridge. The backstory can justify failures (#2). The villain section can throw rocks (#4). The epiphany can encourage dreams (#1).
- **Offer:** The offer itself allays fears (#3) through guarantees and risk reversal.

## Framework Integration Notes

### HSO vs. Existing Frameworks

HSO is not a replacement for AIDA, PAS, BAB, PASTOR, or any framework in `formula-decision-tree.md`. It is the meta-structure that contains them.

- AIDA describes the psychological stages (attention, interest, desire, action). HSO provides the content structure (hook, story, offer) that moves people through those stages.
- PAS is an agitation technique. It fits inside the Story phase of HSO as one way to tell the story.
- PASTOR is a long-form expansion. HSO is the compressed version. A PASTOR sales page is HSO with more room for each section.

### Pre-Frame Bridge + Kennedy Copy Gate

Before writing bridge copy, run Kennedy's 10 Market Diagnosis Questions (see `kennedy-copy-foundations.md`). The answers tell you what the bridge page needs to address for cold traffic. The Secret Formula (4 Questions) sets the strategic direction. Kennedy's 10 questions go deep on psychology. Use both.

### When to Reach for This File

- Starting a new funnel or page from scratch: Begin with Secret Formula (4 Questions), then structure with HSO.
- Copy is not converting: Diagnose which HSO element is broken (weak hook? no story? premature offer?).
- Running paid ads to a sales page: Check traffic temperature and add the right pre-frame bridge.
- Opening paragraphs feel flat: Apply 1-2 Blair Warren levers to the first 2-3 sentences.
