## Part 3: Hook Opening Line Templates

The hook's job: earn the reader's first sentence. These templates adapt to any topic.

### Hook Type 1: Pattern Interrupt
Stops the reader cold by violating their expectation.

Template: "Stop everything you are doing — this changes [topic] entirely."
Example: "Stop writing your emails before you read this. Everything you know about subject lines is costing you opens."
Best for: Cold audiences, social posts, email openers

---

### Hook Type 2: The Contrarian Challenge
Triggers defensiveness that converts to curiosity.

Template: "You are doing [common practice] completely wrong — and here is why."
Example: "You are building your email list wrong. The opt-in form is the last thing that matters."
Best for: Authority positioning, content that challenges established thinking

---

### Hook Type 3: The Statistic That Creates a Problem
Builds credibility and surfaces a problem the reader did not know they had.

Template: "Did you know [surprising statistic about their situation]?"
Example: "Did you know the average landing page converts at 2.35%? The top 10% convert at 11.45%. You are probably not in the top 10%."
Best for: Problem-unaware audiences, ad copy, article leads

---

### Hook Type 4: The Shared Struggle
Creates immediate connection through empathy.

Template: "If you have ever [specific frustration], you know exactly what this feels like."
Example: "If you have ever spent three hours writing an email that got a 12% open rate, you know the feeling."
Best for: Email, sales pages, testimonial leads

---

### Hook Type 5: The Story Hook
Opens a narrative loop the reader must close.

Template: "[Specific moment that creates intrigue]. What happened next changed everything."
Example: "The agency sent us a bill for $47,000. We had made $0 from the campaign. What I learned that day is the only reason this business exists."
Best for: Sales pages, long-form email, VSL scripts

---

### Hook Type 6: The Revelation Hook
Positions the reader's current belief as wrong.

Template: "This is not what you think — [surprising fact] about [topic]."
Example: "This is not what you think. The #1 reason your copy does not convert is not the copy."
Best for: Content marketing, social posts, email subjects

---

### Hook Type 7: The Question Hook
Closes a loop the reader has to open.

Template: "Have you ever wondered why [specific mystery in your niche]?"
Example: "Have you ever wondered why some newsletters charge $50 CPM while yours is stuck at $3?"
Best for: Blog posts, curiosity-based email campaigns

---

### Hook Type 8: The Specificity Hook
Signals credibility through a precise number or claim.

Template: "[Specific number] [specific outcome] in [specific timeframe] — here is how."
Example: "4,127 new subscribers in 23 days from one piece of content — here is the exact process."
Best for: Cold traffic ads, social proof posts, lead magnets

---

### Hook Type 9: The Desired Outcome Hook
Opens with where the reader wants to end up.

Template: "Want to [desired outcome]? Here is the only [framework/system/habit] you need."
Example: "Want to write a high-converting email in under 30 minutes? Here is the only system that actually works."
Best for: Warm traffic, email to an engaged list, YouTube thumbnails

---

### Hook Type 10: The Negative Consequence Hook
Activates loss aversion before the solution appears.

Template: "Every [time period] you [do not solve X], you are losing [specific cost]."
Example: "Every month your landing page sits at 2% conversion, you are leaving $8,000 on the table at 500 visits per day."
Best for: Sales pages, urgency emails, remarketing ads

---

### Hook Type 11: The Social Proof Hook
Leads with the crowd to build trust immediately.

Template: "Over [specific number] [audience type] have already [outcome] — here is what they know."
Example: "Over 12,000 newsletter operators have already made this shift. Here is why the rest are falling behind."
Best for: Skeptical audiences, trust-gap situations, product launches

---

### Hook Type 12: The Insider Knowledge Hook
Implies the reader is about to get exclusive access.

Template: "[Thing experts know] that [everyone else] never talks about."
Example: "The one metric every top copywriter tracks — and why most marketing teams have never heard of it."
Best for: Authority content, premium offer positioning, cold email

---

### Hook Type 13: The Direct Accusation
Calls the reader out on behavior they already know is wrong. Creates instant self-recognition.

Template: "You are [doing wrong thing]. [Consequence they already know]."
Example: "You are writing LinkedIn posts for your mom, not your audience. That is why nobody engages."
Best for: Social posts, email openers, pattern interrupts for warm audiences

**Rules:** Must be true (reader self-recognizes), must be fixable (the content shows how), not mean-spirited.

**Source:** Ruben Hassid (770K LinkedIn followers)

---

### Hook Type 14: The Stolen Thought
Voices what the reader secretly thinks but will not say publicly. Creates a "how did you know?" moment.

Template: "You know [uncomfortable truth]. So does [who else knows]."
Example: "You know your LinkedIn posts are boring. So does everyone scrolling past them."
Best for: Social media hooks, newsletter subject lines, community posts

**Rules:** Thought must be genuinely private. Tone empathetic, not judgmental. Immediately offer the fix.

**Source:** Ruben Hassid (770K LinkedIn followers)

---

### Hook Type 15: The Absurd Reframe
Takes a mundane topic and describes it with life-or-death stakes. Dramatization maps to a real constraint.

Template: "Your [mundane thing] has [dramatic constraint]. [Most fail]."
Example: "Your LinkedIn hook has 1.2 seconds to live. Most die instantly."
Best for: Educational content, platform-specific advice, social posts about craft/technique

**Rules:** Dramatization must map to a real, verifiable constraint. Do not cross into parody.

**Source:** Ruben Hassid (770K LinkedIn followers)

## Part 4: 9-Step Headline Construction Process

Use this to build any headline from scratch. Produces a three-part system: eyebrow, headline, subheadline.

**Step 1: Address Your Target**
Name the ideal reader precisely. Repel everyone else. The more specific, the harder the right person pays attention.

**Step 2: Mention Their Biggest Pain**
Empathize. Name their biggest struggle. Shared pain creates connection.

**Step 3: Create Curiosity**
Mix in curiosity-inducing words and phrases. Tease the solution without revealing it.

**Step 4: Highlight the Benefit**
Show them exactly what is in it for them. Answer the curiosity with an outcome.

**Step 5: Evoke Emotion**
Infuse emotional language so the outcome registers beyond the rational level.

**Step 6: Add a Timeframe**
A specific timeframe converts "sounds good" into "I want that."

**Step 7: Name Your Unique Mechanism**
Present the offer. Then separate it from every competitor with a specific differentiator.

**Step 8: Add Social Proof**
Ratings, customer counts, or third-party endorsements. Pick the strongest.

**Step 9: Format the Headline**
- **Eyebrow (Steps 1-2):** Target + pain. Small text above the main headline.
- **Headline (Steps 3-7):** Curiosity + benefit + emotion + timeframe + mechanism.
- **Subheadline (Step 8):** Social proof. Validates everything above.

**Worked example:**
- Eyebrow: "For working dads over 30 struggling to lose unwanted fat..."
- Headline: "Discover how to lose weight and regain your lean look from your 20s in just 12 weeks with our fit-from-home workout program"
- Subheadline: "Rated 5 stars by 2,500+ (now fit) dads"

## Part 5: Headline Weakness Checklist

Run every headline through this before publishing. A strong headline passes 5+ of 8 criteria.

### Failure 1: Feature-First Language
**Symptom:** The headline describes what the product does, not what the reader gets.
**Test:** Does the headline answer "what is in it for me?" or "what does this product have?"
**Fix:** Find the feature in your headline. Ask "so what does that mean for the reader?" Put that answer in the headline instead.
**Before/After:** "Our platform uses AI-powered matching algorithms" → "Find the right candidate in 4 hours, not 4 weeks"

---

### Failure 2: Vague Qualifiers
**Symptom:** Words like "more," "better," "faster," "results," "success," "improve."
**Test:** Can you replace every adjective and qualifier with a specific number, percentage, or timeframe?
**Fix:** Replace every relative term with a measurable equivalent.
**Before/After:** "Get better results from your email campaigns" → "Increase your email open rate from 22% to 41% in 30 days"

---

### Failure 3: Competitor-Generic Claims
**Symptom:** Your headline could appear on any competitor's website without lying.
**Test:** If a direct competitor could use this headline, it has no differentiation value.
**Fix:** Name your unique mechanism, your specific differentiator, or use "The only [product] that [specific claim]."
**Before/After:** "Reliable CRM software you can trust" → "The only CRM that logs every call automatically without browser extensions"

---

### Failure 4: No Emotional Trigger
**Symptom:** Technically accurate but emotionally flat. Reader processes it without reacting.
**Test:** Read the headline and ask: does this make me feel anything?
**Fix:** Add a curiosity gap, a pain point, or a high-stakes outcome. Use power words from the emotional trigger list.
**Before/After:** "Tips for clearer skin" → "Discover the secret to flawless skin and say goodbye to acne forever"

---

### Failure 5: Too Long to Scan
**Symptom:** Headline exceeds 15-17 words. Loses readers before they finish reading it.
**Test:** Read it aloud in one breath. If you need to pause, it is too long.
**Fix:** Find the single most compelling sentence inside the headline. Cut the rest and make that your headline.
**Before/After:** "Introducing our new software solution that helps remote teams collaborate more efficiently with fewer meetings" → "Collaborate faster. Ship sooner."

---

### Failure 6: Misleading or Unbelievable Claims
**Symptom:** Bold claim without evidence, or a claim that triggers skepticism.
**Test:** Does the headline sound like a scam? Could a reasonable person dismiss it as hype?
**Fix:** Make the boldest true claim you can. Back it with specifics — numbers, names, or mechanisms — that make it plausible.
**Before/After:** "This ONE trick will make you rich overnight" → "The single habit that grew our newsletter revenue 40% in 90 days"

---

### Failure 7: Reader-Absent Language
**Symptom:** "We," "our product," "the company" — brand-centric rather than reader-centric.
**Test:** Count the "you" vs. "we/our" ratio. Strong headlines lead with the reader.
**Fix:** Rewrite every brand-centric phrase as a reader-centric outcome.
**Before/After:** "Our tool transforms your workflow" → "You will never miss another deadline with this system"

---

### Failure 8: Self-Centered Framing
**Symptom:** Headline is about the brand's achievement, not the reader's problem. "We hit 100K users!" or "I grew to 770K followers!"
**Test:** Is the subject of the headline YOU/YOUR BRAND or the READER? Achievement posts get congratulations, not conversions.
**Fix:** Make the reader the subject. Their problem, their outcome, their transformation.
**Before/After:** "I grew my LinkedIn to 770K followers in 3 years" -> "The LinkedIn hook formula that turns 8K followers into 770K (without ads)"

**Source:** Ruben Hassid (770K LinkedIn followers)

## Headline Quality Filter (8 Criteria)

Before publishing, score your headline 0-1 on each criterion. A publishable headline scores 5+.

| Criterion | Test | Fail signal |
|---|---|---|
| Eye-catching | Does it stop the eye immediately? | Blends into surrounding content |
| Emotional | Does it trigger a feeling? | Purely informational, flat tone |
| Scroll-inducing | Does it pull the reader to the next line? | Self-contained, reader can stop here |
| Unique | Could a competitor use this headline? | Generic, category-level claim |
| Credible | Does bold claim have backing? | Unsubstantiated superlatives |
| Curiosity-creating | Does it tease without fully revealing? | Complete information, no gap |
| Benefit-oriented | Does it lead with reader gain? | Feature-first or brand-first |
| Urgent | Does it give reason to act now vs. later? | No time pressure, no loss framing |
