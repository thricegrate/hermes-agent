# Kennedy Copy Foundations

Dan Kennedy's direct response copywriting fundamentals. Use these before writing any marketing copy. They're the foundation layer that goes underneath every framework (AIDA, PAS, BAB, etc.).

## The 10 Smart Market Diagnosis Questions

Answer these BEFORE you write a single word. Not after. Not during. Before.

If you skip this step, you're guessing. And guessing is expensive.

1. **What keeps them awake at night?** Eyes open, staring at the ceiling. Not the surface problem. The thing they can't stop thinking about at 2am.
2. **What are they afraid of?** Real fears. Job loss. Looking stupid. Missing the window. Being left behind.
3. **What are they angry about? Who are they angry at?** Anger is fuel. Tap into it. Are they angry at their boss? The industry? Themselves?
4. **What are their top three daily frustrations?** The small, grinding annoyances they deal with every single day. These are often more motivating than the big fears.
5. **What trends are happening in their business or life?** What's changing around them? What's getting harder? What's getting disrupted?
6. **What do they secretly, ardently desire most?** The thing they want but won't say out loud at dinner. Status. Freedom. Revenge. Recognition.
7. **Is there a built-in bias to how they make decisions?** Do they default to the cheapest option? The most popular? The one their peers use? The one with the most proof?
8. **Do they have their own language?** Every market has jargon, slang, shorthand. Use it. If you sound like an outsider, you are one.
9. **Who else is selling something similar? How?** Not just competitors. Adjacent solutions. DIY alternatives. Free options. "Do nothing" is your biggest competitor.
10. **Who else has tried selling them something similar and failed? Why?** This tells you what NOT to do. Their failed purchases created objections you need to overcome.

### How to Use This

Pull up this list at the start of every copy project. Write out answers for each question. If you can't answer at least 7 of 10, you don't know your market well enough to write copy that converts. Go research first.

The answers become your headline angles, your agitation paragraphs, your objection handling, and your CTA urgency. Everything flows from knowing the customer this deeply.

## PAS: Problem, Agitation, Solution

You probably know PAS already. But most people get it wrong. Here's what Kennedy actually teaches.

### Problem
State the problem clearly. Say just enough to get agreement. "You know that feeling when your inbox has 200 unread emails and you don't know which ones matter?" That's it. Get the nod. Move on.

### Agitation (THIS IS WHERE THE SALE IS MADE)
This is where 90% of copywriters fail. They rush to the solution. Don't.

The agitation phase is where you make the problem feel REAL. Visceral. Urgent. You pour salt in the wound. Not to be cruel. Because the reader needs to feel the full weight of the problem before they'll pay for the solution.

Techniques for agitation:
- **Stack consequences.** "And it's not just the lost time. It's the deals that slip through. The follow-ups that never happen. The revenue you'll never see."
- **Project forward.** "What happens in 6 months if nothing changes? In a year?"
- **Make it personal.** "Your competitor figured this out last quarter. They're not losing deals anymore."
- **Use their language.** Reference frustrations from Question 4 of the Market Diagnosis.

The agitation should be 2-3x longer than the problem statement. If your agitation is shorter than your solution, you've got the ratio backwards.

### Solution
NOW you present the answer. Not before. The solution feels like relief after the agitation. Like cold water after a long run. This is where you introduce your product, service, or offer.

## Feature-to-Benefit Translation

Every feature must be translated into a benefit before it appears in copy. Kennedy's rule: features tell, benefits sell.

### The Translation Process

1. List every feature of your product or service
2. For each feature, ask: "So what? What does this mean for the customer?"
3. Then ask again: "And why does THAT matter?"
4. The second answer is your real benefit

**Example:**
- Feature: "24/7 customer support"
- So what? "You can get help anytime."
- Why does that matter? "You'll never lose a sale because you got stuck at midnight before a launch."

That last line is your copy. Not "24/7 support."

### Benefit Categories

Map each benefit to one of these:
- **Save or make money** (financial)
- **Save time or effort** (convenience)
- **Reduce risk or worry** (security)
- **Gain status or approval** (social)
- **Feel good or avoid pain** (emotional)

The strongest benefits hit multiple categories at once.

## The Damaging Admission Technique

This one is counterintuitive and incredibly powerful.

**The idea:** Openly admit a flaw, weakness, or limitation of your product BEFORE the prospect brings it up. When you do, your credibility on everything else goes through the roof.

**Why it works:** Everyone expects marketing to be one-sided. When you break that expectation by being honest about a weakness, the reader's skepticism drops. They think: "If they're willing to admit that, everything else must be true."

**Examples:**
- "Look, our software isn't pretty. The interface looks like it was designed in 2015. But it processes orders 3x faster than anything else on the market."
- "This course takes real work. 2-3 hours a day for 6 weeks. Most people won't finish it. But the ones who do see results within 90 days."
- "We're not the cheapest option. Not even close. But our clients stay an average of 4.2 years because the ROI makes the price irrelevant."

### Rules for Damaging Admissions
- The flaw must be REAL, not fake-humble
- The flaw must be something they'd discover anyway
- The strength that follows must outweigh the admitted weakness
- Place it early in the copy to set the trust anchor
- One admission per piece. More than that feels like you're apologizing

## Kennedy Headline Formulas

Write 50 headlines before picking one. No editing during generation. Narrow to top 3. Test.

### Proven Formulas

1. **"They laughed when I ________, but when I ________"**
   Example: "They laughed when I said I'd build a $500K business with a newsletter. But when I showed them my Stripe dashboard..."

2. **"Give me ________ and I'll ________"**
   Example: "Give me 30 minutes a day and I'll show you how to build a newsletter that pays your mortgage."

3. **"How to ________ [result] in [timeframe] without [objection]"**
   Example: "How to get 1,000 email subscribers in 60 days without spending a dollar on ads."

4. **"Who else wants ________?"**
   Example: "Who else wants to automate 80% of their business with AI?"

5. **"[Number] ways to ________"**
   Example: "7 ways to double your email open rate this month."

6. **"Warning: ________ [danger they don't know about]"**
   Example: "Warning: Your AI content strategy is killing your deliverability."

7. **"The dirty secret about ________ that your ________ doesn't want you to know"**
   Example: "The dirty secret about newsletter growth that ad networks don't want you to know."

8. **Direct benefit:** "Double your clients in 30 days or less"

9. **Provocative question:** "What's the one thing holding you back from $10K months?"

10. **Intriguing story lead:** "He was rejected by 37 clients until he sent this one email."

### The 50-Headline Process

1. Set a timer for 20 minutes
2. Write 50 headlines. No stopping. No editing. No judging.
3. Walk away for an hour
4. Come back and circle the top 10
5. Narrow to top 3
6. Test all 3 (A/B/C split if possible)

The first 20 headlines will be obvious. The magic happens between 30 and 50, when you've exhausted the clichés and start finding real angles.

## The USP Formula

Kennedy's core question: "Why should I, your perfect prospect, choose to do business with you, over every other option available to me, including doing it myself, going with a competitor, or doing nothing at all?"

If you can't answer that in one sentence, you don't have a USP. You have a hope.

### What a USP is NOT
- A slogan ("Just Do It" is not a USP)
- A mission statement
- A tagline
- A list of features

### What a USP IS
A specific, measurable, meaningful promise.

**Domino's (classic):** "Fresh hot pizza delivered in 30 minutes or less, guaranteed."
- Specific: 30 minutes
- Measurable: you can check the clock
- Meaningful: hungry people care about speed

### How to Build Yours

1. List all features of your product/service
2. Convert each feature to a benefit (use the translation process above)
3. Find the ONE benefit that is unique to you (or that nobody else is claiming)
4. Make it specific and measurable
5. Frame it as a promise

**Template:** "We [specific action] so you get [specific result] in [specific timeframe], guaranteed."

### USP Test
Show your USP to someone unfamiliar with your business. Ask: "Does this make you want to learn more?" If they shrug, it's not specific enough. If they say "how?", you've got a winner.
