## 2. The Tangibility Principle

**Core rule:** Your Offer has to be tangible.

**Intangible** = things you can't hold, can't measure, can't feel, and can't "collect." They're not objects -- they're loose phrases with lots of vague, implied meaning. This is how EVERYONE speaks. Do the opposite.

- Bad: "I'll help you up-level your sales skills."
- Bad: "I'll show you how to become the person you were meant to be."
- Bad: "I'll give you what you need to be a successful change-maker in the world."

**Tangible** = objects, measurable deliverables, specific artifacts. Things you can hold, count, collect.

### How to make it tangible:

Make the **WAY** tangible, OR make the **OUTCOME** tangible. Bonus if you make BOTH tangible.

Tangible "way" examples: Roadmap, Blueprint, Checklist, Excel Template, Notion Dashboard, Keynote Presentation, Data Analysis Whitepaper, 30 Pieces of Published Content.

**Ship 30 for 30 example:** "You'll create 30 pieces of published writing in public."
- You can "hold" 30 pieces of writing.
- You can measure 30 pieces of writing.
- You can feel and "collect" your 30 pieces of writing.
- And it's objective -- you either wrote 30 pieces, or you didn't.

**The Tangible x Objective combination is the most important part of the entire game.** If you nail this, your Offer is impossible to ignore. If your Offer is Intangible x Subjective, it's worthless -- the customer doesn't know what you're promising or why it's valuable.

## 3. "Whoever Frames the Problem Owns the Solution"

Two principles that should be tattooed on every copywriter's brain:

**Principle A:** If you successfully educate someone on a problem, they automatically assume you are equipped and qualified to help them solve it. Otherwise, how would you articulate their problem so accurately?

**Principle B:** The customer can't care about your solution until they first care about their problem. You could have the best solution in the world. But the customer will not see the value UNLESS they have first been made aware of:
- A problem they didn't know they had, OR
- A problem they know they have, but haven't gotten around to solving.

### Lead with 1 specific problem. Not 10.

- If you try to lead with 10 problems, the customer doesn't know which of the 10 is the priority. ("If all are priority, none are priority.")
- If you lead with 1 problem, the customer immediately knows where to put you in their mind. THEN, once you're "in their mind," you can educate them on the other problems you also solve.

**Example:** Ship 30 for 30 = "We help you build a daily writing habit." They also help writers solve all sorts of other problems. But they LEAD with one: you aren't writing consistently, and we can help you fix that.

### Application for copy:
- Pick your 1 biggest problem. Lead every headline, email subject, and CTA with that.
- Use the remaining problems in body copy, email sequences, and FAQs -- but never in the lead.

## 4. Objective vs. Subjective Outcome Language

### Subjective outcomes (avoid):
- "I'll help you live the life of your dreams."
- "I'll help you finally be the person you want to be."
- "I'll help you make that transition and be your best self."

If you ask 10 people what that means, you get 10 different answers. That means nobody actually knows what's being promised.

### Objective outcomes (use these):
- "I'll show you how to land your first $5,000 ghostwriting client."
- "I'll show you how to buy your first single-family rental property."
- "I'll show you how to overcome your fear of social anxiety and go on your first 5 dates."

These are binary. They either happened or they didn't.

### The matrix:

| | Objective | Subjective |
|---|----------|-----------|
| **Tangible** | BEST. Use this. | Decent but unclear promise. |
| **Intangible** | OK but hard to visualize. | WORST. Avoid at all costs. |

**For copy: always aim for the Tangible x Objective combination.**

### Low-ticket nuance:

Low-ticket = information (DIY). You don't control implementation. So promise the information, not the guaranteed result:

- Bad: "I guarantee you will attract your first 10,000 followers."
- Good: "You will have everything you need to attract your first 10,000 followers."

## 5. Offer 1-Liner Templates (3 Formulas)

Use these to write the subtitle/elevator pitch for any product. Lead with them on landing pages, in marketing copy, emails, and social CTAs.

### Template 1: Problem + Outcome + Timeframe
**Formula:** {Problem Solved} AND {Achieve Objective Outcome} in {A Specific Time Frame}

Example: "Overcome your fear of writing online and publish your first 10 Tweets in 10 days."

### Template 2: Problem/Outcome + Specific Method
**Formula:** {Problem Solved or Achieving An Objective Outcome} BY DOING {Specific Way}

Example: "Build the ultimate productivity tracker and instantly reclaim 10 hours per week using The Excel Shortcut System."

### Template 3: Outcome + Specific Person
**Formula:** {Achieving An Objective Outcome} FOR {Specific Person}

Example: "Build your first revenue-generating SaaS tool as a No-Code Developer."

### How to use:
Mix and match the 6 checklist variables (Specific Problem, Specific Person, Specific Way, Objective Outcome, Timeframe, Tangible Name) in any order. These three templates are starting points, not constraints.
