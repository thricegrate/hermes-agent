# Lead Magnet Naming Formula & Offer Stacking (Velocity Framework)

**Source:** Daniel Bustamante (Velocity) -- 30+ lead magnets, millions of impressions. Core insight: most lead magnets fail because of bad COPY, not bad content.

## The Naming Formula

**[Desired Outcome] + [Topic] + [Container Word]**

### Examples
- Million-Dollar Sales Page AI Prompt
- The Infinite Newsletter Idea Generator
- The 6-Figure Newsletter Starter Pack
- Viral Claude Lead Magnet Swipe File

### Container Words That Elevate Perceived Value
Kit, Prompt, Blueprint, Template, Playbook, Roadmap, Swipe File, Launchpad, Starter Pack, Generator

### Rules
- Lead with outcome (what they GET), not format
- Use specific numbers or aspirational modifiers ("Million-Dollar", "6-Figure", "Infinite")
- Container word signals the asset TYPE
- NEVER use generic format words alone ("PDF", "Guide", "Checklist", "Ebook")

### Why This Works
Generic names ("Free Email Template") promise nothing. The formula front-loads the desired outcome so the reader immediately sees the VALUE, then the container word tells them what they're getting. It's the difference between "Free PDF" and "The 6-Figure Newsletter Starter Pack."

## Offer Stacking

Never present a lead magnet (or any offer) as one monolithic thing. Break it into components and stack them.

### Before (Weak)
"Get my free newsletter guide"

### After (Stacked)
"Get:
- 2 proven templates
- 2 AI prompts (customizable)
- 1 eight-step implementation guide
- 1 real-world swipe file
Worth $297 -- yours free"

### Why Stacking Works
- Each bullet = separate piece of perceived value
- More items = more value (quantity heuristic)
- Price anchoring makes "free" feel like a steal
- Specific items > vague promises ("2 templates" > "templates")

## Headline Copy for Landing Pages

Seven rules from Velocity's high-converting pages:
1. **Outcome-focused** -- what they GET, not what it IS
2. **Single CTA** -- no competing links or actions
3. **Above the fold** -- opt-in visible without scrolling
4. **Naming formula in headline** -- the lead magnet name IS the headline
5. **Social proof** -- numbers, testimonials, or logos near CTA
6. **Objection handling** -- address "why free?" and "what's the catch?" upfront
7. **Professional mockup** -- book-style or software box visual of the lead magnet

## The "Reason Why" Principle

When offering something free, always explain WHY. This dramatically increases compliance (Cialdini).

**Weak:** "Download my free guide"
**Strong:** "One of my favorite parts of my job? Brainstorming lead magnets for a living. So for a limited time, I'm creating free Lead Magnet Funnel Blueprints for new subscribers."

The reason doesn't even need to be profound -- just having one increases conversion.

## The Application Model (Exclusivity Copy)

Instead of giving away lead magnets openly, gate them behind an "application":
- "The only catch: You need to apply, and not everyone will qualify."
- "(Full transparency: This is best suited for established businesses with a proven offer.)"
- "Even if you're not selected, you'll get a valuable free resource as a thank you."

This creates scarcity + exclusivity without being sleazy, qualifies leads, and the "even if not selected" line removes the last objection.

## Launch Email Copy Patterns (Bustamante 6-Figure Launch Playbook)

Source: Bustamante's $195.9K launch for Dickie Bush & Nicolas Cole. For full swipe file, see `newsletter-automator/references/bustamante-launch-playbook.md`.

### Pre-Launch: "Sell the POV, Not the Product"

Pre-launch emails educate on the methodology/philosophy behind the offer rather than dripping tactical value. By cart open, the audience already believes in the "method" so buying feels like the logical next step.

**Copy angle:** "Here's why [approach] is the most powerful way to [outcome]" instead of "Here are 5 tips for [topic]."

### Subject Line Patterns by Launch Phase

**Pre-launch:** Question-based ("Is X right for you?"), Problem-based ("What's keeping you from X?"), Future-pacing ("Where you could be..."), Story-based ("How we built a $X/year..."), Framework-based ("5 pillars of...")

**Launch:** Announcement ("[LAUNCH DAY!]"), Bump ("did you see this?"), Story ("Meet [Name]..."), Value ("You also get all of this..."), Proof ("Don't take our word for it..."), FAQ ("Your questions, answered"), Urgency ("Doors close tonight", "[FINAL HOURS]")

### Objection-Handling Copy Structure

State the objection in the reader's voice -> Reframe -> Proof -> CTA.

"I get it. $800 feels like a lot. But here's how I think about it..."

Three universal objections to always address: Price (ROI reframe), "Will it work for me?" (archetype proof), Comparison to alternatives (differentiation).

### Offer Stack in Launch Emails

Session-by-session value breakdown with individual dollar values. Each session described with fascination bullets (what you'll discover + named frameworks). Bonuses listed separately with dollar values. Total value vs. price comparison. VIP tier presented alongside core offer.
