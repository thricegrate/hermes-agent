## Quick Decision Tree

```
START HERE: What is the primary goal of this piece?
│
├── STOP THE SCROLL / INTERRUPT → SLAP or DIC
├── LAUNCH A NEW PRODUCT → ACC or PASTOR
├── BUILD A TRANSFORMATION STORY → BAB
├── AGITATE A KNOWN PAIN → PAS
├── WARM AUDIENCE, PROVE VALUE → 4Ps (PPPP)
├── SELL FEATURES TO RATIONAL BUYER → FAB
├── QUALITY-CHECK COPY → 4Cs

├── OPT-IN PAGE → 3S

├── FULL SALES PAGE → PASTOR
├── EMAIL → BAB or AIDA+B
├── BLOG POST → PAS
└── ADS (COLD TRAFFIC) → AIDA
```

## Stage 2: Audience Awareness Level (Eugene Schwartz)

Before selecting a framework, diagnose where your audience sits on the awareness spectrum. Wrong framework for the awareness level = copy that gets ignored.

| Awareness Stage | Mindset | Best Framework |
|---|---|---|
| **Unaware** | Does not know they have a problem | DIC, statistic hook + AIDA |
| **Problem Aware** | Knows the pain, not the solution | PAS, BAB |
| **Solution Aware** | Knows solutions exist, not yours | PASTOR, 4Ps, PPPP |
| **Product Aware** | Knows your product, unconvinced | FAB, 4Cs, ACCA |
| **Most Aware** | Ready to buy, needs a reason now | SLAP, ACC, simple offer + CTA |

The OATH framework (Oblivious, Apathetic, Thinking, Hurting) maps directly to this:
- **Oblivious** = Unaware → Educate first, then pitch
- **Apathetic** = Problem-aware but unbothered → Agitate the cost of inaction
- **Thinking** = Evaluating → Compare and differentiate
- **Hurting** = Urgently seeking relief → Lead with the solution

## Framework Profiles

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action

**3-line definition:** The foundational awareness-to-action sequence. Moves a cold stranger to a buyer in four steps. Applicable to every format and channel.

**Best use case:** Ads, cold traffic, emails, landing pages. Any copy that starts with a stranger and ends with a purchase intent. Ideal for awareness stage: Unaware to Problem Aware.

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**
1. **Attention:** Hook the reader with a bold claim, statistic, or provocative question. The only job is to stop the scroll.
2. **Interest:** Build relevance. Share one intriguing fact or why this specific person should keep reading.
3. **Desire:** Show the outcome. Make the reader want what you are offering. Translate features into benefits.
4. **Action:** Tell them exactly what to do next. One step, no ambiguity.

**Real example:**
- Attention: "Most email lists generate $0.12 per subscriber per month. Ours generates $4.71."
- Interest: "The difference comes down to one sequence most senders never build."
- Desire: "Implement it once and it runs indefinitely, compounding every month."
- Action: "Get the sequence now."

**Where it fails:** Missing any step breaks the chain. The transition from Desire to Action is most commonly dropped.

---

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solution

**3-line definition:** Opens with the reader's pain, amplifies the emotional cost of leaving it unsolved, then presents the product as the exit. Pain-first persuasion. The empathy formula.

**Best use case:** Short-form ads, email subject lines, social copy, blog posts. Pain-heavy markets where the problem is well-known and deeply felt. Awareness level: Problem Aware.

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**
1. **Problem:** State the reader's specific problem directly. Not the category problem — their individual frustration.
2. **Agitate:** Amplify the pain. Show what it costs in time, money, stress, or missed opportunity. Make inaction feel more painful than action.
3. **Solution:** Introduce your product as the resolution. Not a lengthy pitch — a clean exit from the agitation.

**Real example:**
- Problem: "Your landing page is converting at 1.2%."
- Agitate: "That means 98 out of every 100 visitors you paid to acquire are leaving without buying. At $2 per click, you are burning $196 for every $4 you make."
- Solution: "This conversion audit identifies every leak in under 48 hours."

**Where it fails:** Over-agitating without resolving. Readers feel worse, not motivated. Agitate until they feel it, then stop and solve.

---

### BAB — Before, After, Bridge

**3-line definition:** Paints the reader's current painful situation, shows the vivid positive state after the solution, then positions your product as the bridge between the two. Transformation story structure.

**Best use case:** Email copy, social proof posts, case studies, transformation-heavy products. Best for warm audiences who already trust you enough to picture the "after." Awareness level: Problem Aware to Solution Aware.

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**
1. **Before:** Name the reader's current situation. Be specific. Make them feel seen. Mirror the language they use themselves.
2. **After:** Paint the vivid desired state. Concrete and sensory, not abstract. Make it feel achievable.
3. **Bridge:** Show how your product gets them from Before to After. Not a full feature list — the essential mechanism.

**Real example:**
- Before: "You are sending cold emails to silence. 2% open rates. Zero replies."
- After: "Imagine a 45% open rate and a full calendar of qualified calls."
- Bridge: "Here is the exact sequence that produced that result."

**Where it fails:** Weak "After" states that feel vague or out of reach. The contrast between Before and After creates desire — make it dramatic.

---

### PASTOR — Problem, Amplify, Story, Transformation, Offer, Response

**3-line definition:** The most complete sales framework with six stages. Built for long-form persuasion where trust must be earned before the pitch lands. Story and transformation stages differentiate it from simpler frameworks.

**Best use case:** Long-form sales pages, VSL scripts, webinar presentations, high-ticket offers. Audiences who are solution-aware but unconvinced. Awareness level: Solution Aware to Product Aware.

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**
1. **Problem:** Name the pain point. State it precisely in the reader's language.
2. **Amplify:** Deepen the pain. Make the status quo feel unacceptable. What does life look like if this stays unsolved?
3. **Story:** Share a real transformation story — yours or a customer's. Builds credibility through shared experience.
4. **Transformation:** Walk through how the change actually happens. Make the mechanism tangible.
5. **Offer:** Describe exactly what the buyer gets. Price, bonuses, deliverables, timeline.
6. **Response:** Issue a clear, specific call to action. Tell them what to do now.

**Real example (compressed):**
- Problem: "Most coaches struggle to fill their calendar past the first three clients."
- Amplify: "Every month it does not change costs you $3,000-5,000 in unrealized revenue."
- Story: "I was running webinars to a ghost audience until I changed one thing."
- Transformation: "The change was in the follow-up sequence, not the webinar itself."
- Offer: "This 6-module course gives you the full system, including all templates."
- Response: "Click the button below to enroll before the price increases Friday."

**Where it fails:** Skipping the Story or Transformation stages. These are what separate PASTOR from a generic sales pitch.

---

### SLAP — Stop, Look, Act, Purchase

**3-line definition:** Scroll-interruption framework built for urgency and impulse decisions. Four sequential commands that compress the buying journey. Highest urgency format in the set.

**Best use case:** Social media ads, limited-time offers, flash sales, e-commerce product ads. Most Aware audiences who need a reason to act now, not a reason to consider. Awareness level: Most Aware.

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**
1. **Stop:** Interrupt the scroll with something unexpected — a visual, a provocative claim, or a direct command.
2. **Look:** Hold attention. Give them one compelling reason to keep reading.
3. **Act:** Create urgency. Limited stock, expiring offer, or a time-sensitive benefit.
4. **Purchase:** Make the transaction frictionless. One clear step to buy.

**Real example:** "Wait — check out our summer collection. Limited stock available. Grab yours today before it is gone."

**Where it fails:** Using SLAP for complex, high-consideration products. This framework compresses the journey — works best for products that do not require significant deliberation.

---

### FAB — Features, Advantages, Benefits

**3-line definition:** Translates product specifications into real-world buyer outcomes. Three-level chain: what it has, why that matters, what the buyer gains. Never stop at features.

**Best use case:** Product pages, demo scripts, pitch decks, any copy for feature-rich products where the connection between specs and outcomes is not obvious. Awareness level: Product Aware.

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**
1. **Feature:** State what the product has or does. The technical fact.
2. **Advantage:** Explain why that feature is better than the alternative. Context and comparison.
3. **Benefit:** Describe what the buyer gains in their real life. The outcome that matters to them.

**Real example:**
- Feature: "256-bit AES encryption"
- Advantage: "The same standard used by banks and governments — unbreakable with current technology"
- Benefit: "Your files stay private. No breach. No liability."

**Where it fails:** Stopping at the Feature or Advantage level. FAB requires all three levels — the benefit is where the sale lives.

---

### 4Ps — Picture, Promise, Prove, Push

**3-line definition:** Closing framework for warm audiences. Creates vivid desire with a picture, makes a specific promise, backs it with proof, then drives action. Best used after trust is established.

**Best use case:** Long-form sales pages (bottom third), email sequences (closing emails), warm remarketing ads. Audiences who know your product and need conviction, not introduction. Awareness level: Product Aware to Most Aware.

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**
1. **Picture:** Paint the desired outcome in vivid, sensory detail. Let the reader see themselves after.
2. **Promise:** State clearly and specifically what your product delivers. One clean promise.
3. **Prove:** Back the promise with evidence — testimonials, case studies, data, social proof.
4. **Push:** Drive action now. Urgency, deadline, or framing of what they lose by waiting.

**Real example:**
- Picture: "Imagine waking up on Monday to 14 new leads in your inbox without spending a dollar on ads."
- Promise: "This system generates inbound leads on autopilot once it is set up."
- Proof: "Clients average 11 qualified leads per week in the first 60 days."
- Push: "Set it up this weekend — the next cohort starts Monday."

**Where it fails:** Using 4Ps on cold traffic before trust is established. This framework requires the reader to believe your Promise — which requires prior relationship or context.

---

### 4Cs — Clear, Concise, Compelling, Credible

**3-line definition:** A quality filter, not a sequence. Apply all four simultaneously to any piece of copy to catch weak spots before publishing. Eliminates doubt in skeptical buyers.

**Best use case:** Use as a final review pass on any copy, not as a primary construction framework. Particularly effective for skeptical audiences who evaluate copy critically.

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**
1. **Clear:** Can a stranger understand this in 3 seconds? No jargon. No abstraction.
2. **Concise:** Is every word earning its place? Remove redundancy. Shorter converts harder.
3. **Compelling:** Is there a reason to keep reading? Hook, tension, or clear value proposition.
4. **Credible:** Is every claim backed by evidence? Numbers, testimonials, or verifiable specifics.

**Real example:** "Fast relief. No side effects. Trusted by doctors. Get your sample today."

**Where it fails:** Treating 4Cs as a sequential formula rather than a simultaneous checklist. All four criteria must be met at once.

---

### ACC — Awareness, Comprehension, Conversion

**3-line definition:** Three-stage funnel for introducing new products or new audiences to an existing product. Build awareness first, earn comprehension, then push for conversion. Never push for purchase before earning understanding.

**Best use case:** New product launches, cold traffic campaigns, brand awareness ads. Audiences who do not yet know your product exists. Awareness level: Unaware to Problem Aware.

**Step-by-step walkthrough:**
1. **Awareness:** Surface what you offer. Get on the reader's radar without pitching.
2. **Comprehension:** Explain how it works and why it matters. Close the information gap.
3. **Conversion:** Now ask for the action. Awareness and comprehension have earned the right.

**Real example:** "Meet our new skincare line. Vegan and paraben-free. Shop the collection now."

**Where it fails:** Skipping to Conversion before Comprehension is built. ACC requires patience — cold audiences need to understand before they buy.

## Framework by Channel (Master Reference)

| Channel | Recommended Framework | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Ads (paid) | AIDA | Awareness-to-action arc, works for cold traffic |
| Sales pages | PASTOR | Six stages handle full persuasion arc including trust |
| Emails | BAB | Transformation story fits email length and intimacy |
| Blog posts | PAS | Raises problem, stirs emotion, delivers solution |
| Social media posts | ACE | Adds engagement step other frameworks skip |
| Opt-in pages | 3S | Specific offer, short message, strong CTA |
| Video sales letters | STAR | Situation-task-action-result narrative |
| Product pages | FAB | Translates features into buyer benefits |
| Limited-time offers | SLAP | Urgency sequence for impulse decisions |
| New product launches | ACC | Awareness before comprehension before conversion |

## Framework Combination Techniques

Single frameworks are a starting point. Most high-converting copy blends two or more.

### PAS + FAB
Use PAS to open (establish problem and agitate), then deploy FAB to present the solution with full feature-to-benefit translation.

Best for: Product pages where the audience knows they have a problem but needs to understand how the product solves it.

### AIDA + BAB
Use AIDA structure for the overall arc. Within the Desire stage, use BAB to paint the before/after transformation.

Best for: Email sequences where the first email runs AIDA and the second runs deeper on the BAB contrast.

### PASTOR + 4Ps
Run PASTOR for the full sales page structure. In the closing section, layer in 4Ps to drive the final conversion.

Best for: High-ticket sales pages where the reader needs the full six-stage journey before the closing picture-promise-proof-push sequence.

### SLAP + 4Cs
Use SLAP for structure, run 4Cs as the editorial filter before publishing.

Best for: Time-sensitive ad creative where urgency is the primary lever.

### BAB + FORCEPS
Use BAB for narrative structure, then stack FORCEPS proof types (Factual, Optical, Reversal, Credential, Evidential, Perceptual, Social) in the Bridge section to make the transformation believable.

Best for: Long-form transformation copy where skepticism is high.

## Decision Rules Summary

1. **Cold audience + product unknown** → AIDA or ACC
2. **Pain is the primary lever** → PAS or BAB
3. **Long-form, high-ticket** → PASTOR
4. **Impulse purchase, urgency needed** → SLAP
5. **Feature-heavy product** → FAB
6. **Warm audience, need conviction** → 4Ps
7. **Skeptical audience** → 4Cs (filter) + FORCEPS (proof stack)
8. **New product** → ACC
9. **Email** → BAB
10. **Social post** → ACE
11. **Check copy quality** → 4Cs audit
12. **Building a Big Idea** → Test against WOW, Emotional, Simple, Relatable, Newsworthy, Believable, Tangible

## The Slippery Slope Principle

Every element of copy has one job: sell the next element.

Headline → Subheadline → Lead → Body Copy → CTA → The Click

Break the chain at any link and you lose the reader. Choose frameworks that support this chain — not frameworks that skip steps the reader needs to take.

The headline is the highest-leverage element. Nothing else matters until it works. Spend disproportionate effort here before applying any framework to the body.
