## Why Templates Work

Originality is an illusion. All writers are influenced by others. Templates are not about copying -- they are about expanding your creative toolkit. Remixing ideas is a natural part of the creative process: take techniques and frames that resonate, combine them with your perspective for your niche, create something distinctively your own.

**The key distinction:** Playing "Mad-Libs" works. But understanding WHY a template works is how you really turn up the heat. Blindly following a strict formula results in generic, forced, insincere content. Templates should be tools for guidance and inspiration, not crutches.

## 6 Proven Ways to Write Engaging First Sentences

1. **Open with 1 strong, declarative sentence.** -- Bold claim, no hedging. Sets authority immediately.
2. **Open with a thought-provoking question.** -- Triggers curiosity. Reader's brain can't help but try to answer.
3. **Open with a controversial opinion.** -- Creates tension. Agreement or disagreement both keep them reading.
4. **Open with a moment in time.** -- Drops the reader into a scene. Sensory and immediate.
5. **Open with a vulnerable statement.** -- Builds trust through honesty. Readers lean in when you expose something real.
6. **Open with a weird, unique insight.** -- Pattern interrupt. The unexpected stops the scroll.

**The principle behind all 6:** The clearer you are about the reader's thinking, the more likely you are to speak directly to them. When people feel like you are speaking directly to them, you hook their attention. When you hook attention using a proven format that you understand, you go viral.

## 3-Question Test for Hooks Worth Reverse-Engineering

Every hook you choose to templatize must pass all 3:

1. What makes YOU stop scrolling?
2. What gets YOU to click to read more?
3. What are other readers already clicking on?

If a hook can't prove it stops the scroll and generates interest -- ignore it.

## Hook Library Building Process (4 Steps)

### Step 1: Follow 50 interesting accounts

Your Twitter or LinkedIn feed becomes 100X more valuable when you follow interesting people.

- **Dig into your niche:** Use search for top tweets, posts, and people in your niche.
- **Browse your "Who To Follow" list:** Follow accounts that are followed by people you follow.
- **Search for universal topics:** Dive into health, wealth, and relationships and filter by likes.

Follow until you hit 50 people, then stop. Scroll your feeds until you find something that captures your attention. Grab the link and drop it into a Notion doc or bookmark it. Make it part of your daily/weekly routine to scan this list for scroll-stopping posts.

**Bonus (Twitter):** Create a Twitter list with all the people you followed. When you are ready to go "template hunting" you can skip the ads and noise to go straight to the people you want to study.

### Step 2: Find the highest-performing content for big-name creators in (and out of) your niche

Big accounts on LinkedIn or Twitter are ripe for studying -- there's a reason they are big.

- **Browse the Twitter Leaderboard** (readwise.io/twitter_leaderboard): Go down the list and find accounts that resonate with you.
- **Use Twemex** (tweethunter.io/twemex) to find high-performing content for each account: Twemex automatically surfaces the most interesting ideas for any user account -- which means it's proven.
- **Add proven tweets and posts to your bookmarks:** Grab the link to each proven post and drop it into Notion or bookmark it.

Now you have a collection of posts and tweets waiting to be transformed into templates.

### Step 3: Train ChatGPT to "templatize" scroll-stopping hooks

Use the Viral Hook Reverse-Engineering Prompt (see below) to:

1. Analyze a proven hook from your library.
2. Explain the psychological principles working in the hook, so you understand why it works.
3. Create a reusable template you can use for your own topic.
4. Get a practical suggestion on how to apply the hook.

**Important:** ChatGPT is doing 80% of the work. As the orchestrator, you still need to judge the output. Read the explanation. Review the template. Think about the hook critically on your own -- what made you save it in the first place? Then consider the application. With repeated practice, you'll start recognizing patterns in hooks, enhancing your proficiency in writing them.

### Step 4: Brainstorm 3 new hooks using your niche topic

Use the follow-up prompt (see below) to apply the template to a new topic. Use the suggested application from Step 3 to inform your topic. For example, if the application suggestion was "share a personal story about overcoming challenges with a product," give ChatGPT a product and insight on a product challenge you've had.

## "Goal-Oriented Variables" vs "Generic Variables"

This is the critical concept that separates good templates from useless ones.

**Goal-oriented variables** describe the intended outcome of an action. They are helpful because they provide a clear indication of what information should be entered. Example: if a user sees `{Desired Outcome}`, they know to enter the desired outcome of a particular task or project.

**Generic variables** like `{Noun}` and `{Verb}` are too general. If a user sees `{Noun}`, they won't know what specific type of noun should be entered -- a person's name, a product name, or a specific object.

**Rule:** ALWAYS use goal-oriented variables. NEVER use generic variables.

## ChatGPT Viral Hook Reverse-Engineering Prompt (EXACT TEXT)

Copy/paste this into a new ChatGPT window. Then provide the text of a viral post from your hook library.

```
I am going to train you to analyze viral writing and turn your analysis into a template.

We are going to do 3 things.

1. Analyze the text
2. Create the template
3. Suggest a practical application

1. Analyze the text: In 35 words or less, explain the primary hook, persuasion technique, or motivation used to influence readers—explain why it works (what's in it for the reader?)

2. Create the template: I want you to create a template for the text I provide (mad-lib style) so it's easy to plug my own topic into it.
- A template is a text format that serves as a starting point and guide for a new text.
- A template includes placeholders for content separated by "{}" so that a writer can turn it into their own text by filling in the variable for each placeholder.
- A template, when filled in, should feel like a brand new text.
- You MUST use "goal-oriented variables" because they describe the intended outcome of an action and are helpful to someone trying to input the variables because they provide a clear indication of what information should be entered in that field. For example, if a user sees the variable "{Desired Outcome}", they know that they should enter the desired outcome of a particular task or project in that field.
- Avoid "generic variables" like "{Noun}" and "{Verb}" because they are too general. If a user sees the variable "{Noun}", they won't know what specific type of noun should be entered in that field, such as a person's name, a product name, or a specific object.
- You MUST preserve the formatting and structure of the original text.

3. Practical Application: In 1 sentence, share 1 idea to help me apply the template in my business / how should I think about using it. Write in the 2nd person.

Here's an EXPLANATION, TEXT, TEMPLATE, and APPLICATION so you understand what we are doing:
--
EXAMPLE TEXT
--
The KING of YouTube:

Mr. Beast.

Over the last 12 months, he's gained over 50,000,000 subscribers.

So I binge watched 100 hours of his videos to study how he's done it so quickly.

And here's his 3-part framework for rapid growth (that you can use on any platform):

--
EXAMPLE EXPLANATION
--
The primary hook in this text is the promise of learning the secrets behind Mr. Beast's rapid growth on YouTube. The persuasion technique used is the appeal to curiosity and the desire for success, as the text suggests that by following his framework, readers can achieve rapid growth on any platform. It works because it taps into the reader's aspiration to achieve similar success and offers a solution through the framework.

--
EXAMPLE TEMPLATE:
--
The {Credible Statement} of {Topic}

{Person}

Over the last {Time Period}, {Massive Accomplishment}.

So {What you did} to study {How Accomplishment Was Acheived}.

And here's {Number} part framework for {Desired Outcome}:

--
EXAMPLE APPLICATION:
--
Analyze a successful digital business related in your niche and breakdown their strategy into simple steps.
--

Output: ONLY give me the Explanation, Template, and Application.

Formating: Create sub-headings for the Explanation, the Template, and Application using markdown to make it easy to read the output.

Remember: Use "Goal Oriented Variables" for the Template and preserve the formatting and structure of the original text.

Are you ready for the text I want you to create a template for?
```

## Follow-Up Prompt: Apply Template to Your Niche (EXACT TEXT)

After ChatGPT generates the template, use this prompt to generate new hooks from it:

```
Great! Now, for the template you just created, I need your help filling in all the variables using a new topic.

1. I will provide a new topic.
2. You will replace each variable in the template based on your knowledge of the new topic and return the completed template, preserving the format and structure of the template.

Do you understand?
```

Then provide your topic and let ChatGPT fill in the variables.

## Workflow Summary

1. **Collect** -- Follow 50 accounts, use Twemex, bookmark scroll-stoppers
2. **Templatize** -- Run the reverse-engineering prompt on each bookmarked hook
3. **Understand** -- Read the explanation, think critically about why it stopped YOUR scroll
4. **Apply** -- Use the follow-up prompt to generate 3+ new hooks for your niche
5. **Refine** -- Edit ChatGPT's output with your own judgment and voice
6. **Repeat** -- Make this a weekly habit to build an ever-growing hook library
