# YC Framework: How to Evaluate Startup Ideas (Kevin Hale, Pt 1 & 2)

Source: Y Combinator Startup School 2020 -- Kevin Hale

## The Problem Validation Matrix

Not all problems are worth solving. Plot every idea on two axes:

```
         HIGH SEVERITY
              |
   VITAMINS   |   PAINKILLERS
   (frequent  |   (frequent + severe)
   but mild)  |   = BUILD THIS
              |
--------------+---------------
              |
   IGNORE     |   NICE TO HAVE
   (rare +    |   (severe but rare)
   mild)      |
              |
         LOW SEVERITY
    LOW FREQUENCY -----> HIGH FREQUENCY
```

**Painkillers (high severity + high frequency) = the only quadrant worth building in.**

- Vitamins get abandoned (people forget to take them)
- Rare problems don't generate enough demand
- Mild problems don't motivate switching behavior

**The test:** Ask potential users: "How often does this problem occur?" AND "On a scale of 1-10, how painful is it?" If frequency < weekly OR pain < 7/10, think twice.

## The Painkiller Test

Three questions to determine if your idea is a painkiller or a vitamin:

1. **Would they notice if your solution disappeared tomorrow?**
   - Painkiller: "I'd be screwed" / "I'd have to find something else immediately"
   - Vitamin: "I'd be fine" / "I'd miss it but manage"

2. **Are they actively seeking alternatives right now?**
   - Painkiller: They've tried 2-3 other things and are still looking
   - Vitamin: They haven't really looked (the problem isn't urgent enough)

3. **Have they already spent money trying to solve this?**
   - Painkiller: Yes, they've paid for tools, courses, consultants
   - Vitamin: No, they've lived with it

**Application to CC audience:** The coach app was a vitamin ("coaching sounds nice"). "Learn AI basics" might be a painkiller IF the audience fears being left behind (severity) and encounters AI confusion weekly (frequency). Validate this with `user-research`.

## Founder-Problem Fit

The best founders have unfair advantages in solving their specific problem:

1. **Personal experience:** You've had this problem yourself
2. **Domain expertise:** You've worked in this industry
3. **Unique insight:** You see something others don't
4. **Network access:** You know the customers personally
5. **Technical advantage:** You can build something others can't

**For CC/the product:** Founder-problem fit is strong. Built a $500K+/yr newsletter portfolio with AI automation. That IS the proof of concept. The advantage = "I built this system and it works. I'll show you how."

## Competitive Moat Framework

Competition validates the market (people care about this problem). But you need a reason to win:

| Moat Type | What It Means | CC/the product Application |
|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Network effects | Product gets better as more people use it | Community of the product users sharing skills |
| Switching costs | Hard to leave once you're in | Custom skills + memory = deeply personalized |
| Brand / trust | People choose you because they know you | 215K subscribers, build-in-public credibility |
| Unique data | You have data nobody else has | Newsletter performance data, audience insights |
| Speed | You ship faster than anyone | AI-assisted development, 3-day iteration cycle |

## Market Timing

Being first doesn't matter. Being right about timing does.

**Too early signals:**
- You have to educate the market about the problem (not just your solution)
- Infrastructure needed to use your product doesn't exist yet
- Potential customers say "interesting, but not now"

**Too late signals:**
- Market dominated by well-funded incumbents
- Customers are satisfied with existing solutions
- Switching costs are high for established products

**Right timing signals:**
- Market is growing but no dominant player yet
- Recent change (technology, regulation, culture) created new demand
- Customers are cobbling together workarounds (pain is real, solution is incomplete)

## Start Narrow, Expand Systematically

Don't build for "everyone." Build for the smallest viable audience with the most acute pain.

**Narrowing framework:**
1. Who has this problem the WORST? (not who has it at all)
2. Who would pay the MOST to solve it? (not who might pay something)
3. Who can you REACH most easily? (not who exists in theory)

The intersection of these three = your beachhead.

**Then expand:** Once you dominate the narrow segment, adjacent segments notice and come to you.

**Application:** CC's audience is 215K people, but not all of them want the same thing. Narrow to the segment that is:
- Most frustrated (overwhelmed by AI, don't know where to start)
- Most willing to pay (25% with buy signal from survey)
- Most reachable (active email subscribers, high open rate)

That's your beachhead. Build for them first.

## Idea Evaluation Checklist (Quick Score)

Rate each 1-5, then multiply:

1. **Problem severity:** How painful? (1 = mild annoyance, 5 = hair on fire)
2. **Problem frequency:** How often? (1 = yearly, 5 = daily)
3. **Founder-problem fit:** How well positioned are you? (1 = outsider, 5 = deep expert)
4. **Market size:** How many people have this? (1 = niche, 5 = massive)
5. **Timing:** Is the market ready? (1 = too early/late, 5 = perfect window)

**Score = Severity x Frequency x Fit x Size x Timing**

- 500+ = Strong signal. Move to validation.
- 200-500 = Promising. Needs more research.
- <200 = Weak. Consider pivoting.

This is a gut-check, not a formula. But it forces you to be honest about each dimension rather than falling in love with one strong signal while ignoring weak ones.
