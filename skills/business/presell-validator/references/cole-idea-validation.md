# Cole's Product Idea Validation Framework (Low-Ticket Launchpad)

> Source: Nicolas Cole, Low-Ticket Launchpad course
> Module: 2 - Low-Ticket Product Idea Generation
> Context: Pre-validation framework for finding the RIGHT product idea before testing demand. Use BEFORE the 48-Hour Demand Test. Covers niching down, problem identification, outcome mapping, and stress-testing.

---

## Step 1: Niching Down (Mega-Category -> Niche -> Niche-Within-Niche)

The first step is to get specific. Trying to pinpoint "a problem" inside a mega-category (Healthcare, Technology, Productivity, etc.) is nearly impossible because inside each mega-category are different kinds of people, each faced with different problems, looking to achieve different goals.

**The hierarchy:**

- Mega-Category ->
- Niche ->
- Niche-Within-Niche

### 6 Methods to Niche Down

1. **By Industry:** Take the mega-category and pick a sub-category within it. Example: "Healthcare" -> "Physical Therapy."
2. **By Demographic:** Take the mega-category, pick a sub-category, and then pick a specific demographic. Example: "Healthcare" -> "Physical Therapy" -> "Physical Therapy For Remote Workers"
3. **By Physical Location:** Take the mega-category, pick a sub-category, and then pick a specific physical location or geography. Example: "Healthcare" -> "Physical Therapy" -> "Physical Therapists In Chicago."
4. **By Digital Platform:** Take the mega-category, pick a sub-category, and then pick a specific digital location or platform. Example: "Healthcare" -> "Physical Therapy" -> "Physical Therapists On LinkedIn."
5. **By Price:** Take the mega-category, pick a sub-category, and then pick a price range or socioeconomic status. Example: "Healthcare" -> "Physical Therapy" -> "Physical Therapy For Single Mothers On Food Stamps."
6. **By Distribution:** Take the mega-category, pick a sub-category, and then pick a specific form of delivery. Example: "Healthcare" -> "Physical Therapy" -> "Physical Therapy On Zoom."

**Key principle:** "The riches are in the niches." If you stay broad, all your ideas are going to be vague. Getting hyper-specific about who you help, with what, and how is where the money is.

**Pro Tip:** Your niche-within-niche should be something you have personally experienced. This is the easiest place to start because chances are YOU have experienced these very same problems (and ideally found solutions to them for yourself).

### Ship 30 for 30 Example

- **Mega-Category:** Writing
- **Niche:** Internet Writing (which they later coined as "Digital Writing")
- **Niche-Within-Niche:** Internet Writing For Beginners

The mistake would have been to say "we help ALL writers!" They got specific: "we help beginner writers start writing online."

## Step 2: 10 Biggest Problems Identification

DO NOT begin this step until you have Mega-Category -> Niche -> Niche-Within-Niche defined.

List out the 10 biggest problems in your chosen niche-within-niche. These must be:

- **Painful** -- real problems people struggle with
- **Urgent** -- problems people want solved now
- **Valuable ("expensive")** -- problems correlated to one of the 4 value drivers:
  1. **Making Money**
  2. **Saving Money (or not losing money)**
  3. **Saving Time**
  4. **Unlocking Status**

### Ship 30 for 30 Example (Internet Writing For Beginners)

- Problem #1: Distractions
- Problem #2: Over-Editing
- Problem #3: Perfectionism
- Problem #4: Procrastination
- Problem #5: Self-Confidence
- Problem #6: Generating Ideas
- Problem #7: Imposter Syndrome
- Problem #8: Writing Consistently
- Problem #9: Choosing A Platform
- Problem #10: Finding Time To Write

The game is creating a list of the Top 10 Biggest Problems for this specific niche-within-niche. Not every possible problem -- the top 10.

---

## Step 3: 10 Most Desirable Outcomes

Once you have the Top 10 Biggest Problems, skip to the end and map each to a desirable outcome.

**Three pro tips:**

1. The 10 Most Desirable Outcomes should be outcomes you, yourself have wanted, strived for, and/or accomplished.
2. Just take your list of 10 Biggest Problems and write "the desirable outcome" next to each one.
3. Write these Desirable Outcomes as "I" statements. Put yourself in the shoes of the customer.

### Ship 30 for 30 Example (Full Problem-to-Outcome Map)

- **Problem #1: Distractions** -> "If I wasn't distracted, I would be so much more productive."
- **Problem #2: Over-Editing** -> "If I wasn't constantly over-editing, I would save myself hundreds of hours and publish way more often."
- **Problem #3: Perfectionism** -> "If I wasn't such a perfectionist, I would get out of my own way, publish more, and maybe even live my dream of being a full-time writer."
- **Problem #4: Procrastination** -> "If I stopped procrastinating, I would finally start making progress toward my writing goals."
- **Problem #5: Self-Confidence** -> "If I had self-confidence, I would finally share the stories and insights and frameworks I think are valuable but have been afraid to write and publish."
- **Problem #6: Generating Ideas** -> "If I had a reliable system for generating ideas, I wouldn't feel so reliant on my intuition and so easily talk myself out of writing anytime I was struggling to come up with things to say."
- **Problem #7: Imposter Syndrome** -> "If I didn't have Imposter Syndrome, I would stop comparing myself to other people, publish more often, and just generally feel happier and more fulfilled with my path in life."
- **Problem #8: Writing Consistently** -> "If I was writing consistently, my audience would grow faster, my library would grow faster, and I would achieve my goals as a writer way faster."
- **Problem #9: Choosing A Platform** -> "If I knew which platform to write on, I could finally start my journey of writing online."
- **Problem #10: Finding Time To Write** -> "If I had more time to write, I would be able to write consistently, grow my audience and library faster, and feel like I was on the path to achieving my goals."

## Step 4: Pick the #1 Most Valuable Problem

Lead with ONE problem for positioning. Even if your product helps solve more than one, you HAVE to pick 1 single problem to position your digital product around. Then speak to the other problems on the landing page, in marketing materials, in emails and social content.

If you try to lead with all 10 problems, the customer doesn't know which one is the priority. If they're "all" the priority, then "none of them" are the priority.

---

## Product Idea Stress-Test Checklist (5 Points)

Run your #1 Most Valuable Problem through this checklist:

1. **Is a problem you've successfully solved for yourself?** If no, you've probably picked the wrong problem to start with.
2. **Is a problem you genuinely care about solving?** (It should bother you this problem exists in the world!) If no, you're going to burn out or give up before you see any meaningful rewards.
3. **Is a problem that can be OBJECTIVELY measured?** (Meaning you can "prove" that you've solved it.) If no, you probably don't have clarity over which problem you're actually solving.
4. **Is a problem that requires more than just "an answer" in order to solve?** (Meaning some kind of implementation is needed.) If no, you probably haven't pinpointed the most valuable problem to solve.
5. **Is a problem correlated to MAKING MONEY, NOT LOSING MONEY, SAVING A TON OF TIME, or UNLOCKING STATUS?** If no, you've probably settled for a not-so-valuable problem (change it!).

### Stress-Test Example: "Choosing A Platform" (4/5)

- **Solved for yourself?** Yes. Learned that blogs are terrible for distribution; platforms like X and LinkedIn are much better.
- **Genuinely care?** Yes. Writing online is the highest-ROI career decision you could make.
- **Objectively measurable?** Yes. You either stop blogging and start writing on social platforms, or you don't. Binary, objective, easily measurable.
- **Requires more than "an answer"?** No. "Start writing on X or LinkedIn" is basically the answer. Would need to reframe to "I've picked X -- now I don't know how to write specifically on that platform" for this to require implementation.
- **Correlated to making money / saving money / saving time / unlocking status?** Yes. Their audiences on X and LinkedIn led to millions in digital product sales.

### Stress-Test Example: "Writing Consistently" (5/5) -- Winner

- **Solved for yourself?** Yes. Life-changing outcomes as the direct result of writing online every single day.
- **Genuinely care?** Yes. Writing online is the highest-ROI career decision you could make.
- **Objectively measurable?** Yes. You are either writing every day or you're not. Binary and objective.
- **Requires more than "an answer"?** Yes. Writing consistently requires a combination of breaking faulty beliefs, education, and accountability/measures for success.
- **Correlated to making money / saving money / saving time / unlocking status?** Yes. Consistently writing online generated over $15,000,000 in revenue.

**Key insight:** Anytime your digital product helps people MAKE MORE MONEY, NOT LOSE MONEY, SAVE TIME, OR UNLOCK STATUS, it's easier for customers to rationalize buying it -- because they can imagine a clear ROI.

## Value Anchoring (When Your Niche Isn't "About Money")

If your niche or problem doesn't directly correlate to making money, you have 3 options:

1. Pick a different niche
2. Pick a different problem
3. **Get creative with how you anchor the value** of solving that particular problem

### Example: "15-minute yoga routines for remote employees"

Problem: "Not having enough time to do yoga each day."

**Beginner thinking:** "This niche is too small. Nobody will pay. Can't anchor to money."

**Proficient thinking (with this framework):**

- **Change the problem:** "Not having enough time to do yoga" -> "Suffering from back pain from sitting all day." Then anchor to: a) how much money they're spending on physical therapy, b) how much money they may have to spend later due to bad posture/disc herniation, c) how much time they waste dealing with pain, d) how unproductive they are because they're in pain all day.
- **Reframe the anchor:** "How much is your monthly yoga studio membership that you never use costing you?"

The VAST majority of the time, there's nothing wrong with the niche itself. You just haven't found the right angle to get the target customer to recognize the value of this problem.

## Module 2 Decision Framework (Exercise)

**Define these first:**

1. **Mega-Category** -> What big industry are you in?
2. **Niche** -> What niche, within that industry, are you in?
3. **Niche-Within-Niche** -> Who, specifically, within that industry and in that niche, are you trying to help?

**Then build these lists:**

4. **Top 10 Problems** -> What are the biggest problems that exist in this industry, and niche, and for this person specifically?
5. **Top 10 Most Desirable Outcomes** -> What does this person, in this niche, care about the most? What do they want related to this industry?
6. **#1 Most Valuable Problem** -> From this list of problems and outcomes, which 1 is "worth the most" and leads to the largest/most desirable outcome?

**Finally, stress-test:**

7. Run your #1 Most Valuable Problem through the 5-point checklist above.

## Integration with Presell Validator

**This framework covers the BEFORE stage** -- picking the right idea to validate.

The presell-validator's 48-Hour Demand Test (Bustamante method) starts at "you have an idea, now validate it."

**Cole fills the gap:** how to find the idea worth validating in the first place.

### Recommended Flow

1. **Cole's Framework (this file):** Niche down -> 10 Problems -> 10 Outcomes -> Pick #1 -> Stress-test
2. **Bustamante's 48-Hour Demand Test:** Take the validated idea -> presell it -> measure real demand with money on the table
