# Operational Excellence Reference (MrBeast Production Methodology)

A comprehensive framework for running creative production operations at the highest level. These principles were developed through thousands of videos and hundreds of thousands of collective work hours.

---

## A-Player / B-Player / C-Player Framework

### Diagnostic Criteria

| Category | Traits | Action |
|----------|--------|--------|
| **A-Player** | Obsessive about their craft. Learns from every mistake. Coachable. Intelligent. Makes no excuses. Believes in the mission. Best in the world at their job. | Invest in them. Give them more responsibility and compensation. |
| **B-Player** | New people who need to be trained. Have the raw material but lack the experience and knowledge. | Train them into A-Players. Set clear expectations and mentor actively. |
| **C-Player** | Average. Don't suck but are not exceptional. Just exist, do whatever, collect a paycheck. Not obsessive. Not growing. | Transition them out of the company immediately. C-Players are poisonous to culture. Give severance and move on. |

### Key Principles

- **There is only room for A-Players.** If a person is not growing, the increasing difficulty of the work will outgrow them.
- **Mistakes are investments.** Every veteran has cost a fortune at one point or another. Mistakes are how you learn. Zero tolerance for repeat mistakes, but first-time mistakes are expected.
- **Never fired for a mistake. Fired for not learning.** Own the mistake, learn from it, never repeat it.

## Results Over Hours

The number of hours worked is irrelevant. One person solving a problem in 30 minutes that a team of 5 could not solve in a week is proof that understanding and skill matter more than time.

**Application:**
- Judge people on results, not hours
- The person who deeply understands the work will outperform the person who grinds without understanding
- Invest in understanding: learn why things are done, not just how

## Priority Management

### The Priority List

When assigned tasks, maintain a strict priority list. Nothing on the planet is allowed to come between you and completing your priorities.

**Rules:**
- If another team asks for help and you fall behind on your priorities, that is YOUR fault
- Do not sacrifice your priorities for someone else's urgent request without explicit approval from your lead
- Priorities are sacred

### Multi-Project Daily Work

Never work on only one project in a day. That creates a cascade effect:
- 3 days all-in on one project = 3 days where every other project falls behind
- Falling behind creates urgency which forces more single-project days
- The cycle compounds until you can only fight fires

**Rule:** Touch every active project every day, even if briefly.

## Consultants as Cheat Codes

Consultants are the most underused resource. Before starting any unfamiliar task, ask: "Has someone done this before? Can I talk to them?"

**Why consultants work:**
- They have already done the testing you are about to do
- They can save weeks of work in a single conversation
- Their experience prevents common mistakes you do not even know exist

**Application:** For every task assigned, the first question should be "Is there a consultant for this?" Repeat this mantra: "I will always check for consultants when I am assigned a task."

## The Five Problem-Solving Tools

Everything you need can be solved by one of these five things:

| Tool | What it means |
|------|---------------|
| **Math** | Run the numbers. Calculate. Quantify. |
| **Science** | Test it. Experiment. Prototype. |
| **Vision** | Imagine it. Render it. Sketch it. Make it visual. |
| **Approvals** | Get sign-off. Confirm authority. Verify permission. |
| **Budget** | Allocate money. Adjust spending. Find creative alternatives. |

When stuck, ask: "Which of these five tools solves this problem?"

## "No" Does Not Mean No

When dealing with external people (vendors, venues, partners), never take a "No" at face value.

**Push-Through-No methodology:**
1. Talk to other employees (someone might be a fan or have different authority)
2. Try their boss
3. Try their boss's boss
4. Have the talent or brand DM them on social media
5. Try the social media team
6. If all avenues at one location are exhausted, try other locations of the same company

**Stop when:** ALL conceivable options are exhausted. Not when one person says no.

**Application beyond vendors:** This applies to partnerships, sponsorships, guest appearances, and any external negotiation.

## Say the Negatives

Do not just tell people why something is good. It is infinitely more valuable to tell them why it is NOT good.

**Example:**
- **Bad:** "I found the castle we need for the video!"
- **Good:** "I found the castle we need for the video! But it is booked all year, over budget, and someone died in it last week."

**Application:** Every status update, every recommendation, and every report should include what is wrong, what could go wrong, and what risks exist. Positives without negatives are incomplete information.

## Video Everything

Producing is a team job, not a solo thing. One person's mental model of a location, prop, or setup is worth far less than the entire team seeing video of it.

**Application:**
- Film all scouting trips, research, and progress
- Store videos where the entire team can reference them
- When ordering things from vendors, ask for video
- When checking on contractors, request video updates

**Why:** You cannot recall every detail weeks later. Your team members were not there. Video solves both problems.

## Information Diet

What you consume determines what you can create. You cannot get inspired by things you do not know exist.

**For creative roles:** Consume a wide variety of content, trends, culture, and ideas. The more you know about what exists in the world, the more connections you can make when brainstorming.

**For production roles:** Stay current on what tools, techniques, and resources are available. Know what other creators are doing. Understand trends.

**Rule:** Actively curate your information intake. If your diet is not serving your creative output, change it.

## Feasibility Studies

Before committing full production resources to a concept, test whether the critical components are actually possible.

**Process:**
1. Identify the critical components of the concept
2. Test each one in isolation (can we get this location? Can we build this prop? Is this physically possible?)
3. If feasibility passes, move to full production
4. If feasibility fails, the concept stays in ideation (no one wasted time)

**Why:** Feasibility prevents the expensive mistake of committing a full team to a video that turns out to be impossible.

## Higher Form of Communication

The worst thing you can do when something critical needs to happen is send an email. The best is to talk in person.

**Communication hierarchy (best to worst):**
1. In-person conversation
2. Video call
3. Phone call
4. Text message / instant message
5. Email

**Rule:** The more complex or urgent the issue, the higher the form of communication you should use. Text is fine for restocking toilet paper. One week out from a deadline with a critical component in trouble? Minimum a multi-way call with all stakeholders. Ideally grab them in person.

**Critical addition:** Written communication does not constitute communication until the recipient confirms they read and understood it.

## Own Your Mistakes

Mistakes are expected. Excuses are not. Every mistake is an investment in learning, but only if you own it, analyze it, and commit to never repeating it.

**Framework:**
1. **Own it:** "I made this mistake. Here is what happened."
2. **Analyze it:** "Here is why it happened and what I missed."
3. **Fix it:** "Here is what I am doing to fix it right now."
4. **Prevent it:** "Here is what I will do differently so this never happens again."

**Culture:** There is zero tolerance for saving face at the expense of learning. Honesty about mistakes is more valuable than a perfect track record.
