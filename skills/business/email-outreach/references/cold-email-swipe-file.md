# Cold Email Swipe File

Annotated real-world cold emails organized by framework pattern. Each example includes metadata so you can quickly find the right template for your situation.

**How to use:** Pick the pattern that fits your prospect + situation, grab the template version (with `{{variables}}`), and customize. The real examples underneath show how the pattern sounds in practice.

**Variable conventions** (match `personalization.md`):
- `{{firstName}}` / `{{name}}` - prospect's first name
- `{{company}}` - prospect's company
- `{{product}}` - your product name
- `{{proofCompany}}` - company from your case study
- `{{proofResult}}` - specific result from case study
- `{{trigger}}` - research-based observation or trigger event
- `{{painPoint}}` - specific problem they likely have

## 1. Ultra-Short Permission-Based

> **Framework:** 3C's (Berman) / Mouse Trap hybrid
> **CTA type:** Permission ("Mind if I send more info?")
> **Tone:** Casual, peer-level, 3-4 lines max
> **Best for:** First touch, high-volume campaigns, agency/services outreach
> **Why it works:** Lowest possible friction. Asks for permission, not commitment. The brevity alone signals respect for their time.

### Template A: Compliment + Value + Permission

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey {{firstName}}, huge fan of {{company}}!

I help companies {{valueProposition}}.

Mind if I send more info?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

### Template B: Context + Value + Permission

```
Subject: Do you do outreach?

Hey {{firstName}}, huge fan of {{company}}!

I run {{product}} that can {{specificBenefit}} for your {{team/department}}.

Mind if I send more info?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

### Template C: Peer-Context + Price Anchor

```
Subject: {{product}}?

Hey {{firstName}}, we work with a ton of {{peerGroup}}, that's why I'm reaching out.

I run {{product}} that will give you access to {{specificValue}} for only {{pricePoint}}. That way you can ditch all the other tools, make more money and give your clients high quality service.

Mind if I send more info?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

### Real Example

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey Sarah, huge fan of Acme Corp!

I help companies generate leads and make more sales.

Mind if I send more info?

Thanks,
Alex
```

**Pattern notes:**
- "Quick question" is the highest-volume cold email subject line in the world. It works because it's ambiguous enough to trigger curiosity without triggering the spam filter.
- The compliment ("huge fan") is brief enough to feel genuine, not sycophantic.
- "Mind if I send more info?" is the lowest-friction CTA in cold email. It asks for permission, which psychologically commits the prospect to a micro-yes.
- Total word count: ~25 words. That's the target for this pattern.

## 2. Story-Opener + Case Study

> **Framework:** Star-Story-Solution / 3C's hybrid
> **CTA type:** Meeting request (soft)
> **Tone:** Personal, conversational, name-drops a personal connection
> **Best for:** Warmer outreach, when you have a strong case study, services/agency
> **Why it works:** Personal story creates curiosity and authenticity. Case study provides proof. The combination feels like a real person sharing something relevant.

### Template A: Personal Discovery + Proof

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey {{firstName}},

{{personalStory}} so I had to take a look and reach out.

I help companies {{valueProposition}}. Recently {{proofCompany}} {{proofResult}} and I'd love to help you do the same.

Mind if I send over a few times for a quick call?
```

### Template B: Personal Discovery + Deeper Context

```
Subject: {!company}?

Hi {{firstName}},

Big fan of {{company}}, congrats on the growth this year, that's huge!

My SaaS can help you {{valueProposition}}. Recently we helped {{proofCompany}} {{proofResult}} and would love to do similar with you.

Would you be open to a quick call? Happy to send over a few times.

Best,
{{senderName}}
```

### Real Examples

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey James,

My girlfriend told me about Acme Corp last night so I had to
take a look and reach out.

I help companies generate leads for outbound sales. Recently
Mezzolab booked 96 calls off of 5982 leads and I'd love to
help you do the same.

Mind if I send over a few times for a quick call?
```

```
Subject: Acme Corp?

Hi James,

Big fan of Acme Corp, congrats on the growth this year,
that's huge!

My SaaS can help you generate leads and make more sales.
Recently we helped Mezzolab book 96 calls off of 5982 leads
and would love to do similar with you.

Would you be open to a quick call? Happy to send over a few
times.

Best,
Alex
```

**Pattern notes:**
- "My girlfriend told me about..." is a pattern interrupt. It's unexpected in a business email and creates immediate curiosity.
- The case study is hyper-specific: "96 calls off of 5982 leads." Specific numbers > vague claims.
- "Send over a few times for a quick call" is softer than "book a call" because it lets them choose the time.

## 3. Long-Form Value + Social Proof Stack

> **Framework:** AIDA / PASTOR hybrid
> **CTA type:** Soft meeting request + offer to answer questions
> **Tone:** Enthusiastic, detailed, proof-heavy
> **Best for:** When you have multiple proof points and strong social proof. Higher-ticket services. Prospects who need more convincing.
> **Why it works:** The social proof stack (multiple client names + specific results + revenue figures) overwhelms skepticism. Works when the prospect is solution-aware but hasn't heard of you.

### Template

```
Subject: About {{company}} & {{product}}

Hey {{firstName}}, just found {{company}} and loving your company culture.

I'm the founder of {{product}} - we're a {{oneLineDescription}} - our entire focus is {{missionStatement}} and over the last year we've generated over {{bigProofNumber}} for our clients.

Reaching out because there are a lot of things {{company}} can do to {{improvementArea}}: {{suggestion1}}, {{suggestion2}}, and {{suggestion3}}.

Would love to hop on a call and talk a bit more about how we can help {{company}} grow - whether it's {{useCase1}} like we're doing for {{client1}}, or {{useCase2}} for {{client2}} (we brought in {{impressiveNames}}).

Does that sound like something you'd be interested in? Let me know and I can send over a few times.

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

### Real Example

```
Subject: About Acme Corp & Leadshark

Hey James, just found Acme Corp and loving your company
culture.

I'm the founder of Leadshark - we're a lead database that
powers sales teams - our entire focus is finding leads for
companies like yours and over the last year we've generated
over $15 million in closed business for our clients.

Reaching out because there are a lot of things Acme Corp can
do to generate more leads: targeting a specific industry or
location, writing more powerful case studies, and adding or
improving your outreach campaigns with cold calls and cold
emails.

Would love to hop on a call and talk a bit more about how we
can help Acme Corp grow - whether it's finding very specific
niche clients like we're doing for Mezzolab, or chasing
enterprise clients for D&T (we brought in McDonald's, BCG,
HardRock, Alaska Airlines).

Does that sound like something you'd be interested in? Let me
know and I can send over a few times.

Thanks,
Alex
```

**Pattern notes:**
- This is the longest pattern in the swipe file (~150 words). Use sparingly. Works best when you're reaching out to larger companies where the prospect expects more detail.
- Name-dropping big clients (McDonald's, BCG) is the heavy artillery. Only use if the names are real and relevant to the prospect.
- Three specific suggestions show you've thought about their business, not just blasted a template.
- Two different use cases with two different client names makes the proof feel varied, not rehearsed.

## 4. Phone-First Follow-Up

> **Framework:** Multi-channel bridge
> **CTA type:** Call back request
> **Tone:** Professional, brief, creates urgency
> **Best for:** Follow-up emails, multi-channel sequences, adding legitimacy to cold outreach
> **Why it works:** Referencing a phone call (real or attempted) creates a multi-channel impression. The prospect perceives effort and seriousness. It breaks the "just another cold email" pattern.

### Template A: Simple Call Follow-Up

```
Subject: Quick question

Hi {{name}},

I just tried giving you a call to discuss {{topic}} for {{company}}.

Please give me a call back at {{phone}} or send me a note when you get the chance.

Thank you!

-{{senderName}}
```

### Template B: Call + Value Bridge

```
Subject: Just tried your line / next steps

Hey {{firstName}},

I just left you a voicemail but wanted to drop a line by email in case this is more convenient for you.

I'm reaching out because it looks like your team might be experiencing {{painPoint}}.

We're {{product}} and we {{valueProposition}}.

{{firstName}}, I'd love to connect about your specific needs. I also have a suggestion about how you can {{quickWin}} (with or without us).

Give me a call back at {{phone}} if convenient, or feel free to reply to this email.

Thanks!
```

### Real Example

```
Subject: Quick question

Hi Sarah,

I just tried giving you a call to discuss lead generation for
Acme Corp.

Please give me a call back at (877) 894-5325 or send me a
note when you get the chance.

Thank you!

-Alex
```

**Pattern notes:**
- Works as Touch 2 or 3 in a sequence. Never use as first touch.
- The phone number adds legitimacy. It says "I'm a real person with a real phone number."
- "Or send me a note" gives them the easier path while making the call seem like the primary ask.
- Template B adds value by acknowledging a pain point and offering help regardless of purchase.

## 5. Gatekeeper Navigator

> **Framework:** Direct request
> **CTA type:** Introduction request
> **Tone:** Polite, professional, brief
> **Best for:** Large companies where you don't have the decision-maker's email. Works when you've identified someone adjacent to the buyer.
> **Why it works:** People are psychologically inclined to help with simple, clear requests. Asking "who handles X" is low-effort for the recipient and gives you an internal referral.

### Template A: Department Navigator

```
Subject: Quick question

Good afternoon {{firstName}},

My name is {{senderName}} and I represent {{product}}.

Could you tell me who handles decisions in the {{department}} department and how I may connect with them?

Thank you in advance,
```

### Template B: Role-Based Navigator

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey {{firstName}},

What's up?

{{personalOpener}}

I'm the CEO/Founder of {{product}} and am looking to get introduced to your {{targetRole}} contact.

I am impressed with what I've heard from my friends about your work so far, but I don't have any formal connection yet.

Do you feel comfortable making an introduction for me?

Let me know!

Thanks!
{{senderName}}
```

### Template C: Soft Navigator

```
Subject: Quick question

Hi {{firstName}},

We recently helped a company {{proofResult}}.

Can you point me to the person who handles {{topic}} to discuss further?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

**Pattern notes:**
- Template A is the most formal. Use for enterprise targets.
- Template B adds personality and a personal connection. Use when you have some social proximity.
- Template C leads with proof before asking for the intro. Strongest pattern because it gives the gatekeeper a reason to forward you.

## 6. Database Discovery Trigger

> **Framework:** Reverse-engineered discovery / QVC hybrid
> **CTA type:** Binary yes/no question
> **Tone:** Casual, curious, slightly playful
> **Best for:** When you can plausibly say the prospect's name "came up" in research/database. SaaS tools, data platforms, lead generation.
> **Why it works:** Frames the outreach as discovery rather than targeting. "Your name came up" is flattering and creates curiosity. The binary CTA makes responding effortless.

### Template A: Discovery + Question

```
Subject: {{company}}

Hey {{firstName}},

I was using {{product}} to search for companies that would be likely to {{action}} and your name came up. Is it true?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

### Template B: Discovery + Binary Validation

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey {{firstName}},

I was using {{product}} to search for companies that would be likely to {{action}} and your name came up.

Do you guys {{buyerBehavior}}? Just give me a yes or no so I can see if our database is working properly.

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

### Template C: Icebreaker + Discovery

```
Subject: {{company}}

Hey {{firstName}},

{{icebreaker}}

Are you happy with the quality of {{currentSolution}} that you use for {{process}}?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

### Real Example

```
Subject: Acme Corp

Hey James,

I was using Leadshark to search for companies that would be
likely to purchase leads for outbound campaigns and your name
came up.

Do you guys buy B2B leads? Just give me a yes or no so I can
see if our database is working properly.

Thanks,
Alex
```

**Pattern notes:**
- "Just give me a yes or no" is a micro-commitment CTA. It's almost impossible not to respond because the ask is so small.
- "So I can see if our database is working properly" reframes the ask as helping you, not buying from you. This removes sales pressure.
- Template C with an icebreaker variable works when you have prospect-specific research.

## 7. Humor / Personality-Heavy

> **Framework:** Pattern interrupt / personality-first
> **CTA type:** Varies (permission, meeting, or open question)
> **Tone:** Playful, irreverent, high personality
> **Best for:** Prospects in creative/sales/startup environments. Standing out in crowded inboxes. When brand personality is a differentiator.
> **Why it works:** Humor is a pattern interrupt. In an inbox of professional templates, a funny email gets read. The personality signals confidence and authenticity. Risky for conservative industries.

### Template A: Absurd Personal Story

```
Subject: My son

Hey {{firstName}}, my son won't stop talking about {{company}}.

All day long it's just {{company}}, {{company}}, {{company}}.

He won't stop.. So I looked you up and it turns out your {{team}} would greatly benefit from our {{product}}.

Are you guys {{buyerBehavior}} right now?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}

P.S. he finally stopped

P.S.S. Nevermind
```

### Template B: Self-Aware Cold Email

```
Subject: Hey

You won't have heard of me. (Hi, I'm {{senderName}}!). I got your details from a list *gasp*. But hey, that means you're list-worthy; that's gotta be something, right?

OK, let's get down to brass tacks. I'm writing to you from the office of {{product}}, a {{description}} that I run with my cofounder, and a team of {{teamSize}} people far more talented than me.

For the last few years, we've run our {{product}} and we absolutely love it. It's a wonderful feeling to know we're not ripping clients off and producing the best work of our lives.

We're looking to win new clients over to our side and away from those evil (ok, maybe not evil... but not as good) other {{competitorCategory}}.

If you let me have a chat with you about your {{topic}}, I will buy you lunch/coffee/tequila shots and promise to be somewhat entertaining. If you're lucky, I may even wear a top hat. First off, I'd love to provide you with some ideas you are free to steal.

I'll be in touch on email when you least expect it... Dun Dunn Dunnn!

I await your profanity-filled response.

Have a splendid day,

{{senderName}}
```

### Template C: Love Letter

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey! I was asking some of my friends about companies that would potentially need {{service}} and you guys came up, of course.

Then I dug deeper and fell in love... {{company}} is my soulmate and I want.. no... i NEED to {{service}} for you.

Will you let me live my dream? Can I {{service}} for {{company}}?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

**Pattern notes:**
- Template A is absurd enough to be memorable. The P.S./P.S.S. adds a running joke that makes the whole email feel like a conversation.
- Template B is the longest humor email (~170 words) but the personality carries it. "I await your profanity-filled response" is a strong closer.
- Template C is playful and short. The "soulmate" angle is ridiculous enough to be charming without being creepy.
- **Warning:** Humor emails have higher variance. They get either great replies or confused silence. Don't use for C-suite at Fortune 500s.

## 8. Pain-Agitate

> **Framework:** PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)
> **CTA type:** Offer to help / soft meeting
> **Tone:** Direct, empathetic, consequence-focused
> **Best for:** Problem-aware prospects. When the pain is real and quantifiable. B2B services where the cost of inaction is high.
> **Why it works:** Naming their pain shows you understand their world. The bullet list of consequences amplifies urgency without being pushy. The offer to help positions you as advisor, not vendor.

### Template A: Pain + Bullet Consequences

```
Subject: {{painPoint}} is a huge problem

Hey {{firstName}}!

My team of robots notified me that you are trying to {{goal}}.

I don't know if you're aware, but {{painfulAction}} leads to:

- {{consequence1}}

- {{consequence2}}

- {{consequence3}}

- {{consequence4}}

This could hurt {{company}} in the long run.

If you'd like a second set of eyes on your {{process}}, I'd be happy to share a bit of insight into what's working (and what's not) when it comes to {{topic}}.

Talk soon,
```

### Template B: Observation + Pain Question

```
Subject: Quick question

Hi {{firstName}},

I noticed that your team might be {{painfulSituation}}. This might be causing you to {{negativeOutcome}}.

What if I told you there was a tool that could {{solution}}?

That's exactly what {{product}} helps you do.

Would you be interested in learning more?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

### Real Example

```
Subject: Low quality leads are a huge problem

Hey Sarah!

My team of robots notified me that you are trying to scale
your sales team.

I don't know if you're aware, but doing outreach without a
solid database leads to:

- Wasted time

- An unmotivated sales team

- Much less revenue

- Saying "cold email doesn't work"

This could hurt Acme Corp in the long run.

If you'd like a second set of eyes on your lead generation
process, I'd be happy to share a bit of insight into what's
working (and what's not) when it comes to building lead lists.

Talk soon,
```

**Pattern notes:**
- "My team of robots" is a light personality touch that keeps the tone from being too heavy.
- The bullet list works because each consequence is short (3-5 words) and distinct. Don't use more than 4 bullets in a cold email.
- "A second set of eyes" positions the sender as peer advisor. Much better than "buy our product."
- The consequences build in severity: wasted time → unmotivated team → less revenue → giving up entirely.

## 9. Time-Value Trade

> **Framework:** QVC / Value proposition
> **CTA type:** Demo/call request with time anchor
> **Tone:** Confident, direct, time-conscious
> **Best for:** Busy executives. SaaS products with clear time savings. When you can quantify the trade-off.
> **Why it works:** Trading "ten minutes" for "thousands of hours" or "20 hours/week" is an irresistible value asymmetry. The specific time amounts make the claim feel real.

### Template A: Hours Saved Trade

```
Subject: Quick question

Hi {{firstName}},

My name is {{senderName}}, and I'll keep this quick.

I'm the founder of a software tool that saves {{role}} like you as many as {{hoursSaved}} each week on {{task}}.

Could I have ten minutes of your time next week for a personalized demo that'll make clear why companies like {{client1}}, {{client2}} and {{client3}} love our product?
```

### Template B: Thousands-for-Ten

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey {{firstName}},

Every {{role}} knows how frustrating & time-consuming it is to {{painfulTask}} every day.

Our tool takes care of that for you, helping you save hundreds of hours each month to find and connect with {{desiredOutcome}}.

I want to save you thousands of hours in exchange for ten minutes of your time.

I'll show you how you can get the same results with our platform.

Do you have ten minutes this week?

Let me know!

Best Regards

{{senderName}}
```

### Template C: Idea-in-10-Minutes

```
Subject: 10x {{company}} traction in 10 minutes

Hello {{firstName}},

I have an idea that I can explain in 10 minutes that can get {{company}} its next 100 best customers.

I recently used this idea to help our client {{proofCompany}} almost triple their monthly run rate.

{{firstName}}, let's schedule a quick 10-minute call so I can share the idea with you. When works best for you?
```

**Pattern notes:**
- "10 minutes" is the sweet spot. It's short enough to not feel like a commitment but long enough to be credible.
- Template C is aggressive but effective: "10x traction in 10 minutes" as a subject line is a bold claim that demands attention.
- Using the prospect's name twice (Template C) creates a personal feel but borders on aggressive. Use once in most cases.

## 10. Peer Question

> **Framework:** SCQ / Consultative
> **CTA type:** Question (no meeting ask)
> **Tone:** Curious, peer-level, non-threatening
> **Best for:** Research-first approaches. Building rapport before pitching. When you genuinely want to understand their process.
> **Why it works:** Questions are easier to respond to than requests. Positioning yourself as curious (not selling) lowers defenses. The response gives you intel for a stronger follow-up.

### Template A: Humble Peer

```
Subject: Lead generation question

Hey {{firstName}}, I'm reaching out to a couple {{peerGroup}} that I look up to (so I hope you don't mind the cold approach haha)

Wanted to ask a quick question as we scale up our {{team}} over here:

What tools are you currently using for {{process}}?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

### Template B: Profile-Based Question

```
Subject: Quick question

Hello {{firstName}},

I would like to ask you a few questions about your work to better understand your needs and identify ways to improve my product.

I am the Founder of {{product}}, a start-up that develops {{description}}.

Would you be available {{specificDay}} at {{time1}} or at {{time2}} to talk about it by video (30 Min)?

Yours truly,
```

### Template C: Research Question

```
Subject: {{company}} <> {{product}}

Hi {{firstName}},

I'm reaching out to you because I noticed on your {{platform}} profile that you are working at {{company}}. Given your role as a {{role}}, my assumption is that {{relevantResponsibility}} falls in your wheelhouse.

In short, {{product}} has helped other {{role}} from companies in your industry. Our {{valueProposition}}.

{{firstName}}, is that something you'd be interested in?

Thanks,
-{{senderName}}
```

**Pattern notes:**
- Template A's "(so I hope you don't mind the cold approach haha)" acknowledges the cold email elephant in the room. Disarming honesty.
- Peer questions work best when they're genuinely useful regardless of whether the prospect buys. "What tools are you using?" is a real question, not a Trojan horse.
- Use the response to craft a highly personalized follow-up. "You mentioned you're using X. Here's how we compare..."

## 11. Social Proof Sandwich

> **Framework:** AIDA / Authority
> **CTA type:** Meeting request or interest check
> **Tone:** Confident, proof-heavy, peer-level
> **Best for:** When you have multiple recognizable client names. Competitive markets where credibility is the bottleneck. Mid-to-large company prospects.
> **Why it works:** Stacking multiple proof points creates a "everyone's using this" impression. Specific results (96 calls from 5982 leads) anchor the proof in reality.

### Template A: Result + Client Stack

```
Subject: Short question

Hey {{firstName}},

I saw that you are also in the {{industry}} space. Just wanted to share with you how we have increased the {{metric}} of {{client1}}, {{client2}} and {{client3}} ... in their own market. Think we can get similar or even better results for {{company}}.

Would you be up for a meeting next week?
```

### Template B: Sales-Proof Closer

```
Subject: Sales?

Hi {{firstName}},

I've booked sales calls with most of the Fortune 500. My secret weapon? {{product}}.

Recently {{proofCompany}} {{proofResult}} using our data and {{guarantee}}.

Do you want to save hours of time with {{task}} while making more sales?

If so, I'll show you exactly how we do it. Let me know what you think!

Best regards

{{senderName}}
```

### Template C: Big Number + Client Names

```
Subject: {{company}}

Hi {{firstName}},

I'm reaching out because I think you'll be interested in a product that could save you as many as {{hoursSaved}} every week.

My name is {{senderName}}, and I'm the founder of a software tool that helps busy {{role}} like yourself by {{valueProposition}}.

And let me tell you - {{client1}}, {{client2}} and {{client3}} are all huge fans ;)

Have ten minutes next week for a personalized demo?

Sincerely,
{{senderName}}
```

**Pattern notes:**
- Three client names is the sweet spot. Two feels thin. Four+ feels like bragging.
- The wink emoji in Template C is risky but works in casual B2B contexts. Skip for enterprise.
- "I've booked sales calls with most of the Fortune 500" is a bold opening. Only use if you can back it up.

## 12. Breakup / Voicemail Follow-Up

> **Framework:** Loss aversion / Multi-channel bridge
> **CTA type:** Call back or simple reply
> **Tone:** Brief, respectful, final
> **Best for:** Last touch in sequence (Touch 4-5). After multiple unreturned emails. Voicemail follow-up in multi-channel campaigns.
> **Why it works:** Breakup emails trigger loss aversion. Voicemail references create a multi-channel impression. Both signal that the sender is a real person making real effort.

### Template A: Voicemail Bridge

```
Subject: Followup

Hi {{name}},

I just tried giving you a call to discuss {{topic}} for {{company}}.

Please give me a call back at {{phone}} or send me a note when you get the chance.

Thank you!

-{{senderName}}
```

### Template B: Quick Voicemail

```
Subject: Email

Hey {{firstName}},

I just tried calling you.

Wanted to {{purpose}}.

Would love to showcase a demo over a video call. Do you have 10 minutes on {{day}}?

Best regards,

{{senderName}}
```

### Template C: Casual Check (Pre-Breakup)

```
Subject: Quick Question

Hey, it's {{senderName}} I just gave you a call. I {{valueProposition}} and would love to help your {{team}} {{benefit}}. Any interest?
```

**Pattern notes:**
- These are NOT standalone emails. They belong in positions 3-5 of a sequence.
- Keep breakup/follow-up emails under 40 words. Respect diminishing attention.
- Always include a phone number in voicemail bridges. It signals legitimacy.
- If you send a breakup email, honor it. Don't contact the prospect again for at least 90 days.

## Cross-Reference: CTA Patterns

These CTAs appear across multiple patterns. Choose based on the prospect's seniority and your relationship:

| CTA | Friction Level | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| "Mind if I send more info?" | Lowest | First touch, high volume |
| "Would you be interested in learning more?" | Low | First touch, professional tone |
| "Worth exploring?" / "Any interest?" | Low | C-suite, brief emails |
| "Does that sound like something you'd be interested in?" | Low-Medium | After providing value/proof |
| "Let me know what you think" | Low | Casual, peer-level |
| "Would you be open to a quick call?" | Medium | After demonstrating value |
| "Have ten minutes next week?" | Medium | After social proof |
| "Give me a yes or no" | Low (binary) | Database trigger pattern |
| "Can you point me to the person who handles..." | Low | Gatekeeper pattern |
| "When works best for you?" | Medium-High | After strong proof, confident tone |
