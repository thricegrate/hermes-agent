# Subject Line Arsenal Reference

Synthesized from 80 email marketing transcripts. Covers subject line formulas, curiosity gap techniques, personalization, A/B testing methodology, spam avoidance, and preview text optimization. The most comprehensive single-source subject line reference in this skill.

## Part 1: The 7 Core Subject Line Techniques

Each technique has a specific use case. Match the technique to the email goal. Rotate based on campaign purpose, not habit.

| Technique | Definition | Example | Best Use Cases |
|---|---|---|---|
| Urgency | Real time pressure with a specific deadline | "Only 3 hours left to claim your discount" | Flash sales, limited-time offers |
| Personalization | Name, behavior, or purchase history reference | "John, we thought you might like..." | Newsletters, product recommendations |
| FOMO | Crowd behavior triggers fear of missing out | "Everyone's talking about our latest release. Don't miss out!" | New product launches, trending items |
| Benefit-driven | Lead with the reader's outcome, not the feature | "Boost your productivity by 50%." | Feature/benefit promotions |
| Curiosity gap | Reveal enough to spark interest, withhold enough to demand the open | "The secret to doubling your sales." | Webinars, educational content |
| Testimonial | Open with a real customer result or reaction | "This product changed my life!" | Trust building, product quality |
| Social proof | Highlight adoption numbers or crowd validation | "Join 10,000 others who've made the switch!" | Demonstrating adoption, popular products |

**Key rule**: Map every subject line to one primary technique before writing it. Writing without a technique produces generic subject lines that blend into the inbox.

## Part 3: Curiosity Gap Formulas

The curiosity gap is the distance between what the reader knows and what they want to know. A subject line that creates a gap requires an open to close it. A subject line that announces removes the need to open.

### The 4 Curiosity Gap Triggers

**1. Scarcity + Backstory**
- Formula: "[Event/decision that implies limited supply]"
- Example: "We almost didn't release these"
- Why it works: Implies a story, a decision, and limited supply -- three open triggers in four words

**2. Time Urgency Narrative**
- Formula: "[The thing] disappears [specific time]"
- Example: "This price disappears Friday"
- Why it works: Converts a discount announcement into a countdown narrative with real stakes

**3. Social Proof with Specificity**
- Formula: "[Specific real number] people [action] this [time period]"
- Example: "2,847 people bought this last week"
- Why it works: Real specificity signals genuine data; "bestsellers" is a claim, "2,847 people" is evidence

**4. Exclusivity / Novelty**
- Formula: "You haven't [seen/heard/tried] this yet"
- Example: "You haven't seen these yet"
- Why it works: Positions the reader as an insider getting early access before the general public

### Curiosity Gap Subject Line Rewrites

Practice by taking any announcing subject line and rewriting it as a tease:

| Generic Announcement | Curiosity Gap Rewrite |
|---|---|
| "Our New Spring Collection" | "We almost didn't release these" |
| "20% Off Everything" | "This price disappears Friday" |
| "Check Out Our Best Sellers" | "2,847 people bought this last week" |
| "New Arrivals Are Here" | "You haven't seen these yet" |
| "Newsletter #17" | "The one mistake costing you 30% of your opens" |
| "Monthly Update" | "Something changed last week. Here's what." |
| "Feature Update" | "This one thing saves our users 3 hours a week" |

**The rule**: Don't tell them what's inside. Make them need to find out.

### The Numbered List Curiosity Gap

Numbered list subject lines work because they signal organized, skimmable content and create anticipation for the specific items:
- "7 email mistakes killing your open rates"
- "3 things your welcome email is missing"
- "5 Subject Line Hooks That Lift Open Rates"

Key insight: Specific odd numbers (7, 12, 23) outperform round numbers (10, 20). They signal precision and actual counting. "7 ways" feels empirical. "10 ways" feels like a rounded estimate.

### The Open Question Loop

Questions force the reader to mentally answer them. A question asked creates an open loop that compels the brain to seek closure. Use questions when you want the reader to insert themselves into the scenario:
- "Are you making this [niche] mistake?" (reader asks "Am I?")
- "Need More Time in Your Day?" (reader asks "Do I?")
- "Is this thing on?" (creates playful curiosity about context)

Both question and statement formats work. The choice depends on your list's preferences -- test both.

## Part 4: Personalization Techniques

### Basic Personalization (First Name)

First name in subject lines lifts open rates 26-81% depending on context and implementation. Most effective in plain-text emails and conversational contexts:
- "[First Name], we thought you'd love this"
- "A Special Gift Just For You, [First Name]!"
- "[First Name], Your Customized Offer is Here!"
- "[First Name], this was made for you."

Write the subject line as if it is addressed directly to one person, not a list.

### Behavioral Personalization (Beyond First Name)

These signals drive meaningfully higher relevance than name alone:

**Purchase history triggers:**
- "Based on your recent purchase of [product]..."
- "You bought [X] last month -- you might also like..."
- "Quick note about your [product] order..."

**Browse behavior triggers:**
- "Still thinking about [product name]?" (abandoned browse)
- "You looked at [category] last week -- here's what's new"

**Engagement-based triggers:**
- "You opened 3 of our last 5 emails. This one's for you."
- "Our most clicked email ever. In case you missed it."

**Stated interest triggers:**
- "Your [interest area] guide is ready"
- "For [audience segment]: the [topic] playbook"

### Behavioral Segment Subject Line Patterns

Match the subject line to the specific segment:
- High-value VIPs: "You've earned this. Exclusive VIP access."
- At-risk subscribers: "We don't want to lose you. Here's why."
- One-time buyers: "Score a free [product] with your next order"
- Cold subscribers (re-engagement): "Is this still useful to you?"
- New subscribers: "[First Name], here's exactly what to do first"

## Part 5: A/B Testing Methodology

### What to Test First

Priority order for A/B testing (highest leverage to lowest):

1. **Subject line** -- Single highest-impact variable. Affects whether anyone sees the email at all. Test this on every significant send.
2. **Sender name** -- Subscribers open emails from names they recognize. "Sarah from [Brand]" vs. "[Brand] Updates" can move open rates 10-20%.
3. **Preview text** -- Works as a second subject line. Testing preview text in conjunction with subject line can double the optimization surface.
4. **Send time** -- Same email at different times can produce 15-30% variation in open rates. Test Tuesday vs. Thursday, morning vs. afternoon.
5. **CTA copy** -- "Claim your 20% off" vs. "Shop now" vs. "Get my discount" -- active, specific, first-person CTAs consistently outperform generic ones.
6. **Email length** -- Short (under 150 words) vs. long (800+ words) -- results vary dramatically by audience and email type.
7. **Format** -- Text-only vs. HTML-heavy -- text-only often outperforms in newsletter/personal contexts; HTML performs better in promotional contexts.

### How to Run a Clean A/B Test

**One variable at a time.** Testing two variables simultaneously produces unreadable data. You cannot know which variable drove the result.

**Sample size minimum**: 1,000 recipients per variant for statistical significance. Smaller lists require running tests over multiple sends rather than split-sending one campaign.

**Test duration**: For open rate tests, 72 hours is sufficient. For conversion tests, allow the full purchase cycle (1-7 days depending on product).

**What to record**: Subject line text, open rate, click rate, conversion rate, revenue per send. Build a testing log.

**When to declare a winner**: A/B test ends when one variant achieves a meaningful lift (5%+ for open rate, 10%+ for conversion). Do not end tests early because one variant is ahead by 1-2%.

### Subject Line A/B Test Examples

From transcripts -- these types of pairs yield interpretable data:

- "How to Save Time on Social Media" vs. "Social Media Shortcuts for Busy Entrepreneurs" (specificity vs. benefit framing)
- "Flash Sale -- 30% Off" vs. "This price disappears Friday" (announcement vs. curiosity gap)
- "Newsletter #17" vs. "The one mistake costing you 30% of your opens" (generic vs. specific)
- "[First Name], check this out" vs. "3 things your welcome email is missing" (personalization vs. numbered curiosity)
- "Don't miss out" vs. "2,847 people bought this last week" (vague FOMO vs. specific social proof)

### Interpreting Results

- Higher open rate but same CTR: Subject line improvement only. Email body was already good. Continue testing subject lines.
- Same open rate but higher CTR: Body copy or CTA improved. Keep subject, iterate on body.
- Higher open rate and higher CTR: Both elements improved. Roll out the winner and start a new test.
- Higher open rate but lower CTR: Subject line over-promised. The subject created a gap the email body did not fulfill. Mismatch destroys trust. Realign.

## Part 6: Spam Trigger Words and Inbox Placement Best Practices

### Spam Trigger Words to Avoid in Subject Lines

These words and patterns increase the likelihood of spam filter flagging:
- "Free!!!" (exclamation overuse, especially combined with trigger words)
- "Get Rich Quick"
- "Guaranteed"
- "Act now"
- "Click here"
- "Buy now" (in subject lines, not in body)
- "Make money fast"
- "100% free"
- All caps phrases: "HUGE SALE" (all-caps signals aggression to filters and readers)
- Excessive exclamation marks: "HUGE SALE!!!"

**Replacement patterns:**
- "Get Rich Quick" becomes "Proven Strategies for Financial Growth"
- "FREE" becomes the specific thing: "Your free template" or "Complimentary guide inside"
- All caps becomes sentence case with power words: "Grab this deal today -- while it lasts"

### Technical Inbox Placement Checklist

Before every send, confirm:
- SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records are configured correctly
- Bounce rate is below 2%
- Spam complaint rate is below 0.1%
- List includes only opted-in subscribers (double opt-in preferred)
- Unengaged subscribers (90+ days no open) are excluded or in re-engagement sequence
- Unsubscribe link is visible and functional in every email
- Subject line does not contain spam trigger words

### The Deliverability Reply Tactic

From the `welcome-email-reply-cta-that-builds-domain-reputation` transcript:

The most effective deliverability tactic in the welcome email is asking subscribers to reply. A reply trains inbox placement algorithms (Gmail, Outlook) that your emails belong in the primary inbox. The tactic:

> "If all of this sounds good, there's just 2 quick things you need to do:
> 1. Reply to this email with the word 'sushi'
> 2. Make sure to move this email to your primary inbox if it landed in spam or promotions.
> As a thank you for doing this, I'll send you a little surprise welcome gift.
> Deal?"

Result from real deployment: hundreds of replies, strong domain reputation, better long-term deliverability.

The principle works for any keyword. The conversational "Deal?" close is as important as the instruction itself.

## Part 7: Preview Text Optimization

Preview text is the 40-100 characters displayed next to the subject line in most email clients. It is a second subject line. Most senders waste all of it.

### What Not to Do

- Leave it blank (defaults to "View this email in your browser" or unsubscribe link)
- Repeat the subject line verbatim
- Start with "Hi [First Name]," (wastes the entire character limit on a greeting)
- Use generic boilerplate: "This week's newsletter is here"

### Preview Text Formulas That Work

**Extend the subject line hook:**
- Subject: "This price disappears Friday"
- Preview: "...and we're not bringing it back. Here's what you need to know."

**Add a micro-incentive:**
- Subject: "3 things your welcome email is missing"
- Preview: "...plus the free template inside."

**Tease the most interesting thing:**
- Subject: "Email marketing tips from a $200M sender"
- Preview: "The one change that adds 20-30% more opens to every campaign."

**Direct call-to-open:**
- Subject: "Your abandoned cart"
- Preview: "Open to see what you left behind -- plus a special offer."

### Preview Text Swipe File

These work across a wide range of email types:
- "Surprise inside..."
- "Don't miss out on this!"
- "Open to learn more..."
- "Here's what you get..."
- "Ready to [outcome]?"
- "...plus the free template inside."
- "In case you missed this..."
- "[Specific curiosity gap continuation]"
- "Only [X] spots left."
- "This one surprised even us."

### Character Limits

- Ideal preview text length: 40-90 characters (visible on most clients including mobile)
- Maximum before truncation: 90-100 characters on most clients
- On mobile: preview text over 90 characters gets cut off
- Write preview text as if only the first 60 characters will be seen

## Part 8: Subject Line Rules Reference

### The 8 Rules for Boosting Open Rates

From `8-subject-line-rules-to-boost-open-rates` transcript:

1. **Use personalization** -- Boosts open rates 81%. Put the recipient's name first.
2. **Be personal** -- Write for one person. Use personal pronouns. Avoid generalization.
3. **Be specific** -- Vague subject lines are boring and spammy. Specific lines grab attention and build intrigue.
4. **Leverage curiosity** -- Use bold claims, clear benefits, and emotional statements.
5. **Use preview text** -- Two subject lines: the main one and the preview text. Use both.
6. **Use power words** -- Evoke: scarcity, vanity, reciprocity, FOMO, urgency.
7. **Keep it short** -- Under 43 characters for mobile. Over 50% of subscribers read on mobile.
8. **Use numbers** -- Numbers add specificity, communicate clearly, and save characters.

### The 7 Mistakes That Kill Open Rates

From `7-subject-line-mistakes-killing-your-open-rates` transcript:

1. **Being generic** -- "Newsletter Update" loses. State exactly what's inside.
2. **Being too long** -- Over 50 characters gets cut on mobile.
3. **Using all caps** -- Triggers spam filters and looks aggressive.
4. **Relying on clickbait** -- Overpromising destroys trust. Deliver what you promise.
5. **Skipping personalization** -- Generic subject lines feel cold. Personalized ones are 26% more likely to be opened.
6. **Overusing emojis** -- Limit to 1-2 emojis maximum.
7. **Including errors** -- Typos cut open rates up to 10%. Proofread before every send.
