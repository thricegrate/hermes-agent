# Cold Outreach Sequences

Cold outreach sequences are fundamentally different from lifecycle sequences. The prospect hasn't opted in, doesn't know you, and will delete anything that looks like a template. Every email must stand alone (they may not have read the previous ones) while building toward a response.

For cold email writing principles, frameworks, and annotated examples, see the `email-outreach` skill.

---

## 5-Touch Cold Outreach Sequence Blueprint

The standard cold outreach sequence. Each touch uses a different angle to catch the prospect at different moments and moods.

### Blueprint

| Touch | Day | Angle | Framework | CTA |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0 | Personalized hook + core value | 3C's or Story-Opener | Permission ("Mind if I send more info?") |
| 2 | 3 | New angle + case study | PAS or Social Proof Sandwich | Interest check ("Worth exploring?") |
| 3 | 7 | Phone-first follow-up | Multi-channel bridge | Call back or reply |
| 4 | 14 | Value resource + different proof point | Time-Value Trade or Peer Question | Meeting request |
| 5 | 21-28 | Breakup | Loss aversion | Binary (1-2-3 format) or door-open |

### Angle Rotation Rules

- Each email uses a **different framework**. Never repeat the same angle twice.
- Each email introduces **one new proof point** (different client, different result, different metric).
- CTAs escalate: permission → interest → call back → meeting → breakup.
- If the prospect clicks but doesn't reply, accelerate Touch 3 to Day 5.

## Complete Sequence Example (Annotated)

### Context
B2B SaaS selling a lead database to sales teams. Prospect is a VP Sales at a mid-market company.

### Touch 1: Ultra-Short Permission (Day 0)

**Framework:** 3C's (Compliment + Case Study + CTA)

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey {{firstName}}, huge fan of {{company}}!

I help companies generate leads and make more sales.

Mind if I send more info?

Thanks,
{{senderName}}
```

**Why this works as Touch 1:**
- Lowest friction possible. You're asking for permission, not a meeting.
- 25 words. Respects their time on first contact.
- "Quick question" subject line has the highest open rate for cold email.

---

### Touch 2: Story-Opener + Case Study (Day 3)

**Framework:** Star-Story-Solution

```
Subject: {{company}}?

Hi {{firstName}},

Big fan of {{company}}, congrats on the growth this year, that's huge!

My SaaS can help you generate leads and make more sales. Recently we helped {{proofCompany}} book 96 calls off of 5982 leads and would love to do similar with you.

Would you be open to a quick call? Happy to send over a few times.

Best,
{{senderName}}
```

**Why this works as Touch 2:**
- Different angle from Touch 1 (story + specific proof vs. bare permission ask).
- Company name as subject line is different from "Quick question."
- Specific numbers (96 calls, 5982 leads) anchor credibility.
- "Happy to send over a few times" puts them in control of scheduling.

---

### Touch 3: Phone-First Follow-Up (Day 7)

**Framework:** Multi-channel bridge

```
Subject: Followup

Hi {{firstName}},

I just tried giving you a call to discuss lead generation for {{company}}.

Please give me a call back at {{phone}} or send me a note when you get the chance.

Thank you!

-{{senderName}}
```

**Why this works as Touch 3:**
- Referencing a call attempt (real or attempted) creates a multi-channel impression.
- The phone number adds legitimacy.
- "Send me a note" gives the easier reply path.
- Ultra-short (30 words). At Touch 3, brevity signals respect for their silence.

---

### Touch 4: Pain-Agitate + Value (Day 14)

**Framework:** PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)

```
Subject: Low quality leads are a huge problem

Hey {{firstName}}!

I don't know if you're aware, but doing outreach without a solid database leads to:

- Wasted time
- An unmotivated sales team
- Much less revenue

If you'd like a second set of eyes on your lead generation process, I'd be happy to share a bit of insight into what's working (and what's not).

Talk soon,
{{senderName}}
```

**Why this works as Touch 4:**
- Completely different angle from Touches 1-3 (pain-focused vs. value/proof-focused).
- Bullet list of consequences creates urgency without being pushy.
- "A second set of eyes" positions as advisor, not vendor.
- No mention of your product. Pure value offer.

---

### Touch 5: Breakup (Day 21-28)

**Framework:** Loss aversion

```
Subject: Quick question

Hey {{firstName}},

I've reached out a few times about helping {{company}} with lead generation. I'll assume now isn't the right time.

Before I close the loop: we just helped {{newProofCompany}} triple their monthly meetings. If that changes things, feel free to reply.

Otherwise, no hard feelings. Good luck scaling the team.

-{{senderName}}
```

**Why this works as Touch 5:**
- Acknowledges silence without guilt-tripping.
- One final proof point (different client from Touches 1-4).
- "No hard feelings" removes all pressure.
- **Critical:** If you send this, honor it. Do not contact again for at least 90 days.

## Phone-First Variant (Multi-Channel)

For teams that include actual phone outreach in their process:

| Touch | Day | Channel | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 0 | Email | Ultra-short permission (3C's) |
| 2 | 2 | Phone | Call attempt + voicemail |
| 3 | 2 | Email | "Just tried your line" voicemail bridge |
| 4 | 7 | Email | New angle + case study |
| 5 | 14 | Email | Breakup |

The voicemail bridge email (Touch 3) goes out same day as the call attempt. This creates the strongest multi-channel impression.

## Sequence Design Rules

### What Makes Cold Sequences Different from Lifecycle

| | Cold Outreach | Lifecycle (Welcome/Nurture) |
|---|---|---|
| Opt-in | No | Yes |
| Length | 25-75 words per email | 150-800 words per email |
| Formatting | Plain text, no sub-heads | Sub-heads, bullets, images OK |
| CTA | Reply or meeting | Click, buy, engage |
| Personalization | Must connect to problem | Segment-level OK |
| Tone | Peer, not vendor | Brand voice |
| Subject lines | Boring, internal-looking | Hook-driven, creative |
| PS section | Never (looks like marketing) | Prime real estate |
| HTML/images | Never | Often beneficial |

### Transitioning Cold to Nurture

When a cold prospect replies positively but isn't ready to buy:

1. Move them out of the cold sequence immediately
2. Add to a warm nurture sequence (see email-sequence lifecycle sequences)
3. First nurture email should reference the cold conversation: "Thanks for the reply last week. As promised, here's {{resource}}."
4. Treat them as a warm lead from this point. No more cold email patterns.

### When to Skip the Sequence

Not every prospect needs 5 touches. Skip to breakup if:
- They replied "not interested" (respect it, move on)
- They opened all emails but never replied after Touch 3 (they're reading but not buying)
- Your bounce rate on this prospect's domain exceeds 5% (deliverability risk)
