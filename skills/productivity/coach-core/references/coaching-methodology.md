## GROW Model Conversation Wrapper

Use GROW as the conversational backbone for every coaching session:

| Phase | Focus | Coach Actions |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| **G, Goal** | What do you want to achieve? | Clarify the session goal AND the bigger goal. Make it specific, measurable, time-bound. |
| **R, Reality** | Where are you now? | Explore current state with facts, not feelings. Get exact numbers. Use 5 Whys to go deeper. |
| **O, Options** | What could you do? | Brainstorm without judgment. Push for 5+ options. Use Inversion: "What would guarantee failure?" |
| **W, Will** | What WILL you do? | Lock in specific commitments. Who does what by when? What's the accountability mechanism? |

**How to layer GROW with existing frameworks:**
- **Goal phase** → Use Hormozi 9 Stages to calibrate ambition. Use Future Self visioning.
- **Reality phase** → Use TOC 5 Focusing Steps to find the constraint. Use Throughput Accounting for numbers.
- **Options phase** → Use First Principles, Inversion, Pareto to generate and filter options.
- **Will phase** → Use Hardy/Sullivan commitment devices. Set Gap/Gain measurement baselines.

**Key rule:** GROW is the wrapper, not the content. The content comes from the specialized frameworks (Hormozi, TOC, Hardy/Sullivan, Mental Models).

**Organizations using GROW report 67% increases in employee satisfaction and 89% improvements in retention rates.**

## When to Coach vs Consult

| Mode | When to Use | What It Looks Like |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Coaching** | Client has the answer but can't see it | Ask questions, hold space, reflect back, challenge assumptions |
| **Consulting** | Client lacks specific knowledge or expertise | Teach frameworks, share data, give direct recommendations |
| **Mentoring** | Client needs perspective from experience | Share personal stories, warn about pitfalls, open doors |

**Decision tree:**
1. Start every conversation in coaching mode (ask, don't tell)
2. If the client is stuck and questions aren't unlocking insight → switch to consulting (teach the framework)
3. If the client needs strategic perspective beyond frameworks → switch to mentoring (share experience)
4. Always return to coaching mode for commitment and action planning

**The 3:1 rule:** Ask 3 questions for every 1 piece of advice. When you catch yourself consulting too much, pull back to questions.

**Anti-pattern:** Jumping straight to advice. Even when the answer is obvious to you, the client internalizes it better when they discover it themselves.

## 80/20 Conversation Rule

**In discovery sessions:** Listen 80%, talk 20%.

**In ongoing sessions:** Aim for 60/40 (client/coach), never below 50/50.

**Why this matters:**
- Clients who talk more commit more deeply to their own insights
- Listening reveals the real problem (not just the stated problem)
- Premature advice creates dependency, not autonomy

**Tactical listening moves:**
1. **Reflective repeat**: Repeat their exact words back: "You said 'I feel stuck at $20K.' Tell me more about stuck."
2. **Emotional labeling**: Name the emotion: "It sounds like you're frustrated with the pace of growth."
3. **Strategic silence**: After a powerful question, wait. Count to 10. Let them think.
4. **Summarize and check**: "Here's what I'm hearing: X, Y, and Z. Did I get that right?"

**Red flag:** If you're talking more than 50% of the time, you're consulting, not coaching.

## Five-Element Session Flow

Every coaching session follows this structure:

### 1. Rapport (2-5 min)
- Check in personally before jumping to business
- Reference something from last session to show continuity
- Set the emotional tone: safe but challenging

### 2. Goal (5-10 min)
- "What's the most important thing to focus on today?"
- Align session goal with the bigger 90-day roadmap goal
- If they bring a fire drill, assess: is this the constraint or a distraction?

### 3. Exploration (20-30 min)
- Deep dive into the topic using relevant frameworks
- Apply the coaching vs consulting decision tree
- Use powerful questions (see `references/powerful-questions.md`)
- If they hit a belief or identity block, use breakthrough techniques (see `references/breakthrough-techniques.md`)

### 4. Action (10-15 min)
- Convert insights into 3 specific action items
- Each action: What, Who, By When, Success Metric
- Stress-test with Inversion: "What could prevent you from doing this?"
- Use the Gap/Gain frame: measure backward from where they started

### 5. Accountability (5 min)
- Client restates their commitments out loud
- Schedule next session
- "On a scale of 1-10, how committed are you to each action? What would make it a 10?"
- End with a forward-looking statement: "When we meet next, I expect to hear about X."

**Key stat:** Peer-driven accountability increases goal completion by up to 43% (ICF research).

## The Power of Silence

**Rule:** After asking a powerful question, shut up. Count to 10 before speaking.

**Why silence works:**
- Forces the client to go deeper (past the surface answer)
- Communicates that you trust them to find the answer
- Creates space for breakthrough insights that chatter prevents

**When to use silence:**
- After any question that starts with "What" or "Why"
- When the client gives a vague answer (silence prompts them to elaborate)
- When the client is processing a difficult truth
- After a reframe or challenge (let it land)

**Anti-patterns:**
- Filling silence with another question (stacking)
- Answering your own question
- Softening a tough question because the silence feels uncomfortable

**Quote:** "The quality of the question determines the quality of the silence. The quality of the silence determines the quality of the insight."

## Rapport Building

### First Session Rapport
1. Research the prospect beforehand (LinkedIn, website, social media)
2. Open with genuine curiosity, not a pitch
3. Listen for values, motivators, and communication style
4. Match their energy and pace (fast talker → match speed; slow thinker → give space)
5. Find shared context (mutual connections, similar experiences, common interests)

### Ongoing Session Rapport
1. Reference specifics from prior sessions
2. Celebrate wins before addressing gaps
3. Use their language and metaphors back to them
4. Be direct about difficult truths: directness builds trust faster than tiptoeing
5. Show vulnerability when appropriate (share a relevant mistake you've made)

### Adapting to Communication Styles (DISC-informed)

| Style | Characteristics | Coaching Approach |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Dominance (D)** | Direct, results-focused, impatient | Get to the point fast, challenge them, focus on outcomes |
| **Influence (I)** | Enthusiastic, social, big-picture | Match energy, keep it visionary, watch for lack of follow-through |
| **Steadiness (S)** | Patient, loyal, change-averse | Build trust slowly, small steps, acknowledge discomfort with change |
| **Conscientiousness (C)** | Analytical, detail-oriented, perfectionist | Bring data, be logical, give them time to process |

## Assessment Tools

Use assessments to accelerate diagnosis and personalize coaching:

### DISC Assessment
- **What it measures:** Behavioral tendencies (Dominance, Influence, Steadiness, Conscientiousness)
- **When to use:** At the start of coaching to understand communication style and decision-making
- **Coaching value:** Adapt your delivery, predict resistance points, design action plans that fit their style

### CliftonStrengths (StrengthsFinder)
- **What it measures:** Top 5-34 talent themes out of 34 possible
- **When to use:** When the client feels unfocused or is trying to do everything
- **Coaching value:** Focus on strengths (not weaknesses), identify the 20% of activities that use their strengths, delegate the rest

### 360-Degree Feedback
- **What it measures:** How others perceive the client (direct reports, peers, supervisors, clients)
- **When to use:** For leadership coaching, team dynamics, or when the client has blind spots
- **Coaching value:** Surface gaps between self-perception and reality, create actionable development plans

### Energy Audit
- **What it measures:** Which activities drain vs energize the client
- **When to use:** When the client is burned out or stuck
- **Coaching value:** Redesign their week around energy-giving activities, delegate energy-draining ones

**Rule:** Assessments are conversation starters, not destinations. Use them to open dialogue, not to label.

## ICF Core Principles

Two ICF principles to embed in every session:

### Principle #11: Promotes Client Autonomy
- The goal is to make yourself unnecessary, not indispensable
- Ask: "What would you do if you couldn't ask me?"
- Build their decision-making muscle, don't make decisions for them
- Celebrate independent thinking: "That's a great call: you didn't need me for that"
- Route to self-assessment: "How would YOU evaluate whether this worked?"

### Principle #12: Know Your Limits
- If a client needs therapy (trauma, clinical anxiety, depression), refer out: you're a coach, not a therapist
- If a client needs legal, accounting, or medical advice, refer to professionals
- If a coaching engagement isn't producing results after 3 sessions, reassess fit
- Be transparent: "This is outside my expertise. Here's who can help."

**Boundary markers:**
- Client consistently discusses past trauma → refer to therapist
- Client shows signs of clinical depression or anxiety → refer to mental health professional
- Client needs tax/legal structure advice → refer to CPA/attorney
- Client needs technical implementation → route to the appropriate skill
