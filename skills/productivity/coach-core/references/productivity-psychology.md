## Three Buckets of Stuck

When someone knows what to do but can't get themselves to do it, the cause falls into one of three buckets:

| Bucket | % of Cases | Signal Phrases | Root Cause |
|--------|-----------|----------------|------------|
| **Don't Know How** | ~10% | "I don't know where to start", "What's the right approach?" | Genuine knowledge gap. Information or mentorship solves it. |
| **No Energy** | ~30% | "I'm exhausted", "I just can't", "I'm burned out" | Physical or emotional depletion. Could also be misalignment (doing stuff you hate drains energy). **If direction is clear but energy is gone:** route to `coach-business/references/sustainable-performance.md` (Stress + Rest = Growth). **If direction is scattered:** route to `coach-core/references/performance-equation.md` (Direction Deficit). |
| **Fear** | ~60% | "I keep putting it off", "I know what to do but...", "It feels cringe" | Subconscious threat detection. Something about the task triggers survival instinct. Not cowering-in-a-corner fear -- more like a quiet voice saying "this could go wrong." |

### Diagnostic Questions
1. "Do you know the next step?" (If no → Bucket 1)
2. "If I told you exactly what to do, could you do it right now?" (If "I'm too tired" → Bucket 2)
3. "What happens in your body when you think about doing this thing?" (If tension, avoidance, excuse-making → Bucket 3)
4. **If Bucket 2:** "When was the last full day you didn't work at all -- no email, no Slack, nothing?" (If they can't remember → likely a Regulation problem. See `coach-business/references/sustainable-performance.md`.)

### Key Insight
"If information were the problem, we'd all be billionaires with six-pack abs." (Derek Sivers via Kegley). Most stuck is not an information problem. It's a fear problem wearing a procrastination costume.

## Goal Alignment Audit

### The Giraffe Statue Test (Kegley)
We carry goals around like furniture we never unpacked -- from apartment to apartment, year to year, without ever asking: "Do I even want this, or did I just lug it from a previous version of me?"

### The Empty VIP Tables (Manson)
A nightclub put reserved signs on empty VIP tables. People lined up to buy them. The tables had no value -- the scarcity and status were fabricated. Ask yourself: are your ambitions empty VIP tables? Things you chase because they look impressive, not because they matter to you?

### Mimetic Desire (Rene Girard via Manson)
Everything we want is ultimately a function of what the people around us want. We adopt the desires and ambitions of our environment. This cuts both ways:
- **Weaponized against you:** Social media making you want things you don't care about
- **Used deliberately:** Surround yourself with people who have the ambitions you actually want. You'll naturally start wanting what they want.

### The Audit Process
1. List your current goals
2. For each, ask: "Is this MY goal, or did I inherit it?"
   - From parents? ("You should be a lawyer")
   - From social media? ("You need to build a personal brand")
   - From a younger version of yourself? ("18-year-old me wanted fame")
   - From peers? ("Everyone in my circle is doing this")
3. Cross out anything that isn't authentically yours
4. Notice the lightness. That mental space is now free.

### The Should-to-Could Flip
- "I should work on my business" → "I could work on my business"
- "I should go to the gym" → "I could go to the gym"
- "I should post content" → "I could post content"

This flips obligations into choices. You're no longer failing at things you "should" do -- you're choosing what to do from a menu of options.

### The Superhuman Expectations Trap
Write out everything you think you "should" be doing. Look at the list. Ask: "Is this even possible for any human?" Then cross out everything that isn't important or wouldn't actually benefit you right now.

## The 91% Rule

### The Research (Gollwitzer, cited by Kegley)
Three groups of ambitious-but-stuck people who wanted to exercise:
- **Group 1 (Control):** Tracked exercise over 2 weeks. Result: 35% follow-through.
- **Group 2 (Motivation):** Got motivational talks about exercise benefits + tracked. Result: 35% follow-through.
- **Group 3 (Implementation Intentions):** Got motivational talks + wrote a plan of WHEN and WHERE they'd exercise. Result: 91% follow-through.

Motivation alone did nothing. Planning when and where did everything.

### Implementation Intentions Format
"I will [ACTION] at [TIME] in [PLACE]."

Examples:
- "I will write my newsletter at 7am at my desk."
- "I will record a video at 2pm in the home office."
- "I will review my business metrics at 9am Monday at the kitchen table."

### The GSD Note Card (Kegley)
A 3x5 note card with three sections for the week:
1. What you want to do
2. When you'll do each thing
3. Where you'll do each thing

The weeks with the card: things get done. The weeks without: they don't. No app needed. No notion second brain. Just a card.

## Chunking

### The Principle
If the starting line is the finish line, who would start? A goal that feels too big creates paralysis, not motivation.

### The Method
Break massive goals into sub-goals that feel achievable:
- It's not losing 50 lbs. It's losing 5 lbs, 10 times.
- It's not writing a best-selling book. It's writing the table of contents. Then the first paragraph.
- It's not launching a YouTube channel and getting 10K subs. It's posting one video with 3 good points.
- It's not building a $1M business. It's making your first $100 online.

### Sub-Goal Requirements
Each sub-goal must:
1. Feel achievable (not aspirational)
2. Have a clear completion point (you know when you're done)
3. Be the natural next step (not step 7 when you haven't done step 1)

### Connection to 91% Rule
For each sub-goal, create an implementation intention:
"I will [sub-goal] at [time] in [place]."

## The Edison Principle

### The Core Insight (Manson, citing Edwin Locke 1968)
Extremely ambitious goals usually backfire. Goals only increase motivation when you believe they are actually attainable. As soon as a person believes a goal is out of reach, motivation plummets and they become LESS likely to accomplish it than before.

### Edison's Trick
When Edison's team was demoralized trying to invent the light bulb, he lowered the ambition:
- Old goal: "Invent a long-lasting light bulb" (impossible-feeling)
- New goal: "Test one material at a time. Learn why it failed. Try another." (doable)

The team's mood transformed. What felt impossible became inevitable. Not one big win, but a thousand small wins accumulated gradually.

### The Small Wins Flywheel
1. Lower the ambition until it feels laughably doable
2. Do it. (This is easy because it's laughably doable.)
3. The completion creates momentum
4. Momentum creates motivation for the next small win
5. Small wins accumulate into something massive

### The Catch
Small wins require patience with failures and repetition. Doing the same thing every day without knowing if it's going anywhere. This is where Mission Fuel matters (see below).

### When to Apply
- Person is paralyzed by the size of their goal
- Person has a big vision but zero action
- Person keeps setting ambitious goals and abandoning them
- Energy and motivation are low

### When NOT to Apply
- Person is playing too small (use 10x thinking instead -- see `references/ambition-calibrator.md`)
- Person is taking consistent action (don't mess with what's working)
- Person needs direction, not reduced ambition

## Hugo's Jail Cell

### The Story (Williamson)
In 1830, Victor Hugo was catastrophically behind on The Hunchback of Notre-Dame. He was a spectacular procrastinator -- entertaining visitors, wandering Paris, finding excuses. His solution: he gathered all his normal clothes, gave them to his servant, locked them away, and kept only a massive wool shawl. Too embarrassed to leave his house dressed like a hermit, he confined himself indoors. He drafted furiously, sliding finished papers under the door. He finished the entire novel in the lockdown months.

### The Principle
You will be amazed at what you can complete when you have no other option. The modern world is the antithesis of this -- infinite options, infinite distractions.

### Environment Design for Focus
Remove options rather than trying to resist them:
- Delete social media apps during work blocks
- Use website blockers
- Work in a location where distractions aren't available
- Tell people you're unavailable
- Put your phone in another room
- Close all browser tabs except the one you need

### The Key Insight
Hugo didn't develop more discipline. He removed the need for discipline by designing his environment. Discipline is finite. Environment design is structural.

## Context Window Depth

### No Macro Multitasking (Williamson)
Switching between tasks has a huge cost. But the bigger problem is macro multitasking -- trying to pursue multiple major goals simultaneously.

### The Context Window Analogy
Like an AI's context window: the bigger the context window (the deeper you're immersed in one thing), the more connections you can make. George Mack writing his book: all he does is read, write, train, and sleep. The depth of immersion produces insights that scattered effort never could.

### The 90/180 Day Rule
You will make more progress in 6 months of dedicated focus on ONE thing than in 2 years of half-in, half-out effort on many things.

Commit to one major goal for 90-180 days. Allow it to consume you. Then switch.

### Applied
- "Find something you can get obsessed about. Let it climb inside you and wear you like a parasite."
- Don't try to balance everything simultaneously. Sequence your obsessions.
- 90 days of dedicated health > 2 years of "trying to be healthy"
- 90 days of dedicated business building > 2 years of side-hustling

## Procrastination as Identity Protection

### The Core Mechanism (Williamson)
Procrastination isn't a time management problem. It's a self-protection strategy wearing a Fitbit.

The logic: "If I try and fail, everyone will see. If I never try, the failure is private and deniable and safe."

### The Elegant Trap
1. You procrastinate because you don't want to look bad
2. This fear stops you from doing things
3. You're afraid of failure
4. But by procrastinating, you guarantee failure
5. You inoculate yourself from failure publicly by certifying your failure privately
6. You get to say: "Well, I could have done it if I'd actually tried"

This is an emotional insurance policy -- a psychological loophole that lets you stay intact while your dreams slowly starve.

### What's Really Going On
It's an identity problem. There's a version of you that:
- Doesn't want to look silly
- Doesn't want to be judged
- Doesn't want its self-worth damaged
- Doesn't want others to think less of it

This is a juvenile, immature version of you. It doesn't need as much protection as you think.

### Imposter Syndrome Connection
Every higher rung on the ladder gives you further to fall. "Oh my god, look at what my minimum level of output has to be now." If you don't face this inner protector, it follows you at every level and yelps every time you try to run at something.

### The Antidote
The antidote isn't motivation. Motivation comes and goes.

The antidote is **surrender**:
- Lower the stakes
- Let yourself look foolish
- Accept the embarrassment of being a beginner
- Accept the awkwardness of doing something badly
- Accept the exposure of your real effort being put on the line

Once you remove the need to look good, the need to start becomes easy.

### The Identity Shift
The hardest part isn't the work itself. It's the shift from someone who protects their image to someone who risks it.

"You don't need courage to begin. You just need the willingness to be seen beginning."

### The Final Point
Do you know what people think about the tasks you didn't go for? Nothing. They don't think anything because you didn't try. The thing you're afraid of (becoming irrelevant, people not caring) is what happens when you DON'T go for it.

## Accountability and Stakes

### The Writing Partner Model (Manson)
Manson was stuck on his second book. His friend was stuck on his. They made a pact:
- Write together weekly
- Discuss goals for the ensuing week
- Set timelines
- $1,000 penalty for skipping

Neither wanted to be the one who skipped. The fear of judgment and embarrassment kept them showing up.

### The Insight
Most of getting unstuck comes down to incentives. Make failing to do something far more embarrassing and painful than doing it poorly, and you'll naturally get off your ass.

### Accountability Escalation Ladder

| Level | Mechanism | Strength | Example |
|-------|-----------|----------|---------|
| 1 | **Self-tracking** | Low | Journal, habit tracker, note card |
| 2 | **Public declaration** | Medium | Tell someone your goal and deadline |
| 3 | **Accountability partner** | High | Weekly check-ins with someone who won't let you slide |
| 4 | **Community/group** | High | Join a group pursuing similar goals |
| 5 | **Financial stakes** | Very high | Put money at risk ($100-$1000 penalty for missing) |

### Design Principles
1. Start at Level 2 minimum (self-tracking alone rarely works)
2. Escalate when a level stops working
3. The stakes must be real and enforced (empty threats don't work)
4. Use social pressure deliberately -- pick an accountability partner whose opinion you value
5. The best accountability is mutual -- both parties have skin in the game

### Connection to Mimetic Desire
Your accountability partner/group becomes your environment. You'll naturally adopt their standards and ambitions. Choose carefully.

## Mission Fuel

### The Problem with Frivolous Motivation (Manson)
If you want to accomplish a goal for a shallow reason -- impress parents, attract attention, avoid being made fun of -- you'll eventually lack resilience. Shallow motivation breaks on contact with real setbacks.

### The Deeper Fuel
Human beings are meaning-seeking creatures. We want to feel that our lives mattered, that the world is a better place for us having been here. If you fuel your ambitions with a deeper sense of meaning and purpose, you make it bulletproof.

### Edison's Resilience
Edison and his team tried thousands of filaments. He didn't despair because he had Mission Fuel: each failure was data. "I have not failed. I've just found 10,000 ways that don't work." This reframe is only possible when you have a mission bigger than any single outcome.

### Finding Your Mission Fuel
1. "Why does this matter beyond money/status/approval?"
2. "Who benefits if you succeed? (beyond yourself)"
3. "What would you regret NOT doing, even if you failed?"
4. "If you knew you'd fail, but the attempt itself would teach others something valuable, would you still do it?"

### The Useful Failures Reframe
"When Edison wanted to change the world, he only created failures. But when he sought out useful failures, he eventually changed the world."

Don't dream of the glorious endpoint. Dream of the series of a thousand small failures along the way. Find the failures you're okay having -- the failures you actually WANT to have. Once you find those, you become unstoppable.

## Next Physical Action

### The Principle (Williamson)
When you're stuck, the problem is usually that the task is too abstract. "Write a book" is abstract. "Open a Word document" is physical. Break down to the literal next physical step.

### The Breakdown
"I need to write a book."
- Throw the covers off
- Get one leg out of bed
- Stand up
- Go to the bathroom
- Put pants on
- Go to the living room
- Get the laptop out
- Open it
- Open Word
- Type one sentence

Most people can take one more step. Nobody can run a marathon in a single leap.

### The Two Practical Blockers
1. **Don't know what to do:** Break it down to Next Physical Action
2. **Know what to do but don't know how:** Ask somebody -- Google, YouTube, AI, a friend, a coach. This is easy to solve.

### The Big Blocker (Behind Both)
The real question isn't "what" or "how." It's "why am I scared of even getting to that stage?" That's the identity protection problem (see Procrastination as Identity Protection above).

### Application
When coaching someone who's stuck:
1. "What's the task?"
2. "What's the very next physical thing you'd need to do?"
3. "Can you do that right now?"
4. If yes → do it. Momentum follows.
5. If no → diagnose: fear, energy, or knowledge gap?

## Elimination Creates Clarity (Leila Hormozi)

### Core Principle

> "Clarity = Speed = Momentum = Success"

Elimination creates clarity. Clarity compresses time. Speed creates momentum. Most people try to plan their way to clarity. Leila's approach: eliminate first, clarity follows.

### The Insight

> "Most hard goals aren't actually hard. They're crowded."

A 12-month project isn't 12 months hard. It's 12 months of scattered effort that could be 3 months of focused effort. The time estimate reflects the attention budget, not the actual difficulty.

> "We don't confuse time with difficulty."

### 3 Elimination Questions

1. **"If you could only focus on ONE thing for the next 30-60 days that would meaningfully move the business, your role, or your life forward, what would it be?"**
2. **"What are you currently doing out of habit or obligation that, if eliminated or paused, would dramatically increase your focus and speed?"**
3. **"What goal are you stretching into a year-long effort that could be achieved much faster with intense, concentrated focus?"**

### Connection to Existing Frameworks

- **Context Window Depth** (above): same principle at the execution level. Deep focus on one thing beats shallow attention on many.
- **Sledgehammer Room** (`coach-core/references/performance-equation.md`): Elimination Creates Clarity is the strategic version. Sledgehammer Room is the metaphor. Both say: pick one target.
- **80/20 Elimination** (`coach-personal/references/10x-framework.md`): 80/20 identifies what to keep. Elimination Creates Clarity focuses on what to cut. Same coin, different side.

### Coaching Application

Deploy when client says "this is going to take forever," presents a sprawling plan, or has too many competing priorities. Also deploy during quarterly planning or goal-setting sessions. Pair with the Mark Protocol (`performance-equation.md`) for the execution framework once they've eliminated.

## Keep Stop Start (Leila Hormozi)

### Quarterly Reflection Framework

Simple three-column reflection done quarterly (or whenever a reset is needed).

| Column | Question | Key Insight |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| **Keep** | What's actually working? | REPEAT successful actions. Don't optimize what's broken. Amplify what works. The big wins come from repeatedly doing the boring work. |
| **Stop** | What's not serving you anymore? | Most growth (personally and professionally) comes from stopping habits that keep us small, not starting new ones. |
| **Start** | Where could you add something that would move the needle? | Small changes compound. One new practice per quarter, not ten. A new habit today could pay off massively by quarter-end. |

### The Ownership Principle

> "No one's going to do this for you. It's on each of us to set ourselves up to be as successful as possible."

Take 15 minutes. Write it down. Share it with someone who can hold you accountable.

### Coaching Application

Deploy at quarterly reviews, end-of-year planning, after a big win or failure, or when client feels stuck and needs a reset. This is the simplest, fastest diagnostic for "what needs to change."

Pair with Gap/Gain (`coach-personal/references/gap-and-gain.md`) for the "Keep" column (backward measurement of what's working). Pair with Think Day (`coach-business/references/sustainable-performance.md`) for the space to do this exercise properly. Pair with Elimination Creates Clarity (above) for deeper work on the "Stop" column.
