# Cole's 7 Faulty Beliefs Framework (Low-Ticket Launchpad)

> Source: Nicolas Cole, Low-Ticket Launchpad course
> Module: 1 - All Your Faulty Beliefs
> Context: Framework for dismantling the 7 most common beliefs that prevent people from launching and selling digital products. Use in coaching sessions when clients are stuck in the Fear bucket, particularly around product launches, pricing, and sales.

## Core Premise

The single biggest bottleneck to monetizing digital products is NOT niche, pricing strategy, funnel knowledge, or marketing strategy. It is **faulty beliefs about money and selling digital products.**

People are stuck due to BELIEFS, not strategy, tactics, or skills. The cure is ACTION, not more information. All 7 beliefs below are variations of fear dressed up as logic.

---

## The 7 Faulty Beliefs

### Belief #1: "I'm not ready."

**Dismantling technique:** One question.

> "Define 'ready' for me. At what objective milestone will you be ready?"

Follow-up probes:
- Is it a certain number of followers? What's the exact number?
- Is it a certain number of email subscribers? What's the exact number?
- Is it when a post crosses a certain threshold of likes/views/comments? What threshold exactly?

**Why it works:** When forced to define an exact, objective, measurable moment of "readiness," they can't. Because:
- a) It's not real. They're afraid of taking action.
- b) Even if they pick an objective milestone, they'll kick the can again when they get there.

**The truth:** The goalpost never stops moving. You will never "be ready." You become ready the moment you decide you're ready -- which should be today.

**Coach-core mapping:** Fear bucket (60%). Classic avoidance disguised as prudence. The "define ready" question is a top-tier coaching tool -- add to questions library.

---

### Belief #2: "My audience isn't big enough."

**Dismantling technique:** Challenge the hidden assumptions.

This belief operates under three false assumptions:
1. "When my audience is bigger, I'll launch a better product." (Not true -- building products and building audiences are completely different skills.)
2. "When my audience is bigger, I'll make more money." (Not true -- you'd have made more money had you started monetizing earlier.)
3. "When my audience is bigger, I'll build more trust." (Not true -- having something for sale gives you MORE credibility. Helpful products build MORE trust, not less.)

**The truth:** Don't wait to monetize. If you focus on "audience growth," that's the metric you optimize for -- not money. Audience size gets bigger, but your bank account won't. Followers don't pay the bills.

**Data point:** You'd rather make $10K-$100K/mo with a few thousand followers/subscribers than have 100,000 email subscribers and be "afraid to pitch your list" making $0/mo.

**Key quote:** Many huge creators with massive audiences make this exact mistake. They optimize for reach instead of revenue.

**Coach-core mapping:** Fear bucket (60%). Disguised as strategy ("I need more audience first") but actually avoidance of selling. When a client says this, skip the audience-growth conversation entirely and ask: "What are you actually afraid will happen when you pitch your list?"

---

### Belief #3: "My niche is too small."

**Dismantling technique:** Logical contradiction + reverse question.

> "Do you think I, Nicolas Cole, can make money in your niche?"

The person instantly flip-flops:
- "Well yeah, I think YOU could."
- "Well yeah, because you have credibility / bigger audience / more money / a team."

**Why it works:** If they believe someone else could succeed in their niche, the niche isn't broken. They have a skill deficiency, not a niche problem.

**The truth:** If the niche exists, the industry exists. If the industry exists:
- It isn't "too small"
- It isn't "too saturated"
- Customers have money

You just haven't figured out what's valuable and urgent enough for them to feel excited to give their money to you.

**Example (from user context):** A butcher selling a $1,000 Shopify course to other butchers. If the niche exists, people in it have money.

**Coach-core mapping:** Fear bucket (60%), sometimes overlaps with Don't Know How bucket (10%). The "reverse question" technique works in any coaching context -- "Do you think someone more experienced could solve this problem in your situation?" If yes, it's a skill gap, not a situation gap.

---

### Belief #4: "I can't deliver a $350 product."

**Dismantling technique:** Reframe what you're selling.

> You aren't selling a product. You're sharing how you solved a painful problem, for yourself, with someone else -- in exchange for money.

**The reframe in 3 steps:**
1. Solve a problem for yourself.
2. Break "how you solved that problem" down into steps.
3. Give those steps to someone else who would benefit from solving a similar problem.

**Examples of "problems solved for yourself" that become products:**
- Automated a boring/monotonous task at work? That's a digital product.
- Created a more efficient workout routine? That's a digital product.
- Helped a client solve a specific struggle? That's a digital product.

**The V1 path:** Launch, then one of two things happens:
- a) Customers love it. Great, you built a great V1 on the first try.
- b) Customers have complaints. Also great. Say: "I appreciate you being a beta-user. Let me send you an off-boarding survey / let's jump on a quick Zoom. I want to hear everything that was confusing or not helpful. I'll make upgrades based on your feedback and give you lifetime free access to the improved version."

**The truth:** "Unhappy customers" are the minority AND they're a good thing -- they tell you what to improve. It's easy to make them happy: take their feedback, make improvements, help them for free, give lifetime access to upgrades.

**Coach-core mapping:** Fear bucket (60%). Imposter syndrome variant. The "I've solved this for myself" reframe works for any coaching client who feels unqualified. If they've solved the problem for themselves, they can deliver.

---

### Belief #5: "I'm not an expert."

**Dismantling technique:** The 6th-grader analogy.

When you were in 4th grade wondering about Middle School, who did you want to hear from?
- Your parents (experts who went through it 30 years ago)? No.
- Your teachers (experts who work in education)? No.
- Your best friend's older sister in 6th grade (just two steps ahead)? Yes.

You wanted someone relatable, close to your experience level, who just went through it recently.

**Specific examples:**
- Want to learn to swim? You don't need Michael Phelps. You need someone who knows how to not drown.
- Want to learn piano? You don't need a concert pianist. You need someone who knows a little more than you.

**The truth:** You don't need PhD-level expertise. Just be a few steps ahead. Cole himself wasn't "the world's greatest writer" when he launched Ship 30 for 30 -- he was a few steps ahead of his students.

**Coach-core mapping:** Fear bucket (60%). Identity-level fear ("I'm not enough"). Direct link to coach-core's identity diagnostic: "Is this a skill gap, a strategy gap, or an identity gap?" This is always an identity gap. The coaching move is to reframe expertise as proximity, not mastery.

---

### Belief #6: "Pitching will make me spammy."

**Dismantling technique:** Hard data.

> After running 19 cohorts of Ship 30 for 30 and generating millions in revenue: it takes, on average, upwards of **20 emails** to convert a free newsletter subscriber into a paying customer.

**The data:**
- Cole's business: $15,000,000+ in lifetime digital product sales
- 19 cohorts of Ship 30 for 30
- Average conversion requires 20+ emails
- More emails = more money. Less emails = less money. Years of data, millions in revenue confirm this.

**The truth:**
- Pitching your list once isn't sufficient
- Telling people "my product is live!" and assuming sales roll in forever with no extra effort is a mistake
- Not just beginners struggle with this -- huge creators with massive opportunity struggle too

**Key quote:** "The person who pitches their product 3x is going to sell 10x less than the person who pitches it 23x."

**Coach-core mapping:** Fear bucket (60%). Social fear variant -- "people will judge me / unsubscribe / think I'm a sellout." The 20-email data point is the single most useful weapon against this belief. Use it every time a client says they're being "too salesy." They're almost certainly not pitching enough.

---

### Belief #7: "It must be perfect to sell."

**Dismantling technique:** Show the iteration reality.

Cole's experience building Ship 30 for 30 and Premium Ghostwriting Academy:
- Overhauled the entire product every single cohort
- Re-wrote the entire curriculum multiple times
- Re-wrote all marketing sequences multiple times
- Re-wrote all social content and CTAs multiple times
- Re-wrote all live session presentation decks multiple times
- Millions of words written and re-written

**Two consequences of waiting for perfection:**
1. It never would have happened. They never would have launched. They would have given up.
2. They wouldn't have known what to improve. Every cohort revealed 10+ upgrades they could make.

**The truth:** There's no such thing as a perfect product. V1 won't be perfect. Neither will V2. Even today, Ship 30 for 30 has infinite possible improvements -- but it's great as-is and worth charging for. Products improve through iteration with real customers, not polish before launch. "Perfect" shouldn't be the goal.

**Coach-core mapping:** Fear bucket (60%). Perfectionism is procrastination in a lab coat. The coaching move: "Your V1 is supposed to be imperfect. That's not a bug, it's the design. Real customers teach you what to fix. Your imagination can't."

## Coaching Application

### Power Questions to Add to Questions Library

1. **"Define 'ready' for me. At what objective milestone will you be ready?"** -- Dismantles any "not ready" variant.
2. **"Do you think I could make money in your niche?"** -- Exposes the logical contradiction in "my niche is too small."
3. **"What problem did you solve for yourself?"** -- Reframes product creation from "inventing value" to "sharing a solution."
4. **"How many times did you pitch your product last month?"** -- If the answer is under 20, they're leaving money on the table.
5. **"What would your V1 look like if it only had to be good enough to learn from?"** -- Kills perfectionism by reframing the goal.

### When to Use This Framework

- Client is stuck on launching a product (any of the 7 beliefs)
- Client knows what to do but isn't doing it (Fear bucket diagnosis confirmed)
- Client is optimizing secondary things instead of selling (avoidance pattern)
- Client says "I need to learn more before I can..." (information addiction, not real gap)
- Client has been "about to launch" for weeks/months (goalpost moving)

### The Module 1 Exercise (Assign to Clients)

Cole's exercise for dismantling personal faulty beliefs:
1. Take 15-20 minutes and write out, verbatim, all the faulty beliefs swirling in your head.
2. Put that voice on paper. See it outside yourself.
3. Next to each faulty belief, write the reframe.

Example reframes:
- "You don't know your niche and can't start until you figure it out." --> "There's no such thing as the perfect niche. I just need to pick something and get going. I can always change later."
- "You haven't solved any valuable problems." --> "I know I've found solutions to valuable problems in my life, I'm just not aware of them yet."
- "You can't make millions on the Internet." --> "A lot of people way less capable than me have figured it out. There's no reason why I can't too."
