## Cognitive Reframing (CBT)

**When to use:** Client has a limiting belief that's blocking action. They "know" what to do but won't do it.

### The Process
1. **Identify the thought:** "What's the belief that's stopping you? Say it out loud."
   - Example: "I can't charge premium prices because I'm not experienced enough."

2. **Examine the evidence:**
   - "What evidence supports this belief?"
   - "What evidence contradicts it?"
   - "Would you say this to a friend in the same situation?"

3. **Find the cognitive distortion:**
   | Distortion | Pattern | Example |
   |-----------|---------|---------|
   | All-or-nothing | "If it's not perfect, it's worthless" | "If I can't get to $100K/mo, what's the point?" |
   | Catastrophizing | "The worst will definitely happen" | "If I raise prices, everyone will leave" |
   | Mind reading | "I know what they think" | "My audience thinks I'm not qualified" |
   | Fortune telling | "I know it will fail" | "No one will pay $500/mo for this" |
   | Filtering | Only seeing negatives | "Sure, revenue grew 40%, but churn is up" |
   | Should statements | Rigid rules about behavior | "I should be further along by now" |

4. **Reframe the belief:**
   - Replace the distorted thought with a balanced, evidence-based alternative
   - "I may not have 10 years of experience, but I have results for 5 clients, and they paid premium prices"

5. **Test the new belief:**
   - Assign one small action that tests the reframe
   - "This week, pitch your service at 50% higher. See what happens."

### Coaching Integration
Use CBT reframing whenever a client says:
- "I can't..."
- "It won't work because..."
- "People like me don't..."
- "I should be..."
- "Everyone will think..."

## Pre-Mortem Analysis

**When to use:** Client is paralyzed by fear of failure, or keeps planning without executing. Also useful for stress-testing plans.

### The Process
1. **Set the scene:** "Imagine it's 6 months from now. This plan failed completely. It's a disaster."
2. **Generate causes:** "What went wrong? List every possible reason for the failure."
3. **Categorize:**
   | Category | Example |
   |----------|---------|
   | Within control | "I didn't follow through on outreach" |
   | Partially controllable | "The market shifted" |
   | Outside control | "A competitor raised $50M" |
4. **Address controllables:** For each within-control cause, create a prevention plan.
5. **Accept uncontrollables:** "These risks exist. Can you move forward anyway?"
6. **Strengthen the plan:** Update the action plan with the preventive measures.

### Why It Works
- Transforms vague fear into specific, addressable risks
- Gives the client control over what they CAN influence
- Makes inaction the riskier choice (because now they see what happens if they do nothing)
- Functions as both a psychological tool AND a strategic planning tool

### Coaching Prompt
"Instead of asking 'What if it fails?' let's ask 'HOW would it fail?' Once we know the failure modes, we can prevent them."

## Identity Shift (Hardy)

**When to use:** Client is stuck because of WHO they believe they are, not what they know or what tools they have.

### Signs of an Identity Block
- "That's just not who I am"
- "I've never been good at [sales/leadership/delegation]"
- "People like me don't do that"
- Keeps reverting to old behaviors despite knowing better
- Self-sabotages when approaching a breakthrough

### The Process
1. **Surface the current identity:**
   - "How would you describe yourself as a business owner? In one sentence."
   - Listen for limiting identifiers: "I'm a solopreneur," "I'm not a salesperson," "I'm the creative type"

2. **Introduce the Future Self concept:**
   - "Who do you need to BECOME to reach your goal?"
   - "What does the version of you running a $500K business look like? How do they spend their time? What do they say no to?"

3. **Create the identity gap map:**
   | Current Identity | Future Identity | Gap |
   |-----------------|----------------|-----|
   | "I do everything myself" | "I lead a team" | Delegation, trust, systems |
   | "I'm not a salesperson" | "I enroll people in transformation" | Reframe selling as serving |
   | "I play small" | "I think 10x" | Eliminate 80%, raise standards |

4. **Design identity experiments:**
   - Small actions that the FUTURE self would take
   - "This week, act as if you're the CEO of a $500K company. What's the first thing you'd change?"
   - The action creates the identity (not the other way around)

5. **Reinforce with environment:**
   - "Who do you need to surround yourself with?"
   - "What in your physical environment reinforces your old identity?"
   - "What would your future self's workspace/schedule/circle look like?"

### Key Insight
Identity change follows this sequence: **Decision → Action → Evidence → Identity**
- First, decide who you're becoming
- Then, take one action that person would take
- Then, notice the evidence that you're becoming that person
- Then, the identity solidifies

## Comfort Zone Map

**When to use:** Client is playing it safe, avoiding growth actions, or stuck in "busy but not growing" patterns.

### The Three Zones

```
[COMFORT ZONE] → [GROWTH ZONE] → [PANIC ZONE]
  Known, safe        Challenging         Overwhelming
  No growth          Maximum learning    Shutdown
```

### The Exercise
1. **Map current activities into zones:**

   | Comfort Zone (I do easily) | Growth Zone (Challenges me) | Panic Zone (Paralyzes me) |
   |---------------------------|----------------------------|--------------------------|
   | Writing newsletter content | Pitching sponsors | Public speaking |
   | Running Meta ads | Raising prices 50% | Firing a team member |
   | Responding to emails | Cold outreach | Launching a $10K product |

2. **Identify the Growth Zone activities that align with the constraint:**
   - Not all discomfort is productive
   - Only Growth Zone activities that address the primary constraint matter

3. **Design graduated exposure:**
   - Break Panic Zone items into Growth Zone steps
   - "You're afraid of cold outreach. What's the smallest version that's still uncomfortable?"
   - "Send 5 cold DMs this week. Not 100. Just 5."

4. **Track and celebrate:**
   - Each Growth Zone action completed builds confidence for the next
   - Use Gap/Gain: measure backward from where they started

### Daily Fear Rewiring (Ongoing Practice)

The Comfort Zone Map above is a diagnostic. This turns it into a daily habit.

**The practice:** Do one Growth Zone action every day. Not reckless. Not Panic Zone. Growth Zone.

- Post the content you've been sitting on
- Send the pitch you've been drafting for a week
- Raise the price on the next proposal
- Say no to the obligation you've been tolerating
- Ask for the thing you want but have been hinting at

**Why it works:** Each action that contradicts the survival brain's threat scan weakens the automatic fear response. The brain learns: "I did the scary thing and survived. Recalibrating threat level." Daily repetition rewires more than occasional courage.

**Tracking:** Add a "Fear Action" line to the daily Three Wins practice:
1. Something you accomplished
2. Something you learned
3. Something you're grateful for
4. **Something that scared you that you did anyway**

If #4 is consistently empty, the client is living in the Comfort Zone. That's a coaching conversation.

**Review cadence:** Revisit the Comfort Zone Map monthly. Which Panic Zone items migrated to Growth Zone? Which Growth Zone items became Comfort Zone? That migration is the evidence of growth.

For the full framework on conviction, doubt, and the belief-action cycle, see `references/delusional-confidence.md`.

### Coaching Application
- When client avoids an action: "Is this in your Panic Zone or Growth Zone?"
- When client is too comfortable: "Nothing on your to-do list scares you. That's a problem."
- When client is overwhelmed: "You jumped straight to the Panic Zone. Let's back up to the Growth Zone."

## Accountability Escalation

**When to use:** When a client repeatedly fails to follow through on commitments. Five levels of increasing accountability pressure.

### The Five Levels

| Level | Mechanism | When to Use |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **1. Self-reporting** | Client reports their own progress | Default, start here |
| **2. Metric tracking** | Shared dashboard with real numbers | When self-reporting is vague or unreliable |
| **3. Commitment contracts** | Written commitment with specific consequences | When client repeatedly misses actions |
| **4. Public accountability** | Share goals with team, peers, or community | When private accountability isn't enough |
| **5. Skin in the game** | Financial stake, money at risk | Last resort. Client puts real money on the line |

### Level-Up Rules
- Start at Level 1 for all clients
- Move up one level after 2 consecutive sessions of missed commitments
- Moving up is not punishment: it's support. Frame it as: "You want this. Let's build the structure that makes it happen."
- Never skip levels (e.g., don't go from 1 to 5)

### Implementation Examples

**Level 2: Metric tracking:**
- Shared Google Sheet updated weekly
- Dashboard with actual numbers, not narratives
- "Don't tell me how it felt. Show me the numbers."

**Level 3: Commitment contracts:**
- "I, [name], commit to [specific action] by [date]. If I don't complete it, I will [consequence]."
- Consequences should be meaningful but not destructive (donate to a cause they dislike, do something uncomfortable)

**Level 4: Public accountability:**
- Announce the goal to their team
- Post progress updates in a peer group
- "Tell 3 people what you're going to do this week. Accountability is the product."

**Level 5: Skin in the game:**
- Put money in an escrow or with a friend
- If goals aren't met, the money goes to a cause they don't support
- Nuclear option: only for clients who specifically request maximum pressure

## Gap-to-Gain Flip

**When to use:** Client feels like a failure despite objective progress. Measuring against an ever-moving ideal instead of backward against their starting point.

### Signs of Gap Thinking
- "Yeah, revenue grew 40%, but I'm still nowhere near my goal"
- "Everyone else is further ahead than me"
- "I should be doing better by now"
- Chronic dissatisfaction despite real progress
- Moving goalposts: achieving a goal, then immediately setting a bigger one without celebrating

### The Flip Process
1. **Catch the Gap:** "You just said 'but.' What came before the 'but'?"
2. **Name the Gain:** "Let's look at where you were [time period] ago. What's different?"
3. **Make it concrete:**
   | Metric | Then | Now | Gain |
   |--------|------|-----|------|
   | Revenue | $ | $ | +$X (+Y%) |
   | Team | [size] | [size] | +[N] people |
   | Skills | [what they couldn't do] | [what they can do] | [specific capability] |
   | Confidence | [rating] | [rating] | +[points] |

4. **Reframe the ideal:** "The ideal isn't a measuring stick: it's a compass. It points you in a direction, but you measure distance traveled, not distance remaining."

5. **Install the Three Wins practice:**
   - Every day: 3 things accomplished, learned, or grateful for
   - Every week: 3 business wins
   - Every coaching session: Start with "What's the Gain since last time?"

### Coaching Integration
- Start every session in the Gain: "Before we dig into problems, what went RIGHT?"
- End every session in the Gain: "Looking back at today's conversation, what's the Gain?"
- When the client spirals into Gap thinking mid-session: "Hold on: you're in the Gap. Let's flip to the Gain."
- Use Gap/Gain as a framework alongside Throughput Accounting: measure progress with numbers, but interpret progress with the Gain mindset
