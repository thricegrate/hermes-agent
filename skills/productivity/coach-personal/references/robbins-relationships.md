# Robbins Relationships

Relationship coaching through the Polarity Model, 6 Human Needs applied to couples, and the State of the Union meeting format. Use when personal relationships surface as the block holding the client back.

## When to Use

- Client identifies relationship problems as the primary source of stress or stuckness
- Client's personal growth is being limited by relationship dynamics
- Client reports "passion died" or "we're more like roommates"
- Client and partner keep having the same fight
- Client is high-achieving but relationship is suffering
- Relationship stress is draining energy from everything else

---

## The Polarity Model

Robbins' synthesis of David Deida's work applied to mainstream coaching.

### The Core Principle

Long-term passion requires polarity -- opposing energies in attraction. Friendship lives in sameness. Passion lives in difference.

### Masculine Energy Characteristics

Directional, purpose-driven, focused, decisive, seeks freedom, provides structure and protection. Needs to feel appreciated for what it DOES and provides.

### Feminine Energy Characteristics

Open, flowing, relational, intuitive, seeks love and connection, radiates warmth. Needs to feel understood and appreciated for who she IS, not just what she does.

### These Are NOT Gender Roles

Either partner can carry either polarity. What matters for attraction is that the polarities are DIFFERENT. Two people in the same energy = friendship/companionship, not passion.

---

## Depolarization: Why Passion Dies

Both partners develop the same energy over time. Often happens when one partner suppresses their natural energy to handle responsibilities requiring the opposite.

**Common pattern:** A naturally feminine person who runs a business all day develops strong masculine energy at work and can't "switch off" at home. Their partner, also in masculine energy, experiences no polarity. Both feel something is "off" but can't identify it.

### Depolarization Diagnostic

- "Do you feel like roommates rather than lovers?"
- "Are you both in the same energy at home -- both in control mode, or both in caretaking mode?"
- "When was the last time you felt genuine attraction (not just affection)?"
- "Has one of you taken on a role that doesn't match who you naturally are?"

---

## The Coaching Intervention (Polarity Restoration)

1. **Help each partner identify their natural/core energy.** Not what they do at work -- who they are when they're most relaxed and alive.

2. **Give permission to consciously step into it at home.** High-achieving people often need explicit permission to be vulnerable, to receive, to let go of control in personal relationships.

3. **Create appreciation for the partner's polarity.** Not trying to change them -- appreciating the difference. Masculine appreciates feminine openness. Feminine appreciates masculine direction.

4. **Design transition rituals.** A specific practice for switching from "work energy" to "home energy." This can be as simple as 10 minutes of decompression, a physical change (clothes, environment), or a state-change ritual (see `robbins-state-management.md`).

## 6 Human Needs in Relationships

Each person has a needs hierarchy. Relationship problems frequently come from partners meeting their top needs in conflicting ways.

### The Critical Insight

Two people can both need love/connection but have completely different "rules" for what "counts."

- Partner A feels loved through quality time. Partner B shows love through acts of service.
- Partner A feels unheard ("you never sit and talk to me"). Partner B feels unappreciated ("I do everything around here and you don't notice").
- Both are trying. Both feel like they're failing. The needs are the same -- the rules are different.

### Needs Conflict Patterns

| Partner A's Top Need | Partner B's Top Need | Common Conflict |
|---------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Certainty | Variety | A wants routine, B wants spontaneity. Both feel suffocated or bored. |
| Significance | Connection | A needs recognition, B needs closeness. A's need to stand out clashes with B's need for togetherness. |
| Certainty | Growth | A wants stability, B wants change. A feels threatened by B's ambitions. |
| Significance | Significance | Both compete for who's right, who works harder, who sacrifices more. |

### The Reframe

Thriving relationships adopt the frame: "This relationship is a place I go to GIVE, not to get."

When both partners focus on meeting the other's needs (instead of demanding their own be met), both needs get met as a byproduct.

### Coaching Questions

- "How does your partner need to feel loved? How do YOU need to feel loved? Are those compatible?"
- "What's your partner's top need? Are you meeting it in the way THEY need it met, or in the way YOU would want it met?"
- "What does your partner need to feel appreciated? When was the last time you gave them that?"

## State of the Union Meeting

A weekly structured check-in that prevents small resentments from compounding.

### Format (15-20 minutes, same time each week)

**1. Appreciation (Start Here -- Non-Negotiable)**
Each partner shares 3 specific things they appreciate about the other from the past week.

"I appreciate that you handled the kids' schedule so I could focus on my project."
"I appreciate that you checked in on me when I was stressed on Tuesday."
"I appreciate that you made us dinner on Thursday even though you were tired."

Starting with appreciation creates emotional safety before addressing friction.

**2. What I'd Love More Of**
Each partner shares one thing they'd like more of. Framed as a positive request, not a complaint.

Not: "You never spend time with me."
Instead: "I'd love more one-on-one time this week. Even 30 minutes would mean a lot."

**3. Is There Anything Unresolved Between Us?**
Open the floor for anything lingering. The rule: listen fully before responding. No defending, no counter-attacking.

If nothing is unresolved: "We're clean. That feels good."
If something surfaces: address it with curiosity, not judgment.

### Rules for the Meeting

- No phones
- No interrupting
- No "but" after appreciation ("I appreciate you doing the dishes, BUT you left the pan")
- If it gets heated, take a 10-minute break and come back
- End with physical connection (hug, hand-hold, kiss)

## Identity Work in Relationships

"It's not about the goal. It's about growing to become the person that can accomplish that goal." This applies to relationships too.

### Common Identity Blocks in Relationships

- "I'm not the romantic type" (permission to be vulnerable)
- "I'm the strong one, I can't show weakness" (armor that kills intimacy)
- "I don't need anyone" (independence as a defense mechanism)
- "I'm bad at communication" (learned helplessness in relationships)

### The Coaching Move

Surface the identity block, then apply the same tools:

1. Is this identity serving the relationship or protecting you from vulnerability?
2. Who does your future self need to BE in this relationship?
3. What's one small action that future-self-in-relationship would take this week?

## Coaching Application

### When to Route Here

- Client mentions relationship stress as a recurring theme
- Client's personal growth is visibly constrained by relationship dynamics
- Client reports energy drain from home life affecting everything else
- Client says "things are fine" but body language and energy suggest otherwise

### Session Flow

1. Assess: Is this a polarity issue, a needs-conflict issue, or a communication issue?
2. If polarity: depolarization diagnosis + restoration intervention
3. If needs conflict: map each partner's top needs + rules + vehicles
4. If communication: install State of the Union meeting format
5. Often it's all three. Start with needs (most diagnostic), then polarity (most impactful on attraction), then communication (most actionable week-to-week).

### Boundaries

- This is coaching, not therapy. If there's abuse, addiction, or clinical issues, refer to a professional.
- Relationship coaching works best when both partners are willing. If only one is in the room, focus on what they can control (their own state, energy, actions).
- Don't take sides. The goal is understanding the system, not assigning blame.
