## Hook Anatomy

Every hook has two parts. Miss either one and the hook fails.

**Part 1: Call Out**
The call out cuts through noise and makes the target say "this is for me." This is the Cocktail Party Effect: even in a noisy room, when someone says your name, you hear it. Your hook has to be that signal for your prospect. It identifies WHO should pay attention.

**Part 2: Condition for Value**
The condition for value promises benefit if they keep consuming. It answers: "Why should I keep watching/reading?" It can be explicit or implicit.

**Explicit formula:**
```
If [TARGET] [DO THIS], [THEY WILL GET VALUE]
```

**Implicit formula:**
Show, don't tell. The visual or context implies value without stating it directly.

**How they combine:**
- Call Out (explicit) + Condition for Value (explicit): "Chiropractors, watch this if you want more patients without paid ads"
- Call Out (implicit) + Condition for Value (explicit): Person in a chiropractic office says "11 ways to get more patients without paid ads"
- Call Out (explicit) + Condition for Value (implicit): "Chiropractors..." then shows a packed waiting room
- Call Out (implicit) + Condition for Value (implicit): Video of a packed waiting room with overlay text about patient volume

**Key principle:** The cocktail party effect means even one relevant signal catches attention in noise. Your call out IS that signal.

## Seven Verbal Hook Types

All verbal hooks fall into these seven categories. Each works in any niche by swapping the specifics. Templates shown with a generic example AND a niche-specific example (chiropractor, from the Hooks Playbook checklist).

### 1. Labels

Call out the avatar directly. Name them.

**Template:** `[Avatar], I have a [thing] for you`

**Examples:**
- "Local business owners, I have a gift for you"
- "Chiropractors, I have a gift for you"
- "Newsletter operators, I need to tell you something"

**Why it works:** Labels trigger identity. When someone hears their label, they self-select in. It is the most direct form of call out.

### 2. Questions

Two sub-types: Yes Questions (closed) and Open Questions.

**Yes Question Template:** `Would you [pay/do X] to [get result]?`

**Yes Question Examples:**
- "Would you pay $1,000 to have the business of your dreams in 30 days?"
- "Would you pay $500 to never worry about patient flow again?"

**Open Question Template:** `Which would you rather [option A or B]?`

**Open Question Examples:**
- "Which would you rather be? The guy pushing the boulder or the one at the top?"
- "Which would you rather have? A packed schedule or an empty waiting room?"

**Why it works:** Questions force mental engagement. The viewer has to process the question, which pulls them in. Yes questions presuppose the answer. Open questions create a gap they want closed.

### 3. Conditionals

If-then structure. The condition qualifies the audience. The implication delivers the hook.

**Template:** `If you're [condition], [implication]`

**Examples:**
- "If you're working all the time and your business isn't growing, you're working on the wrong stuff"
- "If you're a chiropractor struggling to fill your schedule, this will change everything"

**Why it works:** The conditional lets the prospect self-qualify. If the condition matches their situation, the implication feels personally directed.

### 4. Commands

Direct instruction. Tells them exactly what to do.

**Template:** `[Action] if you [condition/desire]`

**Examples:**
- "Read this if you're tired of being broke"
- "Watch this if you want more patients"
- "Stop scrolling if you run a business"

**Why it works:** Commands create urgency and authority. The viewer either obeys or consciously decides to disobey, which still costs attention.

### 5. Statements

Declarative. Makes a bold claim using superlatives.

**Template:** `The [superlative] thing you can do [timeframe]`

**Examples:**
- "The smartest thing you can do today"
- "The top 1% of chiropractor clinics follow these rules"
- "The single biggest mistake newsletter operators make"

**Why it works:** Superlatives create curiosity gaps. "The smartest" implies there is one best thing, and the viewer doesn't know it yet.

### 6. Lists / Steps

Numbered format. Promise a specific, countable set of insights.

**Template:** `[Number] ways to [achieve/avoid result]`

**Examples:**
- "28 ways to stay poor"
- "11 ways chiropractors get more patients without paid ads"
- "5 things killing your newsletter open rate"

**Why it works:** Numbers create perceived structure and completeness. Odd or unusual numbers (28, 11) outperform round numbers. Negative framing ("ways to stay poor") often outperforms positive.

### 7. Narratives

Scene-setting openings. Drop the viewer into a story mid-action.

**Template:** Start in the middle of the action. No preamble.

**Examples:**
- "One day I was in the back and this old lady comes in and she was piss angry"
- "All of a sudden, my phone starts ringing off the hook"
- "So I'm sitting in this meeting and the CEO looks at me and says..."

**Why it works:** Narrative hooks hijack the brain's story-processing system. Once a story starts, humans feel compelled to hear the end.

### 8. Exclamations / Provocative Statements

Raw emotion plus a big promise. Breaks pattern with intensity.

**Template:** `[Strong emotion + big promise]`

**Examples:**
- "Ahhhhh... This is the blueprint to becoming a millionaire"
- "OH MY GOD. I just found the hack that changes everything"
- "This is INSANE. No one is talking about this"

**Why it works:** Emotional intensity is a pattern interrupt. The viewer feels the energy before they process the words.

## Hook Testing 70-20-10

Hormozi applies Google's 70-20-10 Innovation Rule specifically to hook creation and testing. The core insight: the hook dictates the content, not the other way around. Once you have a hook dataset, you never run out of content ideas.

### 70% Core (Proven Hooks)

"More of what works." Use hooks already proven in your own content or other people's content. This bucket stabilizes all advertising.

**Where to find core hooks:**
1. Your own prior best-performing ads
2. Your own top-performing organic content
3. Other people's top ads (use platform ad libraries)
4. Other people's best organic content
5. Saved favorite ads from any niche

**Key action:** Winning hooks in one industry often work in others. Cross-pollination is explicitly encouraged.

### 20% Emerging (Winner Adjacent)

Model concepts from other niches. Use outlier detection to find high-performing hooks and adapt them to your space.

**How to find emerging hooks:**
- Monitor other niches' content for sudden spikes
- Use outlier detection tools (VidIQ, Social Blade, ad libraries)
- When you see a hook format crushing in another niche, adapt the structure with your audience's language

### 10% Big New Ideas (Experimental)

Risky. Totally new hooks that have never been tested. If they lose, document the loss to avoid repeating the mistake. If they win, they graduate into the 70% Core bucket.

**Rules for the 10% bucket:**
- Accept that most will fail
- Document every loss (what the hook was, why it likely failed)
- Document every win (what made it work)
- Winners move immediately into the 70% Core bucket

### Testing Ratios

- **Ads:** 10 hooks per 1 ad creative. Mix and match with meats and CTAs.
- **Long content (YouTube, podcasts):** 3-4 hooks recorded per piece. Test different intros.
- **Email:** Active split tests on every campaign. Never send without A/B on subject line.

### The Hook Spreadsheet

Maintain a spreadsheet with tabs per platform. Columns:

```
| Name/Topic | Hook Text | Views/Opens | Link | Date | Notes |
```

Review this spreadsheet before every content session. "Let the data do the teaching." Even with zero experience, your top 10% content tells you what works.

## Awareness Pyramid for Hooks

Adapted from Eugene Schwartz's 5 levels of awareness. Each awareness level maps to a different hook type. The broader (colder) you go, the better your hooks must be. This is the framework for writing "expansion hooks" that reach new market segments.

### Level 1: Most Aware (Bottom, Smallest Audience)

They know you, know your product, and are ready to buy. They just need the push.

**Hook type:** Offer-driven hooks

**Example:** "50% off our best-selling XYZ"

**Characteristics:** Direct, transactional, assumes familiarity. Works on existing customers and warm subscribers.

### Level 2: Product-Aware

They know your product exists but haven't bought yet. They need proof it works.

**Hook type:** Proof-driven hooks

**Example:** "Why 10,000 people chose XYZ"

**Characteristics:** Social proof, case studies, numbers. Removes doubt by showing others already trust you.

### Level 3: Solution-Aware

They know solutions exist to their problem but don't know your specific product. They need a promise.

**Hook type:** Promise-driven hooks

**Example:** "The fastest way to [result]"

**Characteristics:** Leads with outcome. Positions your solution as the best path to something they already want.

### Level 4: Problem-Aware

They feel the pain but don't know solutions exist. They need empathy first, then a bridge to the solution.

**Hook type:** Pain-driven hooks

**Example:** "Tired of [problem]? There's a better way"

**Characteristics:** Validates their experience. Acknowledges the pain before introducing anything else.

### Level 5: Completely Unaware (Top, Largest Audience)

They don't even know they have a problem. They need curiosity to pull them in.

**Hook type:** Curiosity-driven hooks

**Example:** "The hidden danger in your daily routine"

**Characteristics:** No product mention, no solution mention, no problem mention. Pure intrigue. This is where the widest-reaching hooks live, but conversion is the longest path.

### Scaling Principle

Most businesses only run Level 1-2 hooks (offer and proof). They exhaust their warm audience and think they've "saturated the platform." They haven't. They've saturated their hook quality. Moving UP the pyramid (to Level 3, 4, 5) unlocks entirely new audience segments at the cost of needing better hooks.

## Hook Creation Checklist

Two phases. The first time bootstraps your hook bank from scratch. Every time after, the system is self-sustaining.

### First Time (Building Your Hook Bank)

1. **Look at top-performing content** from other people in your niche and adjacent niches
2. **Isolate the hooks** from that content. Strip out the body. Just grab the first 3-5 seconds or the subject line.
3. **Write down your favorite 50.** Not 10. Not 20. Fifty.
4. **Use all of them** across ads and content. Don't try to pick winners before testing. Let the data pick.

### Every Time After (Iterating on Data)

1. **Review your top 10% performers** (top 5 hooks from your last batch)
2. **Make 70% of your next batch** from those top 5. Reword, reframe, apply to different topics. Same structure, different specifics.
3. **Make 20% permutations** of those hooks. Change visuals, change words, change format (text to video, video to image). Same core hook, different execution.
4. **Make 10% completely new hooks.** Go back to the "first time" process. Find fresh hooks from other niches, other creators, other platforms.

### What Makes a Good Hook

Every good hook contains two things:
- **Some way to catch attention** (the call out)
- **Some implication of value if they consume it** (the condition for value)

If your hook has both, it works. If it's missing either one, it won't.

### Time Allocation

Hooks get 80% of your prep time. Meats get 20%. CTAs get almost zero (they're formulaic). This ratio comes directly from the Goated Ads Playbook and reflects the outsized impact hooks have on performance.

## Nonverbal Hook Types

Not all hooks are words. The best-performing content often combines a verbal hook with a nonverbal one.

### Visual Pattern Interrupts

Break the expected visual pattern. Anything that makes the thumb stop scrolling.

**Examples:**
- Catching a banana mid-sentence
- Unusual settings (recording in a warehouse, on a rooftop, in a bathtub)
- Unexpected wardrobe (suit in a gym, gym clothes in a boardroom)
- Motion (walking toward camera, sudden turn)
- Close-up that pulls back to reveal context

### Sound-Based Hooks

Audio that breaks pattern before the first word is spoken.

**Examples:**
- Clink of a glass
- Dropped tray crashing
- Door slam
- Record scratch
- Silence (2-3 seconds of nothing in a feed full of noise)

### Meme-Based Hooks

Culture-specific memes that act as call outs for your target audience. Hormozi describes these as "moth to a flame" for the right viewer.

**Key principle:** The widest possible hooks are meme or meme-like content that attract the largest audience percentage. Meme-based hooks sacrifice specificity for reach. Best used for Level 5 (Completely Unaware) audiences.

**Application:** Pair a meme-based nonverbal hook with a strong verbal call out to get both reach AND targeting.

## Hook by Awareness Level (Expansion Hooks)

This framework connects the Awareness Pyramid to practical hook writing. Use it when you've exhausted your current audience and need to expand to colder segments.

### How Expansion Works

Most businesses only write hooks for Level 1-2 (Most Aware and Product-Aware). They reach their warm audience and think the platform is "saturated." It isn't. They've saturated their hook quality. Expansion hooks move up the pyramid to reach new segments.

### Expansion Hook Framework by Level

**Level 1 -- Most Aware (Offer-Driven)**
- "50% off our best-selling XYZ"
- "Last chance to get [product] at this price"
- "We just added [feature] to [product]"

**Level 2 -- Product-Aware (Proof-Driven)**
- "Why 10,000 people chose XYZ"
- "[Name] used [product] and got [result] in [time]"
- "See what [customer type] are saying about [product]"

**Level 3 -- Solution-Aware (Promise-Driven)**
- "The fastest way to [result]"
- "Get [result] without [common pain/effort]"
- "What if you could [dream outcome] in [timeframe]?"

**Level 4 -- Problem-Aware (Pain-Driven)**
- "Tired of [problem]? There's a better way"
- "Still struggling with [pain]?"
- "[Problem] is costing you more than you think"

**Level 5 -- Completely Unaware (Curiosity-Driven)**
- "The hidden danger in your daily routine"
- "Why everything you've been told about [topic] is wrong"
- "This changes everything"

### The Scaling Sequence

Start at Level 1. When performance plateaus, write hooks for Level 2. When that plateaus, Level 3. And so on. Each level unlocks a bigger audience but requires a longer conversion path. The hook spreadsheet tells you when you've exhausted a level.
