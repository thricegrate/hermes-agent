# Desire-Based Hook Templates

5 proven templates for writing video hooks that convert views into leads and sales. Each template is a different way to combine a **dream outcome** with a **relatable character** under **constraint-free conditions**.

---

## 1. About Me

**Character:** Creator (I)
**Direction:** Looking backward at an achieved result
**Mad-Lib:** "I just [accomplished X dream outcome] using [Y relatable method]"

### Psychology

The creator shares a result they've already achieved. The viewer locks onto the dream outcome, then assesses whether the creator is relatable enough that the result feels achievable for them too. The backward-looking frame ("I already did this") is powerful because it's proof, not theory.

### Constraint-Free Amplifiers

Add a second sentence that removes barriers:
- "And I didn't need [common perceived requirement]."
- "The crazy part? I only spent [surprisingly low effort/cost]."
- "No [common obstacle]. No [common excuse]. Just [simple method]."

### Proven Examples

**Example 1: Social media growth niche:**
> "I took a break from posting for almost a month and still grew by 11,000 followers. And this is why."

- **Dream outcome:** Growing your following without doing any work
- **Constraint-free framing:** "By literally doing nothing": conditions are zero
- **Result:** 2,000 leads from one video
- Creator (Sammy) appears relatable: normal setting, no intimidating credentials

**Example 2: Content monetization niche:**
> "I took my Instagram story views from this to this, and it took my sales from this to this. I don't care what niche you're in. If you use this one story strategy, I promise that you will increase your views and your sales."

- **Dream outcome:** Growing views AND sales simultaneously
- **Constraint-free framing:** "One story strategy" + "I don't care what niche you're in": works for anyone
- **Result:** 1,600 leads from one video
- Creator (Cassie) appears relatable: someone you can see yourself in

### When to Use

Best when the creator has a specific, impressive result they personally achieved. The result should be concrete and measurable (numbers, timeframes, before/after).

## 2. If I

**Character:** Creator (I)
**Direction:** Looking forward at a hypothetical scenario
**Mad-Lib:** "If I wanted to [achieve X outcome], I would [use Y method]"

### Psychology

The creator frames a hypothetical: "If I had to do this again from scratch..." This is powerful because it implies expertise (they've done it before) while making the method feel accessible (they're distilling it down to what actually matters). The forward-looking frame removes the "but you already had advantages" objection, they're saying what they'd do starting from nothing.

### Constraint-Free Amplifiers

- "Not by doing anything crazy, but by doing this one thing from day one."
- "Here's exactly what I would do: in [number] simple steps."
- "And I wouldn't need [common perceived requirement] to do it."

### Proven Examples

**Example 1: Social media growth niche:**
> "Pretty confident that I could rebuild this account back to where it is right now in less than 6 months. Not by doing anything crazy, but by doing this one thing from day one."

- **Dream outcome:** Building 175K followers in 6 months
- **Constraint-free framing:** "Not by doing anything crazy, but by doing this one thing": single, simple action
- **Result:** 1,000 leads to his workshop from one video
- Creator (Sammy): normal house background, chill demeanor, nothing intimidating

**Example 2: Content monetization niche:**
> "If I were starting from scratch, here's exactly what I would do to turn my content into cash flow in three simple steps."

- **Dream outcome:** Turning content into cash flow (even starting from zero)
- **Constraint-free framing:** "Starting from scratch" + "three simple steps": anyone can do this
- **Result:** 3,300 leads from one 45-second video
- Creator (Cassie): high-quality but relatable content, polished yet approachable

### When to Use

Best for educational or mentorship-style content. Works especially well when the creator has significant results but wants to frame the advice as accessible to beginners. The "starting from scratch" framing preemptively handles the "easy for you to say" objection.

## 3. To You

**Character:** Viewer (you)
**Direction:** Direct statement
**Mad-Lib:** "If you're trying to [X outcome], [use Y relatable method]"

### Psychology

By making the viewer the character ("you"), the creator removes themselves from the relatability equation entirely. The viewer doesn't need to assess whether the creator is similar to them, they ARE the character. This makes "To You" hooks the most universally effective template because relatability is guaranteed.

### Constraint-Free Amplifiers

- "This one simple trick will change everything."
- "And you don't need [common barrier] to do it."
- "It works whether you're [beginner state] or [advanced state]."

### Proven Examples

**Example 1: AI/productivity niche:**
> "If you want to get better at using AI, this one simple trick will make your life so much easier."

- **Dream outcome:** Getting better at using AI (broad, aspirational)
- **Constraint-free framing:** "One simple trick": minimal effort required
- **Result:** 21,000 leads from one video (42,000 comments, half converted)
- Character is "you": every viewer with this desire is automatically the character

### When to Use

Best for broad audiences where the creator's personal story isn't the draw. Works especially well when the dream outcome is something a wide range of people want (productivity, money, health, skills). The highest-scale template because it doesn't depend on creator-specific relatability.

## 4. Can You

**Character:** Viewer (you)
**Direction:** Hypothetical question
**Mad-Lib:** "Is it possible for you to [achieve X outcome] under [Y relatable conditions]?"

### Psychology

The question format activates curiosity differently than a statement. Instead of being told something is possible, the viewer is invited to wonder, and then needs to watch to find out. The word "possible" lowers the bar (not "will you" but "could you") which makes the dream outcome feel more accessible. The conditions frame should make the scenario feel achievable.

### Constraint-Free Amplifiers

The conditions themselves serve as the constraint-free framing:
- "...in just one day?"
- "...with no experience?"
- "...starting with nothing?"
- "...using only your phone?"

### Proven Examples

**Example 1: Entrepreneurship niche:**
> "Is it possible to start a business in one day and make $1,000 from it?"

- **Dream outcome:** Starting a business that makes $1,000
- **Constraint-free framing:** "In one day": extreme simplicity of timeline
- **Result:** 5,000-6,000 leads per video; creator (Minnow Lee) ran this format 8 times for 24 million total views
- Creator appears as a regular person waking up in bed: maximally relatable

### Repeatable Format

This template is uniquely repeatable. The same creator can run the same format multiple times with different specific challenges and it works every time. The question frame makes each new variation feel fresh even though the structure is identical.

### When to Use

Best for challenge-style content, documentary-style videos, or "day in the life" formats. Works extremely well when the creator can show the process of attempting the outcome in real time. The question format creates a narrative arc (can they do it?) that keeps viewers watching.

## 5. He/She Just Did

**Character:** Third party (he/she/they)
**Direction:** Case study
**Mad-Lib:** "[Z character] just [accomplished X dream outcome] under [Y relatable conditions]"

### Psychology

A third-party character lets the creator leverage someone else's story for relatability. This is powerful because: (1) the creator can choose the most relatable character for their audience, (2) the third party's conditions can be specifically framed to match the viewer's situation, and (3) it adds social proof, this isn't just the creator's method, others have used it too.

### Constraint-Free Amplifiers

Frame the third party's starting conditions to match the viewer:
- "...and she started with zero followers."
- "...with just his iPhone."
- "...as a broke college student."
- "...using the exact same strategy every time."

### Proven Examples

**Example 1: Creator economy niche:**
> "This creator, Lucas, just sold 10 million worth of products with his iPhone. Let me explain."

- **Dream outcome:** $10 million in product sales
- **Constraint-free framing:** "With his iPhone" + "broke college student": no special resources needed
- **Result:** High lead potential (no lead magnet was attached, but the format is proven)
- Third party (Lucas) is framed as going from broke college student to success: maximally relatable

**Example 2: Content strategy niche:**
> "This creator posted the same hook nine times and went viral every single time."

- **Dream outcome:** Going viral at will, consistently
- **Constraint-free framing:** "The same hook nine times": implies a simple, repeatable system; the nine videos shown look low-effort
- **Result:** 2,000 leads from one video
- Third party is unnamed but shown through their low-effort looking videos: relatable

### When to Use

Best for case studies, client success stories, or when featuring other creators/entrepreneurs. Works especially well when the creator's own results might seem unrelatable (too successful, too different) but they can find a third party who matches their audience better. Also great for adding variety to a content calendar, the creator doesn't always have to be the subject.

## Cross-Template Patterns

### What All 5 Templates Share

1. **Dream outcome in the first sentence**: Never bury the lead
2. **Relatable character**: Whether it's I, you, or they: the viewer must see themselves
3. **Constraint-free method**: "Simple," "one thing," "no experience needed"
4. **Knowledge gap**: The method is teased but not revealed in the hook
5. **1-2 sentences max**: The hook is 3-8 seconds of video

### Choosing Between Templates

| Your situation | Best template |
|---------------|---------------|
| You have impressive personal results | **About Me** |
| You're teaching what you'd do from zero | **If I** |
| You want maximum reach across audiences | **To You** |
| You're doing challenge or process content | **Can You** |
| You're featuring someone else's story | **He/She Just Did** |
| You've run out of personal stories | **He/She Just Did** or **To You** |
| Your audience doesn't know you yet | **To You** or **Can You** |

### The Double-Down Technique

Every top-performing example in this framework uses a two-part structure:

1. **Core hook**: Dream outcome + character (sentence 1)
2. **Constraint-free amplifier**: Removes barriers (sentence 2)

The amplifier serves two purposes:
- If the viewer relates to the character → it reinforces that they can achieve the outcome
- If the viewer doesn't fully relate to the character → it makes the method seem so easy that relatability doesn't matter

### Lead Generation Connection

After the hook, the rest of the video explains the method. The CTA at the end should offer the complete playbook/system/tool in exchange for an action (comment a word, click a link, join a community). The hook pre-qualifies viewers as interested buyers, so conversion rates from hook → lead are significantly higher than other hook formats.
