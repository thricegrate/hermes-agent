# Anti-AI Script Tells

The patterns AI defaults to when writing scripts for spoken delivery. The `humanizer` skill covers prose-level AI tells (writing patterns that look written when read silently). This file covers script-specific tells (writing patterns that sound wrong when spoken aloud).

## The test that matters

Read the script out loud. If it sounds like a transcript of a real person talking, it is working. If it sounds like prose written to be read silently, it is not a script. It is an essay in disguise.

Every line in a script was spoken before it was written. That is the rule.

## Hard bans for scripts

These patterns kill a script in the first 5 seconds. Kill them every time. No exceptions.

### 1. Em dashes are banned. Completely.

Same rule as voice and prose. A creator reading a script does not pause for an em dash. Replace with a comma, a period, or a real spoken pause.

### 2. "It's not X. It's Y." and all variants are banned in scripts.

This is the single most common AI tell in ad scripts. Every UGC creator who sees one in their script knows immediately that a human did not write it.

Includes every variant:
- "It's not a product, it's a..."
- "You're not doing X, you're doing Y"
- "This isn't about X. It's about Y."
- "We don't sell X. We sell Y."

Just say what the thing is, directly.

### 3. "Most guys / most women / most people" script openers are banned.

A script that opens with a demographic generalization reads like an infomercial. The hook needs to be a specific moment, not a category statement.

A scene with a person, a place, and a time will always beat a sentence about a demographic.

### 4. The two-fragment contrast is banned.

Matched short fragments separated by a period. The shape itself is a copywriting tic.

Example of banned:
> "It worked. But not the way I expected."
> "He listened. Then he changed everything."
> "She said yes. He said no."

Real people do not speak in symmetric fragments. Write the full sentence the way a person would actually say it.

### 5. The three-word mic drop is banned.

Stacked three-word commands or alliterative triplet closers.

Examples of banned:
> "Stop. Think. Act."
> "Fast. Easy. Free."
> "Move on. Stay sharp. Win big."

This is the loudest possible signal that a copywriter or AI wrote the script. Real people do not end thoughts in matched three-word phrases.

## The 10 script-specific AI tells

These show up specifically when AI writes for spoken delivery.

### Tell 1: The voiceover cadence

AI defaults to a documentary-narrator rhythm. Measured. Even. Slightly portentous. Lines of similar length stacked together. No rushing, no stumbling, no real-person texture.

If every sentence in the script is the same length, rewrite for rhythm variation.

**Fix:** Mix line lengths. A 4-word fragment between two 12-word lines breaks the cadence and reads as spoken. Real conversation has rhythm variation.

### Tell 2: The therapist reframe

AI loves to insert a "what was really happening was..." moment in the middle of a script. It feels insightful but reads as written-not-spoken every time.

Real people do not narrate their own subtext. They tell what happened. The viewer infers what was really going on.

**Fix:** Cut every "what was really happening" or "what I didn't realize was" insert. Just tell the next thing that happened.

### Tell 3: The stage-direction adjective

AI scripts are full of words a real person would not say out loud. Words like "transformative," "profound," "remarkable," "extraordinary."

These are screen-reading words, not speaking words. If the writer would not use the word in casual conversation, the creator can not say it on camera.

**Fix:** Replace every screen-reading word with the conversational equivalent. "Transformative" becomes "different." "Profound" becomes "weird." "Remarkable" becomes "wild."

### Tell 4: The symptom list that ends in a crescendo

AI scripts list symptoms in order of escalating severity, then land on the worst one for impact. It is a copywriting exercise.

Real people do not list their problems in dramatic order. They mention the one that is most on their mind, then maybe one or two others as they think of them.

**Fix:** Cut the escalating symptom list. Pick ONE specific symptom. Anchor the script in that one. If multiple symptoms matter, mention them in non-dramatic order.

### Tell 5: The fake specificity trap

AI generates "specific" details that are too round, too clean, or too narratively convenient. Real specificity is messy.

Round numbers, clean timelines, and convenient totals all read as fabricated.

**Fix:** Mess up the numbers. "I lost 20 pounds in 8 weeks" reads as too clean. "I lost 23 pounds in about 11 weeks" reads as real. Same goes for timelines: "Three months later" is too clean; "around the end of February" is real.

### Tell 6: The mechanism-reveal that sounds like a press release

When the script gets to the product mechanism, AI defaults to brochure language. Suddenly the creator stops talking like a person and starts reading marketing copy.

This is the most common point in a script where the voice collapses.

**Fix:** Read the mechanism reveal aloud. If the creator's voice register changes when they hit it, rewrite. The creator should explain the mechanism the way they would explain it to a friend at a bar, not the way a brochure explains it.

### Tell 7: The CTA that breaks character

The body of the script sounds like a real person. Then the CTA arrives and suddenly it is a marketer talking. AI does this almost every time because the CTA is where it reverts to training data.

**Fix:** The voice that started the script has to be the voice that ends it. If the hook was casual, the CTA is casual. If the body had hesitation, the CTA has hesitation. No transformation language. No "click below to change your life."

### Tell 8: The emotional beat with no object

AI inserts feelings without specific causes. Generic emotional statements are placeholders, not sentences.

Examples:
> "It was overwhelming."
> "I felt completely lost."
> "Everything changed."

**Fix:** Real people attach feelings to specific moments and objects. "It was overwhelming" becomes "I sat in the parking lot for 20 minutes before I could go in." "I felt completely lost" becomes "I drove home and forgot to pick up my daughter."

### Tell 9: The "imagine if" hypothetical

AI loves to ask the viewer to imagine a future state. Real testimonial-style scripts almost never do this.

Real testimonials report what happened. They do not speculate about what could happen.

**Fix:** Cut every "imagine if" and "picture this." Replace with a specific past event. Tell the story.

### Tell 10: The wisdom-bestowing closer

The script ends with a piece of generalized advice the creator earned the right to give. Except they did not, because the script did not earn it.

Closing with a universal truth ("the lesson is...", "what I learned is...") is almost always bolted on after the fact.

**Fix:** End the script the way a real person ends a story. Sometimes that is just a small specific note. Sometimes it is a soft CTA. Almost never a "here's what I learned."

## What replaces these patterns

The patterns above are what AI does. Here is what real scripts do instead.

### Specific scenes, not statements

A real script names the place, the time, the other person in the room. Scene-level detail is the strongest single signal of authenticity.

### Self-correction

Real people interrupt themselves. They start a sentence one way and finish it another. Scripts that flow too cleanly read as written. A small self-correction reads as spoken.

### Brand-new specificity

A detail that no copywriter would invent because it is too small or too pedestrian. The mundane details are exactly the things that make a script real.

### Numerical hesitation

Real people are uncertain about numbers. Approximations and ranges read as truth. AI gives exact totals. Hesitation reads more honest than precision.

### Spoken contractions and elisions

Use the contractions a person actually uses when they speak. Scripts that read as written prose are scripts that did not get read out loud first.

### Filler that earns its place

One real piece of conversational filler per minute of script can do more for authenticity than a full rewrite. Do not overdo it. One or two per 60 seconds maximum.

## Script-section-specific rules

Different sections of a script have different AI risk profiles. The mechanism reveal and the CTA are the most dangerous sections.

### Hook (first 3 seconds)

Must be a moment, not a claim. A scene with a person, a place, and a specific trigger event will always outperform a category statement.

- Maximum 12 words so the creator can land it inside three seconds
- Question hooks are banned unless the question itself is genuinely strange and specific
- Source material has to come from real customer language: Reddit threads, sales-call transcripts, review mining

If the hook could have been written by someone who has never spoken to a customer, it is wrong.

### Body (seconds 3-30)

Continuation, not pivot. The body picks up the hook's specific moment and extends it. If the hook is a scene, the body is what happened in the next five minutes.

- Roughly one scene per 5-7 seconds of script
- Scripts that try to compress an 18-month timeline into 25 seconds collapse into AI-prose
- Word choice is identity: use the persona's actual vocabulary, not the clinical or professional version of the same words

### Mechanism reveal (seconds 30-45)

Most dangerous section for AI tells. The temptation is to switch into "here's why it works" mode and lose the voice.

- The mechanism should be explained the way the creator would explain it to a friend at a bar, not the way a brochure explains it
- One sentence of mechanism is usually enough
- More than two and the script has stopped being a testimonial and started being an ad

### Offer (seconds 45-55)

Real, casual framing. No urgency theater.

- Generic urgency phrases ("while supplies last," "limited time only") are banned
- If the urgency is real, name the specific reason behind it ("the founder pulled inventory because of supply issues")

### CTA (last 5 seconds)

Must sound like the creator, not the brand.

- The voice that started the script has to be the voice that ends it
- No transformation language
- No "click below to change your life"
- Just the action and a casual reason
- Sign-offs are optional. Most strong scripts do not need one.

## The read-aloud test

Before any script ships to production, the writer reads it out loud, all the way through, in one take, at the pace a real creator would deliver it.

### Things that fail the read-aloud test

- Any sentence the writer stumbles over. Real people do not say things their writers can not say.
- Any sentence that needs a comma the writer did not naturally pause at.
- Any word the writer would not use in conversation.
- Any line that sounds like it has invisible italics on it for emphasis. AI scripts are full of these.
- The mechanism reveal section, specifically. If the writer's voice changes register when they hit it, the script changes register too. Rewrite.

If three or more lines fail the read-aloud test, the script is not ready. Do not ship it to a creator.

## The 60-second script pre-production checklist

Run every script through this before sending to a creator.

- [ ] Search for em dashes. Any hits? Delete and rewrite.
- [ ] Search for "It's not" and "isn't". Any reframe constructions? Kill them.
- [ ] Does the script open with "Most..."? Rewrite the hook as a specific scene.
- [ ] Any two-fragment contrasts? Merge into full sentences.
- [ ] Any three-word stacked closers? Cut.
- [ ] Read the hook out loud. Can you say it in 3 seconds without rushing? If no, cut to 12 words.
- [ ] Read the mechanism reveal out loud. Does your voice change register? If yes, rewrite in casual voice.
- [ ] Read the CTA out loud. Does it sound like the same person who said the hook? If no, rewrite.
- [ ] Count the specific details (places, times, named objects, real numbers). Fewer than 5 in a 60-second script? Add more.
- [ ] Are the numbers suspiciously round? Mess them up. Real numbers are messy.
- [ ] Is there at least one self-correction or hesitation in the script? If not, add one.
- [ ] Final test: would a creator have to "perform" any line, or could they just say it? If anything needs performing, rewrite it.

## The one-line summary

A script is a transcript of speech, not a paragraph of writing. If a real person did not say it before you wrote it down, a real person can not say it now.
