# Video Learning Science

Source: LearnWorlds "Video-Based Learning" (Panos Siozos PhD, Nick Malekos, Joey Tsouvalas)

## Why This Matters for Scriptwriters

These principles are backed by cognitive science research. They apply to any video content — ads, courses, YouTube, tutorials. Violating them measurably reduces viewer retention and comprehension.

---

## 1. Dual Channel Theory (Mayer & Moreno)

Humans process information through two channels:
- **Pictorial channel** (visual input: images, animations, video)
- **Auditory channel** (sound input: narration, music, sound effects)

Combining both channels reduces cognitive load and deepens retention vs. either alone.

**Retention by modality:**
| Method | Retention |
|--------|-----------|
| Read only | 10% |
| Heard only | 20% |
| Seen only | 30% |
| Heard AND seen | 50% |

**Implication:** Every video script should plan what the viewer SEES separately from what they HEAR. The visual and audio tracks should complement, not duplicate.

## 2. Mayer's 5 Multimedia Design Principles

### Signalling Principle
Insert visual cues — highlights, arrows, colored boxes, labels — to show what to study and how to organize it. Don't let the viewer guess what's important.

### Coherence Principle
Remove irrelevant words, images, and sounds. Every extra element that doesn't serve learning actively hurts it. Minimalism wins.

### Redundancy Principle (Most Commonly Violated)
**Do NOT show on-screen text while also narrating the same words.** This hurts learning.
- Audio + visuals = good
- Audio + visuals + identical on-screen text = bad
- The only exception: labels and headings that are DIFFERENT from what you're narrating

**Why:** The viewer's visual channel gets overloaded processing both the images AND the redundant text, stealing resources from actual comprehension.

### Spatial Contiguity Principle
Place text labels close to the related image on screen. Don't make viewers scan back and forth between a label at the top and an image at the bottom.

### Temporal Contiguity Principle
Present narration and its corresponding graphic simultaneously, not sequentially. Don't show an image, then explain it 10 seconds later. Sync them.

## 3. The 6-Minute Rule

**Keep videos under 6 minutes.** This is the hard rule backed by engagement research.

- Screencasts: **30 seconds to 3 minutes** is optimal
- Educational content: **Under 6 minutes** for maximum engagement and recall
- If content must go longer (6-9 min): add a content table/chapter markers inside the video for navigation
- If content is complex: **break it into multiple shorter videos** rather than slowing the pace

### Abandon Rate Curve
Viewers start dropping off almost immediately. The rate accelerates sharply between 30 and 120 seconds. Short wins every time.

**Segmentation options:**
1. Multiple shorter videos (preferred)
2. "Click forward" pause points within a single video

## 4. The 12 Lecture Styles Spectrum

From board-centric (content is the hero) to speaker-centric (instructor is the hero):

| # | Style | Description | Best For |
|---|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | Animated lectures | Animated characters, instructor as voice-over | Complex concepts, entertainment |
| 2 | Slides presentation | Presenter absent or voice-only | Data-heavy, technical content |
| 3 | Whiteboard animation | Words/drawings in real-time | Step-by-step processes |
| 4 | Screencasting | Screen capture with narration | Software tutorials, demos |
| 5 | Instructor over slides | Picture-in-picture overlay | Blended content + personality |
| 6 | Instructor split screen | Green screen illusion of sharing space with slides | Professional presentation |
| 7 | Lightboard | Instructor writes on plexiglass (camera reverses) | Math, diagrams, formulas |
| 8 | Instructor on whiteboard | Narrator acts on content, creates room sense | Workshops, interactive teaching |
| 9 | Instructor full screen | Direct address, speaker-centric | Trust-building, rapport |
| 10 | Selfie videos | Speaker-centric, lively, field-trip style | Casual content, vlogs |
| 11 | Autobiographical | Personal story with clear steps | Origin stories, case studies |
| 12 | Conversation | Two+ people discussing | Podcasts, interviews, panels |

**Decision rule:**
- For **skill-building content**: use board-centric styles (1-6) — content is the hero
- For **trust-building and rapport**: use speaker-centric styles (9-12) — instructor is the hero
- Most instructors never consciously choose. Choosing deliberately based on goal dramatically changes engagement.

## 5. 4-Step Course Video Structure

1. **Welcoming video:** Introduce yourself, topic, expected outcomes, tips to learn faster
2. **Trigger video (under 3 min):** Real-life dilemma or case study to create immediate interest
3. **Mini-lectures:** Core content broken into multiple short videos
4. **Conclusion video:** Summarize and identify the most critical issues

---

## 6. 4-Step Narration/Story Framework

1. **Characters:** Decide who your characters are and how they appeal to your audience
2. **Cognitive Conflict:** Create an obstacle to the characters' happiness or task performance
3. **Quest:** Explain the problem and the path to resolve it
4. **Resolution:** Characters now have the skills or procedure to solve it

This maps to: Hook (conflict) → Journey (quest) → Payoff (resolution).

---

## 7. Narration Guidelines

- **Conversational tone:** Speak informally, not formally. Informal delivery = higher engagement
- **Faster pace:** Speed up to maintain interest. Use chapters and speed control for complexity (don't slow down)
- **Script for audience:** Use short sentences. Read aloud before recording to check conversational flow
- **Vary presenters:** Avoid monotony. Different voices and approaches maintain attention
- **Procedural framing:** "How to do X" framing outperforms conceptual "What is X" framing

---

## 8. Talking-Head Decision Framework

**Use talking-head when:**
- You want personal feel and body language guidance
- Building rapport and explaining goals
- Introduction and conclusion segments

**Avoid talking-head when:**
- It creates split attention and distracts from demonstration
- Detailed technical content where screen is the focus

**Integration styles:** Window overlay, background overlap, green-screen, text superimposed, whiteboard/acting

---

## 9. Screencast Active Learning Framework (Loch & McLoughlin)

Three-stage metacognitive framework for educational screencasts:

1. **Planning & Goal Setting:** Give concept overview, activate prior knowledge, have learner set a session goal
2. **Monitoring & Metacognitive Control:** Present questions and tasks mid-video, prompt self-assessment
3. **Reflection:** Encourage documentation of uncertainty, prepare discussion questions

---

## 10. Video Analytics Framework

### Key Metrics
- Total watches and real-time view counts
- Parts watched, re-watched, and skipped (heat maps)
- Video abandon rate by viewing time
- Plays per user / plays divided by completes
- Attention level score (0-100%) based on reaction to interactive elements
- In-video behavior: pauses, mutes, seeks, replayed sections, speed changes

### How to Use Analytics
- Identify which videos are most useful and cut weak ones
- Cluster viewers by behavior: adaptive, self-regulating, infrequent
- If drop-off spikes at 15 or 30 minutes: content is too long (see 6-Minute Rule)
- Filter by geography and date to spot performance patterns

---

## 11. Interactivity as Retention Strategy

- Learners who interact with video content focus more and increase effort to learn
- Add: embedded questions, pointer highlights, colored frames, personalized paths
- Give learners: speed control, skip-forward/backward, pause during difficult procedures
- Add 5-second pause overlays that summarize steps at key moments
- 360-degree videos produce higher click-to-purchase rates and longer watch times than traditional video
