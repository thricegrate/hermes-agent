# Video Ad Frameworks: Deep Dive

Three proven frameworks for structuring 60-90 second direct-response video ads. Each framework follows a different persuasion path but produces the same output: a take-by-take blueprint with setting, hero, dialogue, text overlay, camera, and edit direction for every shot.

---

## Framework A: Contrarian Expert (5 Acts)

The most structured framework. Opens by challenging conventional wisdom, then backs the contrarian claim with authority, education, social proof, and a proportional offer. Works because the contrarian hook creates cognitive tension that the viewer needs to resolve by watching.

### Act 1: Provoke

**Purpose:** Make a contrarian claim that buys you 60-90 seconds of attention.

**What happens:**
- Open with a statement that directly contradicts what the viewer believes or has been told
- The claim should be specific, not vague: "X is actually hurting your Y" is better than "everything you know is wrong"
- Follow immediately with a preempt: "I know that sounds crazy" or "hear me out" to prevent instant dismissal
- The viewer now has a gap: their belief vs. your claim. They stay to find out who is right

**Visual direction:**
- Setting signals expertise before any words are spoken: home office with whiteboards, studio with good lighting, professional environment
- Hero framing: medium shot, direct eye contact, confident but not aggressive energy
- Text overlay: stagger the contrarian claim word by word for muted viewers. Each word appears on beat with the dialogue
- Camera: static or very subtle push-in. No movement that distracts from the claim

**Timing:** 5-8 seconds (60s ads) / 8-10 seconds (90s ads)

**Example outline:**
> "If you are still [common practice], you are actually [negative consequence]. I know that sounds crazy, but let me show you why."

### Act 2: Credential

**Purpose:** Stack authority quickly so the viewer trusts what comes next. Then move on.

**What happens:**
- Rapid-fire credential stack: 2-3 proof points maximum
- Formats that work: specific results ("I have helped 400+ students do X"), experience ("after 10 years of doing Y"), recognition ("featured in Z")
- Never linger. The credential is a bridge, not the destination. Spending too long here feels like bragging
- Transition line: "and the reason I know this is..." or "so here is what I discovered..."

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: same as Act 1 or subtle shift (move from standing to seated, or cut to tighter frame)
- Text overlay: display credentials as bullet points or lower-third graphics. Stack them visually as they are spoken
- Camera: slight angle change from Act 1 to create visual variety without a full scene change
- Edit note: fastest-paced act. Quick cuts between credential points if using multiple angles

**Timing:** 5-7 seconds (60s ads) / 7-10 seconds (90s ads)

**Rule:** If the credential takes more than 10 seconds, it is too long. Cut it.

### Act 3: Empathize + Educate

**Purpose:** Connect emotionally, then teach the viewer something they did not know. This is the longest act and the core value of the ad.

**Phase 1: Empathize (first half)**
- Acknowledge the viewer's struggle from their perspective
- Name the specific things they have tried that have not worked
- Show you understand their frustration: "I used to do the exact same thing"
- This builds trust: you are not just an expert, you have been where they are

**Phase 2: Educate (second half)**
- Reframe why the conventional approach fails
- Introduce the key insight or mechanism behind your solution
- Use visual aids: diagrams, annotations, whiteboard explanations, screen recordings
- Keep it simple: one core insight, not a lecture. The viewer should think "oh, THAT is why it was not working"

**Visual direction:**
- Phase 1: tighter framing, more intimate. Hero energy shifts from authoritative to empathetic. Warmer tones
- Phase 2: layer visual aids on screen. Diagrams, before/after comparisons, annotated screenshots. The visual explanation is the star, not the talking head
- Text overlay: key insight stated as a short phrase (5-7 words), displayed prominently when the reframe happens
- Camera: in Phase 1, static or subtle movement. In Phase 2, screen recordings or B-roll can dominate
- Edit note: this act has the most visual variety. New element every 5-8 seconds minimum

**Timing:** 20 seconds (60s ads) / 30 seconds (90s ads)

### Act 4: Prove

**Purpose:** Show that this works for real people, not just the expert.

**What happens:**
- 3-5 diverse testimonials, each with a specific measurable result
- Diversity matters: different demographics, situations, and starting points. The viewer needs to see someone like them
- Each testimonial is 3-8 seconds: state the result, show the person, move on
- The testimonials should feel raw and authentic, not scripted. UGC style

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: each testimonial in a different, authentic environment (their home, their office, their car). No studio
- Style: raw, unpolished. Phone-quality video, natural lighting. This contrast with the polished expert segments signals authenticity
- Text overlay: display the specific result as large text over each testimonial ("Lost 30 lbs in 60 days", "$12K in first month")
- Camera: selfie-style or phone-held. Slightly shaky is fine, it reinforces authenticity
- Edit note: quick cuts between testimonials. No long transitions. Each clip is tight

**Timing:** 15 seconds (60s ads) / 25 seconds (90s ads)

### Act 5: Offer

**Purpose:** Make a small ask proportional to the value already delivered.

**What happens:**
- Restate the transformation one final time: "If you want to [desired outcome]..."
- Present the offer clearly: what they get, how to access it, what it costs (if applicable)
- The ask should feel like the natural next step, not a sales pitch
- Instant access, low friction: "click the link below", "comment [word] and I will send it"
- Close with urgency or scarcity only if genuine

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: return to the expert environment from Act 1/2. Visual bookend
- Hero: direct eye contact, calm confidence. Not pushy energy
- Text overlay: CTA displayed clearly with arrow or visual indicator. URL or action shown on screen
- Camera: medium shot, static. Let the words do the work
- Edit note: slightly slower pace than Act 4. Give the offer room to breathe

**Timing:** 15 seconds (60s ads) / 20 seconds (90s ads)

## Framework B: Story Bridge (4 Acts)

A narrative framework built on emotional connection. Mirrors the viewer's situation, walks through a discovery, shows the transformation, and bridges them to the same result. Works because humans are wired for stories: when the viewer sees their own situation reflected, they mentally insert themselves into the narrative.

### Act 1: Mirror

**Purpose:** Describe the viewer's current situation so precisely they feel seen.

**What happens:**
- Address the viewer directly with "you"
- Describe their specific situation, struggle, and emotions
- Be precise: "you have been posting every day but your engagement keeps dropping" is better than "you are struggling with social media"
- The goal is recognition: the viewer should nod and think "that is literally me"

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: relatable environment. Wherever the target viewer would be (home office, kitchen table, commute)
- Hero: casual, approachable. No suit, no studio. Dressed like someone the viewer would be friends with
- Text overlay: key pain point phrase displayed on screen
- Camera: tight to medium shot, intimate framing
- Edit note: slower pace. Let the words land

**Timing:** 8-10 seconds (60s ads) / 10-12 seconds (90s ads)

### Act 2: Journey

**Purpose:** Share the discovery story: what was tried, what failed, what changed.

**What happens:**
- Transition to first person or third person: "I was in the exact same place" or "my client Sarah was stuck"
- Walk through 2-3 things that were tried and did not work (the viewer will recognize their own failed attempts)
- Then introduce the turning point: the moment something clicked or a new approach was discovered
- Keep it conversational: this should feel like a friend telling you what happened, not a presentation

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: can shift to match the story (walking shot, different room, B-roll of the described activities)
- Visual variety: show the failed attempts visually (stock footage, recreations, screenshots)
- Text overlay: list the failed approaches as they are named, then cross them out or fade them
- Camera: more movement than Act 1. Walking and talking, handheld feel
- Edit note: build energy toward the turning point. Music can subtly shift

**Timing:** 17-20 seconds (60s ads) / 25-30 seconds (90s ads)

### Act 3: Transformation

**Purpose:** Show the before/after with specific, measurable results.

**What happens:**
- Describe the outcome in concrete terms: numbers, timeline, tangible change
- Mix proof types: personal footage, screenshots, data, side-by-side comparisons
- The transformation should feel achievable, not extraordinary
- Brief social proof: "and it was not just me" followed by 1-2 quick testimonial clips

**Visual direction:**
- Visual-heavy act: before/after screenshots, graphs going up, product in use, results dashboards
- Text overlay: the specific result displayed large ("0 to 10K followers in 90 days")
- Camera: dynamic. Screen recordings, product shots, B-roll of the transformed state
- Edit note: peak energy of the ad. Fastest visual refresh rate (new element every 3-5 seconds)

**Timing:** 20 seconds (60s ads) / 30 seconds (90s ads)

### Act 4: Bridge

**Purpose:** Connect the viewer to the same path. The CTA is the bridge.

**What happens:**
- "If you want the same results..." or "I put together everything I learned into..."
- The offer is framed as the shortcut to the transformation they just saw
- Low-friction CTA: the bridge should be easy to cross
- End with confidence, not desperation

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: return to intimate, direct-to-camera. Same energy as Act 1 but warmer
- Hero: genuine smile, calm energy. The offer is a gift, not a pitch
- Text overlay: CTA text + visual indicator (arrow, link, comment prompt)
- Camera: static medium shot. Let the connection carry the close
- Edit note: pace slows from Act 3. Breathing room for the decision

**Timing:** 15 seconds (60s ads) / 18-20 seconds (90s ads)

## Framework C: Demo Proof (4 Acts)

A show-don't-tell framework. Leads with the end result, gives brief context, walks through the product step by step, then stacks value and closes. Works because it eliminates skepticism: the viewer sees the product doing what is promised before being asked to buy.

### Act 1: Result First

**Purpose:** Show the end result immediately, no buildup.

**What happens:**
- Open with the finished output, the transformation, or the product in action
- Screen recording showing a completed workflow, a before/after comparison, or the product doing its job
- Minimal narration: let the visual speak. One sentence of context maximum
- This is the "wow, how did they do that?" moment

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: screen recording, product shot, or before/after comparison. The product is the star, not the person
- Text overlay: result stated simply ("Built in 5 minutes" or "Before / After")
- Camera: screen capture for digital products, macro/detail shot for physical products
- Edit note: clean, no music. Let the result land in silence or with minimal ambient sound

**Timing:** 5-8 seconds (60s ads) / 8-10 seconds (90s ads)

### Act 2: Context

**Purpose:** Brief problem setup: why this matters and who it is for.

**What happens:**
- Quick context on the problem this solves: 1-2 sentences
- "If you are a [person] who needs to [task], you know how [frustrating/time-consuming/expensive] it is to [old way]"
- Transition to the walkthrough: "Let me show you how this works"

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: talking head if narrated, or text on screen if voiceover
- Hero: (if on camera) casual, relatable
- Text overlay: problem stated concisely
- Camera: medium shot or screen with annotations
- Edit note: brief act. Do not linger. The viewer wants to see the walkthrough

**Timing:** 10 seconds (60s ads) / 12-15 seconds (90s ads)

### Act 3: Walkthrough

**Purpose:** Step-by-step demonstration with annotations. The meat of the ad.

**What happens:**
- Walk through the product, tool, or template in 3-5 clear steps
- Narrate what is happening on screen as it happens
- Use annotations, highlights, and zoom-ins to focus attention on key moments
- Each step should build toward the result shown in Act 1

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: primarily screen recording or hands-on footage. Talking head in corner (PIP) or voiceover only
- Text overlay: step numbers ("Step 1:", "Step 2:") and key feature callouts
- Annotations: arrows, highlights, zoom-ins on important UI elements or details
- Camera: screen capture with cursor tracking for digital, close-up hands-on for physical
- Edit note: steady pace. Each step gets 5-8 seconds. Trim dead time between steps ruthlessly

**Timing:** 30 seconds (60s ads) / 45 seconds (90s ads)

### Act 4: Stack + CTA

**Purpose:** Stack the value, remove risk, and close with a clear call to action.

**What happens:**
- Recap what the viewer just saw: "So with [product], you can [outcome 1], [outcome 2], and [outcome 3]"
- Stack value: list everything included
- Remove risk: money-back guarantee, free trial, "cancel anytime"
- Clear CTA: what to do next, how to access it, and what happens after they click

**Visual direction:**
- Setting: return to talking head or a clean graphic with the offer summary
- Text overlay: value stack displayed as a list. Price crossed out or compared to alternative costs
- Camera: direct to camera for the close, or clean motion graphic
- Edit note: slightly faster than Act 2, slower than Act 3. Confident energy, not rushed

**Timing:** 15 seconds (60s ads) / 18-20 seconds (90s ads)

## Cross-Framework Principles

These rules apply regardless of which framework you use:

1. **Hook in 3 seconds or less.** The first words and first visual must create a reason to keep watching. Do not ease into it
2. **New visual every 5-10 seconds.** Static frames kill retention. Cut, overlay, animate, or shift something
3. **Assume sound-off.** Every key claim needs a text overlay. A viewer on mute should understand 80% of the message
4. **One core insight per ad.** Do not cram multiple ideas. One problem, one mechanism, one solution
5. **CTA matches value.** A 60-second ad earns the right to ask for a click or a comment, not a credit card. The ask should feel proportional
6. **Testimonials need specifics.** "It changed my life" means nothing. "$12K in my first month" means everything
7. **End abruptly.** After the CTA, stop. Do not wrap up, do not summarize, do not say goodbye
