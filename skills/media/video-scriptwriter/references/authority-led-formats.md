# Authority-Led Formats: Pharmacist/Doctor and Street Interview

Authority-led formats restore credibility in categories where audiences have already seen too much UGC, exaggerated claims, and low-trust creator ads.

A pharmacist explaining something calmly carries more authority than "this changed my life."

A researcher answering casually in a street interview feels more believable than a scripted spokesperson.

## When to use authority-led formats

- Supplements
- Skincare
- Wellness
- Health-adjacent categories
- Technical SaaS where the product needs explanation
- Categories where the audience has already seen 50 UGC ads in the same niche

The key: authority must feel earned, not performed.

## Two formats in this family

### 1. Pharmacist / Doctor in-studio script

Best for:
- Deeper mechanisms
- Credibility-heavy categories
- Technical explanations
- Calmer pacing
- 30-45 second format

The authority starts with expertise already established. The setting (pharmacy counter, exam room, office) does the credibility lift. The script focuses on the mechanism explanation.

Structure:
1. Authority opens with a specific clinical observation
2. Names the mechanism in technical-but-accessible terms
3. References common solutions and their limitations
4. Introduces the product as a narrow recommendation
5. Closes with one clinical-tone fact

For the generation prompt, see [../templates/authority-led-scripts.md](../templates/authority-led-scripts.md).

### 2. Street interview (authority reveal)

Best for:
- Curiosity
- Retention
- Social proof
- Cold traffic
- TikTok, Reels, Shorts
- 25-40 second format

The authority gets revealed gradually. The interviewer asks a casual non-product question. The stranger answers with surprising specificity. The reveal of the stranger's profession creates the legitimacy spike.

Structure:
1. Interviewer asks casual non-product question
2. Stranger answers with surprising specificity
3. Interviewer reacts: "Wait, what do you do?"
4. Stranger's authority is revealed
5. Authority explains mechanism
6. Soft product reference

The reveal must feel earned. The stranger should feel like a normal person for the first 8 seconds.

For the generation prompt, see [../templates/authority-led-scripts.md](../templates/authority-led-scripts.md).

## Why these formats work

Authority formats succeed because they borrow credibility from:

- Role perception (pharmacist, MD, nutritionist, researcher)
- Format structure (in-studio, street interview)
- Informational delivery (calm, measured, specific)

The audience assumes expertise, specificity, and legitimacy before the product even appears. That lowers resistance dramatically.

## The most important rule

The authority should never sound like a creator pretending to be an expert.

The strongest scripts feel:
- Measured
- Observational
- Calm
- Precise

Real authority rarely:
- Overhypes
- Exaggerates
- Sounds emotional

The tone should feel closer to explanation than persuasion.

## Structural difference between the two formats

### In-studio authority

The expertise is established from the first frame. The wardrobe (white coat, button-up, scrubs), the setting (counter, exam room, lab), and the tone (calm, technical) all signal authority before the dialogue starts.

The script can dive straight into the mechanism. The audience accepts the expertise.

### Street interview reveal

The expertise is hidden until the reveal moment. The script trades initial authority for curiosity. The reveal at second 8-12 is the conversion point: the stranger goes from "random person" to "credible expert."

The reveal must feel surprising. If the stranger looks like a doctor in the first frame (lab coat, etc.), the reveal lands flat. The visual setup should suggest "regular person" until the reveal.

## What makes these scripts convert

The audience experiences:

1. Curiosity (the question)
2. Legitimacy (the surprising answer)
3. Explanation (the mechanism)
4. Trust (the credentials)
5. Recommendation (the product)

In that order.

The product enters after credibility already exists. That sequencing matters.

## Worked example

### Street interview format

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Excuse me, random question. What's the most underrated thing for skin?"

**STRANGER:**
"Honestly? Most collagen products never even survive digestion."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"Wait... what do you do?"

**STRANGER:**
"I'm a nutrition researcher. I study bioavailability."

**INTERVIEWER:**
"So most skincare supplements don't actually reach the skin?"

**STRANGER:**
"Most don't. The structure breaks down before absorption."

The viewer experiences the reveal with the interviewer. The "Wait, what do you do?" moment is the legitimacy unlock. After that, the stranger has authority and the mechanism explanation lands.

## Common failure modes

### Authority sounds like a creator in costume

The actor wears a lab coat but speaks like a TikTok creator. The credibility evaporates.

**Fix:** Match how real professionals explain concepts. Calm, specific, measured. Watch how actual pharmacists or doctors talk to patients. Mirror that register.

### Reveal happens too early

In a street interview, the stranger's profession is revealed in line 1. The format collapses.

**Fix:** Authority reveal cannot happen before second 8. The stranger must feel like a normal person for the first half of the clip.

### Product destroys credibility

The authority spends 15 seconds establishing credibility, then pivots to: "And that's why I recommend [Brand], the world's best..."

**Fix:** The product enters as a narrow recommendation, not a sales pitch. "Most don't. The structure breaks down before absorption" is followed by something like: "There are two or three brands that actually use the bonded form. The one I tell my clients about is [Brand]." Soft, specific, narrow.

### Expert uses language too technical

The mechanism is explained in textbook prose. The audience tunes out.

**Fix:** Explain complex ideas simply without sounding dumbed down. The expert speaks like a friend at a bar who happens to have the credentials, not like a textbook.

### Dialogue feels scripted

Both actors speak in complete polished sentences. The interview feels staged.

**Fix:** Use pauses, interruptions, conversational phrasing. The interviewer reacts in real-time. The expert has small hesitations.

## Wardrobe and setting notes

### Pharmacist / Doctor in-studio
- White coat OR scrubs OR professional but casual (button-up rolled up)
- Setting: pharmacy counter, exam room, office, lab bench
- Visible tools (stethoscope, prescription pad, lab equipment) help signal role
- Lighting: even, slightly clinical, not warm

### Street interview
- Stranger wears casual normal clothing for the first 8 seconds
- The reveal can come with a visual cue (badge, lab coat under jacket, business card) but does not need one
- Setting: actual street, sidewalk, outside a building
- Phone-camera framing
- Natural daylight
- The interviewer should look like a regular person with a phone, not a producer

## How this feeds the pipeline

### Authority scripts become

- Educational ads
- Landing-page explainer videos
- Founder authority content (when the founder is the authority)

### Street interviews become

- Cold hooks (the reveal moment is the hook for retargeting)
- Short-form organic content
- Paid social clips

### Mechanism explanations get reused across

- BOF (bottom-of-funnel) content
- Advertorials
- Product pages
- Email sequences

The mechanism explanation is the most reusable asset from any authority-led script. Once the mechanism is explained well by an authority figure, it becomes the canonical explanation for the brand. Reuse it everywhere.
