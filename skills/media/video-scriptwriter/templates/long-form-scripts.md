# Long-Form Script Templates

Two prompts for long-form formats:
- Prompt 7: Long-form yapper / VSL script (3-4 min)
- Prompt 8: POV personal story script (30-90 sec)

For when to use each format and the structural patterns, see [../references/long-form-formats.md](../references/long-form-formats.md).

For voice rules that apply across both formats, see [../references/direct-response-voice-system.md](../references/direct-response-voice-system.md).

## Prompt 7: Long-Form Yapper / VSL Script (3-4 min)

Use this for documentary-style narration or POV creator stories where deep mechanism explanation, emotional storytelling, and skeptical audiences require trust-building before the product reveal.

The product enters at 75-85 percent of runtime. Every beat is timestamped.

```
Write a 3 to 4 minute long-form story script.

Persona:
[persona]

Pain (verbatim customer language):
[specific pain quote pulled from Reddit, sales call, or review mining]

Trigger event:
[the awakening moment, the specific scene where the protagonist realized something was wrong]

Mechanism / villain:
[biological or psychological villain. Name it specifically: "cortisol binding testosterone", "SHBG", "aromatase conversion", "perimenopause histamine response", etc.]

Credibility anchor:
[doctor name, study, statistic, or institution that legitimizes the explanation]

Product:
[product]

Transformation:
[specific after-state, including a specific behavioral or sensory detail, not just "feeling better"]

Structure (timestamp each beat):

0:00-0:10: Hook
Relationship-pain or identity-loss line. No product mention. The hook scores at least 7/10 on stop-power. Maximum 12 words to fit inside 3 seconds when spoken at conversational pace.

0:10-1:00: Story setup
Specific scenario, first-person narration. Names a place, a time, another person in the room. The viewer can picture the scene.

1:00-1:45: Pain validation
The viewer recognizes themselves. Specific moments and behaviors the audience has lived. Not generic descriptions.

1:45-2:15: Villain reveal
Name the biology specifically (or psychology, depending on the niche). The villain is the mechanism causing the pain.

2:15-2:30: Credibility anchor
Doctor name, statistic, study, or institution. One specific reference that legitimizes the explanation.

2:30-2:50: Mechanism explanation
Connect the villain to the category. Why standard solutions in the category do not address the villain.

2:50-3:10: Objection handling
The most common audience objection gets named and dismantled.

3:10-3:25: Product reveal
The product enters here. Not before. Positioned as the narrow solution that addresses the named villain.

3:25-3:45: Proof / testimonial / result
Specific evidence. Numbers if possible. Specific transformation language pulled from real reviews.

3:45-3:55: Identity reinforcement or transformation payoff
The viewer sees themselves on the other side of the mechanism.

3:55-end: CTA
Direct, specific. Names the action and one casual reason.

Output:
- Full script with timestamps for every beat
- Visual notes per timestamp section (camera angle, b-roll cue, on-screen text suggestion, expression cue)
- Estimated runtime
- Hook score (1-10) on each of the 4 dimensions (stop-power, specificity, lever clarity, natural cadence)
- One alternative hook (in case the primary hook does not test well)

Constraint:
The product cannot enter before 3:10 (75% of a 4-minute script). If the script comes in shorter than 4 minutes, the product still cannot enter before 75% of total runtime.

Voice rules:
- No em dashes
- No "It's not X, it's Y"
- No "Most guys / most women / most people" openers
- No "obsessed", "game-changing", "transformational"
- No three-word stacked closers
- No two-fragment contrast
- Use contractions, fragments, self-correction
- One self-correction or hesitation per minute minimum
- The mechanism reveal section gets read aloud separately. If the voice changes register when hitting it, rewrite in casual voice.

Read every section aloud. If 3 or more lines fail the read-aloud test, rewrite before delivery.

Pull verbatim phrases from customer research throughout. Use the persona's actual vocabulary, not the clinical version.
```

## Prompt 8: POV Personal Story Script (30-90 sec)

Use this for first-person creator scripts where relatability and faster emotional connection beat depth. The creator films themselves in a casual setting.

```
Write a 30 to 90 second POV personal story script.

Persona:
[usually the creator filming the ad. Their age, situation, day-to-day context.]

Discovery moment:
[the specific moment they discovered the product. A specific scene with a place, a time, another person involved.]

Product:
[product]

Transformation:
[specific after-state. Not "I feel better" but "I sleep through the night now" or "I stopped checking my phone at 11pm".]

Format:
First-person creator filming themselves.

Casual setting:
[kitchen / bathroom / bedroom / car / walking outside]

Structure:

1. Hook: specific personal moment (1-2 lines)
2. Setup: what was happening before (2-3 lines)
3. Discovery: how product entered (2-3 lines)
4. Result: what changed (2-3 lines, with specific sensory or behavioral detail)
5. Soft close: invitation, not pressure (1-2 lines)

Output:
- Full first-person script
- Camera notes (front-facing selfie, side angle, mid-action filming)
- Setting notes (specific environmental cues that signal "real day, real life")
- Eye-line direction (looking at camera vs side / to the creator's friend implied off-camera)
- Estimated runtime

Constraint:
Must sound conversational.

No:
- "I'm obsessed"
- "You guys need this"
- Hard selling
- Hyperbolic transformation language
- Em dashes
- "It's not X, it's Y" structures
- "Most guys / most women / most people"

Use:
- Contractions (I'm, you're, can't, didn't)
- Fragments
- Side comments ("which is wild")
- Unfinished sentences (the creator catches themselves)
- One or two pieces of conversational filler ("anyway", "honestly", "I don't know")

The creator should sound like a friend confessing something, not a creator performing. Small imperfections increase believability.

The product enters in the discovery moment (around 50-60% of runtime). The result section (after product) is where the specifics land.

Pull verbatim phrases from customer research where applicable. The creator's voice should match the persona's voice exactly.
```

## Worked example: Long-form yapper output (compressed)

For a male hormone health product (mechanism: cortisol binding testosterone):

```
HOOK (0:00-0:10):
"Why do women cheat on men they still love? I mean the slow withdrawal before anyone admits something's wrong."

SCRIPT NOTES:
Hook score:
- Stop-power: 9 (relationship-pain hook with specific behavioral detail)
- Specificity: 8 ("slow withdrawal" is specific behavioral language)
- Lever clarity: 9 (curiosity gap is the dominant lever)
- Natural cadence: 9 (sounds like a confession, not marketing)
Total: 35/40

ALTERNATIVE HOOK:
"I noticed my wife stopped reaching for me at night about a year before I figured out why."

STORY SETUP (0:10-1:00):
[First-person narrator, casual setting like a kitchen at night, soft lighting]
"I'm 43. I've been married seventeen years. About a year and a half ago she stopped reaching for me at night. I told myself she was tired. We had a third kid the year before, so the math made sense..."

[continues with specific scene work, the morning conversation, the avoidance behaviors]

PAIN VALIDATION (1:00-1:45):
"The thing nobody tells you is that the cheating starts with your wife asking her sister something. Then her best friend. Then the GP about quote-unquote depression..."

VILLAIN REVEAL (1:45-2:15):
"The issue usually doesn't start in the bedroom. It starts with cortisol. Stress hormones bind testosterone in the blood. The body has the testosterone but it can't use it..."

CREDIBILITY ANCHOR (2:15-2:30):
"There's a urologist named Dr. [Name] at the [Institution] who's been writing about this for the last decade. The mainstream's just starting to catch up."

MECHANISM EXPLANATION (2:30-2:50):
"What most testosterone supplements miss is the binding step. They add more testosterone but the cortisol binds it just like the body's own. You need a stack that addresses the binding directly..."

OBJECTION HANDLING (2:50-3:10):
"You're probably wondering why your doctor didn't mention this. Mine didn't either. He ran the bloodwork, said I was 'low-normal', which is medical for 'fine but really not'..."

PRODUCT REVEAL (3:10-3:25):
"I switched to a stack specifically designed to unbind testosterone at the source. It's called [Brand]."

PROOF (3:25-3:45):
"Inside about six weeks I started waking up the way I used to. Inside three months my wife told me she'd been worried about me without saying anything for over a year..."

IDENTITY PAYOFF (3:45-3:55):
"I'm not the guy who lost his marriage to a slow biological problem he didn't see coming. I'm not even the guy who almost did. That's the version of me that doesn't exist now."

CTA (3:55-end):
"Link below if you're seeing the same pattern. I'd start with the bloodwork before you do anything."

VISUAL NOTES:
- Hook delivered direct-to-camera, slight angle, soft warm lighting
- Story setup: cuts between narrator and b-roll of a kitchen, a hand on a phone, morning bedroom
- Pain validation: stays narrator-focused, occasional cuts to wife reading her phone (out of focus), woman texting (anonymous)
- Villain reveal: cuts to a simple text overlay or whiteboard showing testosterone-cortisol binding
- Credibility anchor: cuts to a screenshot of a research paper or doctor's bio
- Mechanism explanation: stays narrator-focused with text overlays of key terms
- Product reveal: first product shot here, single bottle on a counter, narrator picking it up
- Proof: cuts between narrator and lifestyle b-roll (waking up, daylight, holding wife's hand)
- Identity payoff: stays narrator-focused, slightly tighter framing
- CTA: direct-to-camera, calm

ESTIMATED RUNTIME: 4:00.

NOTES:
- Product enters at 3:10 (78% of 4:00 runtime), in the 75-85% target range
- Mechanism enters at 1:45 (44% of runtime)
- Hook is a relationship-pain question, no product mention
- One self-correction in the story setup ("seventeen years. About a year and a half ago...")
- Specific details throughout: 43, 17 years married, third kid, 6 weeks, 3 months
- No banned phrases, no em dashes, no "It's not X, it's Y" structures
```

## Worked example: POV personal story output (compressed)

For a sleep supplement (the creator is a 30-something working mom):

```
HOOK (0:00-0:04):
"It's 11:47pm and I'm in the bathroom because if my husband sees me on my phone he's going to be annoyed."

SETUP (0:04-0:18):
"I haven't slept through the night in like 18 months. I'd fall asleep at 10. Wake up at 2. Lie there until 4. Doomscroll until I had to get the kids up. I was a wreck."

DISCOVERY (0:18-0:38):
"My friend, who's a nurse practitioner, tried this magnesium thing. Specifically the threonate form, which crosses the blood-brain barrier. I didn't even know that was a thing."

DISCOVERY (continued):
"She gave me one bottle to test. The brand is [Brand]."

RESULT (0:38-0:55):
"First week I didn't notice anything. Second week I started staying asleep until like 4. Third week I slept through. I cried in the morning. Like, I genuinely cried."

SOFT CLOSE (0:55-1:00):
"I'm not telling you to buy it. I'm just saying I had no idea the form mattered this much. Anyway. Link's in the bio if you want to look at it."

VISUAL NOTES:
- Setting: actual bathroom, actual nighttime, actual phone
- Camera: front-facing selfie, creator slightly off-center
- Eye-line: at the camera most of the time, occasionally to the side (like talking to a friend off-screen)
- No makeup-look. Real face, real bathroom light.
- One small detail: a hairband on her wrist or visible toothbrush in the background

CAMERA NOTES:
- Held at chest height, slightly angled up
- Slight handheld movement (not stabilized)
- One natural reframe in the middle (she shifts her grip)

ESTIMATED RUNTIME: 60 seconds.

NOTES:
- Hook is a specific moment (11:47pm in the bathroom)
- Setup is specific behavioral details (10pm sleep, 2am wake, 4am doomscroll)
- Discovery names the friend's role (nurse practitioner) and the specific form (threonate)
- Product enters at 0:30 (50% of runtime, acceptable for POV)
- Result has specific sensory and behavioral detail ("I cried in the morning. Like, I genuinely cried.")
- Soft close is an invitation, not a pitch
- One self-correction implied (the hesitation when she says "I cried in the morning. Like, I genuinely cried.")
- Conversational filler ("Anyway") at the close
- No banned phrases, no em dashes
```

## Common failure modes when running these prompts

### Long-form yapper sounds like an essay

The script flows as polished written prose. Lines are similar length. No rhythm variation.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "Read each beat aloud at conversational pace. Mix line lengths. A 4-word fragment between two 12-word lines breaks the cadence and reads as spoken. Add at least 2 self-corrections across the script."

### Product enters too early in long-form

The script comes back with product reveal at 1:30 in a 4-minute script.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "The product cannot appear before timestamp 3:10 (75% of 4:00). If the model produces an early product reveal, the structure is wrong. Push pain validation and villain reveal earlier."

### POV creator sounds rehearsed

The creator speaks in complete polished sentences with no hesitation.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "Add at least one self-correction (a sentence the creator starts one way and finishes another). Add at least one piece of conversational filler ('anyway', 'honestly', 'I don't know'). Use fragments. Real people do not narrate their stories in clean arcs."

### Mechanism feels disconnected from pain

The villain reveal is technically correct but does not connect to the pain the viewer felt earlier.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "The villain explanation must directly reframe the original pain. The viewer should hear the mechanism and think 'oh, that's what was happening to me'. If the connection isn't explicit, rewrite."

### Numbers are too round in POV

The creator says "I lost 20 pounds in 8 weeks" or "I've been doing this for 6 months exactly."

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "Mess up the numbers. Real numbers are messy. '23 pounds in about 11 weeks' reads as real. '20 pounds in 8 weeks' reads as fabricated."

### Identity reinforcement section is generic

The identity payoff at 3:45-3:55 reads as "I'm a new person now." Generic.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "The identity payoff should reference a specific behavioral or relational detail, not a category statement. 'I'm not the guy who lost his marriage' beats 'I'm a different man now'."
