# Authority-Led Script Templates

Two prompts for authority-led formats:
- Prompt 5: Pharmacist / Doctor in-studio script
- Prompt 6: Street interview script (authority reveal)

For when to use each format and the structural patterns, see [../references/authority-led-formats.md](../references/authority-led-formats.md).

For voice rules that apply across both formats, see [../references/direct-response-voice-system.md](../references/direct-response-voice-system.md).

## Prompt 5: Pharmacist / Doctor In-Studio Script

Use this for 30-45 second clips where the authority is established from frame one. The setting (pharmacy counter, exam room, office) does the credibility lift. The script focuses on the mechanism explanation.

```
Write a 30 to 45 second in-studio authority script.

Persona:
[persona]

Authority role:
[pharmacist / MD / nutritionist / dermatologist / researcher]

Mechanism:
[mechanism story: the biological, psychological, or category-specific cause]

Product:
[product]

Setting:
[studio / pharmacy counter / office / exam room / lab bench]

Structure:

1. Authority opens with a specific clinical observation (1-2 lines)
2. Names the mechanism in technical-but-accessible terms (2-3 lines)
3. References common solutions and their limitations (1-2 lines)
4. Introduces product as a narrow recommendation (1-2 lines)
5. Closes with one clinical-tone fact (1 line)

Output:
- HOOK (authority's first line)
- Full script with line-by-line breakdown
- Visual cues per line (camera angle, prop, gesture, expression)
- Wardrobe notes (specific to the authority role)
- Setting notes (specific environmental cues that signal credibility)
- Estimated runtime

Constraint:
Tone is calm, measured, and specific.

Never:
- Hype
- Oversell
- Use exaggerated language
- Sound like a creator ad

The authority should sound the way a real pharmacist explains something at the counter, or a real MD explains something to a patient. Calm. Specific. Slightly clinical but not robotic.

Voice rules:
- No em dashes
- No "It's not X, it's Y"
- No "Most guys / most women / most people" openers
- No "obsessed", "game-changing", "transformational"
- Use the actual technical term for the mechanism, then explain it in plain language right after
- The product enters as a narrow recommendation, not a sales pitch ("There are two or three brands that..." not "The best product on the market...")

The mechanism enters in lines 2-3. The product enters in line 4 or later (around 70-80% of runtime).
```

## Prompt 6: Street Interview Script (Authority Reveal)

Use this for 25-40 second clips where the authority is hidden until the reveal moment. The interviewer asks a casual non-product question. The stranger answers with surprising specificity. The reveal of the stranger's profession creates the legitimacy spike.

```
Write a 25 to 40 second street interview script.

Persona:
[persona]

Question topic:
[topic interviewer asks about: usually a casual question that can be answered by either a layperson or an expert]

Authority reveal:
[the interviewee's role, revealed mid-clip]

Product:
[product]

Structure:

1. Interviewer asks casual non-product question (1 line)
2. Stranger answers with surprising specificity (1-2 lines)
3. Interviewer reacts: "Wait, what do you do?" (1 line)
4. Stranger's authority is revealed (1 line)
5. Interviewer follow-up question that lets the authority explain (1 line)
6. Authority explains mechanism (1-2 lines)
7. Soft product reference (1 line)

Output:
- HOOK (interviewer's first question)
- Full script with INTERVIEWER/STRANGER labels per line
- Visual notes (setting, framing, where each actor is positioned)
- Timestamped reveal moment (which second the stranger's profession is revealed)
- Estimated runtime

Constraint:
The reveal must feel earned.

The stranger should feel like a normal person for the first 8 seconds. No visual clues that they are an expert (no lab coat, no clipboard, no badge). The reveal at second 8-12 is the conversion point.

Voice rules:
- No em dashes
- No "It's not X, it's Y"
- No "Most guys / most women / most people" openers
- No "obsessed", "game-changing", "transformational"
- The interviewer reacts authentically (real surprise, not staged)
- The stranger's reveal is matter-of-fact ("I'm a nutrition researcher" not "I happen to be a nutrition researcher")
- The product reference is narrow ("the one I tell my clients about is [Brand]" not "[Brand] is the best on the market")

The mechanism explanation should be brief (1-2 lines max). One sentence of mechanism is usually enough. The format trades depth for the legitimacy boost from the reveal moment.
```

## Worked example: Pharmacist / Doctor in-studio output

For a sleep supplement (mechanism: most magnesium forms do not cross the blood-brain barrier):

```
HOOK (PHARMACIST, 0:00-0:04):
"Patients ask me which magnesium to take for sleep almost every day."

LINE 2 (0:04-0:10):
"Most magnesium supplements never reach the brain. The form matters more than the dose."

LINE 3 (0:10-0:18):
"Magnesium oxide, the most common form, has about a 4 percent absorption rate. The body excretes most of it."

LINE 4 (0:18-0:25):
"What you actually want is the glycinate or threonate form. Threonate specifically crosses the blood-brain barrier."

LINE 5 (0:25-0:32):
"There are a few brands that use the threonate form at the right dose. The one I keep recommending is [Brand]."

LINE 6, CLOSING (0:32-0:40):
"At the right form and dose, magnesium changes sleep architecture within about ten days for most people."

VISUAL NOTES:
- Setting: pharmacy counter or compounding lab
- Wardrobe: white coat over button-up, no tie
- Prop: a couple of magnesium bottles on the counter (visible during line 2-3)
- Lighting: even, slightly cool, clinical not warm
- Camera: medium shot, slight angle, occasional cut to bottle close-up

WARDROBE + SETTING NOTES:
- White coat with name embroidered (does not need to be readable, just visible)
- Counter with prescription pad, pill organizer, and a couple of supplement bottles
- Wall behind has shelves with bottles (out of focus)

ESTIMATED RUNTIME: 40 seconds.

NOTES:
- Mechanism enters at line 2 (second 4)
- Product enters at line 5 (second 25), which is 62% of runtime
- For 30-45 second authority format, this is in the acceptable range (60-75%)
- Voice register stays calm and explanatory throughout
- Product is a narrow recommendation ("the one I keep recommending"), not a pitch
- Closes on a clinical-tone fact ("ten days for most people"), not a sales call
- No banned phrases, no em dashes
```

## Worked example: Street interview output

For a collagen supplement (mechanism: most collagen does not survive digestion):

```
HOOK (INTERVIEWER, 0:00-0:04):
"Excuse me, random question. What's the most underrated thing for skin?"

LINE 2 (STRANGER, 0:04-0:09):
"Honestly? Most collagen products never even survive digestion."

LINE 3 (INTERVIEWER, 0:09-0:12):
"Wait... what do you do?"

LINE 4 (STRANGER, 0:12-0:15):
"I'm a nutrition researcher. I study bioavailability."

LINE 5 (INTERVIEWER, 0:15-0:20):
"So most skincare supplements don't actually reach the skin?"

LINE 6 (STRANGER, 0:20-0:27):
"Most don't. The structure breaks down before absorption."

LINE 7 (STRANGER, 0:27-0:32):
"There are a few that use the bonded form. The one I tell my clients about is [Brand]."

VISUAL NOTES:
- Setting: actual sidewalk, midday, casual
- Stranger wardrobe: t-shirt and jeans, sunglasses pushed up
- Interviewer wardrobe: hoodie, holding a phone (the camera)
- Stranger holds a coffee cup (small prop that signals "regular person")
- The reveal in line 4 has no visual cue. The stranger looks the same as in line 1.
- Phone-camera framing throughout

TIMESTAMPED REVEAL MOMENT: 0:09 (interviewer's "Wait... what do you do?")

ESTIMATED RUNTIME: 32 seconds.

NOTES:
- Hook is a casual non-product question (about skin)
- Stranger answers with surprising specificity in line 2 ("Most collagen products never even survive digestion") which triggers the reveal
- Reveal at line 4 (second 12) is past the 8-second minimum
- Mechanism explained in line 6, briefly (one sentence)
- Product reference in line 7 is narrow ("The one I tell my clients about is [Brand]")
- Stranger looks like a normal person until the reveal
- No banned phrases, no em dashes
```

## Common failure modes when running these prompts

### The pharmacist sounds like a creator in costume

The model produces a script that wears a white coat but speaks in marketing copy.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "Match how real pharmacists explain things at the counter. Watch a YouTube interview with a clinical pharmacist if you need a tone reference. Calm, slightly slow, specific, slightly clinical."

### The reveal happens too early in the street interview

The stranger says their profession in line 1 or line 2. The format collapses.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "Authority reveal cannot happen before line 4 (second 8). The stranger must feel like a normal person for the first 8 seconds. Their first answer should be specific enough to make the interviewer ask, but not so specific that it gives away their profession."

### The product is positioned as the best on the market

The narrow recommendation gets inflated into a sales pitch.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "The product enters as a narrow recommendation. Use phrasing like 'the one I keep recommending' or 'the one I tell my clients about'. Never 'the best on the market' or 'the only one that works'."

### The mechanism explanation goes too deep

The pharmacist or street researcher gets technical for 4-5 lines, losing the audience.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "One sentence of mechanism is usually enough. Two maximum. More than two and the script collapses into an ad."

### The interviewer's reaction sounds staged

"Wait... what do you do?" reads as performance, not real surprise.

**Fix:** Add to the prompt: "The interviewer's reaction should sound like real surprise. Use a brief pause, a slight stumble, or a casual phrase like 'Hold on' or 'Wait'."
